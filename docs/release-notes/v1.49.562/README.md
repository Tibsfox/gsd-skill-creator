## Summary

Three bounces and a retrieval. On April 17, 1967, at 07:05:01 UTC, an Atlas-Centaur rocket rose from Launch Complex 36B at Cape Kennedy carrying the third Surveyor spacecraft on a direct-ascent trajectory to the Moon. Sixty-five hours later, on April 20 at 00:04:17 UTC, Surveyor 3 entered powered descent over Oceanus Procellarum in the same broad basalt plain where Surveyor 1 had landed ten months earlier and where Lunar Orbiter 3 had photographed Surveyor 1 from orbit two months before that. The landing sequence was nominal until the last fourteen meters. Then the radar altimeter, confused by a highly reflective patch of regolith, reported touchdown early and failed to command vernier-engine shutdown. The three 30-pound-force thrusters kept firing. Surveyor 3 bounced. It rose 10 meters, came down, bounced again 11 meters up, came down, bounced a third time to about 3 meters, and finally settled on a 12-degree crater wall 25 seconds after its first contact with the lunar surface. The spacecraft landed intact. Every instrument survived. Every antenna deployed. The soft-landing program that had just clawed back from the total loss of Surveyor 2 seven months earlier now had its second success -- achieved through an uncontrolled maneuver that no mission planner had designed. The machine landed itself, badly, on the third try, and then went to work.

Two and a half years later, on November 20, 1969, Charles P. "Pete" Conrad Jr. and Alan Bean set the Apollo 12 Lunar Module Intrepid down in Oceanus Procellarum 163 meters from Surveyor 3. This was the closest precision lunar landing that had ever been achieved; Apollo 11's target circle had been ten kilometers; Conrad's target was the Surveyor itself. Conrad and Bean walked to Surveyor 3 across a sloping crater wall, approached the spacecraft from its uphill side to avoid overturning it, and cut its television camera free with bolt cutters. They also retrieved the scoop head of the Soil Mechanics Surface Sampler, the aluminum cables of the spacecraft's bulk-regolith bearings, and a section of tubing. The Smithsonian National Air and Space Museum holds the camera today. Conrad's four-mission lunar arc closes in retrospect at engine degree 56: he commanded Gemini 11 at degree 49 (September 1966), watched Surveyor 2 fail at degree 50 (September 1966), watched Surveyor 3 succeed at degree 56 (April 1967), and walked to Surveyor 3 on Apollo 12 thirty-two months after this degree's landing. The engine's first four-degree narrative thread resolves here. The degree 56 dedication reads accordingly.

Surveyor 3 was also the first machine that dug on another world. The Soil Mechanics Surface Sampler, built at Hughes Aircraft Company's Culver City division and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Pasadena under Floyd Roberson and Ronald Scott, was a small aluminum scoop on the end of a motorized pantograph arm. It could extend 1.5 meters, rotate 112 degrees, and close its jaws with 150 newtons of force. Roberson and Scott directed the sampler from the Space Flight Operations Facility at JPL across the Earth-Moon round-trip light delay. They commanded it to dig four trenches in the Oceanus Procellarum regolith, to pick up and drop individual rocks, to press its jaws against the surface and measure bearing strength, and to record every motion on the spacecraft's slow-scan television camera at one frame every 3.6 seconds. The TV footage of the sampler digging came back to Pasadena as the first moving-picture record of an engineered excavation on another planetary body. The SMSS returned soil-mechanics data that cleared the Ocean of Storms as a landing site for Apollo 12 and returned depth-versus-bearing-strength profiles that no later lander has improved upon in the equatorial mare. The first dig preceded the first footprint by two years, five months, and a day.

In Seattle in 2007, on Capitol Hill, Tim Wilson and Ryan Carbary began writing songs together. They had met through the city's network of singer-songwriters and coffee-shop open microphones, and their voices -- Wilson's slightly brighter, Carbary's slightly deeper, both trained in the same harmony tradition that had produced Fleet Foxes on the other side of the hill -- blended on the first attempt. In 2009 they traveled to Los Angeles to record their debut EP *The Verse, The Chorus* with producer Eli Thomson. During the sessions Thomson suggested a band name drawn from Fyodor Dostoyevsky's *The Brothers Karamazov*: Ivan, the intellectual who argues against God in the "Grand Inquisitor" chapter, and Alyosha, the novice monk who embodies compassion without arguing. Thomson's idea was that the ampersand between the two names would hold the unresolved argument in place forever. The band took it. Ivan & Alyosha became the first literary-named band in the Seattle 360 engine. Wilson and Carbary expanded to a five-piece -- Wilson's brother Pete on bass, Tim Kim on second guitar, Cole Mauro on drums -- and signed to Dualtone Records, the Nashville independent that also released the Lumineers. *All The Times We Had* came out on Dualtone in 2013, *It's All Just Pretend* on Dualtone in 2015, *Tears On Soul* on the Vancouver-based Nettwerk Music Group in 2017, and *Labor of Love* on Nettwerk in 2020. Along the way they performed with the Seattle Symphony at Benaroya Hall under conductor Lee Mills, with string arrangements by Andrew Joslyn that had also scored Macklemore, Damien Jurado, and Brandi Carlile records.

The Pass 1 CSV for degree 54 listed Tender Loving Empire as the label for Ivan & Alyosha. Tender Loving Empire is a Portland record shop and independent label with no documented connection to the band. Pass 2 corrects this: the labels of record are Dualtone Records in Nashville and Nettwerk Music Group in Vancouver. The engine's CSV correction history now includes the Ivan & Alyosha label attribution alongside earlier corrections such as the Sonics' Etiquette-to-Jerden reissue path and the Brandi Carlile Columbia-to-ATO transition.

The structural shape of Ivan & Alyosha's music -- bright jangling guitars, close fraternal harmony, Andrew Joslyn string arrangements that expand modest folk-pop songs into symphonic scale, unresolved argument held inside the ampersand -- is the same shape as Surveyor 3's triple bounce. Two brothers, two bounces past stable, three impacts before settling. Dostoyevsky's Ivan and Alyosha argue without resolving; the novel never picks a winner between doubt and faith. Surveyor 3's radar altimeter did not pick a winner between "descend" and "stop" -- it kept firing the verniers and kept bouncing. Both systems refused to shut off at the nominal stopping point, both arrived somewhere other than expected, and both got retrieved. The Surveyor camera went to the Smithsonian; the brothers' argument went to every philosophy seminar since 1880. The degree 56 pairing is not literary ornament; it is a shared operational envelope. Systems that refuse nominal shutdown, that land on the third try, that preserve the argument by refusing to foreclose it. The engine documents the parallel.

In the mixed conifer and riparian alder stands of the Pacific Northwest foothills, at a mature red alder along the edge of a forest creek, a male Red-breasted Sapsucker clings to the bark. The entire head is crimson -- crown, nape, face, throat, upper breast, all one unbroken deep red, the most saturated head color on any North American woodpecker. The belly is yellow. The back is black with white bars and two white "braces" patches. The bird leans forward and taps once. Not a hammer blow. Not a drum roll. A deliberate, measured peck that opens a small round hole through the outer bark to the phloem. The bird moves a centimeter and taps again. And again. A neat horizontal row of tiny wells opens across the alder trunk. Sap beads at each hole like amber. Within minutes a Rufous Hummingbird arrives, hovers behind the sapsucker at a respectful distance, and waits its turn at the wells. *Sphyrapicus ruber*. Picidae. First Picidae in the engine since Northern Flicker at degrees 21 and 22 -- a 32-degree family-return gap, the longest in engine history. New genus *Sphyrapicus* enters. The 23rd bird family holds; the Picidae count rises from 2 to 3. But where the flicker was a ground-foraging ant-eating bird that drummed on hollow snags at a steady 25 strikes per second, the sapsucker is a sap-well specialist on living trees that drums arrhythmically -- starting fast, decelerating, accelerating again, refusing to lock into a tempo. The engine's first arrhythmic Picidae. The jazz drummer of Pacific Northwest woodpeckers. The cat-voiced bird: its mewing territorial call is so catlike that field guides warn novices not to search for a kitten when they hear it. Nasal, descending, peak energy 2.0 to 3.5 kHz. Species-diagnostic even at distances where the red head is invisible through canopy.

The Red-breasted Sapsucker is a keystone ecosystem engineer. Its sap wells are shared infrastructure. The round wells tap ascending xylem sap for quick sugar; the rectangular wells tap descending phloem sap for richer nutrition; the two are drilled on different trees and maintained on different schedules through the season. Once active, a well tree becomes a food resource for at least 42 documented dependent species in the Pacific Northwest. The Rufous Hummingbird is the most famous dependent -- it times its migration northward from Mexico to arrive in the alder zone of coastal Washington just as the sapsucker's early-spring wells are active, before the red-flowering currant and salmonberry have opened. No other nectar source is available that early. The Anna's Hummingbird uses the wells year-round in milder zones. Orange-crowned Warblers, Yellow-rumped Warblers, Ruby-crowned Kinglets, and half a dozen insect-gleaning passerines drink from the wells and catch attracted flies, wasps, and midges. Red Squirrels lick the sap. Rufous Hummingbirds defend active well trees as their own territory. This is infrastructure built by one species that sustains a web of others, and the Red-breasted Sapsucker is the first species in the Seattle 360 / Sound of Puget Sound catalog whose primary ecological role is the physical creation of that infrastructure. The Steller's Jay at degree 53 was a sentinel -- information. The American Dipper at degree 52 was a stream specialist -- movement. The Red-breasted Sapsucker at degree 56 is an engineer -- the first built environment of the SPS corpus.

The Rufous Hummingbird at engine degree 35 becomes meaningful in retrospect at degree 56. At the time of its degree 35 entry, the Rufous Hummingbird's long-distance migration to Alaska was the narrative subject; its dependence on early-season sugar was noted but not centered. The Red-breasted Sapsucker's arrival at degree 56 reveals that the sugar source is the sap well, and the sap well is drilled by a woodpecker that follows a 32-degree absence in the engine. The hidden thread is 21 degrees long -- from degree 35 (*Selasphorus rufus*) to degree 56 (*Sphyrapicus ruber*) -- and it is the longest retroactive species-side cross-reference the engine has produced so far. The degree 35 retrospective did not know about the sapsucker. The degree 56 retrospective knows about both. Every subsequent ecosystem-engineer species entering the engine will reference this pattern.

Two kinds of digging mirror each other across degree 56, in parallel. The Surveyor 3 Soil Mechanics Surface Sampler dug four trenches in Oceanus Procellarum regolith, returned data that cleared the Apollo 11 and Apollo 12 landing sites, and produced the first motion-picture record of an engineered excavation on another world. The Red-breasted Sapsucker drills sap wells in alder and pine bark, returns a food resource that sustains 42+ dependent species, and produces the first documented animal-built public infrastructure of the SPS corpus. Both are load-bearing first-digs. Both return data -- the SMSS returned soil-mechanics bearing strength curves; the sapsucker's wells return sugar and protein to hummingbirds, warblers, and kinglets that would otherwise have nothing to eat in early spring. Both dig at scales mismatched to their purpose -- the SMSS scraped a few hundred cubic centimeters of regolith and the data cleared a mission that placed twelve astronauts on the Moon; the sapsucker drills cubic-millimeter wells and the wells sustain two hummingbird species across half a continent. Small, precise excavation yielding disproportionate downstream effect on both sides. The engine's first cross-track keystone-infrastructure parallel registers here.

The Surveyor program itself has a shape now. Surveyor 1 at engine degree 45 (June 2, 1966) soft-landed on the first attempt. Surveyor 2 at degree 50 (September 20, 1966) tumbled and impacted hard after one of the three vernier engines failed to ignite during midcourse correction. Surveyor 3 at degree 56 (April 20, 1967) soft-landed on its third bounce, proving the Surveyor 2 vernier-valve fix but also exposing a new failure mode in the radar altimeter's interaction with highly reflective regolith. The Hughes Aircraft Company / Jet Propulsion Laboratory engineering team -- the same team dedicated at v1.49.555 for the vernier-valve fix after Surveyor 2 -- corrected the altimeter logic for Surveyor 5 (Surveyor 4 was lost during retro burn in July 1967, the second total loss). Surveyor 5 landed cleanly on September 11, 1967. Surveyors 6 and 7 followed. The program's final record: seven missions, five soft landings, two total losses. The triple bounce of Surveyor 3 is the program's most-discussed non-failure -- a spacecraft that refused nominal shutdown, bounced three times past stable, and arrived somewhere other than expected. Intact. Operational. Retrievable. The fix retrieved.

The Apollo 1 shadow carries forward from degree 54 (January 27, 1967) into degree 56 (April 20, 1967) as L+83 days. The crewed Apollo program is still grounded; the Block II Command Module redesign at North American Aviation is in its second quarter; the ASPO at Houston has just completed its internal review. The robotic program's eighty-three-day interval since the fire includes Lunar Orbiter 3 (February 5), Surveyor 3 (April 17 launch, April 20 landing), and the beginning of preparation for Lunar Orbiter 4 (May 4) and Surveyor 4 (July 14). The ratio of robotic to crewed lunar activity in the Apollo-era shadow quarter is currently infinite: three robotic missions launched, one Block I Command Module burned on Pad 34, zero Block II flights. The engine documents the asymmetry. The unmanned work continues because the unmanned side's discipline holds. Pete Conrad, grounded with the rest of Astronaut Class 3 during the Block II redesign, spent April 1967 rotating through the Block II mockups at Downey while Surveyor 3 was bouncing on the Ocean of Storms. He did not know that thirty-two months later he would walk to that exact spacecraft and cut its camera free. The engine knows. It documents the convergence.

## Part A: Ivan & Alyosha -- THE FRATERNAL JANGLE

- **Capitol Hill formation and the 2007 duo.** Tim Wilson and Ryan Carbary met on Capitol Hill in Seattle through the network of open microphones and singer-songwriter nights that had produced David Bazan's Pedro the Lion (engine degree 47), Damien Jurado (degree 45), and the early cohort around Sub Pop and Barsuk. They began writing together in 2007. The Capitol Hill anchor -- dense walkable neighborhood centered on Neumos, Barboza, the Comet Tavern, and the Egyptian Theatre -- had already produced Shawn Smith at engine degree 41 (Rock/Alternative) and Pedro the Lion at degree 47. Ivan & Alyosha at degree 56 is the third Capitol Hill-anchored artist in the engine, extending the neighborhood's cultural gravity into a new pair.
- **The Dostoyevsky name and Eli Thomson's 2009 suggestion.** During the Los Angeles sessions for the debut EP *The Verse, The Chorus* (2009), producer Eli Thomson suggested Ivan and Alyosha Karamazov -- the intellectual-skeptic brother and the compassionate-monk brother from Fyodor Dostoyevsky's final novel *The Brothers Karamazov* (1880) -- as the band name. The ampersand holds the unresolved argument between reason and faith. Thomson's idea registered as the first literary-named band in the Seattle 360 engine. Every later literary-named band will inherit this template.
- **Five-piece Dualtone era and the brothers-in-a-band-named-for-brothers.** Tim Wilson's brother Pete Wilson joined on bass, Tim Kim on second guitar, and Cole Mauro on drums, forming the five-piece that signed to Dualtone Records in Nashville for *All The Times We Had* (2013) and *It's All Just Pretend* (2015). The Wilson brothers -- real-life siblings performing under a name borrowed from fictional Karamazov brothers -- extend the engine's sibling pattern established by Ann and Nancy Wilson of Heart (degree 43) and by Rocky and Cody Votolato of Waxwing (degree 46). The real and the literary converge inside the five-piece era.
- **Dualtone Records and the Nashville independent lineage.** Dualtone Records, founded in Nashville in 2001 by Scott Robinson and Dan Herrington, is an independent label whose roster at the time of Ivan & Alyosha's *All The Times We Had* (2013) also included the Lumineers, Shovels & Rope, and Old Crow Medicine Show. Dualtone's aesthetic -- acoustic-forward production, emphasis on lyric clarity and vocal harmony, moderate production budgets, strong radio and KEXP support -- defined the album's sound. The label's Nashville base gave Ivan & Alyosha access to Nashville session musicians (string players, pedal steel, Wurlitzer) without forcing the band into a Nashville country-music aesthetic. First Dualtone Records entry in the engine.
- **Nettwerk Music Group and the Vancouver pivot.** *Tears On Soul* (2017) and *Labor of Love* (2020) shifted Ivan & Alyosha to Nettwerk Music Group, the Vancouver-based independent label founded by Terry McBride in 1984 that had built its catalog around Sarah McLachlan, Sinead O'Connor, Coldplay's early licensing, and the Barenaked Ladies. The Nettwerk pivot put Ivan & Alyosha's catalog into a Canadian-Pacific-Northwest independent label that mirrors the band's own Capitol Hill geography. First Nettwerk entry in the engine.
- **Andrew Joslyn string arrangements.** Andrew Joslyn's string arrangements on Ivan & Alyosha records (particularly the orchestral expansion on *It's All Just Pretend*) tie the band into a lineage of Seattle and regional strings-for-folk-pop work that also includes Macklemore's "Same Love," Damien Jurado's late Secretly Canadian records, and Brandi Carlile's *The Firewatcher's Daughter*. Joslyn's discipline is to treat the string section as harmonic scaffolding rather than ornamental top-dressing; his arrangements raise the pop-folk song into symphonic scale without overwhelming the vocal intimacy. Third Joslyn-adjacent engine entry (Jurado at degree 45, Carlile at degree 52, Ivan & Alyosha at degree 56).
- **Benaroya Hall with the Seattle Symphony.** Ivan & Alyosha performed with the Seattle Symphony at Benaroya Hall under conductor Lee Mills, an orchestral-indie-folk collaboration that echoed earlier Benaroya Hall appearances by Brandi Carlile (*Live at Benaroya Hall*, 2011) and Damien Jurado (in support of *Visions of Us on the Land*, 2016). The Benaroya pattern -- Seattle independent artist crossing into the 2,500-seat symphony venue with full orchestra -- is now an engine motif. Ivan & Alyosha's appearance is the engine's fourth Benaroya Hall crossing.
- **The CSV correction: Tender Loving Empire to Dualtone / Nettwerk.** The Pass 1 Seattle 360 CSV listed Tender Loving Empire as the label for Ivan & Alyosha. TLE is a Portland, Oregon record shop and independent label that has released Typhoon, AgesandAges, and Y La Bamba among others, with no documented connection to Ivan & Alyosha. Pass 2 corrects the attribution. The band's actual label history is Dualtone Records (Nashville, 2013 and 2015) and Nettwerk Music Group (Vancouver, 2017 and 2020). The correction is the engine's tenth Pass-2 CSV fix; the Sonics' Etiquette-to-Jerden reissue-path correction at degree 55 is the most recent predecessor. The engine documents data-quality work as first-class artifact.
- **Album arc: *All The Times We Had* (2013) to *Labor of Love* (2020).** The album titles across four studio records trace a relational arc: *All The Times We Had* (2013, memorial), *It's All Just Pretend* (2015, philosophical -- Ivan's doubt wins the argument in the title), *Tears On Soul* (2017, elegiac), *Labor of Love* (2020, committed). The shift from the memorial past tense of 2013 to the committed present of 2020 parallels the shift from the Dualtone Nashville-independent era to the Nettwerk Vancouver-independent era. The Wilson-Carbary core held through all four records; the five-piece band expanded, contracted, and stabilized around the two founders.
- **Folk-adjacent streak reignites at one.** The Sonics at engine degree 55 broke the folk-adjacent streak at ten degrees (45-54, the all-time record), and degree 56's Ivan & Alyosha re-opens folk-adjacent from a new ignition point. ## Part B: Red-breasted Sapsucker -- THE ARRHYTHMIC WELL

- **Picidae returns after 32 degrees.** The Red-breasted Sapsucker is the first Picidae in the engine since Northern Flicker at engine degrees 21 and 22. Thirty-two degrees of absence. This is the longest family-return gap in engine history, exceeding the prior record by a wide margin. The family count rises from 2 (Flicker at 21, Flicker at 22) to 3 (Flicker at 21, Flicker at 22, Sapsucker at 56). Picidae now ties Trochilidae (hummingbirds: Rufous at 35, Anna's at 36, Calliope at 37) and Paridae (chickadees and titmice) at three engine entries each. The 23rd family is held by Turdidae (Varied Thrush at 55); Picidae remains a high-seniority family at position 8.
- **New *Sphyrapicus* genus.** *Sphyrapicus ruber* is the first *Sphyrapicus* genus entry in the engine. Prior Picidae entries were genus *Colaptes* (Northern Flicker). *Sphyrapicus* represents a fundamentally different woodpecker strategy -- sap-extraction specialist on living trees, with specialized brush-tipped tongue, crop adapted for sap storage, and territorial wells drilled in precise horizontal and vertical rows. Same family as the flicker; entirely different ecological niche. The engine now holds two Picidae genera.
- **Keystone ecosystem engineer -- the sap-well infrastructure.** The Red-breasted Sapsucker's sap wells are shared resources. Round wells tap ascending xylem sap (quick sugar, early morning); rectangular wells tap descending phloem sap (richer sugars and nutrients, later in the day). Each well tree is maintained across multiple seasons. At least 42 documented Pacific Northwest species use active sapsucker wells as food resources, including Rufous Hummingbird, Anna's Hummingbird, Orange-crowned Warbler, Yellow-rumped Warbler, Ruby-crowned Kinglet, Red Squirrel, and a suite of insect-gleaning passerines that catch the flies, wasps, and midges attracted to the sap. The sapsucker is the first SPS-corpus species whose primary ecological role is the creation of shared infrastructure -- distinct from the Steller's Jay's role as sentinel (information), the American Dipper's role as stream-specialist (movement), or the Belted Kingfisher's role as predator (extraction). First keystone-infrastructure species in the engine.
- **The Rufous Hummingbird callback after 21 degrees.** At engine degree 35, the Rufous Hummingbird (*Selasphorus rufus*) entered with its long-distance migration northward from Mexico as its narrative subject. At the time, its dependence on early-season sugar before flowers bloom was noted but not foregrounded. Degree 56's Red-breasted Sapsucker reveals that the early-season sugar source is the sap well and that Rufous Hummingbirds time their arrival on the alder zone to coincide with sapsucker well activation. The degree 35-to-56 thread is 21 degrees long -- the longest retroactive species-side cross-reference the engine has produced so far, exceeding the prior record. The engine documents the callback pattern for future species entries.
- **The arrhythmic drumming as species identity.** Unlike every prior Picidae entry in the engine and unlike most birds in the SPS corpus, the Red-breasted Sapsucker's drumming pattern is deliberately arrhythmic. Sequences start fast (~10-15 strikes per second), decelerate (~5-8 strikes per second), and accelerate again. The irregularity is species-diagnostic: where Northern Flicker drumming at degrees 21 and 22 was metronomic resonant power on hollow snags, sapsucker drumming is rubato improvisation on living trunks. The engine's first arrhythmic percussion element.
- **The mewing cat-call and 2.0-3.5 kHz peak energy.** The Red-breasted Sapsucker's territorial call is a nasal descending mew at 1.5 to 4.0 kHz with peak energy between 2.0 and 3.5 kHz, closer in acoustic character to a kitten than to any other Pacific Northwest woodpecker. Field guides warn novices that searching for a cat in a forest without ground cats is the first sign of sapsucker presence. The call is species-diagnostic at distances where the red head is invisible through canopy. First cat-voiced species in the engine.
- **Passeriformes streak breaks.** Engine degree 55's Varied Thrush was Passeriformes (Turdidae). Engine degree 56's Red-breasted Sapsucker is Piciformes. - **Four-level crypsis and the carotenoid-red head.** The Red-breasted Sapsucker exhibits four distinct levels of crypsis: the red head is visible at close range but drab from a distance through canopy; the back-and-wing pattern is cryptic against mottled bark; the drum pattern is cryptic across Picidae species (arrhythmic vs metronomic); the sap-well distribution is cryptic across forest types (alder-riparian vs upland conifer). The crimson head color is carotenoid-based pigmentation (not structural blue as in Steller's Jay at degree 53). Two contrasting optical mechanisms -- carotenoid pigment and structural scattering -- produce the two most visually vivid species in three consecutive degrees (53-56, sapsucker interposed by the Varied Thrush's brown-and-orange palette at 55).
- **Townsend-Nuttall lineage and Fort Vancouver ornithology.** The Red-breasted Sapsucker was described in the mid-nineteenth century from Pacific Northwest specimens by a combination of North American ornithologists including John Kirk Townsend and Thomas Nuttall, whose Oregon Country expedition of 1834 worked from Fort Vancouver and produced type specimens and descriptions for multiple Pacific Northwest endemic species. The engine treats the Townsend-Nuttall lineage as a nineteenth-century taxonomic provenance chain; precise first-description attribution varies across sources and is left to taxonomic authorities. First Townsend-lineage species in the engine.
- **Altitudinal migration and year-round residency.** Unlike the long-distance neotropical migration pattern of Parulidae warblers in the SPS corpus, the Red-breasted Sapsucker is a year-round resident with minor altitudinal migration -- breeding at 300 to 1,500 meters elevation in mixed conifer, wintering at lower elevations in riparian alder and in suburban fruit trees in Seattle, Portland, and Vancouver. Rarely moves more than 500 kilometers between summer and winter range. The second short-distance altitudinal migrant in the engine (Varied Thrush at degree 55 was the first).

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **The triple bounce as narrative thesis.** The Surveyor 3 bounce sequence -- 10 meters up, 11 meters up, 3 meters up, then settled -- was initially classified as a near-failure by the Deep Space Network controllers who watched the telemetry in real time at JPL's Space Flight Operations Facility. Post-flight analysis reclassified it as a margin event: the vernier engines failed in a controllable direction, the crater wall damped the energy, and the spacecraft's triangular landing gear absorbed the impact within design margins. The degree 56 release foregrounds the bounce as the structural thesis -- systems that refuse nominal shutdown, land on the third try, and get retrieved -- and the pairing with Ivan & Alyosha's Dostoyevsky ampersand makes the thesis legible across tracks. The narrative works because both systems share an operational signature.
- **The Conrad arc closure.** Pete Conrad's four-degree arc (Gemini 11 at 49, Surveyor 2 witness at 50, Surveyor 3 success at 56, Apollo 12 retrieval in a future degree) is the engine's first multi-degree astronaut narrative to resolve in retrospect. The dedication to Conrad reads correctly because the arc is now closed on three of four anchors. Future astronaut arcs (Lovell, Aldrin, Armstrong, Borman, Schirra) will inherit this four-degree template.
- **The Rufous Hummingbird / Red-breasted Sapsucker ecological callback.** The 21-degree retroactive cross-reference from degree 35 to degree 56 is the longest species-side hidden thread in the engine. The release documents the callback explicitly and registers the degree 35 entry as partially incomplete in retrospect (the sugar source was noted but not centered). Every ecosystem-engineer species entering the engine in later degrees will reference this callback pattern, and degree 35 will be flagged for a future Pass 3 update that foregrounds the sapsucker dependency.
- **The CSV correction as first-class artifact.** The Tender Loving Empire / Dualtone-Nettwerk correction for Ivan & Alyosha is the tenth Pass-2 CSV fix. Treating label-attribution correction as a documented engine motif (rather than silently fixing the data) produces a running ledger of data-quality work that will carry forward into later passes. Future CSV corrections inherit the "note, document, flag in release prose" template.
- **The Picidae-return-after-32-degrees and new-genus pairing.** The Red-breasted Sapsucker's entry is structurally load-bearing on two axes: longest family-return gap in engine history (32 degrees) and first *Sphyrapicus* genus. Pairing the two facts produces a single high-signal taxonomic note rather than two diffuse ones. Future returning families with new genera will inherit the pairing template.

### What Could Be Better

- **The first-description attribution for *Sphyrapicus ruber* is imprecise.** The release frames the Townsend-Nuttall lineage as a nineteenth-century taxonomic provenance chain rather than citing a specific first-description year and author. This is an honest framing -- sources vary -- but future releases should either resolve the citation through a taxonomic authority (AOU, IOC) or carry the imprecision forward as an explicit engine-corpus open question rather than letting it read as a minor evasion.
- **The Surveyor 3 radar altimeter failure mode is described but not root-caused.** The release states that the radar was "confused by highly reflective regolith" but does not explain why the altimeter's shutdown logic depended on surface reflectivity in the first place. The underlying design issue (the altimeter used reflected pulse strength as a proximity proxy rather than an independent proximity measurement) deserves a one-sentence root-cause note for future engineering retrospectives. Flagged for next-degree amendment if Surveyor 4 or Surveyor 5 enters the engine.
- **Marine species still absent at degree 56.** Fifty-seven degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound" with zero marine bird species. This gap is approaching a two-quartile critical threshold. The next fifteen degrees must close it or the engine's geographic center -- the Sound itself -- will have spent a full quarter of the circle unrepresented on the species side. Flagged at every retrospective since degree 40.
- **The Ivan & Alyosha Andrew Joslyn string arrangement is not quantified.** The release notes Joslyn's arrangements on Ivan & Alyosha records without specifying which tracks or which album. *It's All Just Pretend* (2015) is the likely locus based on general catalog knowledge but specific Joslyn credits per track are not documented in the release. Future Joslyn-adjacent entries should cite specific track credits.

### Lessons Learned

1. **The triple bounce lands.** A system that refuses nominal shutdown can still arrive -- bouncing three times past stable is not the same as failing. Surveyor 3 proves that margin absorption is a valid landing mode when the design reserve is sufficient. Future engineering retrospectives should distinguish "failed" from "bounced-and-landed."

2. **First digging changes the downstream.** The Soil Mechanics Surface Sampler cleared Apollo 12's landing site. The Red-breasted Sapsucker's wells sustain 42+ species including the Rufous Hummingbird. Small, precise excavation produces disproportionate downstream effect on both the lunar and the ecological side. The engine registers first-dig events as load-bearing.

3. **Retrieval closes the loop.** Conrad walked to Surveyor 3 and cut its camera free. The camera is at the Smithsonian. The Karamazov argument is in every philosophy seminar. Retrieval is not extraction; it is the preservation of the artifact beyond the system's operational life. Future mission arcs will inherit the retrieval template.

4. **Narrative arcs resolve in retrospect.** Pete Conrad's four-degree arc (49 -> 50 -> 56 -> future Apollo 12) could not have been written at degree 49. Only degree 56 makes the arc legible. The engine structurally supports retrospective resolution and will continue to track multi-degree astronaut arcs across the Apollo era.

5. **Family returns pair best with new genera.** The Red-breasted Sapsucker's 32-degree Picidae return and first *Sphyrapicus* genus combine into a single high-signal taxonomic event. Pairing the two axes produces cleaner prose than listing them separately. Future returning families with new genera should follow the pairing template.

6. **Hidden threads reveal themselves later.** The degree 35 Rufous Hummingbird entry was complete at the time. The degree 56 Red-breasted Sapsucker entry reveals that the hummingbird's sugar source is the sapsucker's well. Twenty-one degrees of hidden thread. The engine documents retroactive cross-references as a first-class pattern.

7. **Streaks restart after termination.** The Sonics broke the folk-adjacent streak at ten (all-time record) at degree 55. Ivan & Alyosha at degree 56 reignites folk-adjacent at one, not eleven. Termination is not interruption; the broken streak is gone, and the new streak begins fresh. Future retrospectives should follow the same convention.

8. **Keystone ecosystem engineers are rare and load-bearing.** The Red-breasted Sapsucker is the first species in the SPS corpus whose primary role is the physical creation of shared infrastructure. Future keystone-engineer species (beaver, Pacific Salmon, Sitka Spruce if ECO opens) will reference the sapsucker template. The engine's ecological ontology now distinguishes sentinels (information), specialists (movement), predators (extraction), and engineers (infrastructure).

9. **CSV corrections are first-class engine artifacts.** The Tender Loving Empire / Dualtone-Nettwerk fix is the tenth Pass-2 CSV correction. Documenting each correction in release prose produces a running ledger of data-quality work and signals the engine's commitment to honest data rather than silent patches. Future CSV fixes will follow the same visibility convention.

10. **Imprecise citations should be framed explicitly, not evaded.** The Townsend-Nuttall first-description framing is imprecise on purpose -- sources vary -- but the release states the imprecision rather than asserting a false precision. Future releases should follow the same discipline: name the uncertainty, route it to a resolution queue, and avoid confident wrong citations.

*Degree 56: triple bounce to Smithsonian. On April 20, 1967, Surveyor 3 landed on the Ocean of Storms on its third try. The radar altimeter, confused by bright regolith, did not command vernier shutdown at touchdown; the spacecraft bounced three times across twenty-five seconds and settled on a twelve-degree crater wall with every instrument intact. The Soil Mechanics Surface Sampler dug four trenches in the regolith -- the first engineered excavation on another world -- and the photographs returned to Pasadena cleared Apollo 12's landing site. Two and a half years later, Pete Conrad walked to Surveyor 3 and cut its television camera free. The Smithsonian holds it now. In Seattle in 2007, on Capitol Hill, Tim Wilson and Ryan Carbary borrowed Ivan Karamazov's doubt and Alyosha Karamazov's faith from Fyodor Dostoyevsky's final novel, held the unresolved argument inside an ampersand, and made four records of bright fraternal folk-pop with Andrew Joslyn string arrangements on the Dualtone and Nettwerk independent labels. The Benaroya Hall appearance with the Seattle Symphony scaled the duo's intimate harmony into a 2,500-seat concert hall. In a riparian alder along a Pacific Northwest creek, the Red-breasted Sapsucker drilled neat horizontal rows of sap wells and a Rufous Hummingbird hovered behind waiting its turn -- the first Picidae in thirty-two degrees of engine time, the first keystone ecosystem engineer in the SPS corpus, and the twenty-one-degree retroactive callback that reveals why the Rufous Hummingbird's 2006 arrival in the alder zone had always been timed to the sapsucker's wells. Three bounces, two brothers, one keystone. The fix retrieved. Pete Conrad's four-degree arc closed in retrospect across Gemini 11, Surveyor 2, Surveyor 3, and Apollo 12. Persistence is protocol. The mapper mapped, the thrush sang, the Sonics ground, and now the sapsucker digs.*

*"Systems that refuse nominal shutdown can still arrive. Bouncing three times past stable is not the same as failing. The argument preserved by the ampersand is the argument retrieved two and a half years later. The camera on the Smithsonian shelf, the Karamazov dialectic in every seminar, the sap well on the alder trunk, the four trenches in Oceanus Procellarum. The first dig preceded the first footprint by two years, five months, and a day."*
