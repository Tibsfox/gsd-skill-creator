**Old-Growth-Tree Nesting: The Unique-Among-Seabirds Behavior.** The Marbled Murrelet's nesting behavior is ecologically extraordinary: it is the **only seabird** in North America — and one of only a handful globally — that nests in trees rather than in rocky cliffs, burrows, dense colonies, or coastal islands. The species lays its single egg on **moss-covered horizontal branches** high in the canopy of old-growth coniferous trees — primarily **Douglas-fir** (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), **Sitka spruce** (*Picea sitchensis*), and **western hemlock** (*Tsuga heterophylla*) — at heights often exceeding 100 feet above the forest floor. The branches must meet specific structural requirements: sufficient diameter (typically at least 4-6 inches) to support the egg and incubating parent, sufficient horizontal orientation to provide a stable platform, and sufficient moss cover (typically several centimeters deep) to provide the cup-shaped depression in which the egg is cradled. These conditions are generally met only in **old-growth** stands, meaning stands at least 200 years old with trees exceeding approximately 32 inches diameter at breast height; younger second-growth or managed forests rarely provide the required branch structure. The nest site may be as much as **50 miles inland** from the ocean where the adult forages, making the species' life history a daily commute between marine foraging grounds and inland old-growth canopy.

**The Crepuscular Commute and the Keer Keer Keer Call.** The Marbled Murrelet's daily commute between its inland old-growth nest and its coastal foraging grounds operates on a **crepuscular** schedule — dawn and dusk — with adults traveling fast and low through the forest canopy during the low-light windows when visibility to diurnal predators is reduced. The acoustic signature of this commute is the rapid, high, thin **keer keer keer** call — three or more rapid calls delivered in quick succession, each call brief and high-frequency, often described as similar to a rapid alarm-style bird call rather than the sustained vocalizations of most seabirds. The call emerges primarily during the dawn-and-dusk commute windows and is typically heard from the ground as the bird transits overhead at canopy height through the forest-to-ocean corridor. The E=4 calibration reflects the call's clarity at typical detection distances for a trained listener operating at dawn or dusk in old-growth forest interiors, while recognizing that the call's rapid delivery and high frequency make it easy to miss for listeners unfamiliar with the species' vocalization patterns.

**ESA Threatened Status: The Conservation-Legal Framework.** The Marbled Murrelet was listed as **Threatened** under the **Endangered Species Act (ESA)** in **1992** — specifically, the **Washington**, **Oregon**, and **California** populations were listed as a distinct population segment by the **US Fish and Wildlife Service** on October 1, 1992, following extensive scientific review documenting population declines driven primarily by **old-growth logging**. The ESA Threatened status places the species' legal survival in direct correspondence with the preservation of old-growth forest substrate, and the 1994 **Northwest Forest Plan** — the federal management plan establishing late-successional reserves across 24.5 million acres of federal forest land in Washington, Oregon, and Northern California — names the Marbled Murrelet alongside the **Northern Spotted Owl** as the two federally-protected species whose habitat requirements drive federal forest management across the region. The 1992 ESA listing enters the engine's conservation-status vocabulary at degree 178 as the first federally-listed Threatened species documented, establishing the baseline register for future federally-listed species. The direct-proxy framing — wherein the Marbled Murrelet's survival directly tracks the preservation of old-growth forest — is one of the clearest conservation-biology case studies in the Pacific Northwest, and the species' decline documentation across the 1992-present window preserves a continuous record of how federal forest management decisions shape regional seabird populations.

**Olympic National Park, Olympic National Forest, and the Washington Old-Growth Anchor Sites.** The Marbled Murrelet's Washington breeding range is anchored by the **Olympic Peninsula** old-growth forest complex, including **Olympic National Park** (922,650 acres) and surrounding **Olympic National Forest** (633,000 acres), whose temperate rainforest drainages along the Hoh, Queets, Quinault, Bogachiel, and Sol Duc rivers host significant breeding populations. Secondary nesting sites exist in **Mount Rainier National Park** (236,381 acres, established 1899) in the southern Washington Cascade Range, in scattered **Cascade Range** old-growth fragments, and in **Washington State Parks** old-growth parcels including the Quinault Rain Forest viewing sites. The engine's documentation at degree 178 preserves Olympic National Park and Olympic National Forest as the anchor sites for the Marbled Murrelet's Washington range and enters the old-growth Pacific Northwest forest complex into the engine's geography vocabulary as a first-class register.

**The Corvid Predation Pattern and the Recreational Waste Management Conservation Intervention.** The Marbled Murrelet's decline is driven by three primary pressures: (1) **old-growth logging** and the resulting loss of nesting substrate; (2) **corvid predation** of eggs and chicks by **Common Ravens** (*Corvus corax*), **Steller's Jays** (*Cyanocitta stelleri*), and **American Crows** (*Corvus brachyrhynchos*); and (3) **marine prey shifts** driven by climate change, ocean temperature increases, and changing fish population distributions. The corvid predation pattern is particularly significant because it is partially addressable through conservation-management interventions at the regional-human-use scale: corvids are attracted to **human food waste** at **campgrounds**, **trailheads**, **picnic areas**, and **clear-cut edges**, and the elevated corvid density around these human-use zones produces elevated nest predation pressure on Marbled Murrelets nesting within corvid foraging range. ## Part B: Marbled Murrelet — The Fifth Alcidae and the Old-Growth Seabird

The genus-opening establishes the taxonomic register for future *Brachyramphus* references.
- **ESA Threatened Conservation Status.** The Marbled Murrelet was listed as **Threatened** under the **Endangered Species Act (ESA)** in **1992** — Washington, Oregon, and California populations listed as a distinct population segment by **US Fish and Wildlife Service** on October 1, 1992 — entering the engine at degree 178 as the first federally-listed Threatened species and establishing the baseline conservation-status register for future federally-listed species documentation.
- **Old-Growth-Tree-Canopy Nesting.** The species lays its single egg on **moss-covered horizontal branches** high in the canopy of old-growth conifers — primarily **Douglas-fir** (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), **Sitka spruce** (*Picea sitchensis*), and **western hemlock** (*Tsuga heterophylla*) — at heights often exceeding 100 feet above the forest floor, sometimes **50+ miles inland** from the ocean, unique among North American seabirds.
- **Rapid High Thin Keer Keer Keer Call.** The defining acoustic signature is a rapid, high-frequency, thin vocalization delivered as three or more rapid **keer** calls in quick succession during the dawn-and-dusk commute windows between inland nest and coastal foraging grounds, typically heard from the ground as the bird transits overhead at canopy height through the forest-to-ocean corridor at E=4 calibration.
- **Crepuscular Commute Behavior.** The daily commute operates at **dawn** and **dusk** (crepuscular), the low-light windows when the bird can transit the forest canopy with reduced predator visibility; the species' entire life history depends on these crepuscular windows, and the keer keer keer call emerges primarily during these pre-full-light or post-full-light intervals.
- **Olympic National Park and Olympic National Forest Anchor Sites.** The 922,650-acre **Olympic National Park** and 633,000-acre surrounding **Olympic National Forest** on the **Olympic Peninsula** host significant Marbled Murrelet breeding populations across the Hoh, Queets, Quinault, Bogachiel, and Sol Duc river drainages; **Mount Rainier National Park** provides additional nesting habitat at the southern edge of the Washington range.
- **Corvid Predation and Recreational Waste Management Intervention.** Nest predation by **Common Ravens**, **Steller's Jays**, and **American Crows** attracted to human food waste at **campgrounds**, **trailheads**, and **clear-cut edges** is a primary secondary decline driver; the conservation intervention is **recreational waste management** — sealed trash containers, visitor education on food storage, and campground design minimizing food-waste accumulation — preserved in the engine's conservation-intervention vocabulary as a Pacific Northwest seabird conservation first.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Unique-among-seabirds ecological registers warrant distinct documentation from family-shared registers.** The Marbled Murrelet's old-growth-tree-canopy nesting is unique among North American seabirds, placing it in a register distinct from the rocky-coastal, burrow, or cliff-ledge nesting registers that other Alcidae share with broader seabird families. Future species documentation should explicitly flag unique-among-family or unique-among-family-grouping ecological registers as structural firsts distinct from family-shared registers.
2. **ESA Threatened and Endangered listings establish federal-legal framework registers that parallel scientific-observation registers.** The 1992 ESA Threatened listing of the Marbled Murrelet places the species' survival in direct legal correspondence with old-growth preservation, creating a legal-framework register parallel to the scientific-observation register of the species' ecology. Future federally-listed species documentation should preserve both the scientific and legal-framework registers explicitly, and the ESA Threatened baseline established at degree 178 provides the first-entry reference for that register.
3. **Direct-proxy habitat-dependency framing provides the clearest conservation-biology documentation pattern.** The Marbled Murrelet's absolute dependence on old-growth forest makes it a direct proxy for old-growth preservation — the species survives if and only if old-growth survives. The direct-proxy framing is one of the clearest conservation-biology documentation patterns available, and future species documentation should explicitly use the direct-proxy framing where applicable.
4. **Crepuscular operational registers (dawn-and-dusk) are distinct from diurnal and nocturnal registers and warrant distinct documentation.** The Marbled Murrelet's daily commute operates at dawn and dusk (crepuscular), distinct from the diurnal operations of most seabirds and distinct from the nocturnal operations of the Rhinoceros Auklet's darkness-only colony returns. Future behavioral-ecology documentation should explicitly distinguish crepuscular from diurnal and nocturnal registers where the pattern is relevant.
5. **Five-degree family runs produce behavioral-diversity documentation at a higher resolution than shorter runs.** The 174-178 Alcidae run at five consecutive degrees spans five distinct nesting registers and produces the highest-resolution behavioral-diversity documentation available in the species catalog to date. The family-run structure at five consecutive degrees suggests that longer family runs (six, seven, or more consecutive degrees) would produce even higher-resolution diversity documentation and provide forward-looking prediction for future long family runs.
6. **Label-trilogy three-degree documentation preserves the opening-middle-conclusion structural function cleanly.** The Nastymix trilogy (Kid Sensation 177 + High Performance 178 + Sir Mix-a-Lot 179) across three consecutive degrees provides a template for future three-degree focused documentation of other label infrastructures. 8. **The 49.44% rotation mark at degree 178 is two degrees short of the halfway mark and provides structural context for halfway-approach framing.** Degree 178's position at 49.44% of the full 360 rotation places it two degrees short of the symbolic halfway point at degree 180, providing structural context for halfway-approach framing across the adjacent degrees 177-180 window.
