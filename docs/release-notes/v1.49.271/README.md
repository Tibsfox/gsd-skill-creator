Unlike the Canada Goose and Cackling Goose, which are comfortable in fields, parks, and inland lakes, the Brant is a **marine specialist** — almost exclusively found along the coast during winter, feeding in shallow bays and tidal flats. The PNW subspecies is the **Black Brant** (*Branta bernicla nigricans*), distinguished from the Atlantic Brant (*B. b. hrota*) by its darker overall appearance and incomplete white necklace. The Black Brant breeds on the Arctic tundra of Alaska and northern Canada (**Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta**, Banks Island, other Arctic sites) and winters along the Pacific Coast from Alaska through the Pacific Northwest and into **Baja California, Mexico** — particularly the lagoons of the Baja Pacific coast (**San Quintin Bay**, **Guerrero Negro**). The Pacific Brant flyway is one of the longest single-subspecies migrations in North American waterfowl.

**The Low Musical Honk as Acoustic Diagnostic.** The Brant's vocalization is the third acoustic signature in the three-*Branta* sequence, and it differs clearly from both of its cousins. The call is a **low, rolling, throaty "r-r-rronk" or "cronk"** — often described as a musical, gargling sound. Frequency range 200-1,200 Hz, centered lower than the Cackling Goose (400-2,000 Hz) and narrower than the Canada Goose (150-1,500 Hz). Not the resonant brassy "honk" of the Canada Goose; not the sharp squeaky "cack" of the Cackling Goose. The Brant sound is **softer, more rolling, more musical**. The rolled "r" quality is distinctive and immediately recognizable. The defining acoustic experience of the Brant is the **flock murmur**: hundreds or thousands of birds feeding on eelgrass produce a continuous, low-pitched conversational sound — a rolling gabble that fills a bay at low tide. This is not the dramatic V-formation honking of Canada Goose migrations; it is an intimate, busy, communal sound. When disturbed (often by **Bald Eagles**), the entire flock takes off simultaneously, and the wingbeats plus flight calls of hundreds of birds create a brief, intense acoustic event that subsides as the birds settle back to the water.

**The Eelgrass Obligate and Indicator Species Status.** The Brant's relationship with **eelgrass** (*Zostera marina*) is one of the most specialized dietary dependencies in North American waterfowl. During the wintering season on the Pacific Coast, Brant feed **almost exclusively on eelgrass**. They are head-dipping feeders — submerging their head and neck to reach eelgrass in shallow water (typically less than 1 meter deep). They do not dive like ducks. This feeding method restricts them to the shallowest portions of eelgrass beds, typically at low to mid-tide. When eelgrass is unavailable, Brant will eat sea lettuce (*Ulva*), other marine algae, and occasionally upland grasses, but these are secondary foods. The eelgrass dependency makes the Brant the **most ecologically constrained goose in the PNW**. Canada Geese thrive in parks, fields, and urban areas. Cackling Geese use agricultural fields. Brant need healthy coastal bays with intact eelgrass beds. The specialization makes them an **indicator species** for coastal bay ecosystem health. Eelgrass beds in parts of Puget Sound have declined due to water-quality degradation, coastal development, increased turbidity, boat traffic, and climate change. Brant populations track eelgrass availability. Declining Brant numbers at a site can signal eelgrass decline before monitoring programs detect it. The bird is a real-time biological sensor for coastal bay condition — the canary in the eelgrass mine.

**Padilla Bay as Winter Hub.** **Padilla Bay** in Skagit County, Washington, hosts one of the largest eelgrass beds on the Pacific Coast of Washington — approximately **8,000 acres of eelgrass**, protected as the **Padilla Bay National Estuarine Research Reserve** (NERR). Thousands of Brant winter here, feeding on the extensive eelgrass beds at low tide and producing the continuous low-pitched murmuring that defines the bay's winter acoustic character. Padilla Bay is the single most important site for Brant conservation in Washington, and the Padilla Bay Shore Trail affords a direct observational connection to the Brant-eelgrass-Puget Sound system. **Boundary Bay** on the US-Canada border is another major Brant wintering site, where extensive mudflats and eelgrass beds support thousands of Brant alongside other waterfowl and where binational management coordination is required. Smaller numbers winter at **Dungeness Bay**, **Sequim Bay**, **Penn Cove** (Whidbey Island), and various outer-coast Washington and Oregon sites. The eelgrass-Brant system connects to the broader Puget Sound ecosystem in ways that extend far beyond a single species: eelgrass beds are nursery habitat for juvenile Chinook salmon (endangered), Pacific herring (forage fish), and Dungeness crab; they stabilize sediment, reduce erosion, and sequester carbon. Protecting eelgrass for Brant also protects salmon, herring, crab, and the broader marine food web. The Brant is a charismatic entry point for eelgrass conservation — an argument for ecosystem protection that transcends any single species.

## Part B: Brant — The Low Musical Honk and the Eelgrass Obligate

- **Fourteenth consecutive Anatidae extends the all-time family-streak record to five beyond Picidae.** The **Brant** (*Branta bernicla*) extends the **Anatidae** streak to **fourteen consecutive waterfowl** at degrees 118-131. The streak spans Harlequin Duck (118, E=5), Common Merganser (119, E=4), Hooded Merganser (120, E=4), Wood Duck (121, E=3), Bufflehead (122, E=3), Barrow's Goldeneye (123, E=3), Common Goldeneye (124, E=4), Greater Scaup (125, E=3), Canvasback (126, E=4), Tundra Swan (127, E=5), Trumpeter Swan (128, E=6), Canada Goose (129, E=5), Cackling Goose (130, E=4), Brant (131, E=4). Fourteen species. Seven genera. Every major Anatidae subfamily present in the Pacific Northwest. The streak is now **five degrees beyond the Picidae nonet** (107-115, 9 consecutive) that previously held the record.
- **Third *Branta* entry completes the three-consecutive-species genus run.** Degree 129 opened the genus ***Branta*** with the **Canada Goose** (large, deep honk, E=5, partially resident). Degree 130 continued with the **Cackling Goose** (small, higher cackle, E=4, purely migratory tundra breeder). Degree 131 closes with the **Brant** (small-medium, low musical honk, E=4, purely migratory marine specialist). Three species, one genus, three adjacent degrees — the engine's first three-consecutive-species genus run in Anatidae. The completion of the trio demonstrates that same-genus species can occupy three distinct acoustic niches and three distinct habitat specializations.
- **The Black Brant (*B. b. nigricans*) as the PNW wintering subspecies.** The Brant that winter in the PNW are primarily the **Black Brant** subspecies. Appearance: dark overall; black head, neck, breast, and back; **incomplete white necklace** (a patch rather than a ring); dark belly with narrow white barring on the flanks; short black bill; small size (1.0-1.8 kg, 56-66 cm body length). Compared to other geese: distinctly smaller than the Canada Goose and similar in size to the Cackling Goose. The **all-dark appearance without the white cheek patch** (diagnostic of Canada/Cackling Geese) is the quickest field mark. The Atlantic Brant (*B. b. hrota*, winters on the US Atlantic Coast) has a lighter belly and more complete white necklace. Both feed primarily on eelgrass.
- **The low musical honk as distinctive acoustic signature.** The Brant's vocalization is **low, rolling, throaty** — a muttering, musical, gargling sound often written as "crronk" or "r-r-rronk." The **rolled "r" quality** is distinctive and immediately recognizable. Frequency range 200-1,200 Hz, centered lower than the Cackling Goose (400-2,000 Hz) and narrower than the Canada Goose (150-1,500 Hz). The defining acoustic experience is the **flock murmur**: hundreds of birds feeding on eelgrass produce a continuous, low-pitched conversational sound — a rolling gabble that fills a bay. This is an intimate, busy, communal sound, distinct from Canada Goose migration V-formation honking. In flight Brant produce a slightly higher, more nasal version. Alarm calls are sharper and higher.
- **The eelgrass obligate as most specialized dietary dependency among PNW geese.** During wintering on the Pacific Coast, Brant feed **almost exclusively on eelgrass** (*Zostera marina*) — one of the most specialized dietary dependencies in North American waterfowl. Brant are **head-dipping feeders**: they submerge head and neck to reach eelgrass in shallow water (typically less than 1 meter deep). They do not dive like ducks. This feeding method restricts them to the shallowest portions of eelgrass beds, typically at low to mid-tide. Supplementary foods (sea lettuce *Ulva*, other marine algae, occasionally upland grasses) are secondary. The eelgrass dependency makes the Brant the **most ecologically constrained goose in the PNW**, vulnerable to eelgrass decline in ways that generalist Canada Geese and field-using Cackling Geese are not.
- **Padilla Bay National Estuarine Research Reserve as primary Washington wintering site.** **Padilla Bay** in Skagit County, Washington, hosts one of the largest eelgrass beds on the Pacific Coast — approximately **8,000 acres of eelgrass**, designated the **Padilla Bay National Estuarine Research Reserve** (NERR). Thousands of Brant winter here, feeding on exposed eelgrass at low tide and producing the continuous low-pitched murmuring that defines the bay's winter acoustic character. Padilla Bay is the single most important site for Brant conservation in Washington, and the Padilla Bay Shore Trail affords a direct public observational connection to the Brant-eelgrass-Puget Sound system. The concentration of birds at this single bay represents a structural dependency that conservation planning must account for.
- **Boundary Bay as cross-border wintering concentration and binational management convergence.** **Boundary Bay** — the large bay straddling the US-Canada border — is another major Brant wintering site. Extensive mudflats and eelgrass beds support thousands of Brant alongside other waterfowl. The Boundary Bay population connects the US and Canadian management areas, requiring binational coordination. Smaller numbers winter at Dungeness Bay, Sequim Bay, Penn Cove (Whidbey Island), and various outer-coast Washington and Oregon sites. The Pacific Brant flyway extends from Arctic Alaska (Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta, Banks Island, coastal Alaska and Arctic Canada breeding) through PNW wintering bays to **Baja California, Mexico** (San Quintin Bay, Guerrero Negro), making it one of the longest single-subspecies migrations in North American waterfowl.
- **The Brant as indicator species for Puget Sound coastal bay ecosystem health.** The specialization makes Brant an **indicator species**. Eelgrass beds in parts of Puget Sound have declined due to water-quality degradation, coastal development, increased turbidity, boat traffic, and climate change. Brant populations track eelgrass availability: declining Brant numbers at a site can signal eelgrass decline before monitoring programs detect it through direct measurement. The bird is a real-time biological sensor for coastal bay condition — the canary in the eelgrass mine. The Pacific Brant subspecies is considered a species of special concern in Washington State and has shown population fluctuations linked to eelgrass availability. The connection to Puget Sound health extends beyond a single species.
- **Eelgrass as foundational species connecting Brant to juvenile salmon, herring, and crab.** **Eelgrass** (*Zostera marina*) is a foundational species in Puget Sound shallow-marine ecosystems. It provides nursery habitat for **juvenile Chinook salmon** (endangered), **Pacific herring** (forage fish), and **Dungeness crab**. It stabilizes sediment, reduces erosion, and sequesters carbon. The Brant's eelgrass dependency connects its fate to the broader health of Puget Sound — the bird, the plant, and the marine ecosystem are a linked system. Protecting eelgrass for Brant also protects salmon, herring, crab, and the broader marine food web. The Brant is a charismatic entry point for eelgrass conservation — an argument for ecosystem protection that transcends any single species.
- **The sea goose as most marine-adapted Anatidae in the engine.** The Brant is the **most marine-adapted** of North American geese. Unlike Canada Goose (freshwater and terrestrial) and Cackling Goose (agricultural fields), Brant spend their winter almost entirely on **saltwater** — feeding on marine eelgrass, resting on open saltwater, rarely venturing inland. Brant behavior is tied to the **tide cycle** rather than the solar cycle: they feed on eelgrass at low to mid-tide when the beds are accessible, then rest on open water at high tide, socializing and preening. The daily rhythm follows tidal clock. Arctic breeding (dry tundra near coastal areas, ground nests) contrasts with marine wintering — the shift between terrestrial breeding and marine wintering is a seasonal ecological transition few waterfowl execute so completely.
