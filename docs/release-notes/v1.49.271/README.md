## John Richards -- The Morning Voice

John Richards has hosted the morning show on KEXP 90.3 FM since **1997**. Like Cheryl Waters, he joined during the KCMU era and stayed through the transformation to KEXP. The morning show is the station's flagship -- the time slot with the largest audience, the voice that sets the tone for the day.

Richards' style is direct, personal, and emotionally engaged. Where some radio hosts maintain critical distance, Richards connects. He talks about what music means to him, what it does for him, why a particular song matters on a particular morning. The between-song commentary is not filler; it's context. Richards treats each listener as a participant in a shared experience rather than a passive consumer of a playlist.

**Music Heals.** Richards created the Music Heals initiative -- a recognition that music functions as more than entertainment. Music as therapy, as community, as survival mechanism. The initiative brings together listeners around the idea that music has genuine power to heal, connect, and sustain. It's advocacy disguised as programming -- the argument that a radio station is not a jukebox but a public health institution.

E=3. Same as Cheryl Waters. The energy of radio hosting -- the voice, the curation, the human connection. Richards' emotional engagement is high, but the medium is intimate. Morning radio is a one-to-one conversation scaled to thousands. Quiet authority, not broadcast showmanship.

The KEXP host trio at degrees 130-132 documents three different relationships to the same station:
- **Cheryl Waters** (130) -- afternoon, encyclopedic knowledge, discovery radio
- **John Richards** (131) -- morning, emotional engagement, Music Heals
- **Darek Mazzone** (132) -- world music, global scope, Wo' Pop

Three hosts. Three time slots. Three curatorial philosophies. One station. KEXP's strength is that each host brings a distinct voice and vision while serving the same mission: independent music, artist-first programming, listener-supported public radio.

## Brant -- The Coastal Specialist

The Brant (*Branta bernicla*) is a small, dark goose of **coastal bays and estuaries**. Unlike the Canada Goose and Cackling Goose, which are comfortable in fields, parks, and inland lakes, the Brant is a marine specialist -- almost exclusively found along the coast during winter, feeding in shallow bays and tidal flats.

The sound: a **low, musical honk** -- softer and more melodious than the Canada Goose's deep honk, with a rolling, almost conversational quality. In flocks, the overlapping calls create a murmuring chorus that carries across the water. The call has a distinctive two-syllable quality -- a throaty, rolling *rronk* that birders learn to distinguish from the sharper calls of other geese.

E=4. Same as the Cackling Goose. The Brant's call is lower-pitched than the Cackling Goose but less powerful than the Canada Goose. The musical quality -- that rolling, melodious character -- distinguishes it from both.

Three species, one genus, three degrees. The engine's first three-consecutive-species genus run in Anatidae. Each *Branta* goose occupies a different acoustic niche and a different habitat: inland generalist, field migrant, coastal specialist.

**Eelgrass obligate.** The Brant is one of the most diet-specialized geese in North America. During winter, its primary food source is **eelgrass** (*Zostera marina*) -- a marine flowering plant that grows in shallow coastal bays. When eelgrass beds decline (due to water quality degradation, coastal development, or disease), Brant populations decline in lockstep. The bird is an **indicator species** for the health of Pacific Northwest coastal ecosystems. Padilla Bay, Willapa Bay, and other Washington coastal sites are critical Brant wintering grounds.

**14th consecutive Anatidae.** The streak: Harlequin Duck (118) through Brant (131). Fourteen species, fourteen degrees. Mergansers, diving ducks, sea ducks, dabbling ducks, swans, and now three species of geese. The engine's Anatidae survey is approaching comprehensive coverage of Pacific Northwest waterfowl diversity.
