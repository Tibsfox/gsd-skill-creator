**Part B: The Barred Owl and the First Ecological Conflict.** The **Barred Owl** (*Strix varia*) is the third consecutive Strigidae entry on the species axis and the first **ecologically controversial species** documented in the engine's 137-degree history. The "Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?" eight-note territorial phrase is among the most recognizable bird vocalizations in North America — a rhythmic, accented, speech-like call whose English-language mnemonic captures the acoustic rhythm with uncanny accuracy. The Barred Owl's historical range was exclusively eastern North American, from the Atlantic coast to the Great Plains, where extensive mature deciduous and mixed forests provided the large-tree-cavity nesting habitat the species requires. The Great Plains grasslands formed a natural barrier between eastern and western forests, and the species was absent from the Pacific Northwest for the entire pre-twentieth-century record. The westward expansion began in the 1940s-1960s when Barred Owls were first documented in British Columbia, advanced through Washington and Oregon in the 1970s-1980s, established throughout the Pacific Northwest by the 1990s-2000s, and has continued expanding into California through the 2000s-2020s. The expansion is one of the most significant avian range expansions in North American history, and its mechanism — the **Canadian boreal corridor** formed by fire suppression and reforestation in the northern prairies — is itself a story about unintended anthropogenic consequences on continental-scale biogeography.

**The Spotted Owl Displacement and the Conservation Crisis.** The **Barred Owl**'s arrival in the Pacific Northwest has created the region's most contentious interspecific conservation conflict. The **Northern Spotted Owl** (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) — documented at SPS degree 4 (E=3) and now re-encountered at degree 137 through its primary displacement competitor — is a federally listed threatened species whose old-growth-forest habitat specialism placed it at the center of the 1990s Pacific Northwest **timber wars**. In areas where Barred Owls and Spotted Owls co-occur, the Barred Owl consistently dominates through **larger body size** (about 20% larger than Spotted Owls, giving the competitive edge in direct encounters), **broader diet** (mammals, birds, amphibians, fish, crayfish, insects — dietary flexibility that Spotted Owls' narrower diet cannot match), **habitat flexibility** (old-growth, mature second-growth, fragmented forest, riparian corridors, even suburban parks — whereas Spotted Owls require old-growth), and **documented hybridization** that produces **"Sparred Owls"** and threatens the genetic integrity of the Spotted Owl population. Annual Spotted Owl survival rates decline, territory occupancy drops, and reproduction falls off where Barred Owls are present. The **Livezey 2009** *American Midland Naturalist* paper and the **Wiens, Anthony, and Forsman 2014** *Ecological Monographs* paper documented the expansion timeline and the competitive-interaction mechanisms respectively, and the accumulated evidence base led the **U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service** (USFWS) to initiate a controversial 2013 experimental **Barred Owl removal program** — lethal removal of Barred Owls in Spotted Owl habitat to assess whether Spotted Owl populations recover. The empirical results (documented in **Diller et al. 2016** and **Dugger et al. 2016**) have shown that Spotted Owl populations do stabilize or recover when Barred Owls are removed, confirming that the Barred Owl is a primary driver of Spotted Owl decline. The ethical complexity — killing one native species to protect another, whether the Barred Owl's expansion is "natural" (following a natural corridor) or "anthropogenic" (enabled by human land-management that created the corridor) — places the Barred Owl at the center of conservation biology's most challenging dilemmas.

**The "Who Cooks for You?" Mnemonic as Cultural Artifact.** The Barred Owl's primary territorial phrase is the **"Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?"** eight-note call — four notes with emphasis on the final rising "YOU?" followed by four-to-five notes with the distinctive drawn-out falling finish on "you-ALL?" The English-language mnemonic captures the actual acoustic rhythm with remarkable accuracy: the match between the mnemonic and the call is closer than for almost any other North American bird species. The call operates at **400-550 Hz fundamental frequency** — significantly higher than the Great Horned Owl's 250-400 Hz — with a rich, resonant, slightly hoarse or "barking" timbre that carries through dense forest at 200-400 meters. Individual Barred Owls show variation in exact phrasing, timing, and tonal quality, and researchers have used these individual variations for **acoustic fingerprinting** — identifying specific owls by voice. The call is active through the night, but Barred Owls also call during the day, especially on the overcast and rainy days that characterize much of the Pacific Northwest's climate, making daytime Barred Owl vocalizations a common component of the regional acoustic environment. The Barred Owl's documentation at degree 137 is therefore a documentation of a cultural artifact (the mnemonic) as much as a biological one (the call) — the mnemonic has entered the North American birding consciousness in a way that transcends the species' biological identity and gives the species a place in the cultural acoustic landscape distinct from its place in the ecological record.

## Part B: Barred Owl — The "Who Cooks for You?" Mnemonic and the First Ecological Conflict

- **Third consecutive Strigidae entry extends the owl run to three degrees.** The **Barred Owl** (*Strix varia*) is the third consecutive Strigidae entry on the species axis, following the **Great Horned Owl** solo at degree 135 (E=4, *Bubo virginianus*) and the **Great Horned Owl pair duet** at degree 136 (E=5, *Bubo virginianus* male-female call-and-response). - **Genus *Strix* enters distinct from Great Horned Owl's *Bubo*.** The **Barred Owl**'s genus ***Strix*** enters the species axis for the first time at degree 137, distinct from the Great Horned Owl's genus ***Bubo*** that represented Strigidae at degrees 135-136. The *Strix* genus includes the **Spotted Owl** (*S. occidentalis*, documented at SPS degree 4), the **Barred Owl** (*S. varia*, documented here), and the **Great Gray Owl** (*S. nebulosa*, potential future degree). The genus entry at degree 137 expands the Strigidae family representation beyond the horned-owl form (*Bubo*) to include the wood-owl form (*Strix*), and the wood-owl form carries different ecological implications — slightly smaller body size, different vocal register, different habitat preferences, different prey-size range — than the horned-owl form. The genus-*Strix* first-entry is a catalog-expansion event on the species-axis-side parallel to the Stones-Throw-Records first-entry on the artist-axis-side.
- **"Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?" mnemonic enters the catalog.** The **Barred Owl**'s primary territorial phrase is the **"Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?"** eight-note call — four notes with emphasis on the rising "YOU?" at the end of the first phrase, followed by four-to-five notes with the distinctive drawn-out falling finish on "you-ALL?" at the end of the second phrase. The English-language mnemonic captures the acoustic rhythm with remarkable accuracy — closer match between mnemonic and actual call than almost any other North American bird species. The call operates at 400-550 Hz fundamental frequency (higher than Great Horned Owl's 250-400 Hz), with a rich, resonant, slightly hoarse or "barking" timbre. The mnemonic has entered North American birding culture as the exemplar of a bird-call mnemonic, and the degree-137 documentation registers both the biological call and its cultural-artifact status.
- **Eastern North American origin and westward range expansion.** The **Barred Owl** was historically an **eastern North American species**, distributed from the Atlantic coast to the Great Plains in extensive mature deciduous and mixed forests with large-tree-cavity nesting habitat. The Great Plains grasslands formed a natural barrier between eastern and western forests, and the species was absent from the Pacific Northwest for the entire pre-twentieth-century record. The westward expansion began in the 1940s-1960s with first documentation in British Columbia, advanced through Washington and Oregon in the 1970s-1980s, established throughout the Pacific Northwest by the 1990s-2000s, and has continued expanding into California through the 2000s-2020s. The expansion mechanism — the **Canadian boreal corridor** formed by fire suppression and reforestation in the northern prairies — is itself a story about unintended anthropogenic consequences on continental biogeography.
- **First ecologically controversial species documented in 137 degrees.** The **Barred Owl** is the engine's first ecologically controversial species in 137 degrees of documentation. Prior species entries documented non-antagonistic relationships — waterfowl coexisting in wetland habitats, songbirds sharing forest canopies, seabirds distributing along coastlines. The Barred Owl at degree 137 introduces species-level antagonism into the record through its documented displacement of the threatened **Northern Spotted Owl** (*Strix occidentalis caurina*, documented at SPS degree 4). The engine must now reckon with a species whose existence directly threatens another species already in the catalog, and the reckoning sets a precedent for future ecologically conflicted species entries. Conservation biology's central tension — that protecting one species may require harming another — enters the engine at degree 137.
- **Displacement mechanisms: body size, diet, habitat flexibility, hybridization.** The **Barred Owl** displaces the **Northern Spotted Owl** through four documented mechanisms. **Larger body size** — Barred Owls average about 20% larger than Spotted Owls, giving the competitive edge in direct encounters. **Broader diet** — mammals, birds, amphibians, fish, crayfish, insects, giving habitat exploitation flexibility that Spotted Owls' narrower diet cannot match. **Habitat flexibility** — old-growth, mature second-growth, fragmented forest, riparian corridors, even suburban parks, whereas Spotted Owls require old-growth. **Hybridization** — Barred Owls and Spotted Owls have been documented hybridizing to produce **"Sparred Owls,"** which threatens the genetic integrity of the Spotted Owl population. The four mechanisms compound rather than substitute; the Barred Owl wins on multiple axes simultaneously, and the Spotted Owl's only refuge is habitat sufficiently specialized that Barred Owl's flexibility becomes a liability rather than an asset.
- **USFWS 2013 lethal removal program enters the conservation-ethics register.** In **2013**, the **U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service** initiated an experimental **Barred Owl removal program** — lethal removal of Barred Owls in Spotted Owl habitat to assess whether Spotted Owl populations recover. The program's ethical complexity (killing one native species to protect another) places the Barred Owl at the center of conservation biology's most challenging dilemmas. The empirical results have been documented in **Diller et al. 2016** and **Dugger et al. 2016** (demographic response papers) and have shown that Spotted Owl populations do stabilize or recover when Barred Owls are removed, confirming that the Barred Owl is a primary driver of Spotted Owl decline. The program remains controversial — is the Barred Owl's expansion "natural" (following a corridor formed by natural processes) or "anthropogenic" (enabled by human fire suppression and reforestation that created the corridor)? The question has no clean answer, and the degree-137 entry must carry the ethical weight along with the biological documentation.
- **Habitat generalism vs. Spotted Owl habitat specialism.** The **Barred Owl** is a **habitat generalist** compared to the **Spotted Owl**'s **habitat specialism**. Barred Owls thrive in old-growth Douglas fir, mature second-growth mixed conifer, fragmented forest, riparian corridors, and suburban parks with large trees. They show strong water association with streams, rivers, wetlands, and lakeshores. They can exploit diverse habitats. Spotted Owls, by contrast, require old-growth forest with dense canopy cover and complex structural diversity — habitat that has been reduced to a fraction of its pre-logging extent in the Pacific Northwest. The contrast between generalism and specialism is the ecological core of the displacement dynamic: generalists with broader diet, broader habitat, and larger body size consistently outcompete specialists when the two coexist. The degree-137 entry documents the generalist strategy as an explicit ecological variable rather than an implicit background assumption.
- **Day-and-night vocal activity in the PNW's cloudy climate.** Unlike the **Great Horned Owl** (which is primarily nocturnal), the **Barred Owl** frequently calls during the day, especially on overcast or rainy days. In the Pacific Northwest's frequently cloudy climate (Seattle, Portland, and the broader PNW coast receive 150+ overcast days per year), daytime Barred Owl calls are common. The day-and-night vocal activity contributes to the Barred Owl's acoustic dominance in PNW old-growth — the "Who cooks for you?" call is not confined to night and not confined to dusk-and-dawn, and the extended vocal presence gives the Barred Owl an acoustic advantage over more strictly nocturnal species. The species' dominance in the sonic landscape parallels its dominance in the ecological landscape, and the E=4 rating reflects the expanded calling window as well as the measured-phrase delivery.
- **Individual acoustic fingerprinting via call variation.** Individual **Barred Owls** show variation in exact phrasing, timing, and tonal quality of the "Who cooks for you?" call. Researchers have used these individual variations for **acoustic fingerprinting** — identifying specific owls by voice across multiple survey occasions. The fingerprinting capability has been used in studies of Barred Owl territorial behavior, home-range stability, and population demographics, and it provides an observational advantage for monitoring the species without visual detection or physical capture. The fingerprinting phenomenon registers the Barred Owl's call as a cultural artifact (through the "Who cooks for you?" mnemonic) and a biological artifact (through the individual-identifiable variation) simultaneously, and the degree-137 entry reflects both registers.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Era-transition degrees carry structural weight that justifies maximum-energy calibration.** The E=6 rating at degree 137 is not an arbitrary energy assignment — it is the structural consequence of the eleven-degree radio-era close combined with Stones Throw label weight combined with Wake Up Show national amplification combined with battle-turntablism competition-speed execution. Future era-transition degrees should receive energy calibrations proportional to the structural pressure the transition releases, and the E=6 register should be reserved for transition points where the accumulated pressure justifies it.
2. **First-entry events should be framed explicitly rather than absorbed into surrounding documentation.** Stones Throw Records' entry as the first independent-hip-hop label, the "Who cooks for you?" mnemonic's entry as the first cultural-artifact-grade bird-call transcription, the genus *Strix*'s entry as the first non-*Bubo* owl genus, and the Barred Owl's entry as the first ecologically controversial species were all framed explicitly at degree 137. Future first-entry events should receive the same explicit framing rather than being mentioned in passing, and the framing should register the catalog-milestone weight of the new entry.
3. **Ecological conflict demands explicit ethical engagement rather than documentation elision.** The Barred Owl's displacement of the Spotted Owl is an ecologically and ethically contested event, and the degree-137 documentation engages with the displacement directly rather than eliding the conflict. The Spotted Owl at degree 4 is now re-contextualized through its displacement competitor at degree 137, and future ecologically conflicted species entries should follow the same explicit-engagement discipline — name the conflict, name the ethical complexity, cite the peer-reviewed literature (Livezey 2009, Wiens et al. 2014, Diller et al. 2016, Dugger et al. 2016, USFWS), and resist the temptation to sanitize the documentation.
4. **Cross-era bridge figures should be documented by the weightier context but with bridge explicitly preserved.** DJ Revolution's Wake Up Show radio presence and Stones Throw Records label affiliation place him in both the just-closed radio era and the opening performing-artist era. The documentation chose to register him by the weightier performing-artist-label context but preserved the Wake Up Show bridge explicitly. Future cross-era bridge figures should receive the same weightier-context-with-bridge-preserved treatment, and the bridge should be named rather than erased.
5. **Multi-degree sequences reveal their architectural design only at closure.** The four-degree DJ sequence (134-137) exhibited a three-E=5-plateau-followed-by-one-E=6-detonation architecture that was not pre-announced at degree 134 but became legible at degree 137's close. Future multi-degree sequences should expect to reveal architectural design at closure rather than at opening, and closer-degree documentation should explicitly name the architectural shape the sequence has assumed. The plateau-and-break pattern is now named as a recurring sequence-architecture possibility that subsequent sequences may exhibit.
6. **Anthropogenic mechanisms in biogeographical phenomena should be explicitly documented.** The Barred Owl's westward range expansion through the Canadian boreal corridor formed by fire suppression and reforestation is an anthropogenic-consequence story, not a pure natural-biogeography story. The degree-137 documentation framed the anthropogenic mechanism explicitly, and future biogeographical documentation should apply the same discipline — naming the anthropogenic contribution to ostensibly-natural phenomena and preserving the ethical complexity the naming introduces.
7. **Vocal-pattern cultural recognition should be documented in parallel on artist and species axes.** The turntablism vocabulary (flares, crabs, orbits, chirps, beat juggles, needle drops) and the "Who cooks for you?" bird-call mnemonic both have cultural-recognition status that transcends their practitioner communities. The degree-137 documentation framed both as cultural artifacts in addition to biological-or-technical practices, and the parallel framing strengthened the cross-sync resonance between the two halves. Future degrees should look for vocal-pattern cultural recognition on both axes and document the cultural-artifact status when it exists.
8. **Intra-genus antagonism creates structural tension distinct from inter-genus documentation.** The Barred Owl (*Strix varia*) and the Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) share genus *Strix*, and their antagonistic relationship creates intra-genus displacement that is structurally different from an inter-genus conflict. The genus-level shared-identity makes the conflict especially sharp (the displacer and the displaced are close relatives, and hybridization between them produces "Sparred Owls" that further complicate the genetic-integrity question). Future intra-genus antagonism events should be recognized as structurally distinct from inter-genus ones, and the documentation should register the shared-genus as a variable in the conflict dynamic.
9. **Label-axis documentation expands the artist-axis catalog beyond individual-artist entries.** Stones Throw Records' entry at degree 137 through DJ Revolution's affiliation is a label-axis documentation event that expands the artist-axis catalog beyond individual-artist entries. The engine has now registered labels as first-class catalog entities alongside individual artists, and future label-axis expansions should be named as such rather than absorbed into the associated individual-artist entries. The label-axis provides a cross-cutting organizational dimension that complements the individual-artist dimension.
10. **Post-era-close detonation events should be scheduled deliberately to release accumulated pressure.** The eleven-degree radio-era close (126-136) built structural pressure that degree 137's E=6 detonation released through DJ Revolution's maximum-energy performing-artist arrival. The deliberate scheduling of the detonation event at the era-boundary is a reusable engine-design pattern: long institutional-documentation eras should be followed by high-energy practitioner arrivals that release the accumulated-pressure through maximum-register calibration. Future era-close events should anticipate detonation scheduling as a structural-design option.
