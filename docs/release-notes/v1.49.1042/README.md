## Summary

v1.49.1042 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.224 to 1.225, adding RXTE and SUSTAINING the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY axis at obs#3 — a SUBSTRATE-FORM-DISTINCT continuation rather than a new rotation. The axis opened at v1040 with the Chandra X-ray Observatory (substrate-axis rotation #35), the grazing-incidence telescope that brought soft and medium-band cosmic X-rays to a half-arcsecond focus, and continued at v1041 with NuSTAR, the first telescope to *focus* the hard X-ray band (about 3–79 keV) on a deployable mast. Both predecessors read the focused image of the high-energy sky. RXTE reaches a third observation that is again substrate-form-distinct, but the distinguishing trade is wholly different: it does not form an image at all. RXTE is the timing observatory — the first satellite purpose-built to read the time domain of cosmic X-rays, the rapid brightness changes of compact objects that flicker, pulse, and oscillate faster than any imaging optic can resolve in time. Where Chandra and NuSTAR concentrate photons onto a small detector with grazing-incidence mirrors, RXTE abandons the image entirely and collects raw photons over a very large area through simple collimators, time-tagging each arrival to the microsecond. The way of seeing extends from the sharp soft-band picture (Chandra), to the focused hard-band picture (NuSTAR), to the rapid light curve and power-density spectrum (RXTE) — the kilohertz quasi-periodic oscillations, the millisecond pulse profiles, the spin frequencies, the variability classes that a focusing telescope, however sharp, cannot capture in time. The distinction across this continuation is the domain, not the band or the resolution: RXTE reads *when* the high-energy light arrives, not *where*, trading all imaging sharpness for time resolution and collecting area, the two things the time domain demands. The axis sustains at obs#3 (axis-rotation #35 sustains at obs#3) rather than capping after the two focusing telescopes, and the forward cadence can continue to obs#4 — an X-ray polarimeter or a later timing mission — or rotate onward per the substrate-axis-rotation discipline.

RXTE reached orbit on 30 December 1995 (about 13:48 UTC) on a Delta II 7920-10 launched from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station, Space Launch Complex 17A in Florida, into a near-circular low-Earth orbit at about 580 kilometers altitude and roughly 23 degrees inclination (orbital period ~90–100 minutes). It flew under its working name, the X-Ray Timing Explorer (XTE); about two months later, on 28 February 1996, NASA renamed it the Rossi X-Ray Timing Explorer in honor of Bruno B. Rossi (1905–1993), the MIT cosmic-ray physicist and co-founder of X-ray astronomy. RXTE was a NASA Explorer-program mission — Explorer 69 — managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, where the roughly 3,200-kilogram (about 7,055-pound) spacecraft was designed and built in-house by the Goddard engineering team; the Delta II was supplied by McDonnell Douglas. The observatory operated for about sixteen years: it returned its last science data on 4 January 2012 and was commanded into a quiescent state on 5 January 2012, then re-entered Earth's atmosphere on about 30 April 2018. The signature of RXTE at this degree is the trade it makes against the image. Chandra and NuSTAR are focusing telescopes: nested grazing-incidence mirrors concentrate the X-rays from a point on the sky onto a small detector, forming a sharp picture. That optical concentration is exactly what makes them poor timers — a focused instrument collects only the photons its mirror area gathers, and the brightest variable sources deliver too few photons per millisecond to measure rapid change. RXTE is built on the opposite principle. Its pointed instruments carry no focusing optics and form no image; they are large-area collimated detectors that accept X-rays from a roughly one-degree patch of sky and time-tag every photon. By giving up the picture, RXTE buys the two things the time domain needs: a very large photon-collecting area and microsecond time resolution. The result is the first satellite *dedicated* — purpose-built — to high-time-resolution X-ray timing across timescales from microseconds to years, the rapid-variability counterpart to the focusing observatories on the same axis. Earlier missions could *do* fast timing — ESA's EXOSAT in 1983 and Japan's Ginga in 1987 both carried large proportional counters and are milestones in the discipline — but neither was a spacecraft *built around* timing with RXTE's combination of collecting area, microsecond resolution, and continuous all-sky monitoring, and neither could reach the sub-millisecond, kilohertz regime RXTE opened. RXTE carried two co-aligned pointed instruments and one survey instrument, all collimated and none forming an image. The Proportional Counter Array (PCA) was the heart of the mission, built at Goddard under principal investigator Dr. Jean H. Swank: five identical, co-aligned Proportional Counter Units, each a multi-layer xenon-filled proportional counter with a propane layer for anticoincidence veto and xenon layers for detection, together giving a collecting area near 6,500 cm² across the 2–60 keV band with time resolution down to about 1 microsecond — the enormous area for its era that let it count photons fast enough to find signals at kilohertz frequencies. The High-Energy X-Ray Timing Experiment (HEXTE) was built at the Center for Astrophysics and Space Sciences at UC San Diego under principal investigator Dr. Richard E. Rothschild: two clusters of four NaI(Tl)/CsI(Na) phoswich scintillation detectors each (eight detectors total), covering 15–250 keV with about 1,600 cm² of area (two clusters of ~800 cm²) and ~8-microsecond timing, the clusters rocking on and off the source to measure the background continuously and carrying the timing reach into the hard band above the PCA. The All-Sky Monitor (ASM) was built at the MIT Center for Space Research: three Scanning Shadow Cameras, each a one-dimensional coded-mask camera over a xenon position-sensitive proportional counter, covering 1.5–12 keV and scanning about 80% of the sky every orbit (~90 minutes), giving only coarse positions rather than a picture but turning RXTE into a continuous watchtower — when a source flared or a new transient appeared, the ASM caught it and the pointed detectors slewed to follow. Microsecond timing, large area, and continuous monitoring define the form, and all three serve the time domain rather than the image.

RXTE's combination of area and speed opened a regime no instrument had reached. In February 1996, within weeks of first light, the PCA found kilohertz quasi-periodic oscillations (kHz QPOs) in accreting neutron stars — van der Klis and colleagues detected twin kHz peaks in Scorpius X-1 (an upper peak near 1,100 Hz and a lower one near 800 Hz), oscillations near the orbital frequency of matter at the innermost edge of the accretion flow, where the timescale is set by strong-field gravity (van der Klis and colleagues, 1996). In the same season Strohmayer and colleagues found millisecond oscillations during a thermonuclear (Type I) X-ray burst from 4U 1728-34 — a 363 Hz signal recurring across six of eight bursts that revealed, for the first time, the spin frequency of the underlying neutron star through what came to be called burst oscillations (Strohmayer and colleagues, 1996). Two years later RXTE detected coherent 401 Hz (about 2.5-millisecond) pulsations from the transient SAX J1808.4-3658 — the first accretion-powered millisecond pulsar, the long-sought "missing link" confirming the recycling scenario in which an old neutron star is spun up to millisecond periods by accretion before becoming a millisecond radio pulsar (Wijnands and van der Klis, 1998). Beyond these firsts, RXTE's long baseline characterized the microquasar GRS 1915+105 through its dozen-plus distinct X-ray variability classes (Belloni and colleagues, 2000), found pairs of high-frequency QPOs in 3:2 frequency ratios in black-hole binaries such as GRO J1655-40 (300 and 450 Hz) and XTE J1550-564 (184 and 276 Hz) (Remillard and colleagues, 2002), and supplied the rapid-timing data behind the relativistic-precession interpretation of QPOs (Stella and Vietri, 1998–1999) that probes frame-dragging near spinning compact objects. ## Mission Overview

RXTE is the obs#3 continuation of the X-ray-astronomy axis and the form-distinct complement to the two focusing telescopes before it — the first satellite dedicated to high-time-resolution X-ray timing, reading the rapid variability of compact objects rather than forming an image. A NASA Explorer-program mission (Explorer 69), managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center and built in-house by the Goddard engineering team, it launched 30 December 1995 (about 13:48 UTC) on a Delta II 7920-10 from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station, Space Launch Complex 17A, into a near-circular ~580-kilometer low-Earth orbit at about 23 degrees inclination; it flew as the X-Ray Timing Explorer (XTE) and was renamed the Rossi X-Ray Timing Explorer on 28 February 1996 for the MIT physicist Bruno B. Rossi. It carries three co-aligned, collimated, non-imaging instruments — the Proportional Counter Array (PCA: five xenon Proportional Counter Units, ~6,500 cm², 2–60 keV, ~1-microsecond timing, PI Jean H. Swank at Goddard), the High-Energy X-Ray Timing Experiment (HEXTE: two clusters of four NaI(Tl)/CsI(Na) phoswich scintillators, eight total, ~1,600 cm², 15–250 keV, ~8-microsecond timing, the clusters rocking for background, PI Richard E. Rothschild at UC San Diego), and the All-Sky Monitor (ASM: three Scanning Shadow Cameras over xenon position-sensitive counters, 1.5–12 keV, scanning ~80% of the sky each ~90-minute orbit, MIT-built). The roughly 3,200-kilogram (~7,055-pound) spacecraft operated for about sixteen years — last science 4 January 2012, commanded quiescent 5 January 2012, re-entry about 30 April 2018 — and discovered kilohertz QPOs in Scorpius X-1, the 363 Hz burst (spin) oscillation in 4U 1728-34, and the first accretion-powered millisecond pulsar SAX J1808.4-3658, while characterizing the microquasar GRS 1915+105 and the high-frequency QPOs of black-hole binaries; and it SUSTAINS the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#3 (axis-rotation #35 sustains). RXTE operated as a complete, fully successful mission — one of the most productive X-ray-timing observatories ever flown.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis sustains when the next observation reads the same kind of source through a wholly new substrate.** The X-ray-astronomy axis sustains at obs#3 because RXTE keeps the subject — accreting compact objects in the high-energy sky — but reaches it through the time domain rather than the image; the substrate-form distinctness is the domain read, so the cadence continues the axis rather than rotating away from it.
2. **Abandoning the image is what buys the time domain.** A focusing telescope collects only the photons its mirror area gathers and cannot deliver enough per millisecond to time the brightest variable sources; RXTE gives up the picture for large-area collimated detectors that time-tag every photon to the microsecond, the trade the time domain demands.
3. **A first can hinge on the qualifier.** RXTE is the first satellite *dedicated* to high-time-resolution X-ray timing, not the first to do timing at all — EXOSAT and Ginga came earlier; the distinct first is being built around timing with the area, microsecond resolution, and all-sky monitoring to reach the kHz regime, so the framing carries the "dedicated/purpose-built" qualifier.
4. **Microsecond timing and a large area reach phenomena an image cannot resolve.** The kilohertz QPOs of Scorpius X-1, the 363 Hz burst oscillation of 4U 1728-34, and the 401 Hz pulse of SAX J1808.4-3658 all live in the time domain — signals at the dynamical and spin timescales of matter at the edge of a compact object that no imaging optic, however sharp, can capture in time.
5. **One observatory reads many kinds of variable compact object in time.** The same fast-timing detectors found the inner-flow oscillations of an accreting neutron star, the spin frequency revealed in a thermonuclear burst, the first accretion-powered millisecond pulsar, the dozen-plus variability classes of the microquasar GRS 1915+105, and the high-frequency QPOs of stellar-mass black holes.
6. **The time domain sees the dynamics the image freezes.** The orbital frequency at the inner edge of an accretion flow, the spin of a neutron star, the precession that probes frame-dragging — all are timing signatures the focusing telescopes cannot resolve; the third observation is what the first dedicated timing observatory hears that the focusing telescopes could not.
7. **A continuation keeps the predecessors' vocabulary and references them by name.** Because RXTE sustains the X-ray-astronomy axis, the shared X-ray vocabulary is correct and kept, and Chandra (obs#1, soft/medium focusing) and NuSTAR (obs#2, hard-band focusing) are referenced as the focusing complements — but RXTE's own facts (the PCA/HEXTE/ASM collimated detectors, the Delta II launch, the in-house Goddard build, the kHz QPOs) and only its own six anchors are stamped; the predecessors' anchors and predecessor-specific facts stay with their missions.
8. **Accuracy guardrails guard against the stale-infobox and fabricated-citation classes of error.** Using orbit ~580 km / ~23°, mass ~3,200 kg / ~7,055 lb, deactivation 5 January 2012, operating life ~16 years, launch site Cape Canaveral SLC-17A, the Explorer-69 program, PCA ~6,500 cm², the distinct 363 Hz and ~1,100 Hz phenomena, the ASM DOI 10.1086/310260 kept distinct from the Strohmayer 310261, and only the verified citations — keeps the facts load-bearing and correct.

## Surprises

- **You see faster by throwing the picture away.** RXTE's pointed instruments carry no mirrors and form no image at all; by giving up the focused picture for large-area collimated counters that time-tag every photon, the mission reads brightness changes at microsecond resolution that the sharpest focusing telescope cannot follow in time — the opposite trade from its two predecessors on the same axis.
- **A neutron star's spin shows up in the flicker of a thermonuclear burst.** The 363 Hz oscillation Strohmayer and colleagues found riding on a Type I X-ray burst from 4U 1728-34 turned out to mark the spin frequency of the burning neutron star itself — the rotation of the star read directly out of the time series of a single nuclear flash.
- **A forest flycatcher already watches for fast motion the way RXTE watches for fast variability.** The Olive-sided Flycatcher perches motionless at the top of a tall snag, watches, then sallies out in a sudden burst to seize a fast-flying insect in mid-air and returns to the same lookout — exactly the watch-and-catch RXTE makes from low orbit, the all-sky monitor sweeping for the sudden transient and the pointed detectors timing the fast flicker.
- **A forest-floor flower fires faster than the neutron-star pulse it mirrors.** The Bunchberry's petals snap open and a bent stamen catapults pollen upward in under half a millisecond — one of the fastest movements in the plant kingdom — a sub-millisecond timed release that only an instrument built for the time domain, like RXTE with its microsecond resolution, could resolve.

## Dedication

v1.225 RXTE is dedicated to the people who built the first satellite dedicated to reading the time domain of the X-ray sky: Bruno B. Rossi, the MIT physicist for whom the observatory is named and who set the field in motion with the June 1962 sounding-rocket flight that discovered Scorpius X-1; Jean H. Swank, the principal investigator who led the Proportional Counter Array at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center; Richard E. Rothschild, who led the High-Energy X-Ray Timing Experiment at UC San Diego; Hale Bradt and Alan M. Levine of the MIT All-Sky Monitor team; the engineering and operations teams at Goddard who designed and built the observatory in-house and flew it on a Delta II from Cape Canaveral; Michiel van der Klis, Tod Strohmayer, and Rudy Wijnands, whose results found the kilohertz oscillations, the burst oscillation revealing a neutron star's spin, and the first accretion-powered millisecond pulsar; and Riccardo Giacconi, who shared with Rossi the founding of X-ray astronomy. For about sixteen years the large-area counters, the rocking scintillators, and the scanning cameras timed the heartbeat of matter falling onto compact objects at the edge of general relativity.
