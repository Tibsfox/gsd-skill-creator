**Part B: Wood Duck Extends the Anatidae Streak to Four.** The **Wood Duck** (*Aix sponsa*) is widely considered the most beautiful duck in North America, and the species name encodes the recognition — Linnaeus named it *sponsa*, meaning "bride" or "betrothed," for its ornate plumage that appears dressed for a wedding. The male's plumage is extraordinary: iridescent green and purple head with a swept-back crest, bold white chin strap, chestnut breast with white flecks, buff flanks, and an intricate face pattern that looks painted. The female's plumage is more cryptic — grey-brown with a white teardrop around the eye — consistent with the sexual dimorphism of waterfowl where females take primary nest-and-incubation responsibility. The **rising ooo-eek** is the female Wood Duck's flight call, a sharp, upward-inflected squealing whistle that carries across wooded ponds and flooded timber. Unmistakable once heard. The male's call is a thinner, rising whistle. Both are higher-pitched and more urgent than the merganser calls at degrees 119-120 (guttural croak and rolling frog-like call respectively). **Habitat: wooded ponds and flooded timber.** The Wood Duck is a tree-nesting cavity duck — it nests in cavities in trees near water, often quite high above the ground (ten to fifty feet). Day-old ducklings leap from the nest cavity to the ground below within twenty-four hours of hatching, sometimes falling fifty or more feet. The cavity-leap duckling phenomenon is one of the most dramatic precocial-development events in North American waterfowl — ducklings are precocial (self-feeding, capable of swimming and diving from day one), and the cavity-leap selects for robust day-of-hatching physiology. The species nearly went extinct in the early twentieth century due to habitat loss and hunting, then recovered dramatically through nest box programs and habitat protection. A conservation success story, and one of the most successful waterfowl recoveries in North American conservation history. **E=4** correctly reflects the energy: the rising ooo-eek has urgency but not volume, a carrying call rather than a loud one, consistent with the E=4 held by the full Anatidae streak (118-121).

## Part B: Wood Duck — The Rising Ooo-Eek and the Anatidae Streak at Four

- **Fourth consecutive Anatidae (118-121) — streak extended and still open.** The **Wood Duck** (*Aix sponsa*) extends the **Anatidae** streak at degree 121 to four consecutive waterfowl: **Harlequin Duck** (118, bold plumage pattern, generalist bill, squeak + high whistle, E=4), **Common Merganser** (119, sawbill, sleek diving posture, guttural croak, E=4), **Hooded Merganser** (120, sawbill smaller, fan-shaped crest, rolling frog-like call, E=4), Wood Duck (121, ornate plumage, generalist bill, rising ooo-eek, E=4). Four consecutive Anatidae at identical E=4 species-side energy is a notable moderate-continuation streak — longer than the Cinclidae double at 116-117 (two consecutive) and shorter than the record-setting Picidae nonet at 107-115 (nine consecutive) or the prior-record Sciuridae octet at 75-82 (eight consecutive). The streak remains open — future degrees could extend it with Barrow's Goldeneye, Common Goldeneye, Bufflehead, Northern Pintail, Mallard, American Wigeon, Green-winged Teal, Gadwall, Northern Shoveler, Canvasback, Redhead, Ring-necked Duck, or Greater Scaup, all of which have Pacific Northwest populations worth documenting.
- **Most beautiful duck in North America — ornate plumage exceeds earlier Anatidae.** The **Wood Duck** male's plumage is the most ornate in the four-degree Anatidae streak (118-121) and is widely considered the most beautiful duck in North America. The morphology: **iridescent green and purple head** with a swept-back crest, **bold white chin strap**, **chestnut breast** with white flecks, **buff flanks**, and an **intricate face pattern that looks painted**. Linnaeus recognized the ornate plumage when naming the species — *sponsa* means "bride" or "betrothed," invoking the image of a bird dressed for a wedding. The ornate plumage exceeds the earlier Anatidae morphologies in the streak (Harlequin Duck's bold plumage pattern at 118 is distinctive but less multicolored; Common Merganser and Hooded Merganser at 119-120 have striking but more monochromatic color palettes of black-white-green). Wood Duck's plumage occupies the full color spectrum — reds, greens, purples, chestnuts, whites, buffs — and the combination is unique among North American waterfowl.
- **Genus *Aix* — one of two species globally.** The **Wood Duck** (*Aix sponsa*) belongs to the genus *Aix*, which contains only two species globally: *Aix sponsa* in North America and *Aix galericulata* (Mandarin Duck) in East Asia. Both species are cavity-nesting tree ducks with ornate plumage, and the two species are evolutionary sisters despite their geographic separation. The genus structure distinguishes the Wood Duck from the sawbill-focused mergansers at degrees 119-120 (*Mergus* and *Lophodytes*). The Wood Duck has a generalist bill (short, stout, not serrated) adapted for a generalist diet rather than the sawbill's fish-gripping specialization. The genus-level distinction between mergansers and tree ducks is a structural finding within the Anatidae streak: four consecutive waterfowl but multiple distinct sub-taxonomic structures documented, not a mono-taxonomic run.
- **Tree-nesting cavity duck — cavity-nesting cohabitation network continues.** The Wood Duck is a **tree-nesting cavity duck**, extending the **cavity-nesting cohabitation network** documented at the Hooded Merganser (degree 120) entry. Wood Duck nests in cavities in trees near water, often quite high above the ground (ten to fifty feet), in cavities created by woodpeckers (connecting to the Picidae nonet at 107-115), by fire, by decay, or by limb-loss. The nest cavity is lined with down plucked from the female's breast. Clutch size is typically ten to fifteen eggs. Wood Duck and **Hooded Merganser** (120) and **Barrow's Goldeneye** (future degree) all compete for and share large-dead-tree cavities, and nest-box programs targeting Wood Duck also benefit Hooded Merganser and Barrow's Goldeneye populations. The cavity-nesting cohabitation network is an ecological collaboration: resource-sharing among competing species that becomes the functional mechanism of population support, as conservation actions targeting one species support all three.
- **Cavity-leap duckling phenomenon — most dramatic precocial-development event.** Day-old Wood Duck ducklings **leap from the nest cavity** to the ground below within **twenty-four hours of hatching**, sometimes falling fifty or more feet. The cavity-leap duckling phenomenon is one of the most dramatic precocial-development events in North American waterfowl. Ducklings are **precocial** (self-feeding, capable of swimming and diving from day one), and the cavity-leap selects for robust day-of-hatching physiology. The female Wood Duck calls from below the cavity, and the ducklings respond to the call by climbing to the cavity entrance and jumping, one at a time, until the entire brood has leaped. The ducklings land on the ground (sometimes in water, sometimes on soft forest floor, sometimes on harder substrate) and immediately follow the female to water. Mortality during the cavity-leap is low for a behavior that looks high-risk — the small body mass and high surface-area-to-volume ratio of ducklings means falling damage is minimal, and the ducklings' downy feathers provide some cushioning.
- **Conservation success story — near-extinction reversed through nest boxes.** The Wood Duck nearly went extinct in the early twentieth century due to **habitat loss** (draining of wetlands, clearing of bottomland forests for agriculture) and **hunting** (market hunting and unregulated sport hunting in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries). By the 1910s, the species was rare enough that some ornithologists predicted imminent extinction. Recovery began with the Migratory Bird Treaty Act (1918), which regulated hunting, and accelerated through **nest-box programs** developed in the 1930s-1950s that provided artificial cavities to replace the old-growth large-dead-tree cavities lost to logging. By the 1970s the species had recovered to healthy populations, and today Wood Duck is one of the most abundant waterfowl species in the eastern and central United States, with growing Pacific Northwest populations. The conservation recovery is one of the most successful waterfowl stories in North American history, and it directly benefits the cavity-nesting cohabitation network (Wood Duck nest boxes also support Hooded Merganser and Barrow's Goldeneye populations that share cavity requirements).
- **Pacific Northwest habitat — wooded ponds, flooded timber, beaver-modified wetlands.** The Wood Duck's preferred habitat in the **Pacific Northwest** consists of **small-to-medium water bodies with adjacent large trees suitable for cavity nesting**: wooded ponds (including beaver-modified wetlands), flooded timber in riparian zones, slow-moving streams, cattail marshes, and small forested lakes. The species avoids large open water and fast-moving whitewater — preferring calmer water for foraging and forested edges for nest-cavity availability. The Pacific Northwest's abundance of beaver-modified wetlands and second-growth forest with residual large-dead trees provides excellent Wood Duck habitat across western Washington, western Oregon, and coastal British Columbia. The species is year-round resident in milder coastal areas and migratory in higher-elevation or interior sites. The same habitat type that supports Wood Duck also supports Hooded Merganser (120) and future Barrow's Goldeneye, reinforcing the cavity-nesting cohabitation network's ecological coherence.
- **The rising ooo-eek — 122nd species-side acoustic element.** The Wood Duck female's **rising ooo-eek** enters the engine's acoustic element catalog at degree 121. The call is a sharp, upward-inflected squealing whistle produced primarily during flight — hence "flight call." The call carries across wooded ponds and flooded timber at a distance of hundreds of meters, and it is one of the most recognizable waterfowl calls in North America. Birders learning waterfowl calls typically recognize Wood Duck early because the rising ooo-eek is so distinctive. The male's call is a thinner, rising whistle that is quieter and less urgent than the female's. Both calls sit in a higher pitch register than the merganser calls at 119-120 (Common Merganser's guttural croak and Hooded Merganser's rolling frog-like call) — the four-degree Anatidae streak documents the full range of waterfowl vocalizations from low-frequency rolling croaks through mid-frequency whistles through higher-frequency flight-call squeals.
- **Generalist diet distinct from sawbill specialization.** The Wood Duck's **generalist diet** is distinct from the sawbill fish-and-invertebrate specialization of **Common Merganser** (119) and **Hooded Merganser** (120). Wood Duck eats: **acorns and other nuts** (oak mast, beech mast, hickory nuts — the Wood Duck is one of the few North American waterfowl that regularly forages on tree mast), **seeds of wetland plants** (sedges, grasses, bulrushes), **aquatic invertebrates** (insects, crustaceans, mollusks — more seasonal than primary), **tree fruits** (elderberry, dogwood berry, blackberry — seasonal), and **berries**. The generalist diet correlates with the generalist bill (short, stout, not serrated) and with the tree-cavity nesting habit (mast from oaks near cavity trees provides food source close to nest). The dietary breadth distinguishes Wood Duck from the fish-specialized mergansers within the Anatidae streak, documenting that the family contains both generalist and specialist feeding ecologies.
