## Part B: Orca Spy-Hop — Fifth-Degree Mid-Arc Pivot, Visual-Orientation Behavior, SRKW J/K/L Pod Documentation, and the Cross-Generational Behavioral Transmission Register

- **Fifth Degree of the Seven-Degree Orca Arc:** The **Orca Spy-Hop** at degree 274 occupies the fifth degree of the seven-degree **Orca Arc** (270-276) — the most extended single-taxon arc in the engine's catalog. The mid-arc pivot is a structural fulcrum rather than a simple sequential marker.
- **Vertical Rise Behavior and the Pectoral-Fin Height Register:** The spy-hop is the behavioral event of a **Southern Resident Killer Whale** rising vertically from the water to expose its head, often to the level of the pectoral fins or beyond, and holding that position momentarily before submerging. The vertical rise is deliberate, precise, and controlled — the animal propels itself upward with tail-stroke force while maintaining a near-vertical body orientation, and the hold at the top of the rise distinguishes the spy-hop from the incidental surface exposure that occurs during ordinary respiration. The body-position control during the spy-hop is itself a structural property of the behavior.
- **Visual-Orientation Function and Surface Observation:** The spy-hop is associated with visual orientation: whales spy-hop to observe the surface environment above the waterline — to see boats, to see other whales, to observe their surroundings in ways that echolocation does not provide. The behavior is common in the Salish Sea where SRKW encounter whale-watching vessels regularly and where the visual environment above the surface (vessel traffic, neighboring orcas breaching or traveling at the surface, shoreline features) provides observable information that echolocation alone cannot supply.
- **Acoustic Component of the Spy-Hop Event:** The acoustic component of a spy-hop includes the water displacement at entry and exit, the blow at the surface during the exposure, and any vocalizations produced during or following the behavior. E=8 for the spy-hop reflects the full acoustic event of a large cetacean performing vertical propulsion to the height of the surface, though at lower intensity than the full aerial breach documented at degree 273. The spy-hop is precise and deliberate where the breach is explosive and total.
- **J Pod, K Pod, L Pod Documentation Across All Three Resident Pods:** The spy-hop behavior is common in **J pod**, **K pod**, and **L pod** — the three **Southern Resident Killer Whale** pods that together comprise the SRKW population across the **Salish Sea** region. The behavior is documented across all three pods with similar frequency and similar contextual associations, making it a population-wide behavioral register rather than a pod-specific practice. The three-pod documentation baseline is anchored in decades of **Center for Whale Research** photographic identification and behavioral observation work.
- **All Age Classes Including Calves and the Learned-Behavior Dimension:** The spy-hop is documented in all age classes of the SRKW population including calves, and the documented spy-hopping of SRKW calves alongside their mothers is a well-documented behavioral interaction that suggests the behavior is learned as well as instinctive. The cross-generational transmission — calves observing their mothers performing the behavior and replicating it — is itself a structural property of the SRKW behavioral repertoire, and the engine's documentation at degree 274 preserves this cross-generational-transmission context as a first-class element of the record.
- **Curiosity, Orientation, and Social Investigation as Behavioral Contexts:** SRKW spy-hops are associated with curiosity, orientation, and social investigation. Whales in the **Salish Sea** spy-hop to observe whale-watching vessels, to observe other pods, and apparently to investigate any unusual surface phenomena. The behavioral-context triad (curiosity, orientation, social investigation) distinguishes the spy-hop from purely locomotor behaviors (traveling, foraging) and from purely acoustic behaviors (calling, clicking), and the engine's documentation preserves the behavioral-context registration as part of the spy-hop entry.
- **Orcas as Active Observers of Both Aquatic and Aerial Environments:** The visual-orientation function of the spy-hop reveals that orcas are not purely underwater creatures — they are active observers of both aquatic and aerial environments. The engine's documentation of the spy-hop at degree 274 surfaces this dual-environment observational capacity as a first-class element of the SRKW behavioral record, countering any simplification of orca behavior to an underwater-only acoustic-and-locomotor repertoire and establishing the above-surface observational dimension as structurally central to the population's behavioral identity.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Arc-closing documentation at the end of a multi-degree sub-sequence should preserve the arc's structural unity rather than treating the closing entry as a standalone release.** The degree 274 documentation treats Vexed as the arc-closing entry of the five-degree Seattle HC arc rather than as an isolated single-band degree. The lesson is that arc-closing entries should be documented with explicit arc-level framing — naming the arc, naming the other arc entries, computing arc-level summary statistics, and surfacing the arc's structural unity — rather than being documented in the same format as isolated single-band degrees. The arc-level framing is what makes the closing entry legible as an arc-closure rather than a sequence-end.
2. **Mid-arc pivot placements in extended multi-degree sub-sequences warrant first-class structural-fulcrum documentation.** The degree 274 documentation treats the Orca Spy-Hop as the mid-arc pivot of the seven-degree Orca Arc rather than as a middle-of-sequence entry. The lesson is that mid-arc placements in extended sub-sequences are structural fulcrums rather than simple sequential markers, and the documentation should surface the fulcrum function explicitly rather than treating middle-of-sequence entries as interchangeable with arc-opening or arc-closing entries.
3. **Cross-track arc-coordination at shared fifth-position placements creates legible cross-track structural registers.** The degree 274 alignment of the Seattle HC arc's fifth-and-closing position and the Orca Arc's fifth-and-pivot position at the same degree is structurally deliberate and interpretively productive. The lesson is that cross-track arc-coordination — when the engine can align arcs across tracks at shared structural positions — creates legible cross-track registers that are stronger than single-track arc documentation alone. Future arc planning should look for cross-track coordination opportunities where they exist.
4. **Sustained-ceiling amplitude across a multi-degree arc is a distinct register from single-degree peak amplitude.** The Seattle HC arc's weighted mean above E=7.5 across five entries at E=7-8 is a distinct register from single-degree E=8 peaks elsewhere in the catalog. The lesson is that the engine's amplitude vocabulary should distinguish sustained-ceiling arc amplitude from single-moment peak amplitude, and arc-level documentation should explicitly compute and surface arc-level amplitude statistics rather than reducing the arc to its highest-amplitude individual entry.
5. **Outside-commercial-framework work warrants first-class documentation treatment rather than commercial-success-normalized evaluation.** The Seattle HC arc bands did not chart, tour arenas, or benefit from commercial reception, and the engine's documentation treats their work at first-class structural resolution despite the commercial absence. The lesson is that outside-commercial-framework work should be documented on its own terms — for its musical, cultural, and historical properties — rather than being evaluated against commercial-success metrics that the work itself was designed to operate outside of.
6. **Cross-generational behavioral transmission within small-group structures warrants first-class biological-documentation resolution.** The SRKW spy-hop's documented transmission from mothers to calves is treated at first-class structural resolution rather than as a secondary biological detail. The lesson is that cross-generational behavioral-transmission registers — within pods, within scenes, within small-group social structures — are first-class elements of the acoustic-event record rather than background context. The engine's documentation should preserve the transmission dimension at the same resolution as the behavior itself.
7. **Visual-orientation and above-surface observational capacity in primarily-underwater species warrants explicit documentation against the underwater-only simplification.** The Orca Spy-Hop's visual-orientation function surfaces the SRKW's active above-surface observational capacity rather than reducing orca behavior to an underwater-only acoustic-and-locomotor repertoire. The lesson is that primarily-underwater species' above-surface capacities warrant explicit documentation against the default-underwater simplification, and the engine should register dual-environment observational capacity as a first-class behavioral dimension rather than allowing the underwater default to elide the above-surface capacity.
8. **Geographic-transition preparation at the boundary between regional scenes warrants explicit structural marking.** The degree 274 placement as the final Seattle-proper S36 entry before the Olympia transition at degree 275 is explicitly marked as a geographic-transition preparation rather than being treated as an ordinary arc-closing entry. The lesson is that regional-scene transitions in the Seattle 360 engine — Seattle to Olympia, Seattle to Bellingham, Seattle to Tacoma, or any other geographic shift — should be explicitly marked at the closing degree of the outgoing region so that the transition is legible within the documentation rather than emerging as a silent structural shift when the new region's first entry arrives.
