## Summary

**Alcohol Funnycar** arrives at E=7 -- the band that marks **Empty Records' entry into the Seattle 360 engine** and brings a specific brand of grunge-punk that was always more aggressive than the mainstream Seattle sound while staying rooted in the same neighborhood bars and practice spaces. Vocalist Dave Dederer, guitarist Scott McCaughey, and the rest of the band operated on Empty Records through the early-to-mid 1990s, releasing work that sat at the intersection of punk velocity and grunge thickness -- the part of the Seattle spectrum that wasn't trying to cross over, wasn't angling for MTV, and was perfectly content to be loud in small rooms for people who knew the difference. **Empty Records** itself is a crucial label entry: founded in Seattle, it was a parallel universe to Sub Pop's famous roster, signing bands that lived in the harder, faster, less melodically approachable corners of the scene. Alcohol Funnycar on Empty Records is the engine's first documentation of that label ecosystem, and it arrives loud, at E=7, consistent with the elevated energy that closed the raptor arc at degree 228 (Gas Huffer, E=7). Degree 229 is the first degree after the raptor arc, and it opens the next chapter without any drop in intensity. On the species side, the **Ferruginous Hawk** registers at E=6 and continues the raptor arc in a specific taxonomic sense: the Ferruginous Hawk (*Buteo regalis*) is a Buteo, the same genus as the Red-tailed Hawk that closed the arc at degree 228, and it is the **closing member of the Buteo sub-arc** -- the largest buteos documented by the engine. *Regalis* means "regal" in Latin, and it is deserved: the Ferruginous Hawk is the largest hawk in North America, with a wingspan reaching 56 inches. Its rough alarm call -- harsh, descending, issued from the open sky -- registers at E=6, a step down from the Red-tailed Hawk's cinematic scream but still a commanding sound. The Ferruginous Hawk is a bird of open country, of dry grasslands and shrub-steppe, and in Washington it breeds primarily in the Columbia Basin and the eastern slopes of the Cascades. It is not a Puget Sound bird in the way that the Red-tailed Hawk is -- it is an eastern Washington bird that occasionally appears on the west side during winter -- and its entry at degree 229 expands the engine's geographic range beyond the Sound proper into the wider Washington landscape.

- Ferruginous Hawk ecology and Washington distribution -- a grassland specialist that breeds in the Columbia Basin and the dry shrub-steppe east of the Cascades; in Washington the breeding range is concentrated around the Tri-Cities area, the Yakima Training Center, and the Horse Heaven Hills; outside breeding season occasional winter visitors appear in the Puget lowlands; the name comes from the Latin *ferrugineus*, meaning rust-colored or iron-red, referring to the rusty-brown patches on the bird's legs and back; it is listed as a Species of Greatest Conservation Need in Washington due to grassland conversion
