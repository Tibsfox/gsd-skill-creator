## Summary

v1.49.1020 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.210 to 1.211, adding Aqua — the afternoon companion of NASA's Earth Observing System, a single platform carrying six instruments to sound the air in layers and measure the water of the Earth across its forms — as the obs#3 INTRA-AXIS continuation that sustains the EARTH-OBSERVATION substrate-axis and caps it (axis-rotation #30 sustained at obs#3). The earth-observation axis opened at v1018 Landsat with the land-surface form and extended at v1019 Terra with the morning whole-Earth-system flagship form; at v1020 Aqua reaches a third observation that is substrate-form-distinct: the afternoon platform that crosses the equator in the early afternoon, the complement to Terra's morning, built to read the water of the planet. Where Terra measured the whole Earth system in the morning, Aqua reads the water of the Earth — the vapor, the clouds, the precipitation, the sea ice, the snow, the soil moisture, and the ocean surface — in the afternoon, and the axis caps at obs#3.

Aqua is managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center and is an international partnership, with most of the instruments from the United States, AMSR-E contributed by Japan, and HSB by Brazil. The platform reached its Sun-synchronous near-polar orbit on 4 May 2002, launched as EOS PM-1 on a Delta II rocket from Vandenberg Air Force Base, California, crossing the equator northbound in the early afternoon at about 705 kilometers. Designed for about a six-year life, the observatory has operated far beyond it; AIRS, AMSU-A, MODIS, and CERES continue today, while the early microwave instruments HSB and AMSR-E completed their service.

The signature of the afternoon companion is the water-cycle observatory. Aqua carries six instruments on one platform — AIRS, AMSU-A, HSB, AMSR-E, MODIS, and CERES — to read the water of the planet across its forms, extending Earth observation from the morning whole-system view to the water of the Earth seen in the afternoon.

The afternoon orbit reads the planet at a second time of day. Aqua crosses the equator northbound in the early afternoon, the complement to Terra's morning, southbound crossing, so the same planet is read at a consistent afternoon time and the water is followed in particular.

The vertical atmospheric soundings read the air in layers. Aqua's AIRS and AMSU instruments read the temperature and humidity of the air from the surface upward, profiles of the atmosphere rather than images of the surface, reading the air through clouds by combining the infrared and the microwave.

The A-Train lead lets several observatories observe together. Aqua leads the A-Train, a line of Earth-observing satellites flying in formation and crossing the equator together in the early afternoon, so their separate measurements combine into one coordinated, near-simultaneous view of the same scene.

The water-cycle measurement reads the planet's water as one connected system. Aqua's instruments together read the vapor and humidity of the air, the clouds, the precipitation, the sea ice and snow, the soil moisture, and the temperature of the ocean surface, so the movement of water through the Earth can be followed.

The axis-relevant substrate of Aqua at this degree is its CONTINUATION of the EARTH-OBSERVATION substrate-axis at obs#3 and the capping of the axis, the afternoon companion observation of the water cycle. Aqua sustains the EARTH-OBSERVATION-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#3 NEW LOCKED and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#83 cumulative (axis-rotation #30 sustained at obs#3, caps the axis). With the land-surface, whole-Earth-system morning, and afternoon water-cycle forms all held, the forward continuation rotates to an under-served axis (rotation #31) such as a planetary axis at obs#1 of the new axis.

Aqua caps the earth-observation way of seeing on the catalog, and the mission records the Aqua team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, the AIRS, AMSU, CERES, and MODIS instrument teams, the Japanese AMSR-E team at JAXA, and the Brazilian HSB team at INPE. ## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis caps once its substrate-forms are all held.** By extending the systematic Earth observation from the morning whole-system view to the afternoon water of the planet, Aqua reached the third substrate-form on the earth-observation axis — the land surface, the whole system, and the water — so the axis caps at obs#3 and the forward continuation rotates to a new axis, showing that an intra-axis run completes once it has covered its distinct forms.
2. **A second time of day is its own substrate-form.** By crossing the equator in the early afternoon where Terra crosses in the morning, Aqua read the same planet at a second time of day, showing that the time of observation can be a substrate-form-distinct dimension, not only the subject or the instruments.
3. **Sounding reads the medium as a column.** By reading the temperature and humidity of the air in layers from the surface up, AIRS and AMSU profiled the atmosphere through its full height rather than imaging its surface, showing that a sounding measurement is structurally distinct from imaging.
4. **Microwave reads through cloud where the infrared cannot.** By pairing the infrared AIRS with the microwave AMSU, the soundings read the air in clear and cloudy conditions alike, showing that combining wavelengths reads what one alone cannot.
5. **A constellation lets several observatories observe as one.** By leading the A-Train, a line of satellites crossing the equator a few minutes apart on the same track, Aqua let its measurements combine with those of the satellites behind it into one near-simultaneous view, showing that formation flight turns separate observatories into one coordinated instrument.
6. **A shared instrument across companions doubles the cadence.** By carrying the same MODIS imager as Terra on the afternoon platform, Aqua and Terra together image the whole Earth twice a day, showing that a shared instrument flown at two times of day buys a cadence neither could alone.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across an intra-axis continuation.** Because Aqua reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.210 Terra template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the afternoon companion while keeping the warm palette, making the intra-axis continuation a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The water of the planet can be read across all its forms by one platform.** A single afternoon platform carrying six instruments reads the vapor, the clouds, the precipitation, the sea ice, the snow, the soil moisture, and the ocean surface together, so the water cycle is followed as one connected system rather than each form on its own.
- **A stream-diving songbird mirrors the water measurement and the sounding both.** The American Dipper, the only North American songbird that swims and dives beneath the surface, reads the flowing water directly the way Aqua reads the water of the planet, and reads the water through its full depth the way the soundings read the air through its full height.
- **A riparian tree mirrors both the water-tracing and the constellation.** Black Cottonwood follows the rivers and floodplains the way Aqua traces the water of the planet, and grows in stands along the same river the way the A-Train observatories fly the same afternoon track together.

## Dedication

v1.211 Aqua is dedicated to the people who built and have kept the afternoon companion of NASA's Earth Observing System, the observatory that measures the water of the planet: the Aqua team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, who lead the platform and keep it crossing the equator in the early afternoon; the AIRS team at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, whose sounder reads the temperature and humidity of the air in layers; the AMSU-A team, whose microwave sounder reads the air through the clouds; the CERES team at NASA's Langley Research Center, who measure the planet's incoming and outgoing energy; the MODIS team, whose imager views the whole Earth every day or two; the Japanese AMSR-E team at JAXA, whose microwave radiometer read the precipitation, the sea ice, the snow, and the soil moisture; and the Brazilian HSB team at INPE, whose humidity sounder served the early mission. Aqua reads the water of the Earth across its forms and leads the A-Train, and the observatory continues today, far beyond its planned life.
