## What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural structure rather than archived data.** The Aqua water-cycle-observatory shader uses fractal noise and analytic geometry rather than loading actual Aqua MODIS scenes or AMSR-E water products from NASA Earthdata. A future revision could load encoded Aqua imagery and water products for a higher-fidelity rendering keyed to real clouds, sea ice, and soil moisture.
- **The six-instrument-platform diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the platform, the six instruments, the afternoon ground track, and the trailing A-Train satellites.
- **The observatory sidebar table is illustrative rather than exhaustive.** The sidebar lists the axis-lineage and A-Train elements (Aqua, Terra, Landsat, the A-Train) but does not enumerate the full A-Train membership or the per-instrument specifications; a future revision could expand the table with the constellation roster and per-instrument highlights.
- **The sounding retrieval and the water-cycle measurement are described rather than computed.** The radiance-to-profile inversion and the microwave separation of the water are described but not run against the archived data; a future ship could query real Aqua radiances and retrieve a profile to demonstrate the sounding directly.

## Surprises

- **The water of the planet can be read across all its forms by one platform.** A single afternoon platform carrying six instruments reads the vapor, the clouds, the precipitation, the sea ice, the snow, the soil moisture, and the ocean surface together, so the water cycle is followed as one connected system rather than each form on its own.
- **A stream-diving songbird mirrors the water measurement and the sounding both.** The American Dipper, the only North American songbird that swims and dives beneath the surface, reads the flowing water directly the way Aqua reads the water of the planet, and reads the water through its full depth the way the soundings read the air through its full height.
- **A riparian tree mirrors both the water-tracing and the constellation.** Black Cottonwood follows the rivers and floodplains the way Aqua traces the water of the planet, and grows in stands along the same river the way the A-Train observatories fly the same afternoon track together.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis caps once its substrate-forms are all held.** By extending the systematic Earth observation from the morning whole-system view to the afternoon water of the planet, Aqua reached the third substrate-form on the earth-observation axis — the land surface, the whole system, and the water — so the axis caps at obs#3 and the forward continuation rotates to a new axis, showing that an intra-axis run completes once it has covered its distinct forms.
2. **A second time of day is its own substrate-form.** By crossing the equator in the early afternoon where Terra crosses in the morning, Aqua read the same planet at a second time of day, showing that the time of observation can be a substrate-form-distinct dimension, not only the subject or the instruments.
3. **Sounding reads the medium as a column.** By reading the temperature and humidity of the air in layers from the surface up, AIRS and AMSU profiled the atmosphere through its full height rather than imaging its surface, showing that a sounding measurement is structurally distinct from imaging.
4. **Microwave reads through cloud where the infrared cannot.** By pairing the infrared AIRS with the microwave AMSU, the soundings read the air in clear and cloudy conditions alike, showing that combining wavelengths reads what one alone cannot.
5. **A constellation lets several observatories observe as one.** By leading the A-Train, a line of satellites crossing the equator a few minutes apart on the same track, Aqua let its measurements combine with those of the satellites behind it into one near-simultaneous view, showing that formation flight turns separate observatories into one coordinated instrument.
6. **A shared instrument across companions doubles the cadence.** By carrying the same MODIS imager as Terra on the afternoon platform, Aqua and Terra together image the whole Earth twice a day, showing that a shared instrument flown at two times of day buys a cadence neither could alone.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across an intra-axis continuation.** Because Aqua reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.210 Terra template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the afternoon companion while keeping the warm palette, making the intra-axis continuation a clean build.
