### THE YELLOW-BELLIED MARMOT: MOUNTAIN WHISTLER

The Yellow-bellied Marmot is one of the Pacific Northwest's most charismatic alpine mammals -- a large, stocky ground squirrel with a distinctive yellow-orange belly patch, living in colonies on rocky slopes and talus fields at mid-to-high elevations. Weighing 1.6-5 kg (3.5-11 lbs), the marmot is the **largest squirrel-family member** in the PNW and one of the most vocal mammals in alpine habitats. Its piercing whistle alarm call carries across entire mountain valleys, earning it the widespread nickname **"whistle pig."**

The Yellow-bellied Marmot is the engine's **second alpine species** after the American Pika (degree 74). Where the Pika is tiny (120-185 g), solitary-territorial, and makes a sharp "eenk" bark, the marmot is large (up to 5 kg), **colonial** (living in family groups), and produces a loud, sustained whistle. Both inhabit rocky slopes. Both are diurnal. Both are alarm-callers. But their ecological strategies could not be more different: the Pika stores hay for year-round activity; the marmot stores fat for 7-8 months of hibernation. Alpine neighbors, opposite survival strategies.

The marmot is the engine's **first colonial mammal** -- living in family groups of 10-20 individuals with social hierarchies, sentinel systems, communal burrow use, and graded alarm calls. No previous mammal in the engine has exhibited this level of social complexity. The beaver (61) modifies habitat. The marmot creates society.

### TAXONOMY: *Marmota flaviventris*

- **flaviventris** -- from Latin *flavus* ("yellow") + *ventris* ("belly"). Literally "yellow-bellied." Described by John James Audubon and John Bachman in 1841.
- **Common names:** Yellow-bellied Marmot (standard), whistle pig (widespread colloquial), rockchuck (western U.S.), yellow-belly (informal).

- **Order:** Rodentia -- RETURNS. Second rodent family after Castoridae (Beaver, 61). Rodentia is the largest mammalian order (~2,500 species, 40% of all mammals). Key feature: single pair of continuously growing upper incisors.

### THE SOUND: PIERCING WHISTLE ALARM -- 76th Acoustic Element

The Yellow-bellied Marmot's acoustic contribution is its defining characteristic -- the piercing whistle alarm that echoes across alpine terrain:

**The whistle:** A single, sustained, high-pitched whistle -- approximately 0.5-2 seconds in duration, centered at approximately 2,000-6,000 Hz. The call is a clear, pure-toned whistle that rises slightly in pitch before trailing off. One of the **loudest mammalian alarm calls** in the PNW, audible at distances of **300-500 meters** across open rocky terrain.

**Call types:**
1. **Single whistle (alarm):** One sustained whistle when a predator is first detected. Colony-wide alert.
2. **Repeated whistle series:** Multiple whistles in rapid succession (2-5 calls) when threat is persistent. Escalation signal.
3. **Trill/chatter:** Lower-frequency chattering for close-range social interactions.
4. **Scream:** High-pitched scream when captured or in extreme distress. Rare.

**Graded alarm specificity:** Research shows marmot alarm calls contain information about predator type. Calls for **aerial predators** (hawks, eagles) differ from calls for **terrestrial predators** (coyotes, foxes, weasels). Colony members respond accordingly: aerial alarm triggers freezing; terrestrial alarm triggers running to burrows.

**Why E=5:** The marmot's whistle is louder than the Pika's bark (E=4), the fox's bark (E=3), and all other mammalian calls in the engine. Colony-wide function amplifies acoustic impact. The "whistle pig" identity is built entirely around this sound.

### COLONIAL LIVING: THE ENGINE'S FIRST COLONIAL MAMMAL

**Colony composition:** A typical colony contains 10-20 individuals in a harem-polygynous system:
- **One dominant male** defending territory with multiple females
- **2-4 adult females** forming the colony's core (often related -- mothers, daughters, sisters)
- **Yearlings** from previous year
- **Juveniles** of the year, emerging from burrows at 3-4 weeks

**Sentinel behavior:** Individuals perch on prominent boulders scanning for predators while others forage. When a sentinel detects a threat, it delivers the whistle alarm. Cooperative -- individuals take turns.

**Social interactions:** Greeting ceremonies (nose-to-nose contact), allogrooming, juvenile play, territorial aggression between males (boxing, chasing).

**Contrast with Pika (74):** The Pika is territorial and solitary. The marmot is colonial and social. The Pika's bark is individual. The marmot's whistle is communal. Two alpine mammals at consecutive degrees with opposite social strategies.

- **Periodic arousal:** Every 10-14 days, body temperature rises to near normal for 12-24 hours
- **Weight loss:** 40-50% of body weight lost during hibernation

### HABITAT: ROCKY SLOPES AT ELEVATION

- **Rocky slopes:** Boulder fields, cliff bases, talus margins -- rocks provide both basking surfaces and burrow sites
- **Elevation:** In Washington, 1,200-3,500 meters (3,900-11,500 feet). Broader range than Pika (1,500-3,000+ m)
- **Meadow adjacency:** Requires adjacent meadows for foraging. Rocks for safety, meadow for food
- **Burrows:** Actively digs burrows into rocky soil beneath boulders. Systems 2-5 meters deep with multiple entrances, sleeping chambers, and hibernaculum. Multiple generations may use the same system

### BEHAVIORAL ECOLOGY

- **Strictly diurnal** -- morning basking on warm rocks, midday foraging, afternoon retreat, evening return
- **Predator responses:** Aerial predators trigger freezing; terrestrial predators trigger burrow retreat. Graded intensity -- distant threat elicits single whistle, approaching threat elicits escalating series
- **Reproduction:** Breeding after spring emergence. Gestation ~30 days. Litter 3-8 pups (typically 4-5). Born altricial. Emerge at 3-4 weeks. Must fatten for first hibernation by September
- **Lifespan:** 5-10 years in the wild. First-year mortality 30-50%
