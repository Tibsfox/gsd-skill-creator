## Summary

v1.49.1012 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.202 to 1.203, adding WISE, the Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer — a NASA Medium-class Explorer — as the obs#3 INTRA-AXIS continuation that sustains the INFRARED-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis (axis-rotation #27 sustained at obs#3). The infrared-astronomy axis opened at v1010 IRAS with the first survey of the entire sky in infrared and sustained through v1011 Spitzer, the deep pointed Great Observatory; the forward continuation extends the axis to a third observation that is substrate-form-distinct, deepening the infrared all-sky survey IRAS began to a far more sensitive level. Where IRAS made the first census of the infrared sky and Spitzer pointed at chosen targets in depth, WISE re-mapped the whole sphere in infrared with hundreds of times the sensitivity of the first survey, the deep sensitive survey that deepens the first survey.

WISE was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory with Edward (Ned) Wright of UCLA as principal investigator. The spacecraft launched on 14 December 2009 aboard a Delta II 7320-10C from Vandenberg Air Force Base into a Sun-synchronous polar orbit, carrying a 40-centimeter telescope cooled by solid hydrogen, so the instrument's own thermal glow would not swamp the faint infrared signal, with a single imager observing the whole sky simultaneously in four infrared bands (3.4, 4.6, 12, and 22 micrometers).

The signature of the mission is the deep sensitive infrared all-sky survey. WISE surveyed the entire sky in infrared with hundreds of times the sensitivity and far finer resolution than the first all-sky survey, deepening the picture IRAS first sketched and compiling a catalog of hundreds of millions of sources, the most complete infrared census of the sky.

The deep survey reached the coldest and the most dust-shrouded objects. WISE found the coolest class of brown dwarfs, the Y dwarfs, objects so cool they emit almost entirely in infrared, along with some of the Sun's nearest neighbors, and discovered the most luminous galaxy known, a dust-shrouded extremely-luminous infrared galaxy.

The spacecraft's second life was the NEOWISE near-Earth-object survey. When the solid-hydrogen coolant was spent in 2010, WISE continued in its two warm bands and was reactivated as NEOWISE, repurposed to catalog, track, and measure the sizes of the near-Earth asteroids and comets for planetary defense awareness, operating into the 2020s.

The warm-survey repurposing gave the spacecraft a long, productive second life. By turning a completed cryogenic all-sky survey into a sustained small-body survey, WISE charted the near-Earth population for many years past its cryogenic phase, an infrared measurement giving a far more reliable size than reflected sunlight.

The axis-relevant substrate of WISE at this degree is its continuation of the INFRARED-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#3, the deep sensitive all-sky survey that deepens the first survey. WISE opens the INFRARED-ASTRONOMY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#3 NEW LOCKED, sustains the INFRARED-ALL-SKY-SURVEY-LINEAGE thread at obs#2 cumulative (IRAS first survey + WISE deep sensitive successor), and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#75 cumulative (axis-rotation #27 sustained at obs#3). The forward continuation may favor later infrared observatories that build on the survey, pointed-observatory, and deep-sensitive-survey forms, or a substrate-axis rotation #28 to an under-served axis.

WISE carries the infrared way of seeing the sky into the deep sensitive survey on the catalog, and the mission records the WISE team at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, Edward Wright and the UCLA team, and the NEOWISE survey team. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.202 to 1.203, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#86 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A deeper survey of the same sky reveals far more.** By re-mapping the whole sky in infrared with hundreds of times the sensitivity of the first survey, WISE turned the infrared sky from a sketch into a detailed map, showing that depth, not only coverage, drives what a survey reveals.
2. **A deep, uniform survey fills a vast catalog.** By recording every part of the sky to a consistent depth, WISE compiled a catalog of hundreds of millions of sources, showing that a deep, uniform sweep gives a true census rather than a scattered set of fields.
3. **The deepest sensitivity reaches the coldest objects.** By surveying deeply in infrared, WISE found the coolest class of brown dwarfs, the Y dwarfs, and some of the Sun's nearest neighbors, showing that deep infrared sensitivity extends the known range of objects toward the coldest end.
4. **A spacecraft can outlive its coolant in a new role.** By continuing in its two warm bands as the NEOWISE near-Earth-object survey, WISE turned a completed cryogenic survey into a sustained small-body survey, showing that a careful design can repurpose a mission for a long second life.
5. **An axis sustains at a substrate-form-distinct third observation.** By following the first survey and the pointed observatory with a deep sensitive survey, WISE sustained the infrared-astronomy axis at obs#3 without repeating either earlier form, showing that an intra-axis continuation deepens an axis by adding a distinct form.
6. **An infrared measurement gives a more reliable size for a small body.** By measuring a small body's infrared warmth rather than its reflected sunlight, NEOWISE read dependable sizes for the near-Earth asteroids and comets, showing that the thermal emission scales with the surface area where reflected light mixes size with reflectivity.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across a sibling mission.** Because WISE reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.202 Spitzer template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the deep sensitive survey while keeping the infrared-warm palette, making the sibling-infrared mission a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The catalog's deepest infrared survey is also its small-body survey.** WISE was built to re-map the whole sky in infrared, and that same wide survey, repurposed as NEOWISE, became the catalog of the near-Earth asteroids and comets, so the axis's deep sensitive survey is also the mission that charted the small bodies near Earth.
- **A wide-watching alpine sentinel mirrors the survey of the whole sky.** The Hoary Marmot, the whistler that keeps a wide watch from a high vantage and marks what approaches, enters as the wide-survey mirror to the deep all-sky survey and the NEOWISE near-Earth-object survey, so the catalog pairs a sentinel that keeps a wide watch with a survey that swept the whole sky.
- **A groundcover that covers wide ground comprehensively mirrors the survey that mapped the whole sky.** The Kinnikinnick spreads to carpet the whole surface of the ground in a continuous mat and endures through the harsh seasons, so the catalog's deep survey is paired with a plant that covers wide ground comprehensively and persists, the way WISE covered the whole sky and held its long second life.

A future revision could load encoded WISE data for a higher-fidelity rendering keyed to real observations.

## Dedication

v1.203 WISE is dedicated to the teams that built and flew NASA's deep infrared survey of the whole sky: the WISE team at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory who built the spacecraft and led the mission; Edward Wright and the UCLA team who guided the science as principal investigator; the instrument teams at the University of Arizona and the Space Dynamics Laboratory who built the four-band imager; the NASA/IPAC Infrared Science Archive at Caltech who compiled and served the catalog of hundreds of millions of sources; and the NEOWISE survey team who turned the spacecraft's second life toward charting the near-Earth asteroids and comets. It is dedicated as well to the engineers who cooled the 40-centimeter telescope with solid hydrogen and flew it on a Sun-synchronous polar orbit so it could sweep the whole sky deeply in infrared. WISE mapped the infrared sky more deeply than ever before, found the coolest stars and the most luminous galaxy known, and gave the spacecraft a long second life surveying the small bodies near Earth.
