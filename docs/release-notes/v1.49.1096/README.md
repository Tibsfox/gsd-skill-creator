## Mission Overview

Clementine is the obs#4 sustainer of the ROBOTIC-LUNAR-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — a joint NASA + Ballistic Missile Defense Organization (BMDO) technology-demonstration orbiter, managed by the Naval Research Laboratory (NRL), that circled the Moon in an elliptical polar orbit and imaged nearly the whole surface across many wavelengths while a laser altimeter ranged the terrain and a bistatic radar experiment probed the permanently-shadowed polar craters. ★ Its primary purpose was a technology demonstration — flight-testing lightweight BMDO sensors and spacecraft components in the deep-space radiation environment, using the Moon and a planned asteroid flyby as imaging targets; NASA provided science guidance and Deep Space Network tracking. ★ Clementine was NOT a NASA Discovery mission and NOT solely NASA's (Lunar Prospector holds the first-Discovery-to-the-Moon anchor); its "faster, better, cheaper" approach PREDATED and helped PAVE THE WAY for the later Discovery program. The spacecraft launched on 25 January 1994 on a Titan II(23)G (a converted Titan II ICBM, with a Star-37FM upper stage) from Space Launch Complex 4 West (SLC-4W) at Vandenberg Air Force Base, California, cruised to lunar-orbit insertion about 19 to 21 February 1994, and mapped the Moon for roughly two months from an elliptical polar orbit (about 297 orbits; periapsis ~400 km near 30°S, apoapsis ~8300 km), returning about 1.6 to 1.8 million images: a dedicated global optical mapper. The story is the launch from Vandenberg, the cruise into lunar orbit, the pole-to-pole mapping orbit sweeping the whole surface pass after pass, the cameras reading the Moon across roughly eleven wavelengths from the ultraviolet through the near-infrared, the laser altimeter ranging the terrain, the radar signal directed into the dark polar craters, and the turn outward toward the planned asteroid flyby. The science is central: the UV/Visible and Near-Infrared cameras imaged nearly the entire surface in five UV/Visible bands (~415/450, 750, 900, 950, 1000 nm) and six Near-IR bands (1100, 1250, 1500, 2000, 2600, 2780 nm) at ~100 to 200 m/pixel, producing the first global multispectral (reflectance/mineralogical) map of the Moon (Nozette et al. 1994; the UVVIS / global lunar mineralogy work of Lucey and McEwen & Robinson 1997); the LIDAR laser altimeter ranged the surface to produce the first global topographic map of the Moon from laser altimetry — the GLTM-2 model (Goddard Lunar Topography Model 2, spherical-harmonic degree/order 72, ~100 m vertical accuracy, ~2.5° resolution; Zuber et al. 1994), with coverage gaps within ~15° of the poles because the elliptical orbit gave no returns beyond ~600 km; and the Bistatic Radar Experiment directed radio signals into the permanently-shadowed south-polar region and returned a same-sense circular-polarization echo enhancement (Nozette et al. 1996). ★ The radar result is load-bearing: it was interpreted as POSSIBLY indicating low-loss volume scatterers such as water ice (the DoD announced it on 3 December 1996) — the first (ambiguous, debated) radar HINT of polar ice. Simpson & Tyler (1999) could not reproduce the result (similar features appeared off-pole, at other frequencies, and in the other polarization) and later Arecibo Earth-based radar attributed comparable signatures to surface roughness and crater ejecta, so the catalog frames Clementine as having HINTED at possible ice rather than discovered, found, or confirmed it. The lineage is: Clementine HINTED (1994) → Lunar Prospector gave the first STRONG evidence (1998) → LCROSS confirmed (2009). The scoping is load-bearing throughout: the MULTISPECTRAL (reflectance) map — distinct from Lunar Prospector's nuclear GEOCHEMICAL (elemental) maps; the laser-altimetry TOPOGRAPHY (surface shape) — distinct from GRAIL's GRAVITY field (interior mass); and the radar polar-ice HINT — an ambiguous suggestion, not strong evidence or confirmation. ★★ The mission operated in 1994 (launched 25 January 1994, entered lunar orbit about 19 to 21 February 1994, mapped the Moon for roughly two months, and ended the planned asteroid phase after a thruster malfunction on 7 May 1994), so the catalog frames the story in the PAST tense throughout (Clementine launched, orbited, mapped, returned, ended). ★ The dates are kept distinct: launch 25 January 1994, lunar-orbit insertion about 19 to 21 February 1994, thruster malfunction 7 May 1994. After the lunar mapping was complete, Clementine departed lunar orbit (early May 1994) for the planned flyby of the near-Earth asteroid 1620 Geographos; the thrusters fired 3 May 1994 for the Geographos injection, and on 7 May 1994 (about 14:39 UT) an onboard-computer problem caused a thruster to fire and deplete the attitude-control propellant, spinning the craft to ~80 rpm and ending the asteroid phase — the lunar mapping was already complete and successful. ## Surprises

- **Clementine flew eleven wavelengths from the ultraviolet through the near-infrared.** The UV/Visible and Near-Infrared cameras imaged nearly the entire lunar surface across roughly eleven wavelengths — five UV/Visible bands and six Near-IR bands at ~100 to 200 m/pixel — producing the first global multispectral reflectance/mineralogical map of the Moon, a composition map that no single visible light could show.
- **A laser altimeter built the first global topographic map of the Moon.** Clementine's LIDAR altimeter ranged the surface pulse by pulse to produce the first global topographic map of the Moon from laser altimetry — the GLTM-2 model at spherical-harmonic degree/order 72 — with coverage gaps within ~15° of the poles because the elliptical orbit gave no returns beyond ~600 km, the first such global shape model.
- **A bistatic radar probe returned the first ambiguous radar hint of possible polar ice.** Clementine directed radio signals into the permanently-shadowed south-polar craters and returned a same-sense circular-polarization echo enhancement interpreted as POSSIBLY water ice — an ambiguous, debated radar HINT (Nozette et al. 1996; Simpson & Tyler 1999 could not reproduce it), the first such suggestion, four years before Lunar Prospector's first strong evidence and fifteen before LCROSS confirmed it.
- **A songbird wearing a structural blue that shifts with the light, and seeing into the ultraviolet.** The Lazuli Bunting (*Passerina amoena*), a small migratory songbird of Pacific Northwest brushy hillsides, wears a breeding-male blue that is a STRUCTURAL color — it flashes and shifts with the angle of the light rather than a fixed pigment — and like all birds it sees into the ULTRAVIOLET, perceiving colors beyond human vision: a mirror of Clementine reading the Moon in light beyond what one visible band can show (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A wild rose hiding ultraviolet nectar guides invisible to the human eye.** The Nootka Rose (*Rosa nutkana*), a native Pacific Northwest wild rose of streambanks and thickets, bears broad pink five-petalled blooms whose surfaces carry ULTRAVIOLET nectar-guide patterns invisible to the human eye but plain to bee and bird pollinators in ultraviolet light, guiding them to the center (ecological description only) — bound with the throughline of what is revealed in light beyond the visible.

### What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived Clementine mission data.** The Clementine shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual mission data. ## Dedication

v1.279 Clementine is dedicated to the people across the Naval Research Laboratory, NASA, and the Ballistic Missile Defense Organization who built and flew the small orbiter that read the whole Moon in many lights. The teams who lifted it from Vandenberg on a converted Titan II and guided it through the cruise into a polar orbit around the Moon. The engineers behind the lightweight cameras, who imaged nearly the entire surface across roughly eleven wavelengths from the ultraviolet through the near-infrared to draw the first global multispectral map, and behind the laser altimeter, who ranged the terrain pulse by pulse to draw the first global topographic map from laser altimetry. The radar team who turned a quiet signal into the permanently-shadowed south-polar craters and read the faint enhancement that first hinted at possible ice in the dark — a question later missions would carry from hint to strong evidence to confirmation. They mapped a world by what the eye alone could not see, reading reflectance and shape across the visible and beyond, and though a thruster malfunction ended the planned reach toward an asteroid, the Moon they had already mapped, whole and in many lights, remained their lasting work.
