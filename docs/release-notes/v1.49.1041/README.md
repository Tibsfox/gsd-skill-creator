## Summary

v1.49.1041 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.223 to 1.224, adding NuSTAR and SUSTAINING the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY axis at obs#2 — a SUBSTRATE-FORM-DISTINCT continuation rather than a new rotation. The axis opened at v1040 with the Chandra X-ray Observatory (substrate-axis rotation #35), the grazing-incidence telescope that brought soft and medium-band cosmic X-rays to a half-arcsecond focus on four nested iridium-coated mirror pairs, resolving the hot, high-energy universe of black holes, neutron stars, and shock-heated gas. NuSTAR reaches a second observation that is again substrate-form-distinct: where Chandra's single-material iridium optics reflect only the softer X-rays below about 10 keV, NuSTAR is the first telescope to *focus* the hard X-ray band — about 3 to 79 keV — using depth-graded multilayer coatings that reflect the high-energy light Chandra's mirrors pass straight through, and a focal length too long for a rigid spacecraft, reached instead by a boom that unfolds in orbit. The distinction across this continuation is the band, not the resolution. NuSTAR's roughly 18-arcsecond imaging is far coarser than Chandra's sub-arcsecond sharpness, so the axis advances here by spectral reach above the predecessor's cutoff rather than by finer imaging. The two read the same black holes, supernova remnants, and accreting neutron stars in complementary bands — Chandra resolving structure below ~10 keV, NuSTAR catching the penetrating high-energy light above it: the radioactive glow of freshly forged elements, the Compton-scattered emission of obscured nuclei, the pulse of a neutron star outshining its Eddington limit, all of which the softer optics cannot bring to a point. The axis sustains at obs#2 (axis-rotation #35 sustains at obs#2) rather than capping after the soft-band Great Observatory, and the forward cadence can continue to obs#3 — an X-ray timing or polarimetry observatory — or rotate onward per the substrate-axis-rotation discipline.

NuSTAR reached orbit on 13 June 2012 (16:00:37 UTC) not from a launch pad but from the air. A Pegasus XL rocket built by Orbital Sciences Corporation was carried aloft beneath the "Stargazer" L-1011 carrier aircraft to about 40,000 feet and dropped over the Pacific roughly 117 miles (188 km) south of Kwajalein Atoll in the Marshall Islands; the rocket ignited in free fall to lift the observatory to orbit. NuSTAR is a NASA Small Explorer mission — the eleventh, SMEX-11, also catalogued as Explorer 93 — led by the California Institute of Technology with principal investigator Fiona A. Harrison and managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, at a total cost near $165 million. The spacecraft rides on Orbital Sciences' LEOStar-2 bus (Orbital Sciences Corporation was the 2012 builder; the firm became Orbital ATK only after a 2015 merger), with a launch mass near 350 kilograms (sources vary, ~350–360 kg) carrying a 171-kilogram payload. It flies a near-equatorial low-Earth orbit at about 600 kilometers altitude and only about 6 degrees inclination — a deliberately low-inclination path that minimizes time spent crossing the South Atlantic Anomaly, where trapped particles would raise the detector background. Its baseline mission was two years; NuSTAR marked its tenth year in orbit in 2022 and has continued operating in extended mission as of its most recent reporting. The signature of NuSTAR at this degree is the way it focuses light that even Chandra's mirrors pass straight through. Above about 10 kiloelectronvolts a single-material iridium coating stops working — the harder photons graze on through rather than reflect, and every X-ray observatory before NuSTAR that reached those energies had to fall back on non-focusing collimators or coded masks that cannot form a true image. NuSTAR answers with two co-aligned grazing-incidence telescopes, each a conical approximation to the Wolter Type-I geometry built from 133 nested concentric shells, and — the decisive new element — each shell is coated not with one metal but with a depth-graded multilayer: many alternating atomic-scale layers of platinum and silicon carbide (Pt/SiC) on the inner shells and tungsten and silicon (W/Si) on the outer ones, the layer spacings graded with depth so that successive interfaces reflect successively harder photons by constructive interference. That stack is what lets a grazing surface reflect light up to about 79 keV — the band's upper edge falling near 78.4 keV, the platinum K absorption edge of the coating. Grazing-incidence imaging itself was first flown decades earlier, so the new first here is hard-X-ray multilayer focusing, not grazing incidence per se. Focusing those hard X-rays requires a long focal length — about 10.14 meters — far longer than the spacecraft bus, so NuSTAR launched compact and grew in orbit. The two optics modules ride on the bus; the two focal-plane detectors sit at the far end of a deployable mast, an articulated boom of 56 cube-shaped units built by ATK of Goleta, California, that extended to its full length of roughly 10 meters (33 feet) on 21 June 2012, nine days after launch, in about 26 minutes. The boom design descends from the ~60-meter mast of the Shuttle Radar Topography Mission flown on Endeavour in February 2000 — NuSTAR's project manager Yunjin Kim had been the radar mission's project manager — and at full extension it is the longest deployable mast ever used on a space telescope. Because a boom flexes as the spacecraft warms and cools, a laser metrology system continuously measures its deflection so the optics and detectors can be kept in alignment in software. At the mast's far end sit two identical focal-plane modules, FPMA and FPMB, one behind each optic; each is a two-by-two array of four solid-state cadmium-zinc-telluride (CdZnTe, or CZT) pixel detectors, every chip a 32-by-32 grid of pixels at 0.6-millimeter spacing, recording both the position and the energy of each photon. The pair delivers a spectral resolution of about 400 electronvolts at 10 keV and about 900 electronvolts at 68 keV across a field of view near 12 arcminutes, while the optics set an angular resolution of about 18 arcseconds at half-maximum (a 58-arcsecond half-power diameter). The gain over the collimated and coded-mask instruments that preceded it is not in sharpness but in sensitivity: concentrating the X-rays from a source onto a small, low-background detector buys a better-than-hundredfold (factor ~50–100) improvement at these energies. NuSTAR's focused hard band opened views that earlier instruments could only blur. In the young supernova remnant Cassiopeia A it produced the first map of radioactive titanium-44 (⁴⁴Ti) — an isotope forged in the innermost layers of the explosion and so a direct tracer of the engine itself; its clumpy, asymmetric distribution showed the core-collapse explosion was lopsided, driven by sloshing instabilities rather than a narrow jet (Grefenstette and colleagues, 2014). In the galaxy M82 it caught 1.37-second X-ray pulsations from the ultraluminous source M82 X-2, revealing the accretor to be a magnetized neutron star — the first known ultraluminous X-ray pulsar, shining at roughly a hundred times the Eddington limit for a star of its mass (Bachetti and colleagues, 2014). Pairing its hard band with XMM-Newton's soft band on the active galaxy NGC 1365, it broke the long-standing degeneracy between black-hole spin and absorption: the relativistically blurred iron line could only be explained by a supermassive black hole spinning near the maximum rate general relativity allows (Risaliti and colleagues, 2013). It measured the cold-reflection spectrum of the Compton-thick Seyfert nucleus of NGC 1068, whose direct light is so buried that only the hard X-rays scattered off the surrounding torus escape (Bauer and colleagues, 2015). And toward the Galactic Center it discovered the magnetar SGR J1745-2900 by its 3.76-second pulsations, the closest known magnetar to the Milky Way's central black hole (Mori and colleagues, 2013; timing followed by Kaspi and colleagues, 2014). ## Mission Overview

NuSTAR is the obs#2 continuation of the X-ray-astronomy axis and the natural complement to Chandra — the first focusing telescope to bring the hard X-ray band (about 3–79 keV) to an image. A NASA Small Explorer (SMEX-11, Explorer 93) led by the California Institute of Technology with principal investigator Fiona A. Harrison and managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, it was air-launched 13 June 2012 (16:00:37 UTC) on a Pegasus XL dropped from the "Stargazer" L-1011 over the Pacific about 117 miles (188 km) south of Kwajalein Atoll, into a near-equatorial ~600-kilometer low-Earth orbit at only about 6 degrees inclination chosen to keep its detectors clear of the South Atlantic Anomaly; it carries two co-aligned conical-approximation Wolter Type-I optics of 133 nested shells each, coated with depth-graded Pt/SiC and W/Si multilayers that reflect light up to ~79 keV (the band's upper edge near the 78.4 keV platinum K-edge), focused at 10.14 meters along a 56-segment deployable mast that unfolded nine days after launch and is the longest ever flown on a space telescope, read by two CdZnTe focal-plane modules (FPMA and FPMB, each a 2×2 array of four 32×32-pixel chips at 0.6-mm pitch) at about 18-arcsecond resolution; built on Orbital Sciences' LEOStar-2 bus with the mast built by ATK of Goleta and the project led by Yunjin Kim, it has mapped radioactive ⁴⁴Ti in Cassiopeia A, found the M82 X-2 ultraluminous X-ray pulsar, measured near-maximal spin in NGC 1365 and Compton-thick reflection in NGC 1068, and discovered the Galactic-Center magnetar SGR J1745-2900; and it SUSTAINS the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#2 (axis-rotation #35 sustains). NuSTAR operates as a complete, fully successful mission — well past its two-year baseline, in extended operations as of its most recent reporting — one of the most productive hard-X-ray observatories ever flown.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis sustains when the next observation is the same way of seeing carried into a new regime.** The X-ray-astronomy axis sustains at obs#2 because NuSTAR keeps the focusing-telescope idea — grazing-incidence optics bringing cosmic X-rays to a focus — but carries it for the first time into the hard band above Chandra's cutoff; the substrate-form distinctness is the band reached, so the cadence continues the axis rather than rotating away from it.
2. **Light too hard for a single-material mirror can still be focused with a layered stack.** Above about 10 keV an iridium coating passes the photon through, but a depth-graded multilayer — many alternating Pt/SiC and W/Si layers with the spacings graded by depth — reflects successively harder light by constructive interference, extending the reflected band toward the platinum K-edge near 78.4 keV.
3. **A focal length that will not fit on the bus can grow in orbit.** The 10.14-meter focal length is far longer than the spacecraft, so the focal plane rides at the end of a 56-cube articulated boom that unfolded nine days after launch; a laser metrology system measures the boom's flexing so optics and detectors stay aligned in software.
4. **Focusing buys sensitivity, not sharpness.** NuSTAR's ~18-arcsecond imaging is far coarser than Chandra's sub-arcsecond view, but by concentrating the X-rays from a source onto a small, low-background detector it gains a factor ~50–100 in sensitivity over the collimated and coded-mask instruments before it — the right metric for the hard band is the faint source detected, not the fine detail resolved.
5. **One observatory reads many kinds of energetic object in the hard band.** The same focused beam mapped the radioactive engine of Cassiopeia A, caught the pulse of the M82 X-2 ultraluminous pulsar, weighed the spin of the NGC 1365 black hole, read the buried Compton-thick nucleus of NGC 1068, and discovered the SGR J1745-2900 magnetar near the Galactic Center.
6. **The hard band sees the engine, the obscured, and the extreme.** ⁴⁴Ti from a supernova remnant's core, the Compton-scattered light of a buried nucleus, and the pulse of a neutron star above its Eddington limit are all high-energy signatures the softer optics cannot bring to a focus — the second observation is what the first focusing hard-X-ray telescope sees that the soft-band Great Observatory could not.
7. **A continuation keeps the predecessor's vocabulary and references it by name.** Because NuSTAR sustains Chandra's axis, the shared focusing-X-ray vocabulary is correct and kept, and Chandra is referenced as the obs#1 soft/medium-band complement — but NuSTAR's own facts (the multilayer optics, the deployable mast, the CdZnTe focal planes, the air-launch, the ⁴⁴Ti map) and only its own six anchors are stamped; the predecessor's anchors and Chandra-specific facts stay with Chandra.
8. **Accuracy guardrails guard against the stale-infobox and fabricated-citation classes of error.** Using focal length 10.14 m, resolution 18 arcsec, mast deploy 21 June, coatings Pt/SiC and W/Si, band 3–79 keV, builder Orbital Sciences Corporation, qualitative sensitivity, the cautious operational phrasing, and only the verified citations — and keeping the magnetar discovery and timing papers and the pulsar and spin DOIs distinct — keeps the facts load-bearing and correct.

## Surprises

- **A grazing mirror can reflect 79 keV light if it is layered, not solid.** A single iridium coating passes hard X-rays straight through, yet a depth-graded stack of atomic-scale Pt/SiC and W/Si layers reflects them by constructive interference all the way up to the platinum K-edge near 78.4 keV — the hard band brought to a focus by interference physics rather than a single reflecting surface.
- **The telescope launched too short and unfolded to its working length in orbit.** The 10.14-meter focal length is longer than the spacecraft, so NuSTAR flew compact and extended a 56-cube boom nine days after launch to place the focal plane at the focusing distance — the longest mast ever flown on a space telescope, holding alignment under continuous laser metrology.
- **A forest woodpecker already reaches what is hidden under the bark the way NuSTAR reaches the buried source.** The Williamson's Sapsucker drills precise, orderly wells into the trunk to reach the sap and the wood-boring prey hidden deep under the bark that surface-foraging birds cannot get at — exactly the focused hard-X-ray view that reaches the obscured Compton-thick nucleus, the magnetar near the Galactic Center, and the radioactive engine deep inside a supernova remnant.
- **An unfurling fern frond mirrors the mast extending to its focal length.** The Licorice Fern's tightly coiled fiddleheads unfurl and extend outward to reach light and growing space — mirror to NuSTAR's deployable boom unfolding in orbit to its 10.14-meter focal length, both reaching outward to occupy the distance their function requires.

v1.224 NuSTAR is dedicated to the people who carried the focusing telescope into the hard X-ray band for the first time: Fiona A. Harrison, the principal investigator who led the Nuclear Spectroscopic Telescope Array at the California Institute of Technology; Yunjin Kim, the project manager who brought the Shuttle Radar Topography deployable-boom heritage to the observatory; the optics, detector, and operations teams at Caltech and NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory who figured the nested shells, applied the depth-graded Pt/SiC and W/Si multilayer coatings, and built the CdZnTe focal planes; Orbital Sciences Corporation, who built the LEOStar-2 bus and the Pegasus XL that carried the observatory aloft beneath the "Stargazer" L-1011; ATK of Goleta, California, who built the 56-cube mast that unfolded to its 10.14-meter focal length nine days after launch; Brian Grefenstette and Matteo Bachetti, whose results mapped the radioactive engine of Cassiopeia A and found the first ultraluminous X-ray pulsar; and Riccardo Giacconi, who founded the focusing-optics tradition NuSTAR carries into the hard band. For more than a decade the two co-aligned optics, held in alignment along the extended boom by laser metrology, have brought the high-energy sky to a focus and read the buried engines of the universe.
