1. **An axis sustains when the next observation is the same way of seeing carried into a new regime.**
   The X-ray-astronomy axis sustains at obs#2 because NuSTAR keeps the focusing-telescope idea — grazing-incidence optics bringing cosmic X-rays to a focus — but carries it for the first time into the hard band above Chandra's cutoff; the substrate-form distinctness is the band reached, so the cadence continues the axis rather than rotating away from it.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12049_

2. **Light too hard for a single-material mirror can still be focused with a layered stack.**
   Above about 10 keV an iridium coating passes the photon through, but a depth-graded multilayer — many alternating Pt/SiC and W/Si layers with the spacings graded by depth — reflects successively harder light by constructive interference, extending the reflected band toward the platinum K-edge near 78.4 keV.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12050_

3. **A focal length that will not fit on the bus can grow in orbit.**
   The 10.14-meter focal length is far longer than the spacecraft, so the focal plane rides at the end of a 56-cube articulated boom that unfolded nine days after launch; a laser metrology system measures the boom's flexing so optics and detectors stay aligned in software.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12051_

4. **Focusing buys sensitivity, not sharpness.**
   NuSTAR's ~18-arcsecond imaging is far coarser than Chandra's sub-arcsecond view, but by concentrating the X-rays from a source onto a small, low-background detector it gains a factor ~50–100 in sensitivity over the collimated and coded-mask instruments before it — the right metric for the hard band is the faint source detected, not the fine detail resolved.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12052_

5. **One observatory reads many kinds of energetic object in the hard band.**
   The same focused beam mapped the radioactive engine of Cassiopeia A, caught the pulse of the M82 X-2 ultraluminous pulsar, weighed the spin of the NGC 1365 black hole, read the buried Compton-thick nucleus of NGC 1068, and discovered the SGR J1745-2900 magnetar near the Galactic Center.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12053_

6. **The hard band sees the engine, the obscured, and the extreme.**
   ⁴⁴Ti from a supernova remnant's core, the Compton-scattered light of a buried nucleus, and the pulse of a neutron star above its Eddington limit are all high-energy signatures the softer optics cannot bring to a focus — the second observation is what the first focusing hard-X-ray telescope sees that the soft-band Great Observatory could not.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12054_

7. **A continuation keeps the predecessor's vocabulary and references it by name.**
   Because NuSTAR sustains Chandra's axis, the shared focusing-X-ray vocabulary is correct and kept, and Chandra is referenced as the obs#1 soft/medium-band complement — but NuSTAR's own facts (the multilayer optics, the deployable mast, the CdZnTe focal planes, the air-launch, the ⁴⁴Ti map) and only its own six anchors are stamped; the predecessor's anchors and Chandra-specific facts stay with Chandra.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12055_

   Using focal length 10.14 m, resolution 18 arcsec, mast deploy 21 June, coatings Pt/SiC and W/Si, band 3–79 keV, builder Orbital Sciences Corporation, qualitative sensitivity, the cautious operational phrasing, and only the verified citations — and keeping the magnetar discovery and timing papers and the pulsar and spin DOIs distinct — keeps the facts load-bearing and correct.
