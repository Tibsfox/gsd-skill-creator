## Summary

v1.49.1015 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.205 to 1.206, adding the Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope (Fermi) — the observatory of the continuous high-energy all-sky survey — as the obs#3 INTRA-AXIS continuation that SUSTAINS the GAMMA-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis (axis-rotation #28 sustained at obs#3). The gamma-ray-astronomy axis opened at v1013 Compton with the first comprehensive full-sky gamma-ray survey and the all-sky burst monitor, and sustained at v1014 Swift with the rapid precise localization of each burst and the multiwavelength follow-up of its fading afterglow; the forward continuation extends the axis to a third observation that is substrate-form-distinct. Fermi sweeps the whole high-energy sky every few hours, building a continuous, deep, far more sensitive map than the earlier high-energy telescope, taking the high-energy all-sky map the first gamma-ray Great Observatory began and remaking it as a deep, continuous survey.

Fermi was built as a NASA mission developed with the United States Department of Energy and partners in France, Germany, Italy, Japan, and Sweden, managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, and renamed in August 2008 for Enrico Fermi. It reached a low, near-equatorial Earth orbit on 11 June 2008, carried aloft as GLAST by a Delta II rocket from Cape Canaveral, and it continues to operate as an active, productive observatory. The observatory carries two instruments — the Large Area Telescope (LAT), a wide-field pair-conversion telescope, and the Gamma-ray Burst Monitor (GBM), an all-sky scintillation monitor — that together span from the burst regime to the very highest gamma-ray energies.

The signature of the mission is the continuous high-energy all-sky survey. Fermi's Large Area Telescope sweeps its wide field across the whole sky every few hours as the spacecraft rocks north and south on alternate orbits, building a continuous record of the high-energy sky rather than stepping through pointed observations.

The high-sensitivity high-energy map gave the high-energy sky its richest census. With far greater sensitivity, field of view, and resolution than the earlier high-energy telescope, Fermi charted thousands of high-energy sources, where the first high-energy all-sky map held a few hundred.

The gamma-ray pulsar population opened a new way to find and study the spinning stellar remnants. Fermi found a large population of gamma-ray pulsars, including ones that pulse only in gamma rays with no radio beam and many rapidly spinning millisecond pulsars.

The discovery of the giant galactic lobes revealed a previously unseen structure. Fermi's deep continuous map showed two enormous lobes of emission reaching far above and below the center of the galaxy, pointing to a time when the galactic center was far more active than its quiet present suggests.

The multimessenger gamma-ray counterpart helped open multimessenger astronomy. Fermi's burst monitor recorded a brief gamma-ray flash arriving together with a gravitational-wave signal from the merging of two compact stellar remnants, observed in gamma rays and gravitational waves at the same time.

The axis-relevant substrate of Fermi at this degree is its sustaining of the GAMMA-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#3, the continuous, deep, high-sensitivity high-energy all-sky survey. Fermi sustains the GAMMA-RAY-ASTRONOMY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#3 NEW LOCKED, sustains the HIGH-ENERGY-GAMMA-RAY-MAP-LINEAGE thread at obs#2 cumulative, and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#78 cumulative (axis-rotation #28 sustained at obs#3). The forward continuation may favor a later very-high-energy gamma-ray observatory that sustains the axis at obs#4 — especially a ground-based atmospheric-Cherenkov array reaching the highest energies — or a substrate-axis rotation #29 to an under-served axis.

Fermi sustains the gamma-ray way of seeing the sky on the catalog, and the mission records the Fermi-LAT and GBM teams, NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, the Department of Energy, and the international partners. ## Lessons Learned

1. **A continuous survey turns a set of pointings into a film of the whole sky.** By sweeping the whole sky every few hours rather than stepping through pointed observations, Fermi built a continuous record of the high-energy sky, showing that a continuous sweep watches the variable sky in a way a sequence of pointings cannot.
2. **Sensitivity is bought with exposure.** By accumulating a deep continuous exposure, Fermi's faint sources emerged as their photon counts rose above the background, showing that the significance of a faint detection grows as the square root of the exposure and that the deepest map is the longest-observed one.
3. **A wide field gathers the faint signal across the whole reach.** By taking in a large fraction of the sky at once with its wide-field telescope, Fermi gathered the faint high-energy light across the whole sphere, showing that a wide field is what lets a survey cover and deepen the whole sky at once.
4. **Searching one channel for a regular signal reveals what other channels miss.** By searching the high-energy data for the regular pulse, Fermi found gamma-ray pulsars with no radio beam, showing that a population can be hidden until the right channel is searched for the right signal.
5. **An axis sustains at a substrate-form-distinct third observation.** By sweeping and mapping the whole sky continuously rather than catching individual bursts, Fermi sustained the gamma-ray-astronomy axis at obs#3, showing that an INTRA-AXIS continuation extends a domain with a distinct form rather than repeating the prior two.
6. **A deep whole-sky map reveals large-scale structure a narrow view would miss.** By mapping the whole sky deeply, Fermi revealed the two giant lobes above and below the galactic center, showing that faint large-scale structure emerges only when the whole sphere is seen deeply at once.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across an INTRA-AXIS continuation.** Because Fermi reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.205 Swift template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the continuous survey while keeping the warm palette, making the axis-sustaining continuation a clean build.

## Dedication

v1.206 Fermi is dedicated to the teams that built and fly NASA's continuous high-energy all-sky observatory: the Fermi-LAT team across NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, the United States Department of Energy and the SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory, and the partners in France, Germany, Italy, Japan, and Sweden who built and run the wide-field telescope that maps the high-energy sky; the GBM team at NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center and the Max Planck Institute for Extraterrestrial Physics who built and run the all-sky monitor that watches for the bursts; and the international partners whose collaboration made the mission a shared one. It is dedicated as well to Enrico Fermi, for whom the observatory was renamed in 2008. Fermi sweeps the whole high-energy sky every few hours, building the deepest continuous map of the high-energy sources, and it continues to operate.
