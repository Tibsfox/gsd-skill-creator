## Surprises

- **One spacecraft carried a radar built to look straight down.** Rather than a scanning imager, CloudSat carried a single nadir-looking, non-scanning radar — the Cloud Profiling Radar — that sent its 94 GHz pulse straight down and read the echo from each layer of the cloud directly beneath it. The instrument did not sweep a swath; it drew a vertical curtain of the cloud column along the ground track, and from that curtain came the ice over the liquid water, the layering, and the anvils.
- **A millimetre wavelength to see the small particles.** CloudSat's CPR worked at 94 GHz — a wavelength of only about three millimetres — which made it roughly a thousand times more sensitive to the small particles of a cloud than a centimetre-band weather radar, sensitive down to about −30 dBZ. The short wavelength is what let the radar read the faint clouds that a rain radar would miss, a different band and purpose from GPM's dual-frequency precipitation radar.
- **The radar and the lidar flew fifteen seconds apart.** CloudSat did not fly alone: it flew in tight formation with CALIPSO at the head of the A-Train, the two spacecraft only about fifteen seconds apart along the same ground track, so that the CloudSat radar and the CALIPSO lidar read the same column of sky almost at once. The radar caught the cloud ice and water content; the lidar caught the thin cloud and aerosol layers; together they built a three-dimensional view of the clouds and the aerosols among them.
- **A storm-petrel that lives within the sea-fog.** The Fork-tailed Storm-Petrel (*Hydrobates furcatus*; older genus *Oceanodroma furcata*), a small, pale blue-grey seabird of the cool, fog-shrouded North Pacific, flies low over the misty open sea on long wings, feeding far from land on the wing, and comes ashore only at night to nest in burrows on the foggy coastal islands of the Pacific Northwest and Alaska — a creature of the cloud come down to the water, the living version of the fog that CloudSat profiled from above (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A crowberry that holds the foggy coastal bluff.** The Black Crowberry (*Empetrum nigrum*), a low, mat-forming evergreen shrub of the family Ericaceae with tiny needle-like leaves and small black berries, grows on the foggy coastal bluffs, headlands, peat bogs, and exposed subalpine heath of the Pacific Northwest (and circumboreally), holding the thin soil of the cool, misty margins (ecological description only; distinct from the mountain-heaths *Cassiope* and *Phyllodoce* it grows among) — bound with the throughline of the layered sky, holding the foggy bluff where the storm-petrel nests.
