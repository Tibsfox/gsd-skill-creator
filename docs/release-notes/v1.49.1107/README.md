## Mission Overview

CloudSat is the obs#10 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA Earth-observing mission that looked inside the clouds, carrying the first millimetre-wavelength cloud-profiling radar ever flown in space and slicing the vertical structure of Earth's clouds from top to bottom. ★ It was a NASA Earth System Science Pathfinder (ESSP) mission managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory; the Canadian Space Agency (CSA) contributed key Cloud Profiling Radar hardware (the extended-interaction klystrons / RF electronics subsystem), Colorado State University (CIRA) led the science data processing, the spacecraft bus was built by BAE Systems (formerly Ball Aerospace) of Broomfield, Colorado, and operations ran through the U.S. Air Force satellite-control network, with the U.S. Space Force (Space Systems Command) completing the operational mission in the later years. The Cloud Profiling Radar (CPR) was a nadir-looking, non-scanning millimetre-wave radar at 94.05 GHz (W-band, ~3 mm) — about 1000× more sensitive than ground weather radar (to roughly −30 dBZ), with ~500 m vertical and ~1.4 km horizontal resolution — and it measured the vertical profiles of clouds: radar reflectivity read as cloud ice and liquid water content and as cloud layering. The mission launched 28 April 2006 (03:02 PDT) on a Delta II 7420-10C from Vandenberg (Space Launch Complex 2W), together with CALIPSO on the same rocket, and joined the A-Train (the afternoon constellation, ~1:30 PM) in late May / early June 2006 in a sun-synchronous ~705 km orbit: a single spacecraft flying in close formation (~15 s apart) with CALIPSO, the radar and the lidar together reading the clouds below. The story is the launch from Vandenberg, the pair settling into the A-Train, the 94 GHz beam pulsing down and the echo returning from the cloud layers, the vertical structure of the cloud column read top to bottom, the global maps of the layered sky built scan by scan, and the long watch over the clouds that ran for some eighteen years before it ended. The science was central: the CPR sent its 94 GHz pulse straight down and measured the radar reflectivity returning from each layer of the cloud column, recovering the vertical profile of the cloud — the ice over the liquid water, the layering, the anvils — and, joined with the CALIPSO lidar ~15 s away, building a three-dimensional view of clouds and the aerosols among them. CloudSat carried the first spaceborne 94 GHz cloud-profiling radar; it returned the first global spaceborne radar profiling of the vertical structure of clouds; and, with CALIPSO, it flew the first formation pairing a cloud radar and a lidar for a combined 3-D cloud/aerosol view. ★ The instrument-first result is load-bearing: the CPR was the first 94 GHz (millimetre-wave, W-band) cloud-profiling radar in space. ★ The survey result is equally load-bearing: CloudSat returned the first global spaceborne radar profiling of cloud vertical structure — scoped to the global-radar advance (passive cloud imagery and spaceborne lidar profiled clouds before; the advance is the global 94 GHz radar profile). ★ The formation result is load-bearing: CloudSat and CALIPSO flew in tight formation so radar and lidar together saw the clouds and aerosols in three dimensions. ★★★ CloudSat is RETIRED (2006–2024), so the catalog frames its whole story in the PAST (CloudSat launched 28 April 2006, joined the A-Train, profiled the clouds, left the A-Train in February 2018 after a reaction-wheel loss, and ended in 2023–2024 — the CPR permanently turned off 15 December 2023, the spacecraft decommissioned ~20 March 2024). The 2011 battery anomaly drove the move to Daylight-Only Operations (the radar returned November 2011; CloudSat rejoined the A-Train May 2012), distinct from the 2018 reaction-wheel loss that ended its A-Train flight. ## Surprises

- **One spacecraft carried a radar built to look straight down.** Rather than a scanning imager, CloudSat carried a single nadir-looking, non-scanning radar — the Cloud Profiling Radar — that sent its 94 GHz pulse straight down and read the echo from each layer of the cloud directly beneath it. The instrument did not sweep a swath; it drew a vertical curtain of the cloud column along the ground track, and from that curtain came the ice over the liquid water, the layering, and the anvils.
- **A millimetre wavelength to see the small particles.** CloudSat's CPR worked at 94 GHz — a wavelength of only about three millimetres — which made it roughly a thousand times more sensitive to the small particles of a cloud than a centimetre-band weather radar, sensitive down to about −30 dBZ. The short wavelength is what let the radar read the faint clouds that a rain radar would miss, a different band and purpose from GPM's dual-frequency precipitation radar.
- **The radar and the lidar flew fifteen seconds apart.** CloudSat did not fly alone: it flew in tight formation with CALIPSO at the head of the A-Train, the two spacecraft only about fifteen seconds apart along the same ground track, so that the CloudSat radar and the CALIPSO lidar read the same column of sky almost at once. The radar caught the cloud ice and water content; the lidar caught the thin cloud and aerosol layers; together they built a three-dimensional view of the clouds and the aerosols among them.
- **A storm-petrel that lives within the sea-fog.** The Fork-tailed Storm-Petrel (*Hydrobates furcatus*; older genus *Oceanodroma furcata*), a small, pale blue-grey seabird of the cool, fog-shrouded North Pacific, flies low over the misty open sea on long wings, feeding far from land on the wing, and comes ashore only at night to nest in burrows on the foggy coastal islands of the Pacific Northwest and Alaska — a creature of the cloud come down to the water, the living version of the fog that CloudSat profiled from above (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A crowberry that holds the foggy coastal bluff.** The Black Crowberry (*Empetrum nigrum*), a low, mat-forming evergreen shrub of the family Ericaceae with tiny needle-like leaves and small black berries, grows on the foggy coastal bluffs, headlands, peat bogs, and exposed subalpine heath of the Pacific Northwest (and circumboreally), holding the thin soil of the cool, misty margins (ecological description only; distinct from the mountain-heaths *Cassiope* and *Phyllodoce* it grows among) — bound with the throughline of the layered sky, holding the foggy bluff where the storm-petrel nests.

## Dedication

v1.290 CloudSat is dedicated to the people at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, at the Canadian Space Agency, and at Colorado State University, and to the engineers and scientists of NASA and its partners who built and flew the radar that looked inside the clouds. The teams who put a millimetre-wave radar in orbit for the first time, tuned to 94 gigahertz so it could read the faint, small particles of a cloud that a rain radar would miss. The crews who launched it from Vandenberg with CALIPSO and flew the two spacecraft only fifteen seconds apart, so the radar and the lidar together drew the cloud in three dimensions. The people who kept the watch through a battery trouble and the loss of a reaction wheel, carrying the mission on for some eighteen years against a design life of less than two. And the scientists who, from the echo returning layer by layer, read the vertical structure of the sky — the ice over the liquid water, the layers and the anvils — building the first global record of how the clouds are built. The radar is quiet now, and the long record of the layered sky belongs to them.
