### THE GOLDEN-MANTLED GROUND SQUIRREL: THE FOREST EDGE DWELLER

The Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel is a medium-small ground squirrel of the **forest edge** -- rocky clearings, open coniferous forest, and montane meadow margins across western North America. At 170-300 grams (6-10 oz), it is similar in size to the Townsend's Ground Squirrel (78) but inhabits a completely different habitat: the forest-meadow interface rather than open grassland.

This is the engine's **FIFTH consecutive Sciuridae** (Yellow-bellied Marmot 75, Hoary Marmot 76, Olympic Marmot 77, Townsend's Ground Squirrel 78, Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel 79) -- the **Sciuridae quintet**, which **matches the Mustelidae quintet** (63-67) and the **Swallow quintet** (26-30) as the longest family run in the engine. **THREE-WAY TIE**. From marmots (75-77) to ground squirrels (78-79) -- same family, three genera (*Marmota*, *Urocitellus*, *Callospermophilus*), five habitats.

The Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel is the mammal most commonly **confused with chipmunks** in the PNW. Both have lateral body stripes. The key distinction: **no facial stripes**. Chipmunks have stripes running through their eyes and across their cheeks. The Golden-mantled has a clean, unstriped face with a distinctive golden-brown "mantle" across the head and shoulders.

### TAXONOMY: *Callospermophilus lateralis*

- **Callospermophilus** -- from Greek *kalos* ("beautiful") + *spermophilus* ("seed-lover," from *sperma* "seed" + *philos* "loving"). The genus was split from the broader *Spermophilus* in 2009 based on molecular phylogenetics. *Callospermophilus* = "beautiful seed-lover."
- **lateralis** -- from Latin "of the side," referring to the prominent lateral body stripes. Described by Say (1823).
- **Common names:** Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel. The "golden mantle" refers to the golden-brown wash across the head and shoulders.

Classification:

### THE SOUND: SHARP CHIP ALARM -- 80th Species-Side Acoustic Element

The Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel's alarm call is a **sharp chip** -- a single, percussive, high-pitched note:
- **Frequency range:** approximately 4,000-9,000 Hz
- **Duration:** 0.05-0.15 seconds per chip. Very brief -- sharp onset, rapid decay
- **Structure:** Single emphatic note, repeated at 1-3 second intervals when alarmed. Unlike the Townsend's rapid trill (78), the Golden-mantled's alarm is a single note with space between repetitions
- **Amplitude:** **E=4** -- moderate. Audible at 50-150 meters through forest edge habitat. The sharp frequency cuts through ambient forest sound
- **Function:** Alarm. Predator detection. Less colonial than Townsend's -- the alarm serves individual warning plus loose neighbor alert rather than colony-wide diving response

### ECOLOGY: THE FOREST EDGE DWELLER

**Habitat -- Forest Edge:** The interface between coniferous forest and open ground. Elevation 1,000-3,500 meters. Montane to subalpine. Vegetation: open coniferous forest (ponderosa pine, Douglas-fir, spruce, fir), rocky clearings, talus slopes, montane meadow margins. Range: western North America from British Columbia to Arizona. In the PNW: Cascade Range, Blue Mountains, eastern Oregon/Washington montane zones. Returns from pure grassland (78) to forest interface. Each quintet member in a different habitat: alpine rock (75), alpine meadow (76), Olympic alpine (77), grassland (78), forest edge (79).

**Social Structure -- Loosely Aggregated:** Less colonial than Townsend's. Not densely colonial. More solitary than grassland ground squirrels. Males somewhat territorial. Home ranges overlap. Individual alarm chips alert nearby individuals but do not trigger synchronized colony-wide diving.

**Diet -- The Seed-Lover:** True to its genus name ("beautiful seed-lover"). Conifer seeds, grass seeds, nut fragments. Berries and fungi seasonal -- mushrooms an important food source. Small proportion of insects. Scatter-hoarder: caches seeds and nuts for winter. True hibernator with food caches -- 5-7 months dormancy.

**The Chipmunk Confusion:** Body stripes (white central bordered by black) but **no facial stripes**. Chipmunks have stripes through eyes and cheeks. The Golden-mantled has a clean, unstriped face. 170-300 g vs chipmunk 30-70 g. Sits erect on rocks vs more arboreal. The most commonly misidentified mammal in the PNW.

**Predators -- Cross-Quintet Predation:** American Marten (degree 65, Mustelidae quintet) -- canopy-to-ground predator-prey. The marten descends from the conifer canopy to hunt ground squirrels at forest edge. A Mustelidae quintet member (65) preying on a Sciuridae quintet member (79). Red Fox (degree 71) -- hunts at forest margins. Short-tailed Weasel (degree 67) -- forest edge shared, predator-prey.
