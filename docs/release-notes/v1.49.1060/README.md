## Mission Overview

The Mars 2020 rover Perseverance is the obs#6 sustain of the MARS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA surface mission whose defining achievements are the first collection and caching of Mars samples for a future return, the first powered flight on another planet (Ingenuity), and the first in-situ resource production on another planet (MOXIE), all while searching Jezero Crater's ancient delta for signs of past microbial life. It was a wholly NASA / United States mission, developed and operated by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory; the rover was named via a NASA "Name the Rover" student essay contest won by Alexander Mather (then a seventh-grader at Lake Braddock Secondary School in Burke, Virginia; announced 5 March 2020, from roughly 28,000 entries). Perseverance is car-sized — about 1,025 kg on a six-wheel rocker-bogie chassis, built on the Curiosity (Mars Science Laboratory) chassis and slightly heavier than Curiosity (~899 kg) — and is nuclear-powered by a Multi-Mission Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generator (MMRTG) rather than solar; nuclear power is NOT a first here, because Curiosity was also MMRTG-powered. Mars 2020 launched on an Atlas V 541 (AV-088) from Cape Canaveral SLC-41 on 30 July 2020 (11:50 UTC) and landed in Jezero Crater, at "Octavia E. Butler Landing" (~18.4°N, 77.5°E), on 18 February 2021. The rover reached the surface by the sky crane it inherited from Curiosity — after a guided entry, a supersonic parachute, and a rocket-powered descent stage that lowered the rover on tethers — so the sky crane is heritage (Curiosity owns the first sky-crane landing, in 2012, obs#5), not a Perseverance first. What Mars 2020 added were two capabilities used for the first time at Mars: Terrain-Relative Navigation, in which the Lander Vision System compares descent imagery to onboard maps and autonomously diverts from boulders and cliffs toward a safe spot (~40 m accuracy), and the Range Trigger, which timed parachute deploy to position and velocity to shrink the landing ellipse. The science goals are astrobiology — to study habitability and seek signs of ancient microbial life (biosignatures) at Jezero — and sample return: to collect and cache rock and regolith cores for a future Mars Sample Return campaign, while also preparing for human exploration. The payload comprises Mastcam-Z (a zoomable mast color stereo camera); SuperCam (a laser-induced breakdown spectrometer plus Raman spectroscopy and imaging — the successor to Curiosity's ChemCam — that fires a laser to read rock composition several metres away); PIXL, the Planetary Instrument for X-ray Lithochemistry (fine-scale, map-resolution chemistry on the robotic arm); SHERLOC (a deep-ultraviolet Raman and fluorescence instrument for organics and potential biosignatures) with its WATSON close-up imager; MEDA (weather); RIMFAX, a ground-penetrating radar that reads the subsurface; MOXIE; and the Sampling and Caching System — a rotary-percussive coring drill and 43 sample tubes. Jezero Crater held an ancient lake fed by rivers that built a delta about 3.5 billion years ago; its clays and carbonates are exactly the settings that can preserve biosignatures, and SHERLOC, PIXL, and SuperCam read the rocks for organic matter and textures that could record past microbial life. Perseverance is the first mission to drill, hermetically seal, and cache rock and regolith cores on Mars for a planned future return to Earth — the first step of the Mars Sample Return campaign; it carries 43 tubes (38 for rock, regolith, and atmosphere plus 5 witness tubes), took its first rock core ("Montdenier," 6 September 2021), and in early 2023 deposited the first sample depot on another world at "Three Forks" (10 sealed tubes laid out in a zigzag ~5–15 m apart as a backup set, with duplicates kept aboard the rover). The Ingenuity helicopter, a ~1.8 kg solar-powered aircraft carried under the rover, made the first powered, controlled flight on another planet on 19 April 2021 (it climbed ~3 m and hovered ~30 s), then flew as an aerial scout — 72 flights and more than two hours of total flight time through 18 January 2024, over nearly three years, before rotor-blade damage ended its mission on 25 January 2024. The MOXIE experiment produced oxygen from the carbon-dioxide-dominated (~96% CO2) Martian atmosphere by solid-oxide electrolysis (the SOXE stack at ~800 °C), making its first oxygen on 20 April 2021 and producing ~122 g of oxygen across 16 runs (final run 7 August 2023) — the first time a usable resource was made on another world, a demonstration-scale proof of concept for future human missions and propellant. This is the SEARCH for signs of ancient microbial life plus sample caching — NOT a detection of life and NOT a life search; Viking (obs#1) carried the in-situ life-detection experiments, Curiosity (obs#5) assessed PAST habitability, and Perseverance seeks signs of ANCIENT life and caches samples but has NOT detected or found life; the organic matter and textures are potential biosignatures, not life, and the samples are cached for a future return, not yet returned to Earth. Perseverance remains operational, exploring Jezero's delta and crater rim. Perseverance SUSTAINS the MARS-EXPLORATION substrate-axis at obs#6 (substrate-axis rotation #36 sustains, the 5th consecutive sustain).

## Surprises

- **A small helicopter made the first powered flight on another planet.** The Ingenuity helicopter — a ~1.8 kg, solar-powered, four-rotor aircraft carried under Perseverance — lifted off the Martian surface on 19 April 2021, climbing ~3 m and hovering ~30 s in air about one percent as dense as Earth's, the first powered, controlled flight on another planet; it then flew 72 times over nearly three years as an aerial scout, far beyond its five-flight technology demonstration.
- **An instrument made breathable oxygen from Martian air.** The MOXIE experiment drew in the carbon-dioxide-dominated (~96% CO2) Martian atmosphere and split oxygen from it by solid-oxide electrolysis (the SOXE stack at ~800 °C), making its first oxygen on 20 April 2021 and producing ~122 g across 16 runs — the first time a usable resource was made on another world, a proof of concept for future human life support and rocket propellant.
- **A rover sealed rock cores and left a backup depot on Mars for a future return.** Perseverance drilled, hermetically sealed, and cached rock and regolith cores in metal tubes (the first rock core "Montdenier," 6 September 2021) and in early 2023 laid out the first sample depot on another world at "Three Forks" — 10 sealed tubes in a zigzag as a backup set, with duplicates carried onward — the first step of a future campaign to return the samples to Earth.
- **The sky crane that Perseverance reused was an inheritance, not a new first.** Perseverance reached the surface by the sky crane it inherited from Curiosity (the first sky-crane landing was Curiosity's, in 2012); what Mars 2020 added were Terrain-Relative Navigation, in which the descent imagery is compared to onboard maps to autonomously divert from hazards, and the Range Trigger, both used for the first time at Mars.
- **A jay glues thousands of caches into bark and recovers them through winter.** The Canada Jay (*Perisoreus canadensis*, formerly "Gray Jay"), a hardy boreal and montane Pacific Northwest jay, gathers thousands of food items through the warmer months and glues each one with sticky saliva into bark crevices and under lichen above the snow line, then retrieves these caches through the long winter — even nesting in late winter, living off its stores (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Perseverance caching sealed sample tubes to be retrieved later.
- **A plant stores its life in a taproot that can revive from a seemingly dead state.** The Bitterroot (*Lewisia rediviva*), a low perennial of dry, rocky ground east of the Cascades, stores water and energy in a fleshy taproot that lets it survive long dry dormancy and revive — its species epithet *rediviva* ("brought back to life") records that dried, seemingly dead roots can regrow when conditions return (ecological description only) — a reserve stored against a long gap and recovered later, a mirror of a cache that waits to be retrieved.
