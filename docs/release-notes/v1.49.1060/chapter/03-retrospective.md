## What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived mission imagery.** The Perseverance shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual Mastcam-Z panorama frames or a rover-on-the-surface image. A future revision could render a real Mastcam-Z mosaic (a Jezero delta outcrop or the crater rim, publicly available from the Planetary Data System) for a higher-fidelity view.
- **The Perseverance diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of a car-sized six-wheel rocker-bogie rover with its mast, instrument arm, the coring drill, and the Ingenuity helicopter.
- **The Ingenuity flight is depicted rather than driven by the real flight telemetry.** Ingenuity's takeoff and hover are depicted in the shader but not rendered from the archived flight telemetry of 19 April 2021; a future ship could drive the flight mode from the real rotor-speed and altitude profile for a quantitatively faithful first flight.
- **The MOXIE chemistry is depicted rather than computed.** The oxygen-production signatures in the MOXIE-and-the-delta mode are depicted rather than rendered from the archived MOXIE run data (the production-rate and efficiency curves across the 16 runs); a future ship could drive the chemistry mode from the real run records for a quantitatively faithful reading of in-situ oxygen production.

## Surprises

- **A small helicopter made the first powered flight on another planet.** The Ingenuity helicopter — a ~1.8 kg, solar-powered, four-rotor aircraft carried under Perseverance — lifted off the Martian surface on 19 April 2021, climbing ~3 m and hovering ~30 s in air about one percent as dense as Earth's, the first powered, controlled flight on another planet; it then flew 72 times over nearly three years as an aerial scout, far beyond its five-flight technology demonstration.
- **An instrument made breathable oxygen from Martian air.** The MOXIE experiment drew in the carbon-dioxide-dominated (~96% CO2) Martian atmosphere and split oxygen from it by solid-oxide electrolysis (the SOXE stack at ~800 °C), making its first oxygen on 20 April 2021 and producing ~122 g across 16 runs — the first time a usable resource was made on another world, a proof of concept for future human life support and rocket propellant.
- **A rover sealed rock cores and left a backup depot on Mars for a future return.** Perseverance drilled, hermetically sealed, and cached rock and regolith cores in metal tubes (the first rock core "Montdenier," 6 September 2021) and in early 2023 laid out the first sample depot on another world at "Three Forks" — 10 sealed tubes in a zigzag as a backup set, with duplicates carried onward — the first step of a future campaign to return the samples to Earth.
- **The sky crane that Perseverance reused was an inheritance, not a new first.** Perseverance reached the surface by the sky crane it inherited from Curiosity (the first sky-crane landing was Curiosity's, in 2012); what Mars 2020 added were Terrain-Relative Navigation, in which the descent imagery is compared to onboard maps to autonomously divert from hazards, and the Range Trigger, both used for the first time at Mars.
- **A jay glues thousands of caches into bark and recovers them through winter.** The Canada Jay (*Perisoreus canadensis*, formerly "Gray Jay"), a hardy boreal and montane Pacific Northwest jay, gathers thousands of food items through the warmer months and glues each one with sticky saliva into bark crevices and under lichen above the snow line, then retrieves these caches through the long winter — even nesting in late winter, living off its stores (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Perseverance caching sealed sample tubes to be retrieved later.
- **A plant stores its life in a taproot that can revive from a seemingly dead state.** The Bitterroot (*Lewisia rediviva*), a low perennial of dry, rocky ground east of the Cascades, stores water and energy in a fleshy taproot that lets it survive long dry dormancy and revive — its species epithet *rediviva* ("brought back to life") records that dried, seemingly dead roots can regrow when conditions return (ecological description only) — a reserve stored against a long gap and recovered later, a mirror of a cache that waits to be retrieved.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A sustain keeps the predecessor's axis vocabulary and replaces only the predecessor-specific content.** Perseverance shares the Mars-exploration vocabulary with the Curiosity predecessor (Mars, the red planet, the surface, regolith, the atmosphere, dust, in situ, planetary exploration, past water, sedimentary rock, delta, crater lake) — that vocabulary is shared and correct. The rewrite removed only the Curiosity / Mars Science Laboratory-specific content (the Mars Science Laboratory and Curiosity names, Clara Ma, Gale Crater, "Bradbury Landing," Yellowknife Bay, the "John Klein" mudstone, Mount Sharp / Aeolis Mons, the "Hottah" conglomerate, the 6 August 2012 landing, the sky-crane-as-a-first, the habitability anchor as Curiosity's, the Curiosity-only instruments CheMin and SAM with ChemCam becoming SuperCam, and the Brown Creeper + Cobra Lily pairing) and replaced it with Mars 2020 / Perseverance content.
2. **A sustain has no axis rotation to frame — and 1.242 was itself a sustain, so there is no rotation framing to remove.** The Mars axis opened at 1.238 (Viking, substrate-axis rotation #36); 1.239 (Mariner 9), 1.240 (Mars Pathfinder), 1.241 (the Mars Exploration Rovers), 1.242 (Curiosity), and 1.243 (Perseverance) are all SUSTAINS, so the clone source carries no rotation narrative, and at most a single distant note records that the Mars axis itself opened at 1.238 by rotating from the X-ray-astronomy axis (rotation #35, held complete at obs#15). The X-ray-axis vocabulary does not appear.
3. **The sky crane is heritage, not a Perseverance first — it is Curiosity's first (obs#5).** Perseverance reused Curiosity's sky crane (the first sky-crane landing was Curiosity's, in 2012) and added Terrain-Relative Navigation for autonomous hazard avoidance and the Range Trigger to shrink the landing ellipse. The catalog never re-stamps the sky crane and never calls Perseverance's landing a "first" landing of any kind except the Terrain-Relative-Navigation precision and hazard-avoidance addition (the first use at Mars).
4. **The biosignature search is not life detection, not Viking's extant-life search, and not Curiosity's habitability assessment.** Perseverance seeks signs of ANCIENT microbial life and caches samples — the organic matter and textures are potential biosignatures, not life. The catalog states throughout that Perseverance has NOT detected or found life, that this is distinct from Viking's in-situ extant-life search (obs#1) and from Curiosity's habitability assessment (obs#5), and that the cached samples have not yet been returned to Earth. All astrobiology prose is hedged ("signs of," "could record," "potential biosignatures").
5. **Perseverance is NUCLEAR (MMRTG), like Curiosity — nuclear power is not a first here.** Perseverance is powered by a radioisotope generator like Curiosity, so the catalog does not claim nuclear power as a first. Perseverance's distinct firsts are the first collection and caching of Mars samples for return, the first powered flight on another planet (Ingenuity), and the first in-situ resource production (MOXIE); the payload is Mastcam-Z, SuperCam, PIXL, SHERLOC with WATSON, MEDA, RIMFAX, and MOXIE plus the Sampling and Caching System (no CheMin / SAM; ChemCam becomes SuperCam).
6. **Sample caching for return is the distinct mission first — the samples are cached, not yet returned.** Perseverance is the first mission to drill, hermetically seal, and cache rock and regolith cores on Mars for a future return to Earth (the first step of the NASA-ESA Mars Sample Return campaign), with the first rock core ("Montdenier," 6 September 2021) and the first sample depot on another world ("Three Forks," early 2023). The catalog scopes this as the first to COLLECT and CACHE samples FOR RETURN and never claims the samples have been returned to Earth; this is distinct from all prior in-situ analysis (Viking, Pathfinder, the MERs, and Curiosity analyzed samples on Mars).
7. **Ingenuity's flight and MOXIE's oxygen are scoped firsts.** Ingenuity made the first POWERED, CONTROLLED flight on another planet (19 April 2021, 72 flights through 18 January 2024) — Mars's thin air made it a hard first, distinct from unpowered or uncontrolled parachute or aeroshell descent. MOXIE made the first usable resource (oxygen) on another world (first oxygen 20 April 2021, ~122 g across 16 runs) — the first demonstration of in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) on another planet, a demonstration-scale proof of concept.
8. **NASA / U.S.-mission framing is an accuracy requirement here.** Mars 2020 was a wholly NASA / United States mission, developed and operated by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory; the rover was named by Alexander Mather via a NASA student essay contest in 2020. Ingenuity, MOXIE, and the rover are wholly NASA / JPL.
9. **A sustain references its predecessor exactly once, deliberately.** The only correct predecessor reference is a single nav-prev lineage note to v1.242 Curiosity (Mars obs#5), plus lineage references to the Mars Exploration Rovers (1.241, obs#4), Mars Pathfinder (1.240, obs#3), Mariner 9 (1.239, obs#2), Viking (1.238, obs#1), and Mariner 4 (1.36, the first Mars flyby). The predecessors' anchors stay with them — 0 re-stamps.
10. **Clone source is Curiosity (a NASA nuclear habitability-assessment rover, Mars Science Laboratory, 2011–present); Perseverance is a NASA nuclear caching-and-capability rover (Mars 2020, Atlas V 541, 2020–present).** The most structural replacement in the rewrite is removing ALL Curiosity / Mars Science Laboratory-specific content — "Mars Science Laboratory," "Curiosity," "Clara Ma," "Gale Crater," "Bradbury Landing," "Yellowknife Bay," "John Klein," "Mount Sharp / Aeolis Mons," "Hottah," "the sky-crane-as-a-first," "the habitability anchor as Curiosity's," "CheMin," "SAM," "ChemCam" (which becomes SuperCam), "the 6 August 2012 landing," and "the Brown Creeper + Cobra Lily pairing" — and replacing with the Mars 2020 mission and the rover Perseverance, the Alexander Mather naming, the Atlas V 541 launch (30 July 2020) from SLC-41, Jezero Crater and "Octavia E. Butler Landing" and the inherited sky crane with Terrain-Relative Navigation (18 February 2021), the MMRTG nuclear power (not a first), the car-sized ~1,025 kg Curiosity-derived rover, the payload (Mastcam-Z, SuperCam, PIXL, SHERLOC with WATSON, MEDA, RIMFAX, MOXIE) plus the Sampling and Caching System, the sample caching for return ("Montdenier," "Three Forks"), Ingenuity (the first powered flight), MOXIE (the first in-situ oxygen), the biosignature search at the delta, the still-operating status, NSSDCA/COSPAR 2020-052A, and the Canada Jay + Bitterroot pairing.
