**Trumpeter Swan + Sandhill Crane** arrives at E=8 on the SPS track -- and with it arrives the SPS track's first Gruidae (crane family) entry, the oldest extant bird call in the engine's acoustic record, and one of the Pacific Northwest's most evocative migratory bird acoustic events. The Trumpeter Swan (*Cygnus buccinator*) is North America's largest native waterfowl: a massive white bird (wingspan up to 8 feet, weight up to 30 lbs) whose deep, resonant bugling call is among the most distinctive waterfowl vocalizations in the Pacific Northwest, a low-frequency nasal trumpet produced by a windpipe that coils within the sternum -- an anatomical adaptation that extends the resonating tube beyond what the neck's length would otherwise allow. The Sandhill Crane (*Antigone canadensis*) is the focal species of this entry: the Pacific Northwest's representative of the family Gruidae and the bearer of the most ancient confirmed bird call in the fossil record.

**FIRST GRUIDAE. SANDHILL CRANE: 2.5 MILLION YEAR FOSSIL RECORD.** The Sandhill Crane's appearance at degree 330 is the SPS track's first Gruidae (crane family) entry. The Gruidae are among the most ancient families of extant birds, with a fossil record extending to the Eocene (approximately 50 million years ago) and with specific Sandhill Crane fossils (*Antigone canadensis*) confirmed at 2.5 million years old in Nebraska -- making the Sandhill Crane the oldest bird species currently extant whose fossil record definitively confirms the presence of the modern species at that geological date. The call of the Sandhill Crane that is audible at a Pacific Northwest staging area today is acoustically similar to the call that was produced by individuals of the same species 2.5 million years ago. No other bird vocalization in the engine's 330-degree record carries a confirmed fossil record of equivalent age.

The Sandhill Crane call at degree 330 is the same call that was produced 2.5 million years before the present, in the Pleistocene, before *Homo sapiens* existed.
