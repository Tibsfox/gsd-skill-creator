1. **Post-streak resets follow a pattern: hold energy, dramatically simplify everything else.**
   Long neighborhood streaks (six-degree Capitol Hill) end with bands that close the neighborhood-centric arc; the next degree typically pairs the same energy level (E=5 held across 115 to 116) with dramatic simplification across genre, label tier, band format, and production approach. The post-streak reset is the 117th artist-city pattern, and the degree 116 execution confirms the structural principle: reset means recalibration, not decline, and held energy is the bridge across the transition.

2. **New taxonomic families often arrive immediately after record-setting streaks close.**
   The Picidae nonet's all-time-record close at degree 115 made space at degree 116 for a brand-new family — Cinclidae — to enter the engine. The catalog sequencing is deliberate: record-setting streaks compress the engine's family diversity rapidly during the streak and then release into new family territory immediately upon closure. Future streak-closure degrees should be evaluated for new-family opening opportunities at the next degree.
   _⚙ Status: `applied` (applied in `v1.49.273`) · lesson #6528_

3. **The DIY absolute (self-released, no label) is a meaningful structural category distinct from "indie.**
   Self-released artists occupy a specific endpoint of the label-independence spectrum — no label at all — that is structurally distinct from Sub Pop independence (which still involves a label institution with distribution, marketing, and press) and from Matador prestige indie (a well-funded indie with national reach). Pony Time's self-released status at degree 116 opens a new band of label-independence documentation and should be tracked across future degrees to see whether self-released artists recur and how they differ from labeled DIY acts.
   _⚙ Status: `applied` (applied in `v1.49.290`) · lesson #6529_

4. **Two-piece rock bands are a distinct format, not merely a reduced four-piece.**
   The duo format (Luke Beetham + Stacy Peck; historically The White Stripes, early Black Keys, Lightning Bolt, Death From Above 1979) produces a specific acoustic character — what two people can make in real time — that is not merely a subtraction from four-piece sound but a positive declaration of minimalism. Future duo-format degrees should be documented with attention to what the format adds (directness, immediacy, audible single-instrument clarity) rather than only what it removes (bass, keys, samples).
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #6530_

5. **Aquatic adaptations in songbirds are globally rare and structurally significant.**
   Cinclidae is the only aquatic passerine family in the world — five species globally, one in North America — and the American Dipper's seven-adaptation suite represents an evolutionary lineage that entered an ecological niche no other songbird entered. Future taxonomic-first degrees should include an explicit framing of the family's global context (number of species, geographic distribution, ecological niche uniqueness) to situate the local species within the larger taxonomic landscape.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #6531_

6. **Frequency-placement is a songbird's primary defense against broadband environmental noise.**
   The American Dipper places its song at 2,000-8,000 Hz specifically to clear the sub-2,000-Hz peak of rushing-water broadband noise. Similar frequency-placement strategies likely operate for other birds in noisy environments (wind-exposed habitats, urban traffic zones, waterfall-adjacent territories) and should be considered explicitly when documenting species in acoustically adverse environments. The river song's 117th acoustic element contributes a frequency-placement principle that can be applied across future degrees.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #6532_

7. **Year-round singing is rare among North American songbirds and is diagnostic of unusual territoriality.**
   Both the American Dipper male and female sing year-round including in winter — unusual among North American passerines — and this both-sexes year-round pattern correlates with year-round territorial defense of a permanent linear stream territory. Future resident-species degrees should check for year-round singing as an indicator of resident territoriality rather than assuming breeding-season-only song delivery.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #6533_
