**Part B: American Dipper Opens Cinclidae — The River Singer Arrives with a New Family.** The **American Dipper** (*Cinclus mexicanus*) is a stocky, dark-gray songbird of fast-flowing mountain streams across western North America — from Alaska south to Panama — and is **North America's only truly aquatic songbird**. It is the sole North American representative of **Cinclidae**, the dipper family, which contains five species globally (American, White-throated, Brown, White-capped, and Rufous-throated Dippers) distributed across the Americas, Europe, Asia, and Africa. Cinclidae is the **only aquatic passerine family in the world**: the dippers are the only songbirds on the planet that routinely submerge themselves in water, walk along stream bottoms, and forage underwater. The American Dipper enters cold fast-flowing mountain torrents, submerges completely, walks upstream against currents that would sweep a human off their feet, grips rocks with powerful feet, partially spreads its wings as hydrofoils to maintain position against the flow, and captures aquatic insect larvae (especially **caddisfly**, **mayfly**, and **stonefly** larvae), small fish, and fish eggs from between rocks on the stream bottom. The species has evolved a suite of **seven unique aquatic adaptations**: a **nictitating membrane** (translucent third eyelid) for underwater vision, **dense waterproof plumage** of approximately 3,000 feathers (nearly twice the count in comparable land birds), a **preen gland** ten times larger than in non-aquatic passerines for producing waterproofing oil, **closable nasal flaps** that seal the respiratory system during submersion, **strong legs and feet with sharp claws** for gripping slippery rocks, **elevated blood hemoglobin** concentrations for oxygen transport during 15-30 second dives, and a **low metabolic rate** relative to body size that reduces heat loss in near-freezing water. Also known as the **Water Ouzel** — an older name shared with the European Dipper — the American Dipper is a **year-round resident** in the **Pacific Northwest**, defending a **linear stream territory** typically 300 meters to 2 kilometers long, building a remarkable **dome-shaped moss nest** on a rock ledge, cliff face, or bridge support always immediately adjacent to fast-flowing water, and delivering a **loud, complex, bubbly song** that must compete with the roar of rushing water. The species is documented by the **Cornell Lab of Ornithology**, **Kelly Sullivan's** 1973 PhD dissertation on dipper ecology at the University of Montana, **Hugh Kingery's** 1996 *Birds of North America* species account, **Macaulay Library** audio recordings, and **USGS** stream ecology publications. The arrival at degree 116 opens **Cinclidae** as a new family (a major taxonomic event following the all-time record Picidae nonet at degree 115) and establishes the **river song** — a 2,000-8,000 Hz loud, varied, continuous bubbling stream of notes delivered from mid-stream rocks — as the 117th species-side acoustic element.

- **Garage rock fundamentals — loud, fast, distorted, immediate.** **Pony Time's** sound is built on **garage rock** fundamentals: **loud** (overdriven guitar, hit-hard drums), **fast** (short-form delivery, typical 1-3 minute songs), **distorted** (intentional fuzz and grit rather than clean tones), and **immediate** (no buildup, no slow introduction, no atmospheric preamble — the song starts, the song delivers, the song ends). This approach is **antithetical to the extended-form, atmospheric, and hypnotic approaches** that dominated the Capitol Hill streak: **Kinski's** motorik jams at degree 110, **Minus the Bear's** intricate math rock at degree 113, **Haunted Horses'** industrial noise at degree 114. After six degrees of elaborate construction on one neighborhood, degree 116 delivers **raw transmission** as the corrective: the simplest possible rock band playing the simplest possible version of rock music. The garage rock tradition persists across Pacific Northwest history — from **Mudhoney** (degree 28, Sub Pop, 1988) through Pony Time (degree 116, self-released, 2010s-present) — and the 2010s iteration places the tradition in the DIY context where cassettes and Bandcamp replaced college-radio airplay as the primary dissemination channel.
- **Bandcamp distribution as the DIY infrastructure.** **Pony Time's** catalog lives on **Bandcamp** — the platform that gives self-released artists direct distribution to listeners without intermediaries. Bandcamp is significant to degree 116 because it is the infrastructure that makes the DIY absolute viable as a long-term trajectory. Before Bandcamp, fully self-released artists depended on physical media (cassettes, 7" records, CDRs) and local distribution (in-person sales at shows, consignment at record stores, mail-order). Bandcamp centralizes direct-to-listener distribution with pay-what-you-want pricing, artist-retained rights, high-quality downloads, streaming previews, and a fan-following mechanism that creates a durable relationship between artist and listener. Pony Time's self-released catalog is possible in part because Bandcamp exists — the band's reach extends beyond physical proximity to shows, and the catalog persists even when the band is between releases.
- **The raw transmission — 117th artist-side acoustic element.** The **raw transmission** manifests as: **duo minimalism** (two instruments with vocals, no bass, no keys, sound is what two people can make in real time), **overdriven immediacy** (distorted guitar, loud drums, vocals cutting through on urgency alone, minimal signal chain instrument → amplifier → room → listener, minimal processing), **short-form delivery** (songs arrive immediately and end quickly, 1-3 minutes, no extended introductions no gradual builds no codas), and **lo-fi honesty** (production quality intentionally rough, room audible, bleed between instruments audible, imperfections uncorrected, recording as document of performance rather than construction in a studio). The 117th acoustic element contrasts with **the burning elegance** (degree 115, Pretty Girls Make Graves, angular architecture): the raw transmission is anti-architecture. Pretty Girls Make Graves was precise; Pony Time is blunt. Structure versus impulse. The raw transmission also contrasts with **the hypnotic engine** (degree 110, Kinski, extended patient layered): short urgent minimal rather than extended patient layered. 2-minute garage explosions versus 12-minute motorik jams. And with **the collapse frequency** (degree 109, No-Anchor, structured collapse): the raw transmission doesn't collapse because there is no structure complex enough to collapse. The simplicity is the resilience.
- **E=5 — garage rock as sustainable high intensity.** **Pony Time's** garage rock is high energy but not unhinged. Loud, fast, distorted, shouted, but structured around hooks that arrive immediately and songs that end before they overstay. The duo format limits sonic density by design. **E=5** correctly reflects energy delivered through directness rather than through overwhelming force — the garage-rock mid-intensity band that **Mudhoney** occupied at degree 28, that the duo format maintains through Pony Time, and that distinguishes E=5 from E=6 (overwhelming, unsustainable combustion). The energy level holds across the closure-opening boundary: degree 115 closed at E=5 on the artist side (Pretty Girls Make Graves' burning elegance), and degree 116 opens at E=5 on the artist side (Pony Time's raw transmission). Continuity at the intensity band while everything else resets.
- **The post-streak reset — 117th artist-city pattern.** Long neighborhood streaks end with **dramatic simplification**. Six degrees of Capitol Hill density (110-115) are followed by one degree of DIY minimalism (116). The energy level holds (E=5 at both 115 and 116), but everything else resets: genre (post-punk → garage rock), label (Matador → self-released), format (four-piece → duo), production (full studio → lo-fi practice-space), neighborhood (Capitol Hill → broader Seattle). The reset is not a decline — it is a recalibration. The engine strips away complexity to find the foundation. The foundation is: two people, amplified, playing for whoever shows up. The pattern becomes structural principle: long streaks end with resets that pair a held energy level against dramatic simplification across every other dimension. Track whether the post-117 trajectory confirms or breaks this pattern.
- **KEXP, The Stranger, and the DIY documentation network.** **Pony Time** is documented by **KEXP** (Seattle's listener-supported public radio station that covers independent music at every scale from the smallest house show to the largest stadium date), **The Stranger** (Seattle's alternative weekly that has chronicled Seattle music culture since 1991), and the Bandcamp platform's fan-following mechanisms. The documentation is adequate but thinner than for bands with label press infrastructure — which is appropriate, because the DIY approach by design reduces institutional paper trails. KEXP sessions and Stranger mentions provide enough external documentation to verify the band's activity, catalog, and live performances without requiring the Pitchfork review cycles or NME coverage that labeled bands accumulate.
- **Garage rock lineage — Mudhoney to Pony Time across Seattle decades.** The **Seattle garage rock tradition** runs from **Mudhoney** (degree 28, Sub Pop, 1988, E=5, grunge-adjacent garage rock that helped define the Sub Pop sound) through various iterations including **The Hookers** (Derek Fudesco's earlier project pre-Pretty Girls Make Graves), **The Murder City Devils** (post-punk with garage energy), and on through Pony Time (degree 116, self-released, 2010s-present, E=5, DIY duo garage rock). Same energy, same genre territory, different eras and different label infrastructure. The tradition persists across four decades because garage rock's appeal — loud, fast, immediate, direct — never requires updating. Pony Time's 2010s iteration places the tradition in the streaming/Bandcamp era but retains the core sound from 1988 Sub Pop Mudhoney through 2010s self-released Pony Time.

## Part B: American Dipper — The River Singer and Cinclidae's Opening

- **First Cinclidae entry — new family, major taxonomic event.** **Cinclidae** enters the engine for the first time at degree 116 through the **American Dipper** (*Cinclus mexicanus*). This is a **major taxonomic event**: Cinclidae is an entirely new family, distinct from every prior family in the engine. Globally, Cinclidae contains five species — **American Dipper** (*C. mexicanus*, North America), **White-throated Dipper** (*C. cinclus*, Europe and western Asia), **Brown Dipper** (*C. pallasii*, eastern Asia), **White-capped Dipper** (*C. leucocephalus*, northern South America), and **Rufous-throated Dipper** (*C. schulzi*, Andean South America) — distributed across the Americas, Europe, Asia, and Africa wherever fast-flowing clear mountain streams provide habitat. **Kingdom:** Animalia. **Phylum:** Chordata. **Class:** Aves. **Order:** Passeriformes. **Family:** Cinclidae. **Genus:** *Cinclus*. **Species:** *C. mexicanus*. The new family opens directly after the **Picidae nonet** (107-115) closed as the all-time record — a structural counterpoint where the engine's longest taxonomic family streak gives way immediately to a brand-new taxonomic family. The catalog sequencing is deliberate.
- **North America's only truly aquatic songbird — unique evolutionary lineage.** The **American Dipper** is **North America's only truly aquatic songbird** — the only passerine on the continent that routinely submerges itself in water, walks along stream bottoms, and forages underwater. Cinclidae globally occupies a unique ecological niche: **aquatic songbirds**. No other passerine family on the planet has entered this niche. The dippers diverged from other passerines to exploit an ecological opportunity that no other songbird has exploited, and the aquatic adaptations are profound and unique. The American Dipper is not a bird that lives near water; it is a bird that lives **in** water. It enters fast-flowing cold mountain streams, submerges completely, walks along the rocky bottom against currents that would sweep a human off their feet, captures aquatic insect larvae and small fish and fish eggs from between the rocks, and emerges dry and warm because its plumage is waterproof. This is an evolutionary achievement unique among passerines.
- **Seven unique aquatic adaptations — the integrated solution.** The American Dipper's aquatic capabilities rest on a **suite of seven integrated adaptations** that together enable underwater foraging: (1) **nictitating membrane** — a translucent third eyelid that covers the eye while submerged, specially adapted (thicker and more transparent than in non-aquatic species) for underwater vision; (2) **dense waterproof plumage** — approximately 3,000 feathers (compared to 1,500-2,000 in comparable-sized land birds), trapping an insulating air layer against the skin; (3) **oversized preen gland** — the uropygial gland is approximately ten times larger than in comparable-sized land birds, producing waterproofing oil the bird spreads across its plumage during preening; (4) **closable nasal flaps** — movable flaps over the nostrils that seal when the bird submerges; (5) **strong legs and feet with sharp claws** — powerful relative to body size, gripping slippery rocks on stream bottoms, using partially opened wings as hydrofoils against the flow; (6) **elevated blood hemoglobin** — oxygen-carrying capacity above non-aquatic passerines, supporting 15-30 second underwater dives; (7) **low metabolic rate for body size** — reducing heat loss during cold-water immersion. Seven adaptations, integrated. No other songbird has this combination. This is how a passerine becomes aquatic.
- **Foraging behavior — walking, swimming, diving underwater.** The **American Dipper's** foraging sequence: the bird perches on a mid-stream rock, **dipping** rhythmically (the knee-bend behavior that gave the family its name); plunges into the water either by walking in from a rock or by flying directly into the current; underwater, walks along the stream bottom gripping rocks with strong feet, partially spreading wings to control position against the current; probes between and under rocks for **caddisfly**, **mayfly**, and **stonefly** larvae, small fish, and fish eggs; after 10-30 seconds underwater, surfaces — often popping up on a rock downstream with prey in its bill. The bird forages in water up to a meter deep and in currents that would sweep a human off their feet, in water temperatures 0-4°C routinely. The constant dipping when perched may serve visual communication, depth-gauging, or nervous-behavior functions — the function is debated, but the behavior is diagnostic. If a small gray bird is bobbing up and down on a rock in a stream, it is an American Dipper.
- **Territory and nesting — linear stream territory and dome moss nests.** The American Dipper is a **year-round resident** in the **Pacific Northwest**. It does not migrate seasonally (though it may move to lower elevations in winter when high-elevation streams freeze). Its territory is **linear** — a stretch of stream, typically 300 meters to 2 kilometers long — defended against other dippers. The territory must include foraging habitat (rocky-bottomed stream with fast current), nest sites (cliff faces, bridge undersides, or other structures over or adjacent to water), and sufficient food (aquatic invertebrates). The nest itself is a remarkable structure: a **dome-shaped moss nest** built on a rock ledge, cliff face, or bridge support, always over or immediately adjacent to fast-flowing water, with the nest entrance facing the stream. The dome shape provides protection from rain and predators; the moss construction allows the nest to blend with cliff faces and bridge structures; the over-water placement ensures constant acoustic backdrop of rushing water for chicks. The chicks grow up hearing the river.
- **The river song — 2,000-8,000 Hz continuous bubbly projection.** The American Dipper's song is a **continuous, varied, bubbling stream of notes** — high and low, trills and warbles and chattering phrases, delivered in a rapid seemingly improvised sequence that can last for many minutes. The song is often compared to a **Winter Wren's** song but louder, longer, and more varied. Individual phrases are strung together in unpredictable sequence, creating a complex flowing acoustic stream that parallels the physical stream below. The song is delivered from a prominent rock in mid-stream with the bird facing upstream (the direction from which competitors would approach). **Both males and females sing** — unusual among North American songbirds. The song is heard **year-round, including in winter**, when the bird sings even as it forages in near-freezing water. Fundamental frequency range: **2,000-8,000 Hz**.
- **Acoustic adaptations for stream noise — frequency placement above the broadband roar.** The dipper's song is specifically adapted to cut through the broadband noise of rushing water: **frequency range** placed above the dominant energy of stream noise (which peaks below 2,000 Hz), **high amplitude** relative to the bird's 55-75g body size, **varied complex structure** that creates a signal acoustically distinctive against the uniform broadband character of water noise, and **continuous delivery** that ensures territorial neighbors hear the song even if individual phrases are momentarily masked by particularly loud water surges. The acoustic challenge is the loudest natural environment any songbird routinely encounters. The solution is engineering: frequency placement, amplitude, complexity, continuity. The dipper does not just sing near water — it sings **over** water, **through** water, **despite** water.
- **E=5 — singing over the river.** **E=5** is the correct energy level for the American Dipper. The bird must generate enough volume to be heard above rushing water — this requires genuine acoustic power. The song is loud, assertive, and carries along stream corridors. It is not the overwhelming E=6 of the **Pileated Woodpecker's** primal scream in silent old-growth forest (degree 108), but it is arguably more impressive acoustically: the dipper achieves audibility in the noisiest natural environment a songbird encounters. The acoustic achievement: a 60-gram bird singing over a mountain torrent. The E=5 placement reflects high sustained intensity engineered through frequency placement and acoustic structure rather than raw amplitude alone.
- **Fire-to-water transition — most dramatic ecosystem shift the engine has documented.** Degree 116 completes the engine's **most dramatic ecosystem transition**: from **burn forest** (Black-backed Woodpecker, degree 115, fire ghost, obligate fire-follower in post-fire snag fields) to **fast mountain stream** (American Dipper, degree 116, river singer, only aquatic songbird in cold flowing water). Fire to water. Charred snag fields to rushing mountain streams. Dead trees to living water. The contrast is total: **habitat** — post-fire snag field (115) versus mountain stream with rapids and cascades (116), one defined by death and aftermath, the other by constant motion and life; **sound environment** — open canopy-free burn with unobstructed acoustics (115) versus rushing water noise that drowns everything (116); **ecological strategy** — fire-following specialist that requires destruction (115) versus stream-resident that requires permanent flowing water (116); **movement** — nomadic following fire across landscapes (115) versus territory-faithful occupying the same stream stretch year after year (116). The transition marks the end of the Picidae disturbance-ecology era and the beginning of something entirely new. The engine has moved from the forest — in all its forms, from old-growth to charred remains — to the stream.
- **Cornell, Sullivan 1973, Kingery 1996, Macaulay Library — the source base.** The American Dipper is documented by the **Cornell Lab of Ornithology** (the definitive life-history repository for North American birds), **Kelly Sullivan's** 1973 PhD dissertation on the ecology of the American Dipper at the University of Montana (the foundational ecological treatment), **Hugh Kingery's** 1996 *Birds of North America* species account (No. 229, the standard species monograph), **Macaulay Library** audio recordings (the reference collection for dipper vocalizations), and **USGS** stream ecology publications that contextualize the dipper within Pacific Northwest aquatic ecosystems. The source base is substantial and institutional — as it must be, because the species' remarkable adaptations have attracted scientific attention across multiple decades and disciplines. The degree 116 research synthesizes these sources into the dipper profile documented in the Part B artifacts.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **The raw transmission concept accurately captured Pony Time's acoustic quality.** Two-person garage rock with no mediation — overdriven guitar + pounding drums + vocals + lo-fi production + short songs. The description matches the sound verified from Bandcamp recordings and KEXP session documentation. The 117th acoustic element integrates cleanly into the engine's accumulating acoustic-element ledger without requiring revision of prior elements, and the contrast framing against **the burning elegance** (115), **the hypnotic engine** (110), and **the collapse frequency** (109) clarifies what is distinctive about the raw transmission rather than simply restating its features.
- **The river singer concept accurately captured the American Dipper's acoustic achievement.** The dipper's song competing with rushing water is one of the most remarkable acoustic feats in North American ornithology. The concept is well-supported by the Cornell Lab of Ornithology life-history account, Macaulay Library audio recordings, Sullivan 1973, and Kingery 1996. The 117th species-side acoustic element integrates cleanly into the ledger and the acoustic-environment framing (the loudest natural environment a small songbird encounters) clarifies why E=5 is impressive here rather than merely adequate.
- **E=5 on both axes is defensible and synchronized.** Pony Time's garage rock is high energy but structured — loud, fast, immediate, but not the overwhelming E=6 of unsustainable combustion. The American Dipper's song projects above 2,000 Hz at remarkable amplitude for body size but is not the overwhelming E=6 of Pileated Woodpecker primal screaming in silent forest. Both axes arrive at E=5 through different routes and for different reasons, and the synchronized placement preserves the four-cell E=5 block across the 115-116 closure-opening boundary on both axes.
- **The fire-to-water transition framing is structurally accurate and verifiable.** Black-backed Woodpecker at degree 115 is a true fire-dependent specialist documented by Hutto 2008, Nappi 2015, Siegel 2018, and USFS fire ecology sources. American Dipper at degree 116 is a true aquatic-stream specialist documented by Cornell, Sullivan 1973, Kingery 1996, and Macaulay Library. The contrast between these two habitats (post-fire snag field vs. rushing mountain stream) is maximal, and the 115→116 degree-adjacency makes the transition structurally meaningful rather than merely thematically noted.
- **The first Cinclidae framing as a major taxonomic event is correct and significant.** Cinclidae is genuinely a new family for the engine — no prior degree included a Cinclus species. The globally-five-species family count, the "only aquatic passerine family in the world" framing, and the seven-adaptation integrated-solution summary all rest on solid Cornell Lab and ornithological-literature sourcing.

### What Could Be Better

- **Pony Time's source base is thinner than for major-label artists.** Bandcamp catalog, KEXP sessions, The Stranger mentions, and the inferred duo format and DIY network participation are adequate but do not match the Pitchfork-NME-Spin-critical-discography documentation that Pretty Girls Make Graves (degree 115) or Minus the Bear (degree 113) accumulated across their active years. This is structurally appropriate — DIY artists by design reduce institutional documentation — but future self-released-artist degrees may benefit from more explicit acknowledgment of the thinner paper trail and from earlier incorporation of primary-source audio listening into the research methodology rather than relying on aggregated press mentions.
- **The duo format's specific discography (album names, release dates, song titles) is underspecified in the current research artifacts.** The research captures the duo's aesthetic and network participation cleanly but does not enumerate specific releases with years the way Pretty Girls Make Graves' *Good Health* / *The New Romance* / *Elan Vital* discography was documented. Subsequent DIY-act degrees should aim to include at least 2-3 specific release titles with years, even when the catalog is self-released and lacks institutional Wikipedia-scale documentation.
- **The seven aquatic adaptations could be paired with more explicit citations to the primary physiological literature.** Sullivan 1973 and Kingery 1996 are referenced in aggregate, but individual adaptations (nictitating membrane, preen gland ten-times-larger claim, elevated hemoglobin) would benefit from per-adaptation source attribution. This is a minor refinement that would strengthen the research artifacts' ability to be audited against primary sources.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Post-streak resets follow a pattern: hold energy, dramatically simplify everything else.** Long neighborhood streaks (six-degree Capitol Hill) end with bands that close the neighborhood-centric arc; the next degree typically pairs the same energy level (E=5 held across 115 to 116) with dramatic simplification across genre, label tier, band format, and production approach. The post-streak reset is the 117th artist-city pattern, and the degree 116 execution confirms the structural principle: reset means recalibration, not decline, and held energy is the bridge across the transition.

2. **New taxonomic families often arrive immediately after record-setting streaks close.** The Picidae nonet's all-time-record close at degree 115 made space at degree 116 for a brand-new family — Cinclidae — to enter the engine. The catalog sequencing is deliberate: record-setting streaks compress the engine's family diversity rapidly during the streak and then release into new family territory immediately upon closure. Future streak-closure degrees should be evaluated for new-family opening opportunities at the next degree.

3. **The DIY absolute (self-released, no label) is a meaningful structural category distinct from "indie."** Self-released artists occupy a specific endpoint of the label-independence spectrum — no label at all — that is structurally distinct from Sub Pop independence (which still involves a label institution with distribution, marketing, and press) and from Matador prestige indie (a well-funded indie with national reach). Pony Time's self-released status at degree 116 opens a new band of label-independence documentation and should be tracked across future degrees to see whether self-released artists recur and how they differ from labeled DIY acts.

4. **Two-piece rock bands are a distinct format, not merely a reduced four-piece.** The duo format (Luke Beetham + Stacy Peck; historically The White Stripes, early Black Keys, Lightning Bolt, Death From Above 1979) produces a specific acoustic character — what two people can make in real time — that is not merely a subtraction from four-piece sound but a positive declaration of minimalism. Future duo-format degrees should be documented with attention to what the format adds (directness, immediacy, audible single-instrument clarity) rather than only what it removes (bass, keys, samples).

5. **Aquatic adaptations in songbirds are globally rare and structurally significant.** Cinclidae is the only aquatic passerine family in the world — five species globally, one in North America — and the American Dipper's seven-adaptation suite represents an evolutionary lineage that entered an ecological niche no other songbird entered. Future taxonomic-first degrees should include an explicit framing of the family's global context (number of species, geographic distribution, ecological niche uniqueness) to situate the local species within the larger taxonomic landscape.

6. **Frequency-placement is a songbird's primary defense against broadband environmental noise.** The American Dipper places its song at 2,000-8,000 Hz specifically to clear the sub-2,000-Hz peak of rushing-water broadband noise. Similar frequency-placement strategies likely operate for other birds in noisy environments (wind-exposed habitats, urban traffic zones, waterfall-adjacent territories) and should be considered explicitly when documenting species in acoustically adverse environments. The river song's 117th acoustic element contributes a frequency-placement principle that can be applied across future degrees.

7. **Year-round singing is rare among North American songbirds and is diagnostic of unusual territoriality.** Both the American Dipper male and female sing year-round including in winter — unusual among North American passerines — and this both-sexes year-round pattern correlates with year-round territorial defense of a permanent linear stream territory. Future resident-species degrees should check for year-round singing as an indicator of resident territoriality rather than assuming breeding-season-only song delivery.

8. **Ecosystem transitions between adjacent degrees produce structural weight.** The fire-to-water transition from degree 115 (burn forest) to degree 116 (fast stream) is the engine's most dramatic ecosystem shift documented to date, and the adjacency (consecutive degrees) makes the contrast maximally visible. Future degree-pair planning should consider whether two consecutive degrees can span a dramatic ecosystem boundary (forest-to-water, low-elevation-to-high-elevation, intact-to-disturbed, marine-to-terrestrial), because the adjacency produces structural weight that non-adjacent pairings cannot replicate.
