**Naked Giants and the Power-Trio Convergence Format.** **Naked Giants** formed in **Seattle** in 2013 from the University of Washington music community around core members **Grant Mullen** (guitar and vocals), **Gianni Aiello** (bass), and **Henry LaVallee** (drums). The band's power-trio configuration is not a minimalist arrangement of convenience; it is a structural convergence format in which each of three musicians must occupy a complete instrumental role rather than the rhythmic-support subordination a four-or-five-piece configuration would typically assign. Mullen's single-guitar part fills the harmonic and lead-line space a two-guitar band distributes across rhythm-and-lead roles. Aiello's bass operates as a co-lead instrument — fat, distorted, melodic — carrying harmonic content and melodic motion that a conventional-rock bass would cede to the absent second guitar. LaVallee's drumming functions as both anchor and accelerator, pushing the trio forward with relentless dynamic variation. The power-trio format is a trust exercise: each member fills the assigned sonic space completely because any vacant space would collapse the formation's integrity. Naked Giants' two **Sub Pop** LPs *SLUFF* (2018) and *The Shadow* (2020) are the direct documentation of the power-trio convergence, and both records deliver volume-per-capita efficiency that exceeds the output-to-personnel ratio of larger-headcount bands in the engine's band-format vocabulary.

**Bighorn Sheep Clash and the First Impact-Generated Sound.** The **Bighorn Sheep** (*Ovis canadensis*) is the North American wild sheep of the rocky-slope-and-canyon ecosystem, a Bovidae caprid whose **Okanogan Highlands**, **Yakima Canyon**, **Hells Canyon**, **Rocky Mountain**, and **Cascade Range** populations span some of the most dramatic canyon-and-rocky-slope terrain on the continent. Rams reach body masses of 200-300 pounds with combined horn masses reaching 30+ pounds (up to 12% of ram body weight) at full curl, and ewes typically reach 120-160 pounds with smaller horns. The species was nearly extirpated in Washington by the early 1900s through over-hunting, habitat loss, and disease transmission from domestic sheep; a multi-decade **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife** transplant-and-reintroduction program has restored the statewide population to 1,500-2,000 animals across primary herds in the Okanogan Highlands, Yakima Canyon, and Hells Canyon. The engine's documentation at degree 205 focuses specifically on the **ram clash** — a ritual-combat behavior during the fall rut (October-December) in which two mature rams charge head-on from 20-40 feet apart at individual speeds of 20-40 mph, producing combined closing speeds exceeding 60 mph and impact forces measured up to 800 pounds. The clash is the **first impact-generated sound** in the SPS engine, distinct in acoustic-mechanism from every prior SPS entry (all vocalizations — calls, cries, howls, huffs, bleats) and opening a new acoustic-category that the engine's vocabulary had not previously documented.

**The Cranial-Engineering Register: Double-Layered Skull and Frontal Sinus Shock Absorption.** The Bighorn ram's capacity to repeat 800-lb cranial-impact events dozens of times in a single rutting-contest session without routine neurological damage depends on an impact-absorption chain that is among the most sophisticated biomechanical systems in North American mammalian biology. The ram skull's **double-layered bone** construction pairs an outer layer of cortical bone with an inner layer separated by a **cancellous-bone strut structure** that functions as a biological crumple zone — the outer layer absorbs the initial impact, the cancellous struts distribute the force across the inner structure, and the brain remains protected within the inner layer. **Frontal sinuses** — air-filled cavities in the frontal bone substantially larger in rams than in ewes — function as pneumatic shock absorbers that further distribute impact energy before it reaches the brain. The **horn bases** (bony cores covered in keratin sheath) rather than the horn tips are the impact points, and the **horn curl** directs impact force away from the skull and into the horn-base-to-skull junction where the cancellous-and-sinus absorption chain activates. **Cervical vertebrae** reinforced with massive tendons and thick vertebral processes absorb residual impact force that passes through the skull, completing the anterior-body impact-absorption chain from horn tips through horn bases through outer skull layer through cancellous struts through inner skull layer through frontal sinuses through cervical vertebrae into the shoulder girdle. The engineering is not incidental; it is the evolved precondition without which the ritual-combat behavior could not exist.

## Part B: Bighorn Sheep Clash — The Resonant Cranial Impact and the First Impact-Generated Sound in the Engine

Body mass reaches 200-300 pounds in rams and 120-160 pounds in ewes, with ram combined horn mass reaching 30+ pounds (up to 12% of body weight) at full curl and continuing to grow through 7-8 years of age. The species is a rocky-slope-and-canyon specialist whose ecological niche concentrates in terrain that excludes most competing mammalian herbivores.
- **Pacific Northwest and Continental Distribution:** Bighorn Sheep populations are distributed across the **Okanogan Highlands** in north-central Washington, the **Yakima Canyon** in south-central Washington, the **Hells Canyon** along the Snake River on the Idaho-Oregon-Washington border, the **Rocky Mountain** populations across Montana-Wyoming-Colorado-Idaho-Utah-Nevada, the **Cascade Range** populations in eastern Washington, and smaller California and Mexican populations. Washington statewide population is estimated at 1,500-2,000 animals following the **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife** multi-decade transplant-and-reintroduction program that restored the species after near-extirpation by the early 1900s through over-hunting, habitat loss, and domestic-sheep disease transmission (particularly *Mycoplasma ovipneumoniae* respiratory infection).

The clash duration is a fraction of a second per impact event, but rutting contests involve repeated charges — two rams may clash dozens of times over hours with rest periods between bouts. The stakes are mating dominance: the winner mates with the ewes, the loser walks away, and the system is designed to determine dominance without lethal damage through the ram's specialized impact-absorption biomechanics.
