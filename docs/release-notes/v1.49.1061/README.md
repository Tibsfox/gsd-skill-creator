## Mission Overview

InSight is the obs#7 sustain of the MARS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA geophysics lander whose defining achievements are the first dedicated study of the deep interior of Mars, the first seismometer to record marsquakes, the first determination of a planet's deep interior structure beyond Earth, and the first interplanetary launch from the US West Coast. It was a NASA Discovery Program mission managed by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, with the lander built by Lockheed Martin (derived from the Phoenix lander) and major international instruments: SEIS (the Seismic Experiment for Interior Structure, an ultra-sensitive seismometer led by CNES, France, with IPGP), HP3 (the Heat Flow and Physical Properties Package, "the mole," led by DLR, Germany), and RISE (the Rotation and Interior Structure Experiment, a JPL X-band radio-science measurement of Mars's rotation and wobble). A stationary, solar-powered lander — NOT a rover; it did not drive or traverse — InSight used a roughly 1.8 m robotic arm to lift SEIS off the deck, set it on the ground, and lower a wind-and-thermal shield over it, and to place the HP3 mole. It launched on an Atlas V 401 from Vandenberg Air Force Base SLC-3E, California, on 5 May 2018 (04:05 PDT) — the first interplanetary launch from the US West Coast — and, after a roughly 6.5-month cruise, landed on Elysium Planitia (4.502°N, 135.623°E; a flat, smooth, low-elevation equatorial plain chosen for entry-descent-landing safety and a stable seismometer platform) on 26 November 2018 by a Phoenix/Viking-heritage propulsive descent (aeroshell, supersonic parachute, retro-rockets, and three legs — NOT airbags and NOT a sky crane). The science is its listening: SEIS recorded the first marsquakes (the first confirmed event ~6 April 2019, Sol 128; ultimately more than 1,300 seismic events), the first successful seismic monitoring of another planet — distinct from Viking 2's deck-mounted seismometer of 1976, which was dominated by wind and never confirmed a marsquake (and the Viking 1 seismometer never uncaged), and from the Apollo seismometers, which recorded moonquakes on the Moon, a satellite. From the marsquake waves and the RISE measurement of Mars's wobble, the team made the first determination of the layered deep interior of another planet: a crust a few tens of kilometres thick (commonly cited as roughly 20–40 km, layered), a mantle, and a surprisingly large liquid (molten iron-alloy) core of radius about 1,830 km (confirmed 2021, Stähler et al.); Earth's interior is read by terrestrial seismology and the Moon's by the Apollo seismometers, but the Moon is a satellite, so InSight is the first for a planet beyond Earth. The HP3 "mole" was designed to hammer about 5 m down to measure heat flowing out of Mars, but the unexpected "duricrust" soil did not provide the friction it needed; it could not burrow to depth, and the heat-flow measurement was not achieved — InSight stamps no heat-flow first, and its science is the seismology and interior structure. InSight also detected meteorite impacts seismically and recorded a large ~M4.7 quake in May 2022. Dust on the solar panels slowly reduced power; the last contact was about 15 December 2022, and the mission was declared complete on 21 December 2022, about four years after landing. ## Surprises

- **A seismometer set on the ground recorded the first marsquakes.** InSight's SEIS instrument, lifted off the deck by a robotic arm, set on the surface, and covered by a wind-and-thermal shield, recorded the first marsquakes (the first confirmed event ~6 April 2019, Sol 128) and ultimately more than 1,300 seismic events — the first successful seismic monitoring of another planet, where Viking 2's wind-swamped deck seismometer of 1976 never confirmed a quake and the Apollo seismometers worked on the Moon, a satellite.
- **The waves of distant quakes read the inside of a planet for the first time beyond Earth.** From the way marsquake waves bent and reflected through the planet, plus the RISE radio measurement of Mars's rotation and wobble, the team determined Mars's layered deep interior — a crust a few tens of kilometres thick, a mantle, and a surprisingly large liquid (molten iron-alloy) core of radius about 1,830 km (confirmed 2021) — the first such reading of a planet's interior beyond Earth.
- **A Mars mission launched toward another planet from the US West Coast for the first time.** InSight lifted off on an Atlas V 401 from Vandenberg Air Force Base on 5 May 2018 — the first interplanetary launch from the US West Coast, where every prior US interplanetary mission had launched from Cape Canaveral, Florida; the Atlas V 401's performance margin overcame the energy penalty of the southerly Vandenberg trajectory.
- **The heat-flow "mole" could not get a grip in the soil.** The HP3 mole was designed to hammer about 5 m down to read heat flowing out of Mars, but the unexpected "duricrust" soil did not give it the friction it needed; it could not burrow to depth, so the heat-flow measurement was not achieved — the mission's lasting science is the seismology and the interior structure.
- **A badger digs into the ground to reach prey hidden below the surface.** The American Badger (*Taxidea taxus*), a powerful, low-slung fossorial carnivore of the open, dry interior West, uses its long front claws to dig rapidly into the ground and excavate burrowing rodents — ground squirrels and pocket gophers — from below the surface, reaching prey that is hidden underground (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of InSight reading the hidden deep interior of Mars.
- **A shrub sends a deep taproot to reach water hidden far below the surface.** The Big Sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*), the defining shrub of the dry interior West, sends a deep taproot several metres down to reach moisture far below the surface (plus shallow lateral roots near the top) — a still sentinel rooted into the hidden subsurface (ecological description only) — a mirror of a mission that reaches into what lies deep beneath the surface.

- **The InSight diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the lander with its solar panels, the robotic arm, the dome-shaped SEIS seismometer under its shield, and the HP3 mole.
- **The marsquake mode is depicted rather than driven by real seismograms.** The concentric seismic waves in the listening-to-marsquakes mode are depicted but not rendered from an archived SEIS seismogram of a real marsquake; a future ship could drive the mode from the recorded ground-motion trace of a catalogued event for a quantitatively faithful reading.
- **The interior cutaway is depicted rather than computed.** The crust/mantle/core layering in the reading-the-interior mode is depicted rather than rendered from the published interior model (the seismic velocity profile and the core radius from the InSight inversions); a future ship could drive the cutaway from the real interior model for a quantitatively faithful view.

## Dedication

v1.244 InSight is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's first mission to listen to the inside of Mars. The Jet Propulsion Laboratory teams who developed and operated the Discovery Program mission; the Lockheed Martin engineers who built the lander from the Phoenix heritage; the CNES and IPGP teams in France who built SEIS, the seismometer that recorded the first marsquakes; the DLR teams in Germany who built the HP3 "mole" and worked tirelessly to free it from the stubborn soil; the RISE radio-science team who tracked the planet's wobble to weigh its core; the entry-descent-landing engineers who steered the lander through the aeroshell, the parachute, and the retro-rockets to a gentle touchdown on Elysium Planitia in November 2018; and the seismologists who read the bending waves of distant quakes to map a crust, a mantle, and a surprisingly large liquid core. Year after year, a single fixed station sat quietly on a flat plain and listened — to faint marsquakes and distant impacts — and from that listening drew the first picture of the deep interior of another planet, reaching into depths no one had ever read before.
