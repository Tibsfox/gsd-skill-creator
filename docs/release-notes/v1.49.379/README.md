**PUSA and the Modified-Instrument Philosophy.** **Chris Ballew** is the Presidents of the United States of America basitar player, lead vocalist, and primary creative force whose home-built two-bass-string modified instrument is the engine's first documented instance of a home-built rock instrument as the defining sonic identity of an entire catalog. The **basitar** is a guitar-necked / guitar-bodied instrument strung with two bass strings and played with a pick. The engineering problem Ballew was solving is concrete: he wanted to play in a band with only guitar and drums (no separate bass player) but wanted the low end of bass with the melodic capability of guitar in a single instrument. Standard bass is melodically constrained; standard guitar lacks low-end authority. The basitar is the combined solution — bass strings on a guitar neck and body, producing a low-to-midrange frequency response that sits differently in a mix than either a guitar or a bass would. **Dave Dederer**'s **guitbass** is the companion modification that complements the basitar's low-midrange presence. With the basitar occupying the low-midrange territory, a standard six-string guitar would clash frequency-wise with the basitar's output. The guitbass (three strings on a standard guitar body) covers upper-midrange and melodic territory, producing a slightly thin and slightly bright guitar sound with more space in the higher registers. The two instruments were designed together, in relationship with each other. **The same** modified-instrument-as-defining-identity principle that organizes the basitar-guitbass pair sits at the exact structural opposite of the polish-forward arena-rock instrument configurations that dominated mid-1990s major-label alt-rock, and the contrast between the home-built instrument pair and the platinum-certified Columbia Records distribution is much of what makes the PUSA catalog cohere.

**The Eastlake Geographic Entry.** **Eastlake** is the Seattle neighborhood running along the eastern shore of **Lake Union**, roughly between Capitol Hill to the south and Fremont to the north. The neighborhood's character is residential and water-adjacent, with houseboats, boat yards, small businesses along Eastlake Avenue East, and proximity to the University of Washington Medical Center. It is quieter than Capitol Hill, less overtly bohemian, but has historically housed working musicians and artists who wanted proximity to Seattle's music scenes without the Capitol Hill density. Ballew lived and worked in the Eastlake area during the band's 1993 formation period, and the neighborhood's practical working-class water-adjacent character is consistent with the band's aesthetic: not precious, not self-consciously arty, just inventive and direct. The basitar was not invented in a Capitol Hill art loft; it was built by a working musician in the Eastlake neighborhood who wanted the sound he heard in his head and could not get from a standard instrument. **The same** practical-geographic-context structural principle that organized earlier Capitol Hill entries in the engine (Love Battery at 221, Foo Fighters at 237) extends to Eastlake with a different register — a working-class water-adjacent counterweight to the Capitol-Hill-centric Seattle documentation that dominated the mid-230s.

**The 1995 Debut and the Platinum Certification.** The **Presidents of the United States of America** self-titled debut (1995, Columbia Records) is one of the most unusual platinum albums in the history of alternative rock. The album was recorded and released at the height of the post-Nirvana commercial boom for Seattle alternative rock. A record label executive (Dan Perlman at PopLlama, then a Columbia deal) recognized that the band's combination of extreme catchiness, genuine weirdness, and family-friendly humor could reach a wide audience. The album sold over 1 million copies in the US and achieved platinum certification. **"Lump"** is the album's opening track and one of the band's signature songs — three minutes of melodic alt-rock about a woman who sits atop a floating mound in a swampy bog, with surreal lyrics, an inescapably catchy tune, and a deceptively simple basitar-guitbass-drums arrangement. **"Peaches"** is a song about eating peaches and moving to the country, musically simple, instrumentally odd, lyrically committed to its absurdist premise, with one of the catchiest choruses in 1990s alternative rock. **"Kitty"** is a three-minute melodic guitar pop song about the narrator's affection for his cat — the cat is on the computer, the cat wants to be a kitty all day long — that became a genuine alternative radio hit at a time when the dominant grunge-era mode was angst and alienation. **The same** comedy-as-aesthetic-commitment principle that organized the three debut singles extends through "Boll Weevil," "Naked and Famous," "Feather Pluckin'," and the remaining debut tracks, producing a catalog that is consistent in aesthetic across all its material: melodic, rhythmically propulsive, lyrically committed to unusual topics, never quite normal.

**The II Follow-Up and the 1998 Hiatus.** ***II*** (1996, Columbia) appeared one year after the debut's success. It was recorded under pressure — the band had to follow a million-selling debut — and is slightly more varied and slightly more polished than the debut but consistent in aesthetic. **"Mach 5"** is the standout track, a propulsive relentless alt-rock song that was one of the better singles of the mid-1990s. **"Volcano"** is a meditation on geological forces; **"Lunatic to Love"** is a return to the unusual-topic comedy of the debut. *II* sold well but not as dramatically as the debut — the typical pattern in which a debut that achieves platinum status from a standing start is followed by a second album with a built-in audience but also built-in expectations. The original run of the band ended in 1998 when Ballew and Dederer decided to pursue other projects. The hiatus was not acrimonious. The band reunited in 2004, recorded *Love Everybody* (2004), and has continued to record and tour intermittently since. The reunion albums are less commercially significant than the original run but demonstrate that the creative core of the band — Ballew's modified-instrument philosophy, the three-piece melodic weirdness — remains intact.

**Coho Salmon and the Salmonidae Arc Continuation.** The **Coho Salmon** (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*) enters the engine at E=6 as the second species-specific Pacific salmon entry of the Salmonidae arc (Pacific Salmon genus at 236 → Chinook at 237 → Coho at 238 → Steelhead at 239 projected → Bull Trout / Dolly Varden / Cutthroat Trout at 240-242 projected). Coho are called **Silver Salmon** for their bright silver ocean-phase coloration — a flash of metallic silver in the water, difficult to see until the moment it breaks the surface. At spawning, males develop a dark red to greenish coloration on the sides, with a green head and hooked kype jaw. The spawning male Coho is dramatically different in appearance from the ocean-phase fish. Adult Coho are 45-75 cm in length and 2.5-8 kg in weight, significantly smaller than Chinook but larger than Pink — the medium size range of Pacific salmon. **The same** Salmonidae-family-arc-continuation structural principle that organized the genus-level Pacific Salmon entry at 236 and the Chinook species-specific entry at 237 extends to the Coho species-specific entry at 238 as the arc's third documented member, establishing the species-by-species continuation pattern as an engine documentation structure for multi-degree taxonomic arcs.

**The Stream Specialist and the Small Tributary Network.** Unlike Chinook (degree 237), which require large deep rivers, Coho are more tolerant of smaller streams. They use small tributary systems — sometimes streams barely a meter wide — that Chinook rarely enter. This makes Coho ecologically important in smaller watershed systems that Chinook do not reach. The Coho's ability to penetrate small headwater streams means it delivers marine nutrients (from its decomposing carcass) into parts of the watershed that no other Pacific salmon reaches. The PNW has a vast network of small streams — first, second, and third-order tributaries that drain the forested hillsides before joining larger rivers. These small streams are critical habitat for Coho juveniles (young Coho rear in small streams for one to two years before migrating to the ocean) and spawning adults. **The same** small-tributary-specialist principle that organizes the Coho's niche extends to the American Beaver documentation at degree 61, because beaver ponds are particularly important for Coho juvenile rearing — the slow, deep water of beaver impoundments provides winter refugia and food production. The engine's documentation of the American Beaver at degree 61 intersects here with Coho ecology in a concrete cross-reference that organizes an ecological-relationship-as-cross-degree-reference pattern.

**The Aggressive Male Splashing Sound Profile at E=6.** On the spawning grounds, male Coho compete intensely for access to females and redd (spawning nest) locations. The competition involves **chasing** (a dominant male pursues and chases subordinate males away from a female and her redd, often in very shallow water with the dorsal fin visible above the surface, producing rapid irregular splashing), **fighting** (when two males confront each other, the fight involves circling, biting at the jaw and body, and physical collision — in shallow water of 15-30 cm, the collision of two 3-5 kg fish creates substantial splashing), and **spawning-position defense** (the dominant male positions himself alongside the female as she digs the redd and deposits eggs, with other males making runs at this position repeatedly and creating a cycle of fighting and chasing and splashing). Females are less aggressive than males but also produce sound — they dig the redd by turning on their sides and sweeping their tails through gravel (the same tail-slap documented for the genus at degree 236), deposit eggs in the gravel depression, and cover the completed redd by moving upstream and displacing gravel. **The same** shallow-water-acoustic-amplification principle that organizes the Coho sound profile produces the defining acoustic feature of Coho spawning: shallow riffles (with less water to absorb splash energy) produce louder, more irregular splashing than deeper pools. A Coho chase in 20 cm of water sounds very different from a Chinook leap into a deep pool — it is irregular, chaotic, immediate, physically present. The energy cannot go downward (not enough depth), so it goes outward and upward. **6PPD-Quinone and the 2020 Identification.** The most important recent finding in Coho salmon conservation is also one of the most surprising in any field of environmental science. Researchers had observed for decades that Coho returning to urban streams in the Pacific Northwest were dying before they could spawn — collapsing at the stream edge, showing erratic behavior, and dying within hours of entering freshwater. The mortality was severe: in some heavily urbanized streams, up to 90% of returning Coho died before spawning. The cause was unknown for years, though researchers established that the mortality was associated with urban stormwater runoff (the rain that washes off roads, parking lots, and urban surfaces into streams). **In 2020**, a research team led by **Zhenyu Tian** and **Edward Kolodziej** at the **University of Washington** identified the causative chemical as **6PPD-quinone** — a transformation product of 6PPD (6-p-phenylenediamine), an antiozonant added to tire rubber to prevent ozone cracking. When 6PPD in tires reacts with atmospheric ozone (which it does continuously, as that is its purpose), it produces 6PPD-quinone. The 6PPD-quinone is present on tire surfaces and washes off into stormwater when it rains. Coho salmon are extraordinarily sensitive to 6PPD-quinone — the lethal concentration 50% (LC50) for Coho is approximately **0.8 micrograms per liter**, a very low threshold. The mechanism involves inhibition of an enzyme (**DHODH**, dihydroorotate dehydrogenase) that is essential for mitochondrial function. Coho lack a key mutation at the DHODH active site that would confer resistance, making them exceptionally susceptible. Not all salmonids are equally sensitive — Chinook and Rainbow Trout (the freshwater form of Steelhead) are less sensitive, making Coho uniquely vulnerable among the Salmonidae. Washington State passed legislation (**Senate Bill 5804**, 2023) requiring the study and phase-out of 6PPD in tires sold in the state — the first legislation anywhere in the world directly targeting tire rubber chemistry for wildlife protection. The federal EPA has also begun regulatory review. Tire manufacturers are under pressure to find alternative antiozonants.

## Part A: Presidents of the United States of America — The Modified-Instrument Three-Piece, the Eastlake Entry, and the Comedic Alt-Rock Platinum Debut

- **Chris Ballew and the Basitar Engineering Solution:** **Chris Ballew** is the Presidents of the United States of America basitar player, lead vocalist, and primary creative force whose home-built two-bass-string modified instrument is the engine's first documented instance of a home-built rock instrument as the defining sonic identity of an entire catalog. The basitar is a guitar-necked and guitar-bodied instrument strung with two bass strings and played with a pick. The engineering problem Ballew was solving is concrete — he wanted to play in a band with only guitar and drums (no separate bass player) but wanted the low end of bass with the melodic capability of guitar in a single instrument. Standard bass is melodically constrained; standard guitar lacks low-end authority. ## Part B: Coho Salmon — The Silver Salmon, the Stream Specialist, the 6PPD-Quinone Vulnerability, and the Salmonidae Arc Third Entry

- **Species Baseline and Salmonidae Arc-Continuation Position:** **Coho Salmon** (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*) is a member of **Order Salmoniformes / Family Salmonidae / Genus Oncorhynchus / species kisutch**. Adult body length is 45-75 cm (18-30 in) and body mass ranges from 2.5-8 kg (5.5-17.6 lbs), placing Coho in the medium size range of Pacific salmon between Chinook (the largest, documented at degree 237) and Pink (the smallest). The species is the second species-specific Pacific salmon entry in the engine's Salmonidae arc, positioned between the Pacific Salmon genus-level overview at degree 236 and the Chinook species-specific entry at degree 237, and anchoring the arc-continuation position that organizes the species-by-species progression through the six-degree Salmonidae arc (236 genus → 237 Chinook → 238 Coho → 239 Steelhead → 240-242 Bull Trout / Dolly Varden / Cutthroat Trout projected).
- **The Silver Salmon Ocean-Phase Identifier:** Coho are called **Silver Salmon** for their bright silver ocean-phase coloration — a flash of metallic silver in the water, difficult to see until the moment it breaks the surface. The silver-phase coloration is the species' ocean identifier and is the origin of the common name that parallels the King Salmon designation for Chinook. At spawning, males develop a dark red to greenish coloration on the sides, with a green head and hooked kype jaw. The spawning male Coho is dramatically different in appearance from the ocean-phase fish, and the color-morphology transition from silver ocean-phase to red-green-hook-jawed spawning-male-phase is one of the more visually dramatic within-species transformations in Pacific-Northwest fish biology. The *kisutch* species name derives from the Ainu language term for the species and was carried into scientific nomenclature through Steller's Russian-American natural-history observations.
- **Stream Specialist and Small-Tributary Use:** Unlike Chinook, which require large deep rivers, Coho are more tolerant of smaller streams. They use small tributary systems — sometimes streams barely a meter wide — that Chinook rarely enter. This makes Coho ecologically important in smaller watershed systems that Chinook do not reach. The Coho's ability to penetrate small headwater streams means it delivers marine nutrients (from its decomposing carcass) into parts of the watershed that no other Pacific salmon can access. The PNW has a vast network of small streams — first, second, and third-order tributaries that drain the forested hillsides before joining larger rivers. These small streams are critical habitat for Coho juveniles (young Coho rear in small streams for one to two years before migrating to the ocean) and spawning adults. Coho juveniles depend on beaver ponds for slow-water winter refugia and food production, establishing an ecological cross-reference with the American Beaver documentation at degree 61.
- **Fall-Running Timing and October Puget Sound Peak:** Coho run September-November in most Washington rivers — peak October in many Puget Sound systems. This fall timing places Coho at the peak of the spawning season, when salmon streams are at their most active acoustically. The Coho fall-run position in the Pacific-salmon annual calendar is the second major spawning window after Chinook's summer-and-fall runs and before the winter-running Steelhead that will be documented at degree 239. The fall-run timing means that Coho spawning activity on Puget Sound tributaries is concentrated in the October window when the stream temperatures and flows align with the species' spawning requirements, and the October peak is when the Coho aggressive male splashing sound reaches its maximum engine-documentation amplitude.
- **Aggressive Male Splashing at E=6 in Shallow Spawning Riffles:** On the spawning grounds, male Coho compete intensely for access to females and redd (spawning nest) locations. The competition involves chasing (a dominant male pursues and chases subordinate males away from a female and her redd, often in very shallow water with the dorsal fin visible above the surface), fighting (when two males confront each other, the fight involves circling, biting at the jaw and body, and physical collision in shallow water of 15-30 cm), and spawning-position defense. In shallow water, the collision of two 3-5 kg fish creates substantial splashing, and the sound is high-frequency, irregular, energetic — bursts of chaotic splashing punctuating the background riffle of the stream. The E=6 amplitude reflects higher energy than the E=5 genus-level spawning overview at degree 236 (because Coho spawning specifically is more aggressive and more continuous and more acoustically irregular than the averaged genus) and equal energy to the Chinook leap at degree 237 (which is episodic heavy impact versus Coho's continuous lighter chaos — equivalent energy levels but completely different acoustic character).
- **6PPD-Quinone and the Urban Pre-Spawn Mortality Discovery:** In 2020, a research team led by **Zhenyu Tian** and **Edward Kolodziej** at the **University of Washington** identified **6PPD-quinone** as the causative chemical behind urban Coho pre-spawn mortality — a transformation product of 6PPD (6-p-phenylenediamine), an antiozonant added to tire rubber to prevent ozone cracking. When 6PPD in tires reacts with atmospheric ozone (which it does continuously, as that is its purpose), it produces 6PPD-quinone. The 6PPD-quinone is present on tire surfaces and washes off into stormwater when it rains. Coho salmon are extraordinarily sensitive to 6PPD-quinone — the lethal concentration 50% (LC50) for Coho is approximately **0.8 micrograms per liter**, a very low threshold. The mechanism involves inhibition of an enzyme (**DHODH**, dihydroorotate dehydrogenase) that is essential for mitochondrial function. Coho lack a key mutation at the DHODH active site that would confer resistance, making them exceptionally susceptible among the Salmonidae. Not all salmonids are equally sensitive — Chinook and Rainbow Trout (the freshwater form of Steelhead) are less sensitive — making Coho uniquely vulnerable.
- **Washington State SB 5804 (2023) and the Tire-Rubber Phase-Out Legislation:** Washington State passed legislation (Senate Bill 5804, 2023) requiring the study and phase-out of 6PPD in tires sold in the state — the first legislation anywhere in the world directly targeting tire rubber chemistry for wildlife protection. The federal EPA has also begun regulatory review. Tire manufacturers are under pressure to find alternative antiozonants. The regulatory-response register adds to the engine's SPS vocabulary at degree 238 as the first documentation of wildlife-protection-driven industrial-chemistry regulation, and the regulatory-response pattern will be tracked in future releases as the 6PPD phase-out progresses and as alternative antiozonants are evaluated for their biological impact.
- **Puget Sound ESU Species-of-Concern and Lower Columbia ESU Threatened Status:** Puget Sound Coho are listed as a **Species of Concern** under the Endangered Species Act — not Threatened or Endangered, but in need of monitoring and protection measures. The Puget Sound Coho ESU includes fish from rivers draining into Puget Sound: Nisqually, Puyallup, Green, Duwamish, Cedar, Snoqualmie, Snohomish, Stillaguamish, Skagit, Samish, Nooksack, and many smaller rivers. Coho in the **Lower Columbia River ESU** are listed as **Threatened** — a stronger conservation status than Puget Sound fish. Coastal Coho (rivers draining directly to the Pacific, west of the Cascades) are generally more robust than Puget Sound populations. The absence of urban development pressure, lower stormwater loading, and better-preserved riparian habitat contributes to healthier coastal Coho numbers. Before the 6PPD-quinone discovery, the scale of urban Coho pre-spawn mortality was not fully appreciated. Studies in heavily urbanized Puget Sound tributaries found mortality rates of 40-90% before the cause was known.
- **Key Coho Sites in Washington and the Public Viewing Infrastructure:** Four Coho spawning-stream sites organize the Puget Sound Coho documentation and the public viewing infrastructure. The **Cedar River (Maple Valley area)** is one of the most productive Coho streams in the Puget Sound watershed, receiving both Chinook (documented at degree 237) and Coho returning to spawn in fall. **Thornton Creek (Seattle, urban)** drains the north Seattle urban area and has been subject to restoration efforts that include Coho passage — representative of the 6PPD-quinone problem through its stormwater-laden urban drainages. **Issaquah Creek** drains the Issaquah and Sammamish valleys into Lake Sammamish and then Lake Washington, with the Issaquah Salmon Hatchery at the creek's lower reach as a public facility where spawning Coho can be viewed. **Skagit River tributaries** support some of the most productive wild Coho populations in the Puget Sound area. The accessibility of these four sites organizes Seattle-area salmon viewing and sustains the citizen-science monitoring that supplements the NOAA Fisheries ESU-level data.
