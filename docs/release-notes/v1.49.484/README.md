**Blood Brothers** arrive at E=9 -- the Seattle post-hardcore/metal band formed in 1997 by Johnny Whitney (vocals, keyboards), Cody Votolato (guitar), Morgan Henderson (bass), Mark Gajadhar (drums), and Jordan Billie (guitar), releasing *...March on Electric Children* (2000) on Triphammer Records, *Burn, Piano Island, Burn* (2003) on Artist Direct, and *Crimes* (2004) on V2 Records, and representing one of the most distinctive and demanding entries in the Pacific Northwest's post-Metal Summit heavy underground: a band whose post-hardcore approach pushed beyond the established registers of grunge, metal, and mathcore into a more jagged, angular, and vocally extreme territory -- dual vocalists (Whitney and Billie) delivering lyrics and melodic lines with a shrieking, dissonant intensity that combined hardcore's physical commitment with a theatrical, almost operatic willingness to occupy the uncomfortable register of voices pushed past comfort into controlled damage. The Blood Brothers' guitar architecture -- angular, dissonant, rhythmically irregular in ways that resemble mathcore without committing to mathcore's precise polyrhythmic grammar -- generates harmonic content designed to be wrong in ways that are specific rather than accidental, producing a dissonance that is composed rather than simply noisy.

**Peregrine through tern colony** arrives at E=9 on the SPS track -- the Pacific Northwest's most kinetically extreme bird acoustic event: a peregrine falcon (*Falco peregrinus*) diving through an active colony of Caspian terns (*Hydroprogne caspia*) or common terns (*Sterna hirundo*), producing the full acoustic sequence of a high-speed predatory approach and colony defense response. The peregrine's hunting stoop -- the fastest sustained movement of any animal on Earth, reaching 240 mph in a full power dive -- generates the acoustic signature of its approach before it arrives: the aerodynamic whistle of the bird's form at speed, the sound of air being displaced by a small, dense, extremely fast projectile, audible to the terns' acute hearing at distances that trigger the alarm response before the predator arrives. The tern colony's alarm response is one of the Pacific Northwest's most acoustically complex group-behavior events: hundreds to thousands of terns, each producing the species' harsh alarm call simultaneously, rising into the air in a coordinated mobbing response that fills the colony's acoustic space with a wall of alarm vocalization -- the aggregate of the colony's calls, the wing beats of hundreds of birds launching simultaneously, and the peregrine's continued approach whistle all arriving in the same acoustic field at the same moment. If the strike succeeds, the impact is audible -- a sharp, high-velocity collision at the end of the stoop. Whether successful or not, the colony remains at maximum alarm density for minutes after, the tern calls sustaining above their resting level until the threat has departed.

E=9 for the peregrine-through-tern-colony event is the correct assignment for the Pacific Northwest's maximum kinetic bird acoustic event.
