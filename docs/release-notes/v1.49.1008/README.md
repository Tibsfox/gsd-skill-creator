## Summary

v1.49.1008 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.198 to 1.199, adding IRIS — the Interface Region Imaging Spectrograph — as the obs#4 INTRA-AXIS continuation that sustains the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY substrate-axis (axis-rotation #26 sustained at obs#4). The axis opened at v1005 OSO with the continuous solar-cycle monitoring program, sustained at v1006 TRACE with the high-resolution extreme-ultraviolet imaging of the corona, and sustained at v1007 RHESSI with the hard-X-ray and gamma-ray flare imaging spectroscopy. IRIS turns that solar watch on the interface region: where TRACE imaged the corona from above and RHESSI imaged the high-energy flare, IRIS images and takes spectra of the chromosphere and transition region between the Sun's surface and corona, the thin layer where most of the Sun's ultraviolet light is emitted and where the heating that powers the corona and the solar wind begins. The axis now holds four observations on the Sun, the continuous-monitoring program at OSO, the high-resolution EUV imaging at TRACE, the high-energy flare imaging spectroscopy at RHESSI, and the interface-region UV imaging spectroscopy at IRIS, sustaining at obs#4 cumulative.

IRIS is a NASA Small Explorer (SMEX) with the instrument built and led by the Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory, managed through NASA Goddard Space Flight Center, with mission operations at NASA Ames Research Center and Alan Title as principal investigator — the same laboratory and the same principal investigator that led TRACE, a shared heritage in high-resolution solar instrumentation. The mission launched on 27 June 2013 aboard an air-launched Pegasus-XL into a Sun-synchronous orbit and continues to operate. A single 20-centimeter ultraviolet telescope feeds a high-resolution imaging spectrograph and a slit-jaw imager, imaging and taking spectra of the interface region in far-ultraviolet and near-ultraviolet light including the Mg II, C II, and Si IV lines at about a third of an arcsecond.

The signature of the mission is the first high-resolution imaging spectroscopy of the interface region. The slit-jaw imager records the spatial context of the interface scene while the spectrograph reads the spectrum along its slit, so IRIS resolves the chromosphere and transition region in both space and spectrum at the same time, anchoring every spectral measurement to its location on the imaged Sun.

The mission opened a fine-resolution view of the energy that feeds the corona. The energy that heats the corona and drives the solar wind passes up through the interface region from the surface, and the flows, heating, waves, spicules, and transient ultraviolet bursts within the chromosphere and transition region carry that energy upward. By resolving these motions and brightenings in both image and spectrum, IRIS traced where the energy that feeds the corona begins its journey upward, the first fine-resolution diagnostics of the interface energy flow.

The 20-centimeter ultraviolet telescope gave the observation its foundation. By feeding both the imaging spectrograph and the slit-jaw imager through one aperture, the telescope let IRIS read the interface as an image and a spectrum together, the combined imaging and spectroscopy central to resolving the layer.

The axis-relevant substrate of IRIS at this degree is its continuation of the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY substrate-axis at obs#4 on the Sun, turning the solar watch on the interface layer below the corona TRACE imaged. IRIS sustains the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#4 NEW LOCKED, extends the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY-IMAGING-LINEAGE thread at obs#3 cumulative, and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#71 cumulative (axis-rotation #26 sustained at obs#4). The forward continuation favors later solar observatories — heliospheric and in-situ solar-wind missions that fly out toward the Sun, dedicated coronagraphs, and full-disk high-cadence dynamics observatories.

IRIS opens the interface-region ultraviolet end of the solar-imaging lineage on the solar axis, and the mission records the IRIS team at the Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory, Alan Title and the mission team, NASA Ames Research Center, and NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.198 to 1.199, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#82 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Imaging and spectroscopy of a layer together resolve it in both space and spectrum.** Rather than imaging the chromosphere or taking its spectrum, IRIS did both at high resolution at once, showing that pairing a slit-jaw imager with a spectrograph anchors every measurement to its place on the resolved Sun.
2. **A dedicated instrument turns a boundary into a resolved layer.** By building specifically for the interface region, IRIS resolved the chromosphere and transition region directly rather than inferring them from the photosphere below or the corona above, showing that a dedicated focus turns a boundary into a layer.
3. **Resolving the interface traces where the corona's energy begins.** By measuring the flows, waves, and brightenings of the interface, IRIS traced where the energy that heats the corona begins its journey upward, showing that the thin connecting layer carries the story of the heating.
4. **High resolution reveals the fine-scale dynamics a coarse view averages over.** By resolving the interface at about a third of an arcsecond in space, time, and spectrum, IRIS revealed the spicules, fast flows, and ultraviolet bursts of the layer, showing that fine resolution opens dynamics a coarse view sums away.
5. **A continuation can deepen an axis to a new layer of the same body.** By turning the solar watch from the corona to the interface below it, IRIS sustained the solar-observatory axis at obs#4 on the same target, showing that an axis can deepen to a new layer without rotating away.
6. **A shared laboratory heritage carries a method to a new layer.** By reusing the high-resolution solar instrumentation the Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory established with TRACE under Alan Title, IRIS carried the resolved-observation method from the corona to the interface, showing that a lineage of instrumentation can move down through the solar atmosphere.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across a distinct-palette mission.** Because IRIS reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.198 template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the interface-region imaging spectroscopy with a distinct interface-violet / line-magenta / plasma-teal palette, making the distinct-palette mission a clean build.

## Surprises

- **A thin connecting layer holds the story of the corona's heating.** The interface region is a thin layer between the cool surface and the hot corona, yet IRIS showed that resolving it traces where the energy that heats the corona begins, so the catalog's interface-region entry is the layer where the corona's heating starts.
- **A wading bird that works the water's edge mirrors the interface imager.** The Great Blue Heron, which works the thin zone where water meets land, enters as the interface-working mirror to the instrument that resolved the interface region between the Sun's surface and corona, so the catalog pairs a boundary-working wading bird with an interface-imaging mission.
- **A named iris mirrors the spectrograph that spreads the light.** The Oregon Iris shares the mission's name and spreads its banded color across its petals, so the catalog's interface-region imaging spectrograph is paired with a native iris named, like the instrument, for the spread of color, the spread the spectrograph mirrors in wavelength.

## Dedication

v1.199 IRIS is dedicated to the team that conceived and flew the Interface Region Imaging Spectrograph: the IRIS team at the Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory who built and led the instrument and its ultraviolet telescope, imaging spectrograph, and slit-jaw imager; Alan Title and the mission team who guided the high-resolution imaging spectroscopy of the interface region; NASA Ames Research Center, which operates the mission; NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, which manages it within the Small Explorer program; and the operations and science teams who hold the pointing steady and read the spectra of the chromosphere and transition region. It is dedicated as well to the wider community of solar physicists whose picture of the interface layer these observations grounded, carrying forward the high-resolution solar instrumentation the laboratory established with TRACE. IRIS images and takes spectra of the interface region of the Sun, resolves the chromosphere and transition region in both space and spectrum, and traces how energy and plasma move up through the interface toward the corona.
