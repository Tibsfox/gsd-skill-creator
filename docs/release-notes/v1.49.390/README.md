## Summary

**The Black Tones** arrive at E=7 -- the Seattle blues rock duo of twin siblings Eva Walker (guitar, vocals) and Cedric Walker (drums). Formed in 2013, The Black Tones play a two-piece format that draws from the blues tradition without nostalgia: their music is loud, contemporary, and structurally economical in the way that only a two-piece band can be. Eva Walker's guitar work carries the full harmonic load -- rhythm, lead, and bass register simultaneously -- while Cedric Walker's drumming is the kind of high-volume work that two-piece bands require when there is no bass player to anchor the low end. The result is a sound that is conspicuously spare and conspicuously powerful.

The twin sibling dimension of The Black Tones is part of the story because it is audible: Eva and Cedric Walker communicate rhythmically with the ease of people who have been listening to each other their entire lives. The locked groove between guitar and drums that the best two-piece bands achieve -- where the instrument and the percussion are so tightly coordinated that the absence of a bass player stops being noticeable -- is present in The Black Tones' recordings and is especially evident live. Their 2019 debut *Cobain and Cornbread* takes its title from two foundational elements of the Seattle music canon and Black American tradition respectively, a pairing that is direct about who The Black Tones are and where they come from. The blues vocabulary they work in is not borrowed; it is inherited.

On the soundscape side, **Wind Sitka Spruce** enters the engine as the **FIRST WIND ENTRY in the SPS sequence** -- the first time the engine documents atmospheric motion rather than water. The **organ moan** produced by wind moving through a stand of Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*) is one of the defining sounds of the Pacific Northwest coast and islands. Sitka spruce is the dominant canopy tree of the coastal temperate rainforest from Northern California to Alaska. Its needles are short and stiff; its branches are arranged in ways that channel and split wind; and at moderate wind speeds, the result is a low-frequency resonance -- the organ moan -- that comes from the interaction of moving air with branching structures at multiple scales simultaneously. The E=4 rating reflects the intimacy and steadiness of this sound: it is not loud, but it is constant and penetrating.

The organ moan of wind in Sitka spruce is a PNW coastal signature sound. Anyone who has stood at the edge of a coastal forest on a windy day has heard it -- the low register that underlies the higher frequencies of the rustling needles. It is the sound of a very large organism in motion, the sound of a tree that can reach 300 feet in height and 18 feet in diameter responding to a weather system.
