**The Blues Foundation — Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, and the Electric Chicago Tradition.** The Black Tones' blues work is not an art-school exercise in genre appropriation but a live continuation of the electric Chicago blues tradition whose lineage runs directly through their Rainier Valley community roots. **Muddy Waters** (1913-1983) arrived in Chicago from Stovall Plantation, Mississippi, in 1943 and electrified the Delta blues vocabulary he had grown up with, producing recordings for **Chess Records** that established the electric Chicago blues form through the 1950s. **Howlin' Wolf** (1910-1976) arrived in Chicago from West Point, Mississippi, in 1948 and produced his own Chess Records catalog of electric blues whose aggressive intensity matched Waters' depth. **Buddy Guy** (born 1936) arrived in Chicago from Lettsworth, Louisiana, in 1957 and brought the next-generation electric blues virtuosity that would influence the British blues revival (**Cream**, early **Rolling Stones**, early **Led Zeppelin**) and the American rock tradition that descended from it. **Willie Dixon** (1915-1992) wrote many of the songs that defined the Chicago blues catalog for both Waters and Wolf. The Black Tones work in this direct lineage: the electric guitar vocabulary that Eva Walker plays is the electric blues vocabulary established by Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, and the Chess Records catalog, filtered through the punk-blues synthesis of the 1990s-2000s and delivered from Rainier Valley rather than from the Chicago South Side. **In parallel**, the aeolian-tone physics documented at degree 249 is not a novel acoustic mechanism but a continuation of fluid-dynamics principles established by **Theodore von Kármán** in 1911 through his description of the Karman vortex street, the alternating vortex pattern that forms on the leeward side of a cylindrical object in moving fluid. Pacific coast storms are not incidental weather events for coastal communities — they are recurring, powerful presences that shape how people live, build, travel, and relate to the landscape. The sound of the forest in storm is part of the full experience of a Pacific coast storm: the smell of the salt air, the physical impact of the wind, the noise of the ocean surf, and the moaning of the spruce.
- **Aeolian-Harp Tradition and the Cultural Framework of Wind-as-Music:** The **aeolian harp** — strings set in the wind to produce tones — was a popular instrument and poetic image in Romantic literature and philosophy. **Samuel Taylor Coleridge** (1772-1834) wrote "The Eolian Harp" in 1795, anchoring the English-language reception of aeolian sound. **Percy Bysshe Shelley** (1792-1822) referenced aeolian sound in multiple poems. The cultural tradition of wind-as-music provides the interpretive framework within which the Sitka spruce wind sound has been consistently described in musical terms across multiple naturalist and explorer records from the 19th and 20th centuries — "moaning," "singing," "organ-like." The descriptions appear across multiple independent sources, demonstrating that the sound is distinctive enough to be consistently remarked upon and suggesting that the musical vocabulary is not imposed arbitrarily but reflects something specific about the acoustic character.
- **E=4 Placement — Present, Substantial, Sustained:** The sustained moan of Sitka spruce in a coastal storm registers at **E=4**. The level is accurate: present, substantial, noticeable. Not the dramatic punctuation of a waterfall (E=5-6) but not the near-silence of calm water either. A coastal storm through a large old-growth Sitka spruce stand produces continuous, audible, physically present sound at moderate energy level. The E=4 placement also opens the meteorological sub-phase's acoustic-amplitude arc: wind through Sitka spruce (249, E=4), old-growth fir wind (250, E=5 projected), Pacific rain on water (251, E=3 projected), tidal surge on cobble (252, E=4 projected). The sub-phase amplitude range E=3-5 is moderate, reflecting the ambient rather than punctuating character of meteorological sound.
- **Gustiness and the Breathing Quality of the Sound:** Coastal storm winds are typically gusty — they fluctuate in velocity on timescales of seconds to minutes. This gustiness creates amplitude modulation of the aeolian tones: the sound swells with each gust and diminishes in the lulls between gusts. The amplitude modulation produces the "breathing" quality that creates the impression that the tree is alive and breathing. The perceived periodicity of the breathing tracks the gust cycle of the storm — faster breathing during steeper gradient passages, slower breathing during sustained high-wind periods. This temporal structure is part of why the sound is described in biological terms even though its acoustic mechanism is entirely mechanical and atmospheric.

## Lessons Learned

1. **First-category entries require explicit physics or mechanism grounding rather than generic description.** The FIRST WIND ENTRY IN SPS HISTORY at degree 249 is grounded in the Karman vortex street physics, the aeolian-tone formula f = 0.198v/d, and the specific frequency calculations for Sitka spruce needles at storm wind speeds. Future first-category entries should anchor their category openings in specific mechanism grounding rather than generic description, because the mechanism grounding enables subsequent entries in the category to reference a shared framework and avoid re-justifying the category's existence.
2. **Long gap closures require explicit naming of both the gap-opening and gap-closing endpoints.** The 76-degree Rainier Valley gap from degree 173 to degree 249 is a substantial structural feature that warrants explicit documentation of both endpoints. Future gap-closure documentation should cite the earlier entry by artist or sound name rather than only by degree number, because the structural relationship between the two endpoints is interpretively meaningful and requires both endpoints to be fully named.
3. **Thread-continuity documentation should anchor thread members to specific engine degrees rather than projecting them.** The PNW Black community music lineage referenced at degree 249 (Robert Cray → Quincy Jones → Jimi Hendrix → Ayron Jones → The Black Tones) includes some members at specific engine degrees (Hendrix at 245-246) and others at projected positions (Ayron Jones). Future lineage-documentation should anchor all thread members to specific degrees when possible, because the thread register carries more interpretive weight when its members are anchored rather than projected.
4. **Sub-phase opening entries establish the frame within which subsequent arc members operate.** Degree 249's opening of the meteorological sub-phase establishes the four-degree arc (249-252) frame that subsequent entries fulfill. Future sub-phase opening entries should explicitly project the arc's coverage and amplitude range so that the opening functions as frame-establishment rather than as isolated single-entry documentation, because the opening-as-frame-establishment is structurally distinct from mid-phase or closing-phase positions.
5. **Twin-sibling and long-partnership musical relationships produce ensemble behavior that cannot be replicated through independent-unit aggregation.** The Walker twins' musical partnership is shaped by their shared childhood and shared developmental trajectory; the partnership communication depth precedes the band rather than being built through it. Future engine documentation should surface shared-development dynamics when they organize significant ensemble behavior, because the register documents a partnership category that is structurally distinct from non-twin partnership dynamics.
6. **Acoustic converters mediate between forcing agents and audible output and warrant first-class documentation.** Wind is inaudible in free air; wind becomes audible through an acoustic converter (Sitka spruce needles acting as aeolian-tone generators). Electric guitar sound requires the converter chain of strings, pickups, amplifier, and speaker. Future engine documentation should identify acoustic converters explicitly when they organize significant sound-production mechanisms, because the converter register documents a structural element that generic sound-source documentation does not capture.
7. **Range-restricted species define geographically specific acoustic signatures that cannot be substituted by related species.** Sitka spruce grows only in the narrow 80-100 km coastal strip from northern California to Alaska, and its specific needle morphology produces a specific aeolian-tone spectrum distinct from inland conifers (Douglas fir, western red cedar, western hemlock). Future SPS documentation should identify range-restricted species explicitly when their geographic specificity organizes their acoustic identity, because the range-restriction register documents a biogeographic element that generic conifer-wind documentation does not capture.
8. **Deep-tradition continuity (cultural or scientific) extends from multi-decade or multi-century origins into the specific release moment.** The Black Tones' electric Chicago blues tradition extends from Muddy Waters in the 1940s-1950s across eight decades. The Karman vortex street physics extends from Theodore von Kármán's 1911 description across eleven decades. Future engine documentation should surface deep-tradition continuity explicitly when it organizes the specific release moment, because the tradition register documents that the moment is a living extension of the tradition rather than a static historical category.
9. **Label-catalog continuity is a structurally significant lens for understanding current-artist positioning.** The Black Tones' Sub Pop relationship places them in direct catalog continuity with Mudhoney, Nirvana (early), Soundgarden (early), and the full Sub Pop discography. Future engine documentation of Sub Pop (and comparable-label) artists should reference the label-catalog continuity explicitly, because the catalog register documents an artistic-positioning element that single-artist documentation does not capture.
10. **Indigenous cultural landscapes are constitutive contexts for acoustic phenomena rather than backdrops.** The Olympic coastal rainforest is not a scenic attraction for the Quinault, Hoh, Quileute, and Makah peoples; it is the landscape in which they have lived for millennia. Future SPS documentation should acknowledge indigenous cultural landscapes as constitutive contexts when the acoustic phenomena occur within tribal homelands, because the cultural-landscape register documents a relational element that generic geographic documentation does not capture.
