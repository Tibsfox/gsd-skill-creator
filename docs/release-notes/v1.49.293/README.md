**The Wild Turkey — The Largest North American Gamebird and First Phasianidae.** The **Wild Turkey** (*Meleagris gallopavo*) is the largest gamebird in North America — adult males can weigh 16-24 pounds with wingspans approaching five feet. The species is one of only two domesticated birds native to the **New World**, the other being the **Muscovy Duck** (from Central and South America). Pre-Columbian indigenous peoples in what is now the American Southwest and Mesoamerica domesticated the turkey thousands of years before European contact, and the domestic turkey lineage traces directly to the Wild Turkey's species stock. In the Pacific Northwest, Wild Turkeys are an introduced but now established population — birds released into Washington and Oregon in the twentieth century have formed self-sustaining breeding populations, particularly in the **oak woodlands** and mixed forests of western Washington and the Cascade foothills, and the species is now common enough to support regulated hunting seasons managed by the **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW)**. The introduced-but-established status is structurally different from the native-status of the Columbidae quartet's native members (Band-tailed Pigeon, Mourning Dove) and from the invasive status of the Eurasian Collared-Dove (151), and the Wild Turkey's presence in the engine at degree 153 documents a third Pacific Northwest status category: **established-through-introduction-for-management**, distinct from both spontaneous invasion and native range.

During vigorous gobbling, the snood elongates from a few centimeters to nearly its full length (10-15 cm), and the wattles engorge with blood and flush bright red as part of the full display. The mechanical modulation by these secondary sexual characteristics adds both visual and acoustic components to the display, and the coupling of acoustic and visual signals in the full male display is an unusually complete multi-modal courtship performance.
