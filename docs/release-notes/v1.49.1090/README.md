## Mission Overview

OSIRIS-REx is the obs#3 sustainer of the COMET-AND-ASTEROID-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's third New-Frontiers mission, an asteroid sample-return mission whose defining firsts are the first US sample of an asteroid returned to Earth, the largest sample returned from beyond the Moon, and the smallest body ever orbited by a spacecraft. The spacecraft was built by Lockheed Martin Space (Littleton/Denver, CO) and the mission was managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center (GSFC), with principal investigator Dante Lauretta of the University of Arizona. OSIRIS-REx launched 8 September 2016 on an Atlas V 411 from Cape Canaveral (SLC-41), cruised with an Earth gravity assist to the near-Earth, potentially-hazardous, carbonaceous B-type asteroid 101955 Bennu — a "rubble-pile" (a loosely bound aggregate, ~490 m across, a primitive relic of the early solar system) — and arrived in December 2018. It surveyed Bennu and orbited it closely, setting records for the smallest body ever orbited by a spacecraft and the closest orbit then achieved; Bennu's surface proved unexpectedly rough and boulder-strewn, and the team selected the "Nightingale" site for sampling. The reach was the mission: on 20 October 2020 the spacecraft's TAGSAM arm briefly touched Bennu's surface in a Touch-And-Go (TAG) maneuver (~6 seconds), fired a burst of nitrogen gas to fluidize regolith into the collector head, and backed away — gathering more than expected (some leaked before the head was stowed). The story is the launch from Cape Canaveral, the cruise to Bennu, the arrival and the close survey orbits of the smallest body ever orbited, the reach and the touch of the Touch-And-Go, the departure from Bennu (May 2021), the Sample Return Capsule released and reentering over the western United States, and the parachute landing at the Utah Test and Training Range (24 September 2023) — the first US asteroid sample returned to Earth. The science is central: the Bennu samples contained hydrated clay minerals, carbon-rich organic matter (including reported amino acids and nucleobases), and evidence of past water and brines on the parent body — the chemical building blocks relevant to the origins of water and life. The final curated sample mass was ~121.6 g (the first extraction from the collector head was 70.3 g in October 2023), the largest sample returned from beyond the Moon. 101955 Bennu is a near-Earth, potentially-hazardous, carbonaceous B-type asteroid, a primitive relic studied for what it preserves of the early solar system. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: OSIRIS-REx is an ASTEROID / small-body mission on the comet & asteroid axis (NOT a solar / heliophysics mission, and it re-stamps NO heliophysics-axis anchor); it was NASA's THIRD New-Frontiers mission (after New Horizons #1 and Juno #2), NOT a Discovery mission (NEAR and Stardust were Discovery); it returned the first US asteroid sample (Japan's Hayabusa returned the first asteroid sample of all in 2010, Hayabusa2 in 2020 — keep "US"); its ~121.6 g sample is the largest returned from beyond the Moon; it orbited the smallest body ever orbited (a SIZE superlative, distinct from NEAR's "first to orbit an asteroid"); and it is NOT claimed as the fastest reentry (its ~12.4 km/s SRC reentry was second to Stardust's ~12.9 km/s). ★★ The Bennu sample-return mission is COMPLETE (launched 2016, collected 2020, returned 24 September 2023), so the catalog frames that story in the PAST tense throughout (OSIRIS-REx flew, reached, collected, returned); the spacecraft continues as OSIRIS-APEX (Apophis Explorer), redirected toward asteroid (99942) Apophis with arrival expected 2029 — noted as the ongoing extension (present/future). ★ The dates are kept distinct: launch 8 September 2016, arrival December 2018, Touch-And-Go 20 October 2020, sample return 24 September 2023, Apophis arrival expected ~2029. ## Surprises

- **A spacecraft reached out and touched an asteroid, then backed away with a generous handful.** OSIRIS-REx's TAGSAM arm made brief (~6 s) contact with Bennu on 20 October 2020 and fired a burst of nitrogen gas to fluidize regolith into the collector head — and the unexpectedly loose rubble pile let the arm sink in further than modeled, so the head gathered far more than expected (some leaked before stowage). The final curated mass was ~121.6 g, the largest sample returned from beyond the Moon.
- **The first US sample of an asteroid brought home.** OSIRIS-REx returned the first US sample of an asteroid to Earth when the Sample Return Capsule landed at the Utah Test and Training Range on 24 September 2023; Japan's Hayabusa (JAXA) returned the first asteroid sample of all in 2010 (Itokawa) and Hayabusa2 in 2020 (Ryugu), and OSIRIS-REx complemented Stardust's first comet-coma samples by bringing home the first US asteroid sample, and far more of it.
- **The building blocks of life in a rubble-pile asteroid.** The Bennu samples contained hydrated clay minerals, carbon-rich organic matter (including reported amino acids and nucleobases), and evidence of past water and brines on the parent body — the chemical building blocks relevant to the origins of water and life, preserved in a primitive relic of the early solar system.
- **The smallest body ever orbited, and a spacecraft redirected to a second asteroid.** OSIRIS-REx orbited 101955 Bennu (~490 m) at the closest orbit then achieved — the smallest body ever orbited by a spacecraft — and after releasing the Sample Return Capsule the still-healthy spacecraft was redirected as OSIRIS-APEX (Apophis Explorer) toward the asteroid (99942) Apophis, with arrival expected 2029.
- **A small ground squirrel that reaches out, gathers a handful, and carries it home.** The Yellow-pine Chipmunk (*Neotamias amoenus*), a small ground squirrel of the open ponderosa-pine and sagebrush country of the interior / east-Cascades Pacific Northwest, reaches out, packs seeds into its expandable cheek pouches, and carries the gathered handful home to scatter-hoard in buried caches (many of which it never retrieves, so the cached seeds sprout) — a mirror of the mission that reached out, gathered a handful of regolith, and carried it home.
- **A dry-country lily that keeps its store underground and lifts a deep star-cup.** The Sagebrush Mariposa Lily (*Calochortus macrocarpus*), a perennial lily of the dry shrub-steppe and ponderosa-pine country of the interior / east-Cascades Pacific Northwest, grows from an edible bulb stored underground through the dry season and lifts a large, deep, lavender star-cup flower above the bunchgrass and sagebrush (ecological description only) — bound with OSIRIS-REx's gathering and keeping of what is carried home.

## Dedication

v1.273 OSIRIS-REx is dedicated to the people across NASA, the Goddard Space Flight Center, the University of Arizona, and Lockheed Martin who built and flew the first US mission to gather a handful of an asteroid and carry it safely home. The engineers in Colorado who built a spacecraft to reach out and touch a small, dark world, and the flight team who guided it to the rubble pile of 101955 Bennu and held it in the closest orbit ever flown around so small a body. The teams behind the TAGSAM arm, that careful reach which brushed the surface and lifted the loose grains with a breath of nitrogen gas. The navigators who flew the Sample Return Capsule back to a soft landing on the salt flats of Utah, and the lab scientists who opened the head and read the regolith — the hydrated clays, the carbon-rich organic matter, and the trace of water that once flowed on the parent body. The mission carries the work of Dante Lauretta. They reached out, gathered a handful, and carried it home — the largest sample from beyond the Moon, and the first US sample of an asteroid returned to Earth.
