## Jerry Cantrell's harmonic language


- Cantrell's guitar writing on *Dirt* is the most immediately recognizable harmonic approach in the Pacific Northwest metal tradition: the preference for tritones (the interval that classical harmony designated "diabolus in musica" -- the devil in music) over the power chord's fifth, the choice of scale degrees that produce unresolved tension rather than the expected resolution, the riff that ends on a chord that the ear expected to move somewhere but instead sits in its wrong-right position and waits; the harmonic approach is not merely heavy -- it is emotionally specific, producing the particular quality of inescapable dread that characterizes *Dirt*'s emotional register

## Gray wolf reintroduction in Washington


- the gray wolf (*Canis lupus*) was extirpated from Washington by the early twentieth century; natural recolonization from Idaho began in 2008 with the Lookout Pack in the Kettle Range; as of the mid-2020s, Washington's wolf population exceeds 200 individuals in multiple packs, primarily in the northern Cascades and northeast corner of the state; the acoustic presence of wolf howling in Washington's backcountry is a recovered phenomenon, not a historical one -- the wolf howl at degree 336 documents an animal that has returned to a landscape from which it was absent for nearly a century

## Pacific Northwest thunderstorm distribution


- the Pacific Northwest's thunderstorm frequency is lower than most of North America: Seattle averages fewer than 10 thunderstorm days per year compared to 40-60 in the Midwest and Southeast; the Pacific Northwest's thunderstorms, when they occur, tend to be convective events associated with upper-level disturbances rather than the frontal systems that drive Great Plains thunderstorm activity; the relative rarity of Pacific Northwest thunderstorms makes the wolf-howl-in-thunder combination a genuinely rare acoustic event -- a low-probability meeting of two independently infrequent phenomena
