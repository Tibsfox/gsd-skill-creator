## Mission Overview

CYGNSS is the obs#22 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — the NASA Earth Venture Cyclone Global Navigation Satellite System, a constellation of eight microsatellites that measure the ocean-surface wind by GNSS-reflectometry, reading the GPS signal reflected off the sea. ★ It is a NASA Earth Venture mission led by the University of Michigan (PI Christopher Ruf), with the spacecraft built and operated by the Southwest Research Institute (SwRI). CYGNSS carries, on each of its eight microsatellites (~29 kg each), a Delay Doppler Mapping Instrument (DDMI) — a GNSS-reflectometry receiver with two nadir-pointing antennas and one zenith-pointing antenna — that receives the direct GPS signal and the GPS signal reflected off the ocean surface; the roughness of the reflected signal (the spreading of the delay-Doppler map) gives the ocean-surface wind speed, and each satellite measures four simultaneous reflections (≈32 wind measurements per second across the constellation). ★ KEY ADVANCE: the L-band GPS signal penetrates heavy rain, so CYGNSS measures the wind in the rain-filled inner core of tropical cyclones where Ku-band scatterometers are attenuated. The mission launched 15 December 2016 (13:37 UTC) on an Orbital ATK Pegasus XL, air-launched from the "Stargazer" L-1011 aircraft ~201 km off Cape Canaveral, and flies as a constellation of eight microsatellites in a ~510 km altitude, ~35° low-inclination tropical orbit (giving tropical / subtropical coverage out to ~±38° latitude, the cyclone belt, with a mean ~7 h / median ~3 h revisit). The story is the Pegasus XL air-launch from the "Stargazer" L-1011 over the Atlantic, the deployment of the eight microsatellites into the ~510 km / ~35° tropical orbit, each microsatellite receiving the direct GPS signal and the GPS signal reflected off the sea, the bistatic geometry of the reflection, the roughness of the reflected signal (the spreading of the delay-Doppler map) giving the ocean-surface wind speed, the L-band signal seeing through the rain into the cyclone inner core, and the eight satellites giving rapid revisit of the tropics so the wind of a storm's inner core is mapped from above. The science is central: from the low tropical orbit CYGNSS measures the ocean-surface wind — each DDMI receiving the direct GPS signal and the GPS signal reflected off the water and turning the roughness of the reflected delay-Doppler map into wind speed — and turns the rapid revisit of the eight satellites into frequent wind measurements of the rain-filled inner core of tropical cyclones. ★★★ CYGNSS is OPERATING, so the catalog frames its whole story in the PRESENT (CYGNSS orbits the Earth, receives the reflected GPS signal, measures the ocean-surface wind, and maps the wind of the cyclone inner core); it launched 15 December 2016 — a 12 December attempt was aborted for a hydraulic issue, a PAST sub-event — and operates today, with seven of the eight satellites still measuring (FM06 ceased operations in 2022, a PAST sub-event) and the mission extended well past its 2-year design life (9+ years elapsed) and still meeting its science requirements. CYGNSS's firsts are scoped carefully: it is the first constellation dedicated to measuring tropical-cyclone ocean-surface wind, it performs the GNSS-reflectometry ocean-surface-wind sensing from eight microsatellites, and it performs the rain-penetrating, rapid-revisit wind measurement of the cyclone inner core. ★ The scope is load-bearing: ocean-surface wind was measured from space before CYGNSS — QuikSCAT / SeaWinds in 1999, ERS-1/2, and ASCAT — and spaceborne GNSS-reflectometry was demonstrated before it by UK-DMC (2003) and TechDemoSat-1 (2014), so CYGNSS is NOT "the first ocean-wind mission" and NOT "the first GNSS-reflectometry"; its distinction is being the first dedicated tropical-cyclone-wind constellation and the operational GNSS-reflectometry wind constellation. ## Surprises

- **The wind read from a signal the satellite never sent.** CYGNSS does not transmit its own radar — it listens. Each microsatellite's Delay Doppler Mapping Instrument receives the L-band GPS signals already broadcast by the GPS constellation and catches the version of those signals reflected off the sea; the roughness of that reflected signal, the spreading of its delay-Doppler map, encodes the speed of the wind blowing across the ocean surface. A bistatic measurement of the wind made from borrowed signals.
- **Eight small satellites instead of one large one.** Rather than a single large spacecraft, CYGNSS flies eight microsatellites (~29 kg each) in a low-inclination orbit, so that at any moment several of them are passing over the tropics. The eight-fold spread gives a rapid revisit (a mean of about seven hours, a median of about three) — far faster than a single satellite could manage — so the fast-changing wind of a tropical cyclone can be sampled again and again.
- **The wind seen through the rain.** A tropical cyclone's heaviest rain falls in its inner core, the very place where the wind matters most and where Ku-band scatterometers are attenuated and blinded. CYGNSS's L-band GPS signal passes through that heavy rain, so the constellation can measure the ocean-surface wind in the rain-filled inner core of the storm where other techniques cannot see.
- **A mission that has long outlived its design life.** CYGNSS was built for a two-year mission. It launched in December 2016 and is still measuring more than nine years later — seven of the eight satellites are still operating (FM06 ceased operations in 2022), and the constellation still meets its science requirements, a small Earth Venture mission that has kept on going far past its plan.
- **A PNW phalarope that spends the year out on the wind-driven open sea.** The Red Phalarope (*Phalaropus fulicarius*), a small phalarope, breeds in the high Arctic but spends the non-breeding year far out on the open ocean, riding the swell and the wind well offshore of the Pacific Northwest coast and spinning on the very sea surface to stir up its food — a bird of the wind-driven open sea, the very surface whose wind CYGNSS measures from above (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A cushion plant that takes the full force of the ocean wind.** The Sea Thrift / Sea Pink (*Armeria maritima*, Sea Thrift), a PNW coastal-headland cushion plant of wind-blasted sea cliffs and rocky bluffs, holds a tight tuft of grass-like leaves and round pink flower-heads against the full force of the ocean wind where the land meets the sea — a plant of the wind-blasted headland, bound with the throughline of the wind over the sea.

## Dedication

v1.302 CYGNSS is dedicated to the people at NASA, the University of Michigan, and the Southwest Research Institute, and across the ocean-wind and GNSS-reflectometry science teams, who built and fly the constellation that reads the wind across the sea from a signal it never sent. To Christopher Ruf and the Michigan team who led the mission and wrote the algorithm that turns the roughness of a reflected GPS signal into a wind speed, and to the SwRI engineers who built and operate the eight small spacecraft and the Delay Doppler Mapping Instrument receivers that catch the direct signal and its echo off the water. The crews who carried the constellation aloft on a Pegasus XL air-launched from the "Stargazer" over the Atlantic in December 2016 and the controllers who keep the eight satellites on their tropical orbit, seven of them measuring still. The scientists who learned to read the wind in the rain-filled inner core of a storm where other instruments are blinded, and who keep the mission going far past its planned two years. From the low tropical orbit the receivers still listen for the signal reflected off the sea, and the wind over the ocean belongs to them.
