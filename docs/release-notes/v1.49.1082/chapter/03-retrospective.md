## Surprises

- **A telescope as sharp as Hubble but with a field about 100x wider.** Roman's 2.4 m primary mirror is the same diameter as Hubble's — and indeed originates from a telescope asset donated by the National Reconnaissance Office — yet its field of view is designed to be about 100x wider, so a single Roman exposure is designed to cover an area of sky that would take roughly a hundred Hubble pointings ("100 Hubbles"); the field-of-view area and the survey-speed gain (up to ~1,000x faster than Hubble for large areas) are two distinct figures, the latter the product of aperture and field.
- **A surplus spy-telescope mirror reborn as a wide-field survey flagship.** The 2.4 m mirror at the heart of Roman came from a telescope asset donated to NASA by the National Reconnaissance Office, and the wide-field survey instrument was designed around it — a Hubble-diameter optic given a new life as the heart of NASA's next wide-field infrared survey flagship, rather than as a narrow-field pointed telescope.
- **A planned exoplanet census by a wholly different method — gravitational microlensing.** Roman's planned Galactic Bulge Time-Domain Survey is designed to find planets not by the dimming of a transit (Kepler, TESS) or the wobble of radial velocity, but by gravitational microlensing — the brief, symmetric brightening that occurs as a foreground star and its planets bend and magnify the light of a background star — which is sensitive to planets at wide orbital separations and even to free-floating (rogue) planets adrift between the stars, a population the transit surveys cannot easily reach.
- **The observatory was finished before it ever flew — assembled at NASA Goddard in late 2025.** Roman's construction and assembly were completed 25 November 2025 at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, ahead of its scheduled launch, and the launch was moved up to 30 August 2026 from the prior "no later than May 2027" commitment — a flagship observatory readied, integrated, and tested on the ground, awaiting its Falcon Heavy ride to the Sun-Earth L2 point.
