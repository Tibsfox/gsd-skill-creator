# v1.49.106 "The AM Radio Dial"

**Released:** 2026-03-28
**Code:** AMR
**Series:** PNW Research Series (#106 of 167)

## Summary

A broadcast heritage study tracing the full arc of AM radio on the West Coast -- from KJR's first licensed transmission in 1921 Seattle to the all-digital AM frontier of 2026. This release adds the AMR research project -- covering AM band physics and regulation, station-by-station chronicles of Pacific Northwest and California broadcasters, spectrum licensing pathways for prospective operators, FCC rules under 47 CFR Part 73, and the digital future including HD Radio, EV interference, and streaming competition. The through-line: before the internet, the radio dial was the only place where a single voice could reach the entire Pacific Northwest at once -- in the dark, in the mountains, across the water. That architecture still exists.

1. **M1: AM Band Foundations** -- Physics of amplitude modulation, AM band 535-1705 kHz, Federal Radio Commission (1927), FCC succession, Radio Act 1927, Communications Act 1934, NARBA treaty, station classes A through D
2. **M2: Pacific Northwest Stations** -- Station chronicles: KJR (1921), KGW Portland (1922), KOMO, KIRO, KVI, Golden Age network radio, Top 40, news-talk pivot, Telecom Act 1996 consolidation, regional stations in Tacoma, Spokane, Eugene
3. **M3: California Stations** -- Bay Area: KGO/KSFO, KNBR, KCBS (1909 origins), Los Angeles: KFI, KNX, KABC, KMPC, Don Lee Network, NBC Pacific Coast, 34% minority-serving California AM stations

6. **M6: The Digital Future** -- HD Radio (IBOC hybrid digital), FCC all-digital AM approval (MA3 mode, 2020), AM Revitalization proceeding, EV radio interference controversy, streaming competition (36% audio share by 2023)

## Retrospective

### What Worked
- Two parallel tracks (Alpha: station histories by region, Beta: regulatory and spectrum) naturally separate the narrative from the technical, allowing both to go deep
- Station-by-station chronicles with founding dates, call sign changes, and format pivots produce a reference that survives consolidation and ownership churn
- Including practical licensing pathways (LPFM, FM translator, Part 15) alongside historical narrative makes the research immediately actionable for prospective broadcasters

### What Could Be Better
- Indigenous broadcasting in the PNW (KWSO Warm Springs, KYNR Toppenish) deserves dedicated coverage as sovereign spectrum holders
- The FM translator loophole that has enabled AM-to-FM migration deserves deeper regulatory analysis

## Lessons Learned

- The AM dial is a stratigraphic record: each layer of regulation, technology, and format reveals the social and technical forces that shaped it -- the Radio Act of 1927, the network era, the Top 40 format revolution, the 1996 consolidation wave, and the digital transition are all legible in the call sign history
- KCBS San Francisco traces its origins to 1909 experimental station FN (later 6XF, then KQW), making it one of the oldest continuously operating broadcast operations in the world -- broadcast heritage is measured in generations, not product cycles
- The EV interference problem is not hypothetical: electric vehicle motor controllers generate broadband noise in the AM band that physically prevents reception, creating a genuine existential threat to AM infrastructure that regulation alone cannot solve
