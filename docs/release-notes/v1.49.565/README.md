## Summary

An anchored listener listens from a fixed position over long time. That is the principle at the heart of Explorer 35's mission, Modest Mouse's career, and the Pacific harbor seal's life history, and that is the principle that v1.60 encodes as its §6.6 PRINCIPLE-TRINITY alignment — one principle, three parallel coupling layers. Explorer 35 launched from Cape Canaveral Launch Complex 17B on a Thor-Delta E1 at 14:19:02 Greenwich Mean Time on the nineteenth day of July, nineteen sixty-seven — five days and ten hours after Surveyor 4 was lost at Sinus Medii, five years and eleven months before Explorer 35 would be commanded off — and entered a stable 800-by-7,692-kilometer selenocentric orbit at 147.30 degrees retrograde inclination three days later via a 23-second retrorocket burn. The orbit was stable. The nine instruments onboard — two fluxgate magnetometers (GSFC under Norman Ness, Ames under Charles Sonett), an MIT Faraday cup (Herbert Bridge), a UC Berkeley electron-proton detector (Kinsey Anderson), an Iowa energetic-particle detector (James Van Allen), a Goddard micrometeorite detector (Otto Berg), a Langley selenodetic gravity Doppler-tracking experiment (William Michael Jr.), a Stanford bistatic-radar experiment (Martin Tyler + Von Eshleman), and the S-band telecommunications subsystem — pointed at three scientific questions. What is the lunar magnetic environment? How does the solar wind flow past the Moon? How many energetic particles are out here, outside Earth's magnetospheric shielding? The nine instruments operated together. The Faraday cup failed about a year in, in July 1968, due to an electronics fault; the other eight continued for another five years. The mission did not end; the instrument did. The other eight instruments returned a six-year, 2,167-day, 15-million-three-axis-vector continuous time series that established the three foundational lunar-plasma-physics findings still cited in 2026 textbooks: that the Moon has no intrinsic global magnetic field to precision better than 1 nanotesla; that the solar wind flows past the Moon as if the Moon were electromagnetically transparent, because the Moon is smaller than the solar-wind ion gyroradius and has no ionosphere to deflect the plasma; and that the Moon nevertheless carves a cavity, a wake, downstream of its position, with an internal Mach-cone boundary set by the ratio of solar-wind velocity to local Alfvén speed. Explorer 35 was commanded off on June 24, 1973 — not because anything failed, but because NASA had moved on to newer IMPs (41, 43, 47, 50) in the continuing sequence. The spacecraft was retired. The mission architecture — anchor a platform in stable lunar orbit, listen for years, return foundational data — became the template for every subsequent orbital-science mission: Pioneer Venus Orbiter at Venus for fourteen years, Galileo at Jupiter for eight, Cassini-Huygens at Saturn for thirteen, Mars Global Surveyor at Mars for ten, Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter at Mars and still active, Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter at the Moon and still active in 2026, ARTEMIS P1 and P2 in direct architectural lineage from Explorer 35 measuring the same lunar wake plasma environment forty-four years after Explorer 35 began.

Seventeen years after Explorer 35's last day — in a suburb at the eastern foot of Tiger Mountain, twenty-four hundred miles northwest of Cape Canaveral, in a Seattle-area town called Issaquah that had spent the twentieth century moving from coal mining to lumber to technology-sector suburb — a boy named Isaac Brock, having arrived in Issaquah at age eleven from a Montana-and-Oregon-commune-and-church childhood that included a stretch of living in a shed on his mother's property, met another boy his age named Jeremiah Green at the video store where both worked, and in 1993, at eighteen, they started a band called Modest Mouse and rehearsed in the shed. Eric Judy joined on bass. The band's first EP was called *Blue Cadet-3, Do You Connect?* on K Records out of Olympia; the first LP was *This Is a Long Drive for Someone with Nothing to Think About* on Up Records out of Seattle; the second LP was *The Lonesome Crowded West*, seventy-four minutes of meditations on suburban sprawl and the loss of the rural American West; the third LP was *The Moon and Antarctica*, produced by Brian Deck at Clava Studios in Chicago from July to November of 1999 and released on June 13, 2000 on Epic Records at fifty-nine minutes and forty-three seconds. Pitchfork gave *The Moon and Antarctica* a nine-point-eight. The album was about death and the afterlife, sustained at a register that Mike Richardson's review called "strangely subdued," with a production precision that did not feel overpolished. NME, in a 2021 retrospective, called it one of the greatest records ever made. Track three was called "Gravity Rides Everything." Its chorus was: Everything will fall right into place. Track nine was called "The Stars Are Projectors." The album reached Billboard 200 number 120, the Heatseekers chart number five, and received RIAA Gold certification in March 2009 after approximately 492,000 US sales by October 2006. Four years later, *Good News for People Who Love Bad News* (April 6, 2004) was RIAA Platinum by August 2004 and received two Grammy nominations — Best Alternative Music Album and Best Rock Song for "Float On." Three years after that, *We Were Dead Before the Ship Even Sank* (March 20, 2007) debuted at Billboard 200 number one — Modest Mouse's first and only number-one album. Johnny Marr of The Smiths joined the band as a full member in 2006 and stayed through 2014. Eric Judy and Joe Plummer departed in 2012; Russell Higbee and Ben Massarella joined. Lisa Molinaro of Talkdemonic joined in 2011 (signed to Brock's Glacial Pace label); she and Brock became a couple and separated in 2017. Brock's adopted brother Ansel Vizcaya died in a 2004 Mount Rainier avalanche. On December 31, 2022 — the last day of that year — Jeremiah Green died of Stage IV cancer at age 45, twenty-nine continuous years into Modest Mouse's run. Isaac Brock, now forty-seven and living in Portland, Oregon, kept the band going. The eighth studio album, *An Eraser and a Maze*, is scheduled for release on June 5, 2026, six weeks after this degree's ship date — it is the first Modest Mouse studio album without Jeremiah on drums, and it is partially a grief-processing work for him. The band continues. The organization absorbs the loss.

Approximately thirty miles northwest of Issaquah — through Lake Washington, through the Ballard Locks, out past Alki Point and Discovery Park and Shilshole — begins the Salish Sea, a 16,900-square-kilometer inland sea shared by Washington State and British Columbia consisting of Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the Strait of Georgia, and their tributary inlets. In it live approximately fifty thousand Pacific harbor seals — *Phoca vitulina richardii*, described by John Edward Gray of the British Museum in 1864 from skulls and skins collected by Royal Navy Pacific Northwest expeditions and named after Sir John Richardson, the Scottish naval surgeon and Arctic naturalist who survived the 1819 Coppermine expedition with John Franklin and collected the type material. The Salish Sea harbor seal population is among the densest for the species globally, at approximately three seals per square kilometer. Four subpopulations: the San Juan Islands at approximately eleven thousand (declining since approximately 2013), Hood Canal at approximately fifteen hundred (increasing ten-to-twelve percent annually), South Puget Sound at approximately fifteen hundred (also increasing ten-to-twelve percent annually), and the outer Washington-Oregon coast plus British Columbia mainland at approximately thirty-six thousand combined (stable at carrying capacity). Each seal lives in a stable home range of five to ten kilometers across for its twenty-five-to-thirty-year lifespan, returning generation after generation to the same haulout rocks and sandbars. Adult males produce low-frequency broadband underwater territorial roars during the May-to-July breeding season — one to five seconds long, one hundred to five hundred hertz dominant frequency with upper harmonics to about twelve hundred hertz, source level one hundred thirty-five to one hundred forty-five decibels re one micropascal at one meter, carrying one to ten kilometers through the water column. The roars overlap directly with the acoustic spectrum of every ferry, container ship, and tugboat in the Salish Sea shipping lanes. The three harbor seal vocalization classes — male territorial roar, above-water haulout social calls (barks, honks, grunts, growls), and mother-pup contact calls — form a structural resonance with Explorer 35's three-instrument decomposition and Modest Mouse's three-decade career. One principle, three coupling layers, three scales. The §6.6 PRINCIPLE-TRINITY alignment.

Harbor seals are not prey of the famous Southern Resident Killer Whales (J, K, L pods — v1.59's organism) that live in these same waters. The Southern Residents feed almost exclusively on salmonids — approximately 98% Chinook salmon by stomach-contents analysis. The killer whales that DO eat harbor seals are a separate, genetically distinct ecotype of *Orcinus orca*: the Bigg's (transient) orcas, which specialize in marine mammals (harbor seals, harbor porpoises, Dall's porpoises, sea lions, occasionally cetaceans) and have had zero gene flow with the resident ecotype for tens of thousands of years. Morin et al. (2024) proposes formal species-level taxonomic revision to *Orcinus ater* for residents and *Orcinus rectipinnus* for transients. The v1.59→v1.60 chain captures this distinction as the first cross-ecotype predator-prey thread in the 60-degree corpus: v1.59 SRKW is the resident-ecotype first instance; v1.60 harbor seal is prey of the transient-ecotype (implicitly introduced via the predator-prey network). Two *Orcinus orca* modules in ecologically non-overlapping contexts.

Before 1972, Washington State paid fishermen to shoot harbor seals under a commercial-fisheries-motivated bounty program. By the mid-1960s, the Salish Sea harbor seal population had been reduced to approximately three thousand individuals — about six percent of the 2020s population. The Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972, signed by President Nixon on October 21, 1972, ended the bounty. The population recovered at ten-to-fifteen percent annual growth through the 1970s and 1980s, stabilizing at carrying capacity through the 1990s and 2000s. The current 2020s population (~50,000) is approximately 16.7× the 1960s low. This is the harbor seal's narrative backbone at v1.60 — the first species in the corpus whose canonical story centers on the MMPA as a pivot point, the first MMPA-recovery-arc narrative. Future MMPA-recovery species (sea otter, gray whale, humpback whale, Steller sea lion) will cite v1.60 as MMPA-narrative origin.

## Part A — Modest Mouse, Isaac Brock, and the Anchored Career

Modest Mouse's story is an anchored-listener story across 31+ years in 2026.

**The Issaquah origin (1993).** Isaac Brock was eleven when his family moved from a Montana-and-Oregon-commune-and-church childhood to Issaquah, Washington — a small Seattle-area suburb at the eastern foot of Tiger Mountain, then approximately 5,000 population, by 2020 around 42,000. Issaquah is the first Eastside (east-of-Lake-Washington) S36 anchor in the engine's 60-degree history. Brock was home-schooled initially. His mother's flood-prone house left him living for a stretch in a backyard shed while permanent housing was built. At the Issaquah video store where he worked as a teenager, Brock met Jeremiah Green. In 1993, aged 18, Brock + Green + Eric Judy (bass) formed Modest Mouse. Rehearsals were in the shed in Brock's mother's backyard. The band's name comes from a phrase by Virginia Woolf.

**The early era (1993-1999) on Up Records.** After an opening-years EP *Blue Cadet-3, Do You Connect?* on K Records out of Olympia, the first LP *This Is a Long Drive for Someone with Nothing to Think About* landed on Up Records (Seattle) in April 1996 and the second LP *The Lonesome Crowded West* followed in November 1997 as a 74-minute double-length meditation on suburban sprawl and the loss of the rural American West. Songs like "Cowboy Dan" and "Trailer Trash" established Brock's rural-American lyric anchor that would recur across his career. The band's voice was already established by 1997: long-form meditative arrangements, Brock's distinctive stutter-falsetto vocals, lyrics that used wordplay and philosophical-existential-rural-authenticity themes.

**The major-label transition and The Moon & Antarctica (2000).** In 1999 Modest Mouse signed with Epic Records. *The Moon & Antarctica* was produced by Brian Deck at Clava Studios in Chicago from July through November 1999 and released on June 13, 2000. Fifty-nine minutes and forty-three seconds. Themes of death and afterlife. Production described as "strangely subdued" and atmospheric. Pitchfork 9.8. Metacritic 82 ("universal acclaim"). Billboard 200 #120; Heatseekers #5. RIAA Gold March 2009 (~492,000 US sales by October 2006). Pitchfork ranked it #3 album of 2000, #7 of 2000-2004, #6 of the decade. NME (2021 retrospective) called it "one of the greatest records ever made." Track 3 "Gravity Rides Everything" was licensed for a Nissan Quest advertisement. The album's cosmological title — the Moon and Antarctica as paired end-zones of isolation — made the band's cosmological thematic register explicit; this is the album that justifies pairing Modest Mouse with Explorer 35 at degree 60, the lunar-orbital mission.

**Mass-market recognition: Good News and We Were Dead (2004-2007).** *Good News for People Who Love Bad News* (April 6, 2004) was RIAA Platinum by August 2004 and received two Grammy nominations — Best Alternative Music Album and Best Rock Song for "Float On" (single reached #68 on the Hot 100). Pitchfork 7.8, Metacritic 76. *We Were Dead Before the Ship Even Sank* (March 20, 2007) debuted at Billboard 200 #1 — the band's first and only #1 album. Johnny Marr of The Smiths joined as a full member 2006-2014. Eric Judy and Joe Plummer departed in 2012; Russell Higbee and Ben Massarella joined.

**The late-career arc and Jeremiah Green (2015-present).** *Strangers to Ourselves* (2015), *The Golden Casket* (2021), *An Eraser and a Maze* (June 5, 2026, eighth studio album). Jeremiah Green — Modest Mouse's drummer for twenty-nine continuous years (1993-2022) and founding member — died of Stage IV cancer on December 31, 2022 at age 45. *An Eraser and a Maze* is the first Modest Mouse studio album without Green on drums, and is partially a grief-processing work for him. Simon O'Connor and Damon Cox are the new lineup additions. Brock remains in Portland OR with three children (b. 2002, 2018, 2020/2021). The Glacial Pace label (founded mid-2000s) continues; Sub Pop A&R stint led to the Wolf Parade 2004 signing.

## Part B — Pacific Harbor Seal, the Salish Sea's Anchored Marine Mammal

Pacific harbor seals (*Phoca vitulina richardii*, Gray 1864) are the second marine mammal in the 60-degree corpus — closing the §6.4 FIRST-INSTANCE → CLASS TRANSITION that v1.59 SRKW opened.

**Taxonomy and naming.** John Edward Gray of the British Museum described *Phoca vitulina richardii* in 1864 from skulls and skins collected on mid-19th-century Royal Navy expeditions to the Pacific Northwest. The subspecies epithet *richardii* honors Sir John Richardson (1787-1865), a Scottish naval surgeon and Arctic naturalist who had been Sir John Franklin's companion on the Coppermine Expedition in 1819 and survived the expedition's terrible winter. Richardson collected the type specimen; Gray described it. The species *Phoca vitulina* was described by Linnaeus in 1758 and encompasses five recognized subspecies globally: *P. v. vitulina* (eastern Atlantic), *P. v. concolor* (western Atlantic), *P. v. stejnegeri* (western Pacific / Japan), *P. v. mellonae* (Lake of the Seals, Quebec, the only freshwater subspecies), and *P. v. richardii* (eastern North Pacific). The engine's first "Gray 1864" taxonomic authority extends the authority register into mid-19th-century British Museum zoology; prior engine authorities include Linnaeus 1758 (multiple), Latham 1790, Audubon 1827, and Morin et al. 2024.

**Physical characteristics.** Adult Pacific harbor seals are compact phocids: females ~1.5 m / 75 kg, males ~1.85 m / 100 kg; pups 10-12 kg at birth, weaned at 22-25 kg in 4-6 weeks. Females reach sexual maturity at 3.72 years mean; first parturition at 4.64 years (Pearson et al. 2025, *Marine Mammal Science* 41). Wild lifespan 25-30 years with females typically outliving males; oldest documented ~35 years. Highly polymorphic pelage (dark gray with silver spots, silvery with dark rings, cream-white with dark speckles, blue-black with white spots) enables individual photo-identification via Seal Sitters and SeaDoc Society volunteer programs. Thick subcutaneous blubber (3-5 cm typical) provides thermoregulation in 8-12 °C year-round Salish Sea water. Respiratory adaptations enable 500+ m dives with 30-minute maximum duration (though typical foraging dives are 50-100 m, 3-7 min).

**Salish Sea population structure.** Approximately 50,000 harbor seals across the Salish Sea — one of the highest-density harbor seal populations in the world, at approximately 3 seals per square kilometer. Four principal subpopulations: **San Juan Islands / Strait of Juan de Fuca** (~11,000, DECLINING since 2013 by ~3-5% annually — possibly Bigg's transient orca predation + forage-fish competition + marine-traffic disturbance); **South Puget Sound** (~1,500, INCREASING 10-12% annually); **Hood Canal** (~1,500, INCREASING 10-12% annually); **Outer WA/OR coast + British Columbia mainland** (~36,000 combined, STABLE at carrying capacity). U.S. Salish Sea abundance 2023: >13,000 individuals.

**Three vocalization classes.** (1) **Underwater male territorial roar** during May-July breeding season: 1-5 s duration, 100-500 Hz dominant frequency (peak 200-300 Hz), upper harmonics to ~1.2 kHz, source level 135-145 dB re 1 μPa at 1 m. Overlaps directly with anthropogenic vessel-noise spectrum creating acoustic-masking pressure in shipping-lane-adjacent populations. (2) **Above-water short calls**: barks, honks, grunts, growls, moans during haulout social interactions, <0.5 s duration, 200 Hz-2 kHz. (3) **Mother-pup contact calls**: pair-specific acoustic signatures during 4-6 week lactation period, 0.2-1.0 s duration, 300 Hz-1.5 kHz. Mother-pup recognition via playback experiments (Renouf 1985) shows ~95% individual discrimination accuracy.

**MMPA 1972 recovery arc — the canonical narrative.** Before 1972, Washington State paid fishermen under a commercial-fisheries-motivated bounty to kill harbor seals. By the mid-1960s, the Salish Sea harbor seal population was reduced to approximately 3,000 individuals. The Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972, signed by President Nixon on October 21, 1972, ended the bounty. Growth at 10-15% annually through the 1970s and 1980s, stabilization through the 1990s and 2000s. Present population is ~16.7× the mid-1960s low. This is the first MMPA-recovery-arc narrative in the 60-degree corpus and establishes the pattern for future MMPA-recovery species.

**Cross-ecotype predator-prey (v1.59→v1.60 chain).** Harbor seals are NOT prey of Southern Resident Killer Whales (v1.59's organism). SRKW feed ~98% on Chinook salmon. Harbor seals are prey of the genetically distinct Bigg's (transient) orcas, which co-occupy the Salish Sea but have had zero gene flow with residents for tens of thousands of years (Hoelzel et al. 2007). Morin et al. (2024) proposes formal species-level revision to *Orcinus ater* and *Orcinus rectipinnus* respectively. The v1.59→v1.60 chain is the first cross-ecotype predator-prey thread in the corpus — same binomial *Orcinus orca*, ecologically non-overlapping networks.
