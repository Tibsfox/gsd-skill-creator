## Part B: Pied-billed Grebe -- The Submerge Call

Full deep research covering the Pied-billed Grebe (*Podilymbus podiceps*) as the most biologically unique species since the American Dipper, the engine's first grebe, and the aquatic trilogy closer:

- **New Order Podicipediformes -- Four New Orders in Five Degrees.** At degree 60, the engine admits a species from a new order for the fourth time in five degrees. The streak: Columbiformes (56, Band-tailed Pigeon), Coraciiformes (57, Belted Kingfisher), Anseriformes (58, Wood Duck), Podicipediformes (60, Pied-billed Grebe). Only degree 59 (Mallard, same Anseriformes as 58) interrupts. Five consecutive non-passerine species (56-60) constitute the longest non-passerine streak in the engine's history. After degree 60: 49 of 61 entries (80.3%) are Passeriformes, dropping below 81% for the first time. The 56-60 burst is the most intense order-diversification event in the engine's history.
- **New Family Podicipedidae -- Grebes Enter the Engine.** The 26th family. Characterized by a suite of unique adaptations: lobed feet, rear-mounted legs, feather eating, floating nests, adjustable buoyancy, vestigial tail, and back-riding chicks. The Pied-billed Grebe is the most widespread New World grebe and the only grebe regularly seen on small ponds in the PNW lowlands.
- **Aquatic Trilogy Complete (58-59-60) -- Surface to Subsurface to Submersion.** Three consecutive aquatic-primary species, three aquatic strata, three nesting strategies, two evolutionary lineages. Wood Duck (58, Anatidae): surface dabbler, tree-cavity nester, webbed feet, the rising whistle. Mallard (59, Anatidae): surface dabbler, ground nester, webbed feet, the descrescendo quack. Pied-billed Grebe (60, Podicipedidae): sustained diver, floating-nest builder, lobed feet, the accelerating submerge call. The sequence maps surface to subsurface to full submersion. The engine descends through the water column with each degree. Two degrees of true ducks followed by one degree of not-duck teaches the homology/convergence distinction through sequential example: Wood Duck and Mallard share features through common ancestry (homology); Mallard and Grebe share features through independent evolution (convergence).
- **First Sustained Diver -- 20-30 Seconds Submerged via Lobed Feet.** The engine's first bird that stays underwater as a primary feeding strategy. Dives from the surface, swims underwater propelled by lobed feet, pursues and catches prey, remains submerged for 20-30 seconds. Three underwater strategies now documented: American Dipper (52) walks on stream bottom (seconds, wings + feet), Belted Kingfisher (57) plunge-dives from air (1-2 seconds, gravity + wings), Pied-billed Grebe (60) sustained surface dive (20-30 seconds, lobed feet). Walking, striking, swimming. The engine's underwater ecology is now three-dimensional.
- **Sinking Without Diving -- Controlled Buoyancy as Alarm Response.** The most remarkable adaptation. When alarmed, the Grebe can gradually sink beneath the surface without diving by compressing its plumage to expel trapped air. The bird simply sinks. Slowly. Silently. Like a submarine taking on ballast. Only the head may remain above water, periscope-like. This is fundamentally different from diving -- no forward thrust, no splash. The bird transitions from floating to submerged through internal adjustment alone. No duck can do this. Flight is energetically expensive and conspicuous. Sinking is silent, invisible, and costs nothing. The Grebe's alarm response involves becoming less visible rather than more active -- the opposite of every other waterbird in the engine.
- **Feather Eating -- Gut Feltworks.** Grebes eat their own feathers. Habitually. The feathers form a feltlike mass in the stomach that protects the intestines from sharp fish bones and crustacean exoskeletons. Parent grebes feed feathers to their chicks from the first day of life. Found in all grebe species and no non-grebe species.
- **Lobed Feet, Cannot Walk on Land, Floating Nest, Carries Chicks on Back.** Each toe has individual flattened lateral lobes that expand during the power stroke and fold during recovery, reducing drag -- a completely different solution to underwater propulsion than the duck's webbed foot. Legs are set so far back that the bird cannot walk on land; if forced ashore, it can only push itself along on its belly. Nest is a platform of decaying aquatic vegetation anchored loosely to emergent reeds, rising and falling with the water level. When the adult leaves, it covers the eggs with nesting material, staining them from white to brown for camouflage. Chicks ride on the parent's back, nestled among the back feathers; the parent swims and dives with chicks aboard.
- **Not Related to Ducks -- Convergent Evolution Documented.** The Pied-billed Grebe looks like a small, compact duck. It swims on the same ponds. A casual observer might reasonably assume it is some kind of duck. It is not. Podicipediformes and Anseriformes occupy entirely different branches of the avian phylogenetic tree. Their similarity is convergent evolution: independent lineages evolving similar body plans under similar environmental pressures. Grebes' closest relatives are flamingos (Phoenicopteriformes) in the clade Mirandornithes. Convergent evolution gave grebes a duck-like appearance; their actual evolutionary affinities are entirely different.
- **Taxonomy: Podilymbus Podiceps -- "Rump-Footed Rump-Foot".** Podilymbus is a contraction of Podiceps + Latin colymbus ("diver"). Podiceps is from Latin podex ("rump") + pes ("foot"). Full binomial Podilymbus podiceps translates to approximately "rump-footed rump-foot" -- one of the most tautological scientific names in ornithology. Common name "Pied-billed" refers to the breeding-season bill: pale with a prominent black vertical band. "Grebe" derives from French grebe, possibly from Breton krib ("crest"), though the Pied-billed Grebe lacks a crest. Compare the aquatic trilogy's nomenclature: Aix sponsa (58, "Aristotle's waterfowl in bridal dress"), Anas platyrhynchos (59, "flat-billed duck"), Podilymbus podiceps (60, "rump-footed rump-foot"). Three naming philosophies: beauty, function, anatomy.
- **The Submerge Call -- 61st Acoustic Element, Tempo Direction Introduced.** The Pied-billed Grebe's accelerating "cow-cow-cow" series becomes the 61st acoustic element. A loud, cuckoo-like series starting slow (2-3 notes per second) and accelerating to a chattering climax (5-6 per second) before stopping abruptly. Frequency range approximately 500-2,000 Hz. Duration 3-8 seconds. Carries 200-400 meters across open water. The submerge call introduces tempo direction as a new acoustic property. Previous elements have had pitch direction (ascending, descending, flat) and amplitude direction (crescendo, descrescendo). The Grebe adds a fifth directional character: accelerating tempo. Three consecutive aquatic species, three directional characters: ascending (Wood Duck), descending (Mallard), accelerating (Grebe).
- **E=4 Common but Cryptic -- The Hidden E=4.** E=4 captures a paradox: the species is widespread and the call is loud, but the bird is secretive and easy to miss. The cow-cow series carries 200-400 meters across open water, but the bird skulks in vegetated margins, sinks when approached, and spends significant time submerged. Many birders report hearing it more often than seeing it. Loud when it calls, silent and invisible between calls. The aquatic trilogy's energy curve: E=3 (Wood Duck), E=5 (Mallard), E=4 (Grebe) -- up then slightly down. The Mallard was the peak; the Grebe steps back one level.
- **CSV Substitution: 16th Consecutive -- Green Frog Replaced.** Green Frog (*Lithobates clamitans*) was the original degree 60 species -- a native species (unlike the Bullfrog at 59, which was invasive). Replaced by Pied-billed Grebe per SPS scope rule (birds and mammals only). Both occupy the vegetated margins of freshwater lakes and ponds. Lake edge habitat and E=4 energy preserved. Substitution streak preserves: 56 Ensatina to Band-tailed Pigeon, 57 Pacific Giant Salamander to Belted Kingfisher, 58 Red-legged Frog (2nd) to Wood Duck, 59 Bullfrog to Mallard, 60 Green Frog to Pied-billed Grebe.
