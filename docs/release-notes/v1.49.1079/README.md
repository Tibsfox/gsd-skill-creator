## Surprises

- **A whole space telescope stared at one patch of sky and never looked away.** Kepler fixed its 0.95 m photometer on a single ~115-square-degree field in the Cygnus–Lyra region and measured the brightness of about 150,000 stars at once, continuously, for years — a still, patient stare that traded the whole sky for the depth of one field, exactly what catching a faint, once-a-year dip requires.
- **An Earth crossing a Sun dims it by only about 84 parts per million.** Kepler's transit method had to detect that an Earth-size planet passing in front of a Sun-like star dims the star by only about 84 parts per million, about once a year — a tiny, brief, repeated dip that demanded extraordinary photometric precision and a multi-year continuous baseline to confirm.
- **A single mission found more planets than every mission before it combined.** Kepler discovered more exoplanets than any mission before it — about 2,600+ confirmed Kepler planets, with thousands more candidates — and the census revealed that small planets (Earth-size through sub-Neptune) are common around other stars, the first large statistical census of transiting exoplanets.
- **When a wheel failed, sunlight itself became the third wheel.** After a second of Kepler's four reaction wheels failed in May 2013, the K2 mission balanced the spacecraft against the gentle pressure of sunlight (photon pressure) as a "virtual third wheel," holding a stable enough pointing to keep surveying fields along the ecliptic from 2014 to 2018 — an innovative recovery (though not the first use of solar-radiation pressure for attitude control; Mariner 10 and Hayabusa used it earlier).
- **A small bottom fish watches the streambed motionless for the faint passing.** The Prickly Sculpin (*Cottus asper*), a small, mottled, bottom-dwelling fish of Pacific Northwest streams, rivers, and lakes, lies still on the bottom and senses the faint passing of prey through its sensitive lateral line, then strikes (behavioral / ecological natural history only, framed at the *Cottus*/sculpin level) — a still watch for the faint passing, a mirror of the field Kepler watched.
- **A dry-slope wildflower turns a profusion of bright sun-yellow faces to the open sky.** Oregon Sunshine (*Eriophyllum lanatum*, the common woolly sunflower), a Pacific Northwest native of dry, open, rocky slopes and meadows in the sunflower family, opens bright golden-yellow daisy-like flower heads above woolly silvery-gray foliage — a profusion of bright sun-yellow faces turned to the open sky across the dry slope (ecological description only) — a bright field turned to the open sky, a mirror of the bright field Kepler held its still watch on.

## Dedication

v1.262 Kepler Space Telescope is dedicated to the people across NASA, the Ames Research Center, the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, Ball Aerospace, and the mission's science teams who built, launched, and held the still watch on one bright field of stars for more than nine years. The engineers who built the single photometer — a wide aperture feeding ninety-five million pixels across forty-two detectors — that read the brightness of about one hundred fifty thousand stars at once; the launch team who sent it up on a Delta II from Cape Canaveral in March 2009 and set it drifting behind Earth around the Sun; the founding investigator William Borucki, who argued for decades that a patient stare could catch the faint, once-a-year dimming of an Earth crossing a distant sun; the scientists who turned that stare into thousands of new worlds and the finding that small planets are common; and the operators who, when a wheel failed, balanced the spacecraft against the gentle pressure of sunlight and kept it surveying until the fuel ran out and the last goodnight in November 2018. They held one still watch on one bright field, and the small worlds crossed it one by one.
