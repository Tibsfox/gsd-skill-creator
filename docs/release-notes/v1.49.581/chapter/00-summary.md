2. **First controlled liftoff from another celestial body.** The Surveyor 6 vernier hop on November 17, 1967 at 10:32 UTC (2.5-second coordinated three-vernier burn at 150 lbf total thrust, ~4 m vertical, ~2.4 m lateral west, soft reland) is the first instance of a spacecraft commanded to lift off a planetary surface other than Earth under autonomous-sequenced control. The maneuver predates Apollo 11 ascent (July 21, 1969) by approximately 20 months and established the operational confidence basis for subsequent ascent maneuvers (Apollo LM ascent, Mars sample-return ascent stages). The vernier hardware that did the work was not redesigned for the maneuver — the same MMH/MON-10 bipropellant feed, the same throttle servos, the same propellant valves that had executed terminal descent on every previous Surveyor were commanded to a new function via new commanded sequences.

3. **First stereo image pair of any planetary surface.** Pre-hop and post-hop imagery from the same Sinus Medii scene at vantage points displaced approximately 2.4 m apart constitutes the first stereo image pair of any planetary surface. The displacement was sufficient for the depth perception that Apollo lunar-site selection would later need. Eugene M. Shoemaker (USGS), leading the Surveyor Television Experiment Team, treated the stereo pair as the program-level science objective for the hop, intended to establish stereo-photogrammetry technique for use in Apollo site selection. The technique that Shoemaker's team established with the November 17, 1967 stereo pair would inform the Apollo Hasselblad stereo-photogrammetry program and, subsequently, the Mars Exploration Rover Mastcam, MSL Curiosity Mastcam, and Mars 2020 Perseverance Mastcam-Z stereo-imaging programs.
