## What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural structure rather than archived data.** The Kepler exoplanet-transit shader uses analytic geometry and procedural noise rather than loading actual Kepler light curves from the Mikulski Archive for Space Telescopes. A future revision could load encoded Kepler light curves for a higher-fidelity rendering keyed to real transit signals.
- **The photometer-and-orbit diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the observatory, its single photometer, and the Earth-trailing orbit.
- **The observatory sidebar table is illustrative rather than exhaustive.** The sidebar lists representative axis-lineage and observatory elements (Kepler, Fermi, Compton, IRAS) but does not enumerate the full set of Kepler results; a future revision could expand the table with per-result highlights.
- **The census statistics are described rather than computed.** The transit-stacking statistics and the occurrence-rate correction are described but not run against the archived catalog; a future ship could query a real Kepler light curve and measure a transit depth and period directly.

## Surprises

- **The hardest-to-detect worlds turned out to be the most common.** Small, distant worlds make the shallowest and rarest dips, yet once the survey's reach is corrected for, they are the most abundant kind in the galaxy, so the census is filled most of all by exactly the worlds that are hardest to find.
- **A large patient owl mirrors the fixed, high-precision watch of one field.** The Great gray owl, the owl that fixes on one patch of ground and detects the smallest faint movement beneath it, enters as the fix-on-one-field-and-detect-the-smallest-faint-signal mirror to Kepler holding its gaze on one field of stars and registering the tiny periodic dimming of a small world.
- **A prairie of countless small blooms mirrors the census of small worlds.** Common camas, whose countless small star-shaped flowers turn a whole meadow blue, enters as the reveal-the-abundance-of-a-whole-field-at-once mirror, so the catalog's first exoplanet-census observatory is paired with a plant whose abundance is read across a whole field at a glance.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A faint, repeating signal stands out when it is stacked.** By stacking many transits of the same world on its period, Kepler made a dip too faint to see in one crossing rise above the noise, showing that the significance of a faint detection grows as the square root of the number of crossings and that the deepest census is the longest-watched one.
2. **Watching a whole field at once turns discovery into a census.** By measuring 150,000 stars together rather than one at a time, Kepler measured not just individual worlds but how common worlds of each size are, showing that a survey of a whole field can become a statistical census of a population.
3. **Correcting for what a survey can see reveals what is truly common.** By correcting the count of detected worlds for the geometry of edge-on orbits and the survey's reach, Kepler showed that small worlds — the hardest to detect — are in fact the most common kind, showing that a census must account for its own blind spots to measure the truth.
4. **One field, held steady, finds the smallest worlds.** By holding its gaze fixed on one field for years, Kepler accumulated enough crossings to find Earth-size worlds at temperate distances, showing that a patient, fixed watch reaches signals a sweeping survey would miss.
5. **An axis opens at a substrate-form-distinct first observation.** By reading the faint shadows worlds cast rather than the light of distant sources, Kepler opened the exoplanet-detection axis at obs#1, showing that a substrate-axis rotation begins a new way of seeing distinct from the axis it rotates away from.
6. **A clever use of a constant force can revive a mission.** After two reaction wheels reached the end of their working lives, Kepler used the gentle pressure of sunlight for balance and was reborn as K2, showing that a constant, predictable force can be turned into a stabilizing one.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across an axis rotation.** Because Kepler reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.206 Fermi template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the transit survey while keeping the warm palette, making the axis-opening rotation a clean build.
