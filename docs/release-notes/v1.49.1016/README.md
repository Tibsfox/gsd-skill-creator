## Summary

v1.49.1016 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.206 to 1.207, adding the Kepler Space Telescope (Kepler) — the first space mission built to find planets around other stars by the transit method — as the obs#1 first INSTANCE that OPENS the EXOPLANET-DETECTION substrate-axis (axis-rotation #29 opened). The gamma-ray-astronomy axis sustained three observations — v1013 Compton opened it with the first comprehensive full-sky gamma-ray survey, v1014 Swift sustained it with rapid precise burst localization, and v1015 Fermi sustained it with the continuous deep high-sensitivity survey — and reached the all-sky-survey, burst-localization, and continuous-deep-mapping forms; with the gamma-ray axis now holding three observations, the forward continuation rotates to a new substrate-axis per the rotation discipline. Kepler turns the catalog to a new way of seeing the sky: not the light of distant high-energy sources, but the faint, periodic shadows that distant worlds cast as they cross the faces of their stars.

Kepler was built as the tenth mission of NASA's Discovery Program, proposed and led by principal investigator William Borucki and managed by NASA's Ames Research Center, with the spacecraft built by Ball Aerospace, and named for Johannes Kepler, who derived the laws of planetary motion. It reached an Earth-trailing heliocentric orbit on 7 March 2009, carried aloft as a Delta II payload from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station, and it concluded in October 2018, reborn along the way as K2. The observatory carries a single instrument — a 0.95-meter-aperture Schmidt-design photometer with a 1.4-meter primary mirror and a 95-megapixel camera of forty-two charge-coupled devices, the largest camera then flown in space.

The signature of the mission is transit photometry. Kepler finds a world by measuring the small, periodic drop in a star's brightness that occurs when a planet crosses in front of it, a dimming of only about one part in ten thousand for an Earth-size world, finding worlds far too small and faint to image directly.

The continuous single-field watch made the small repeating signals detectable. Kepler stared without interruption at one fixed field of about 150,000 stars in Cygnus and Lyra, measuring each star's brightness about every half hour for about four years to a few parts in a hundred thousand.

The first statistical census of exoplanets turned the study of other worlds into a population science. By monitoring so many stars at once, Kepler measured not just individual worlds but how common planets of each size are across the galaxy, showing that planets are common and outnumber the stars.

The abundance of small worlds reshaped expectations of what planetary systems look like. Kepler found that small worlds — Earth-size and super-Earth, between Earth and Neptune in size — are the most common kind in the galaxy, far outnumbering the giant planets found earlier.

The first Earth-size worlds in habitable zones answered a long-standing question. Kepler found the first Earth-size worlds at temperate distances from their stars, including Kepler-186f, Kepler-452b, and Kepler-22b around a Sun-like star.

The axis-relevant substrate of Kepler at this degree is its opening of the EXOPLANET-DETECTION substrate-axis at obs#1, the transit-photometry survey of a single fixed field. Kepler opens the EXOPLANET-DETECTION-AXIS thread at obs#1 NEW LOCKED and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#79 cumulative (axis-rotation #29 opened). The forward continuation may favor a later exoplanet mission that sustains the axis at obs#2 — an all-sky transit survey of the nearest, brightest stars, a transit-spectroscopy mission of planetary atmospheres, or a direct-imaging mission — or a substrate-axis rotation #30 to an under-served axis.

Kepler opens the exoplanet way of seeing the sky on the catalog, and the mission records the Kepler team at NASA's Ames Research Center, principal investigator William Borucki, the Ball Aerospace builders, and Johannes Kepler. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.206 to 1.207, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#90 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A faint, repeating signal stands out when it is stacked.** By stacking many transits of the same world on its period, Kepler made a dip too faint to see in one crossing rise above the noise, showing that the significance of a faint detection grows as the square root of the number of crossings and that the deepest census is the longest-watched one.
2. **Watching a whole field at once turns discovery into a census.** By measuring 150,000 stars together rather than one at a time, Kepler measured not just individual worlds but how common worlds of each size are, showing that a survey of a whole field can become a statistical census of a population.
3. **Correcting for what a survey can see reveals what is truly common.** By correcting the count of detected worlds for the geometry of edge-on orbits and the survey's reach, Kepler showed that small worlds — the hardest to detect — are in fact the most common kind, showing that a census must account for its own blind spots to measure the truth.
4. **One field, held steady, finds the smallest worlds.** By holding its gaze fixed on one field for years, Kepler accumulated enough crossings to find Earth-size worlds at temperate distances, showing that a patient, fixed watch reaches signals a sweeping survey would miss.
5. **An axis opens at a substrate-form-distinct first observation.** By reading the faint shadows worlds cast rather than the light of distant sources, Kepler opened the exoplanet-detection axis at obs#1, showing that a substrate-axis rotation begins a new way of seeing distinct from the axis it rotates away from.
6. **A clever use of a constant force can revive a mission.** After two reaction wheels reached the end of their working lives, Kepler used the gentle pressure of sunlight for balance and was reborn as K2, showing that a constant, predictable force can be turned into a stabilizing one.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across an axis rotation.** Because Kepler reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.206 Fermi template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the transit survey while keeping the warm palette, making the axis-opening rotation a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The hardest-to-detect worlds turned out to be the most common.** Small, distant worlds make the shallowest and rarest dips, yet once the survey's reach is corrected for, they are the most abundant kind in the galaxy, so the census is filled most of all by exactly the worlds that are hardest to find.
- **A large patient owl mirrors the fixed, high-precision watch of one field.** The Great gray owl, the owl that fixes on one patch of ground and detects the smallest faint movement beneath it, enters as the fix-on-one-field-and-detect-the-smallest-faint-signal mirror to Kepler holding its gaze on one field of stars and registering the tiny periodic dimming of a small world.
- **A prairie of countless small blooms mirrors the census of small worlds.** Common camas, whose countless small star-shaped flowers turn a whole meadow blue, enters as the reveal-the-abundance-of-a-whole-field-at-once mirror, so the catalog's first exoplanet-census observatory is paired with a plant whose abundance is read across a whole field at a glance.

## Dedication

v1.207 Kepler is dedicated to the team that built and flew NASA's first exoplanet transit-photometry mission: principal investigator William Borucki, who proposed and led the mission and championed the transit method for years before it flew; the Kepler team across NASA's Ames Research Center who managed the mission and turned its continuous brightness records into the first statistical census of the planet population; and the Ball Aerospace team that built the spacecraft and its single, exquisitely precise photometer. It is dedicated as well to Johannes Kepler, for whom the observatory was named, who derived the laws of planetary motion. Kepler held its gaze on one field of about 150,000 stars for years, registering the faint, periodic dimmings of small worlds, and it showed that planets outnumber the stars and that small worlds are the most common kind.
