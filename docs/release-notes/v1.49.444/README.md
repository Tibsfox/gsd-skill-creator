- ***Canis lupus* Species Overview and the Pacific Northwest Subspecies.** The **gray wolf** (*Canis lupus*, described by Linnaeus in 1758) is the largest wild canid in North America. Adult Pacific Northwest wolves weigh 70-120 pounds (32-54 kg) for females and 80-140 pounds (36-64 kg) for males, with the eastern and interior populations running larger than the coastal populations. The Pacific Northwest populations are the **northwestern wolf** subspecies (*Canis lupus occidentalis*) from the interior and the **coastal wolf** (sometimes assigned as a separate subspecies *Canis lupus crassodon* or the coastal morph of *occidentalis*) from the coastal rainforests. The species is one of the most widespread canids in the world, historically occupying the entire Northern Hemisphere above approximately 20° latitude, and the Pacific Northwest populations are continuous with the broader Rocky Mountain and British Columbia distributions. The scientific name *Canis lupus* translates as "wolf dog" from the Latin *canis* (dog) and *lupus* (wolf). Gray wolves are highly social predators, forming packs that are typically extended family groups of 4-9 individuals centered on a breeding pair and their offspring of the previous 1-3 years. The pack structure is the acoustic foundation of the multi-voice harmonic pack howl.
- **The Multi-Voice Harmonic Pack Howl as Acoustic Chord.** The primary territorial vocalization is the pack howl: a coordinated multi-voice event in which each wolf in the pack contributes a different frequency layer. The fundamental frequencies for adult wolves run approximately 150-780 Hz, with the adults typically in the 150-350 Hz range and the yearlings in the 300-780 Hz range. The harmonics extend up through 4000-5000 Hz. The howl is not a unison call but a chord: the adults' lower fundamentals underpin the higher harmonics of the yearlings, the whole assembly producing a chord that can sustain for several minutes. The Beau Geste effect — documented in acoustic research on gray wolf pack howls — describes the tendency of wolves to vary their individual pitches precisely to create the impression of a larger pack than is actually present, and the effect is a direct behavioral adaptation to the territorial function of the howl. Pack howls function as territorial declaration (signaling pack presence to neighboring packs), rallying call (reuniting pack members after separation), coordination signal (communicating hunt initiation or pack movement), and acoustic map (encoding pack size and age structure for any wolf within the 10-kilometer carry radius).
