## Mission Overview

The Mars Science Laboratory rover Curiosity is the obs#5 sustain of the MARS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA surface mission whose defining achievement is the first sky-crane landing and the first in-situ determination that ancient Mars once had a habitable environment. It was a wholly NASA / United States mission, developed and operated by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory; the rover was named via a NASA student essay contest won by Clara Ma (then about 12, of Lenexa, Kansas; announced 2009, from more than 9,000 entries; she signed the rover at JPL on 8 June 2009). Curiosity is car-sized — about 899 kg (about 1,982 lb), roughly 3 m long, with a mast about 2.2 m tall, on a six-wheel rocker-bogie chassis — and is nuclear-powered by a Multi-Mission Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generator (MMRTG, about 110 W, fueled by roughly 4.8 kg of plutonium-238 dioxide), rather than the solar arrays the Mars Exploration Rovers carried; the radioisotope generator frees it from solar, dust, and seasonal limits. MSL launched on an Atlas V 541 from Cape Canaveral SLC-41 on 26 November 2011 (15:02 UTC) and landed in Gale Crater, at "Bradbury Landing" (~4.59°S, 137.44°E), on 6 August 2012 (UTC; evening of 5 August PDT). Because the ~899 kg rover was too heavy for the airbag system used by Mars Pathfinder and the Mars Exploration Rovers, Curiosity reached the surface by the sky crane: after a guided atmospheric entry and a supersonic parachute, a rocket-powered descent stage hovered on its retrorockets and lowered the rover to a wheels-down touchdown on a ~7.5 m nylon bridle (three tethers plus an umbilical for power and communications), then cut the bridle and flew off to crash a safe distance away — the "seven minutes of terror," the first sky-crane landing (a new entry-descent-landing architecture later reused by Perseverance in 2021). The science goal was habitability: to assess whether Gale Crater ever had environmental conditions favorable to microbial life. The 10-instrument payload comprised Mastcam (mast color stereo imaging); MAHLI, the Mars Hand Lens Imager (arm-mounted close-up imaging); MARDI, the Mars Descent Imager; ChemCam, a laser-induced breakdown spectrometer (LIBS) with a remote micro-imager that fires a laser to vaporize rock several metres away and reads the resulting spark — the first laser instrument on a planetary surface; APXS, the Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer (elemental chemistry — a Canadian Space Agency / MDA contribution, the same instrument class flown on the Mars Exploration Rovers); CheMin, an X-ray diffractometer for definitive mineralogy from drilled powder; SAM, Sample Analysis at Mars — a gas chromatograph, a quadrupole mass spectrometer, and a tunable laser spectrometer that detect organics, methane, and isotopes; RAD, the Radiation Assessment Detector; DAN, Dynamic Albedo of Neutrons (subsurface hydrogen and water); and REMS (weather). A drill collects powdered rock from a rock interior and delivers it to the SAM and CheMin onboard laboratories — the first rover to drill and ingest rock samples into onboard analytical laboratories on another planet. At Yellowknife Bay in Gale Crater, Curiosity drilled mudstone (the "John Klein" target) and found an ancient fluvio-lacustrine (river-and-lake) environment that was habitable — near-neutral, low-salinity liquid water, the biogenic elements (carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus, sulfur), and chemical energy sources (redox couples) — conditions that could have supported chemolithoautotrophic microbial life: the first in-situ determination that ancient Mars once had a habitable environment. The SAM laboratory detected in-situ organic molecules preserved in roughly three-billion-year-old mudstone (chlorobenzene in 2014; thiophenes and aromatic and aliphatic organics in 2018) and measured a low background of atmospheric methane (~0.4 ppbv) with seasonal variation. This is habitability plus organic-molecule detection — NOT a detection of life and NOT a life search; Viking (obs#1) carried the in-situ life-detection experiments, while Curiosity assessed PAST habitability and did not search for or find life, organic molecules are chemical building blocks and not evidence of life, and the methane's source is debated and not attributed to life. Early in the mission Curiosity imaged the "Hottah" conglomerate, rounded and cemented pebbles interpreted as an ancient streambed of flowing water. It drove to and has been climbing Mount Sharp (Aeolis Mons), the layered central mound, reading an ancient wet-to-dry climate transition (clay minerals lower, sulfates higher), and RAD measured the surface radiation dose relevant to future human missions. Curiosity remains operational after roughly 14 years and is approaching, but (as of 2026) has NOT yet surpassed, Opportunity's ~14-year-136-day Mars surface-longevity record (set 2004–2019, still standing; Curiosity would not pass it until about 2027). Curiosity SUSTAINS the MARS-EXPLORATION substrate-axis at obs#5 (substrate-axis rotation #36 sustains, the 4th consecutive sustain).

## Surprises

- **A car-sized rover was lowered onto Mars by a hovering rocket stage on tethers.** Because the ~899 kg rover was too heavy for airbags, Curiosity reached the surface by the sky crane — a rocket-powered descent stage that hovered on its retrorockets and lowered the rover to a wheels-down touchdown on a ~7.5 m bridle, then cut the tethers and flew off to crash a safe distance away — the first sky-crane landing, a new entry-descent-landing architecture later reused by Perseverance in 2021.
- **A rover drilled the rocks and judged an ancient lakebed habitable.** Within months of landing, at Yellowknife Bay, Curiosity drilled mudstone (the "John Klein" target) and found an ancient fluvio-lacustrine environment that was habitable — near-neutral, low-salinity liquid water, the biogenic elements, and chemical energy — conditions that could have supported microbial life, the first in-situ determination that ancient Mars once had a habitable environment (not a detection of life).
- **Onboard laboratories detected organic molecules in three-billion-year-old mudstone.** The SAM laboratory detected organic molecules preserved in roughly three-billion-year-old mudstone (chlorobenzene in 2014; thiophenes and aromatic and aliphatic organics in 2018) and measured a low background of atmospheric methane (~0.4 ppbv) with seasonal variation — chemical building blocks and a debated trace gas, neither attributed to life.
- **A nuclear rover keeps working through dust and seasons that limit solar rovers.** Curiosity is powered by an MMRTG (about 110 W from roughly 4.8 kg of plutonium-238 dioxide) — the first nuclear-powered rover on another planet — freeing it from the solar, dust, and seasonal limits that ended the solar Mars Exploration Rovers; it has operated more than a decade and continues to climb Mount Sharp.
- **A small songbird reads a tree trunk surface by surface.** The Brown Creeper (*Certhia americana*), a cryptic, bark-brown Pacific Northwest songbird, forages by spiraling upward on a tree trunk from the base, methodically probing every bark crevice with its slender decurved bill, then flying down to the base of the next tree to climb and probe again (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Curiosity's methodical, layer-by-layer reading of Gale Crater and Mount Sharp.
- **A carnivorous plant draws sustenance from a barren place by reading what it traps.** The Cobra Lily / California Pitcher Plant (*Darlingtonia californica*), a carnivorous pitcher plant of nutrient-poor serpentine seeps in southwestern Oregon and northern California, traps insects in its hooded, cobra-like leaf and lets a symbiotic microbial community (with at least one of the plant's own enzymes) break them down to obtain the nutrients its mineral-poor ground cannot supply — a self-contained processing system that extracts sustenance where the substrate alone could not support it, a mirror of Curiosity's onboard chemical laboratories.
