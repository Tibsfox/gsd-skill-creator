1. **A sustain keeps the predecessor's axis vocabulary and replaces only the predecessor-specific content.**
   Curiosity shares the Mars-exploration vocabulary with the Mars Exploration Rover predecessor (Mars, the red planet, the surface, regolith, the atmosphere, dust, in situ, planetary exploration, past water, sedimentary rock, mudstone) — that vocabulary is shared and correct. The rewrite removed only the MER-specific content (Spirit, Opportunity, MER-A, MER-B, the twin-rover framing, Gusev Crater, Meridiani Planum, Eagle / Endurance / Victoria / Endeavour craters, jarosite, hematite "blueberries," goethite, opaline silica, Home Plate, Columbia Hills, Homestake, the Athena payload name, the Mössbauer spectrometer, the airbag landing as the EDL, the ~185 kg solar twin rovers, the ~45 km / first-off-Earth-marathon endurance-distance records, the first-in-situ-MINERAL-water-evidence anchor, and…

2. **A sustain has no axis rotation to frame — and 1.241 was itself a sustain, so there is no rotation framing to remove.**
   The Mars axis opened at 1.238 (Viking, substrate-axis rotation #36); 1.239 (Mariner 9), 1.240 (Mars Pathfinder), 1.241 (the Mars Exploration Rovers), and 1.242 (Curiosity) are all SUSTAINS, so the clone source carries no rotation narrative, and at most a single distant note records that the Mars axis itself opened at 1.238 by rotating from the X-ray-astronomy axis (rotation #35, held complete at obs#15). The X-ray-axis vocabulary does not appear.

3. **The sky crane is the new EDL, claimed as the first sky-crane landing and distinct from airbags and from Viking's soft landing.**
   Because the ~899 kg rover was too heavy for airbags, Curiosity landed by the sky crane — a rocket-powered descent stage that hovered and lowered the rover on tethers — the first sky-crane landing (reused by Perseverance in 2021). The catalog scopes it as "first sky-crane landing on Mars," never "the first soft landing on Mars" (Viking, obs#1), and never conflates it with the airbag bounces of Mars Pathfinder (obs#3) and the Mars Exploration Rovers (obs#4).
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12288_

4. **Habitability is not life detection and not a life search, and organics and methane are not evidence of life.**
   Curiosity assessed whether ancient Gale Crater could have supported microbial life and detected organic molecules and a methane background — but this is PAST habitability, not a detection of or search for life. Viking (obs#1) carried the in-situ life-detection experiments; the catalog states throughout that Curiosity did not search for or find life, that organic molecules are chemical building blocks and not life, and that the methane's source is debated and not attributed to life. Curiosity's habitability finding is distinct from the MERs' "liquid water once existed" (obs#4) and is not a re-stamp.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12289_

5. **Curiosity is NUCLEAR (MMRTG), not solar, and the APXS is "Alpha PARTICLE" (the same instrument class as the MERs).**
   Curiosity is powered by a radioisotope generator, the first nuclear-powered rover on another planet, distinct from the solar MERs; the Viking landers carried RTGs but were stationary landers, so the catalog scopes Curiosity as the first RTG-powered ROVER. Its APXS is the Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer, not Pathfinder's "Alpha Proton," and the catalog does not re-claim Pathfinder's mobile-rover rock-geochemistry first; Curiosity's instrument firsts are the onboard SAM and CheMin laboratories plus the ChemCam laser.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12290_

6. **The first onboard analytical laboratories are the distinct instrument first.**
   A drill delivers powdered rock to the CheMin X-ray diffractometer and the SAM gas-chemistry suite — the first rover to drill and ingest rock samples into onboard laboratories on another planet — together with the ChemCam laser (the first laser instrument on a planetary surface). The Mars Exploration Rovers (obs#4) were solar with arm-mounted contact instruments and a Rock Abrasion Tool but no sample-ingesting laboratory; Sojourner / Pathfinder (obs#3) had only the APXS on undisturbed surfaces.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12291_

7. **The longevity status is precise: still operating, approaching but NOT surpassing Opportunity's record.**
   Curiosity remains operational after roughly 14 years and is approaching Opportunity's ~14-year-136-day Mars surface-longevity record, but (as of 2026) has NOT yet surpassed it (Opportunity's mark, 2004–2019, still stands; Curiosity would not pass it until about 2027); the catalog never claims Curiosity is "the longest-operating Mars surface mission" or that it "surpassed Opportunity."
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12292_

8. **NASA / U.S.-mission framing is an accuracy requirement here.**
   The Mars Science Laboratory was a wholly NASA / United States mission, developed and operated by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, with no foreign-partner caveat (the APXS was a Canadian Space Agency / MDA contribution); the rover was named by Clara Ma via a NASA student essay contest in 2009.
