## What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived mission imagery.** The Curiosity shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual Mastcam panorama frames or a rover-on-the-surface image. A future revision could render a real Mastcam mosaic (a Gale Crater outcrop or the Mount Sharp slopes, publicly available from the Planetary Data System) for a higher-fidelity view.
- **The Curiosity diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of a car-sized six-wheel rocker-bogie rover with its mast and instrument arm and the drill against an outcrop.
- **The sky-crane arrival is depicted rather than driven by the real descent profile.** The sky-crane arrival is depicted in the shader but not rendered from the archived entry-descent-landing telemetry and the dated touchdown profile; a future ship could drive the arrival mode from the real descent profile and the 6 August 2012 touchdown for a quantitatively faithful landing.
- **The habitability chemistry is depicted rather than computed.** The water, biogenic-element, and energy signatures in the drilling-and-laboratories mode are depicted rather than rendered from the archived SAM and CheMin measurements; a future ship could drive the chemistry mode from the real spectra for a quantitatively faithful reading of the ancient habitable environment.

## Surprises

- **A car-sized rover was lowered onto Mars by a hovering rocket stage on tethers.** Because the ~899 kg rover was too heavy for airbags, Curiosity reached the surface by the sky crane — a rocket-powered descent stage that hovered on its retrorockets and lowered the rover to a wheels-down touchdown on a ~7.5 m bridle, then cut the tethers and flew off to crash a safe distance away — the first sky-crane landing, a new entry-descent-landing architecture later reused by Perseverance in 2021.
- **A rover drilled the rocks and judged an ancient lakebed habitable.** Within months of landing, at Yellowknife Bay, Curiosity drilled mudstone (the "John Klein" target) and found an ancient fluvio-lacustrine environment that was habitable — near-neutral, low-salinity liquid water, the biogenic elements, and chemical energy — conditions that could have supported microbial life, the first in-situ determination that ancient Mars once had a habitable environment (not a detection of life).
- **Onboard laboratories detected organic molecules in three-billion-year-old mudstone.** The SAM laboratory detected organic molecules preserved in roughly three-billion-year-old mudstone (chlorobenzene in 2014; thiophenes and aromatic and aliphatic organics in 2018) and measured a low background of atmospheric methane (~0.4 ppbv) with seasonal variation — chemical building blocks and a debated trace gas, neither attributed to life.
- **A nuclear rover keeps working through dust and seasons that limit solar rovers.** Curiosity is powered by an MMRTG (about 110 W from roughly 4.8 kg of plutonium-238 dioxide) — the first nuclear-powered rover on another planet — freeing it from the solar, dust, and seasonal limits that ended the solar Mars Exploration Rovers; it has operated more than a decade and continues to climb Mount Sharp.
- **A small songbird reads a tree trunk surface by surface.** The Brown Creeper (*Certhia americana*), a cryptic, bark-brown Pacific Northwest songbird, forages by spiraling upward on a tree trunk from the base, methodically probing every bark crevice with its slender decurved bill, then flying down to the base of the next tree to climb and probe again (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Curiosity's methodical, layer-by-layer reading of Gale Crater and Mount Sharp.
- **A carnivorous plant draws sustenance from a barren place by reading what it traps.** The Cobra Lily / California Pitcher Plant (*Darlingtonia californica*), a carnivorous pitcher plant of nutrient-poor serpentine seeps in southwestern Oregon and northern California, traps insects in its hooded, cobra-like leaf and lets a symbiotic microbial community (with at least one of the plant's own enzymes) break them down to obtain the nutrients its mineral-poor ground cannot supply — a self-contained processing system that extracts sustenance where the substrate alone could not support it, a mirror of Curiosity's onboard chemical laboratories.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A sustain keeps the predecessor's axis vocabulary and replaces only the predecessor-specific content.** Curiosity shares the Mars-exploration vocabulary with the Mars Exploration Rover predecessor (Mars, the red planet, the surface, regolith, the atmosphere, dust, in situ, planetary exploration, past water, sedimentary rock, mudstone) — that vocabulary is shared and correct. The rewrite removed only the MER-specific content (Spirit, Opportunity, MER-A, MER-B, the twin-rover framing, Gusev Crater, Meridiani Planum, Eagle / Endurance / Victoria / Endeavour craters, jarosite, hematite "blueberries," goethite, opaline silica, Home Plate, Columbia Hills, Homestake, the Athena payload name, the Mössbauer spectrometer, the airbag landing as the EDL, the ~185 kg solar twin rovers, the ~45 km / first-off-Earth-marathon endurance-distance records, the first-in-situ-MINERAL-water-evidence anchor, and the Wolverine + Common Juniper pairing) and replaced it with Mars Science Laboratory content.
2. **A sustain has no axis rotation to frame — and 1.241 was itself a sustain, so there is no rotation framing to remove.** The Mars axis opened at 1.238 (Viking, substrate-axis rotation #36); 1.239 (Mariner 9), 1.240 (Mars Pathfinder), 1.241 (the Mars Exploration Rovers), and 1.242 (Curiosity) are all SUSTAINS, so the clone source carries no rotation narrative, and at most a single distant note records that the Mars axis itself opened at 1.238 by rotating from the X-ray-astronomy axis (rotation #35, held complete at obs#15). The X-ray-axis vocabulary does not appear.
3. **The sky crane is the new EDL, claimed as the first sky-crane landing and distinct from airbags and from Viking's soft landing.** Because the ~899 kg rover was too heavy for airbags, Curiosity landed by the sky crane — a rocket-powered descent stage that hovered and lowered the rover on tethers — the first sky-crane landing (reused by Perseverance in 2021). The catalog scopes it as "first sky-crane landing on Mars," never "the first soft landing on Mars" (Viking, obs#1), and never conflates it with the airbag bounces of Mars Pathfinder (obs#3) and the Mars Exploration Rovers (obs#4).
4. **Habitability is not life detection and not a life search, and organics and methane are not evidence of life.** Curiosity assessed whether ancient Gale Crater could have supported microbial life and detected organic molecules and a methane background — but this is PAST habitability, not a detection of or search for life. Viking (obs#1) carried the in-situ life-detection experiments; the catalog states throughout that Curiosity did not search for or find life, that organic molecules are chemical building blocks and not life, and that the methane's source is debated and not attributed to life. Curiosity's habitability finding is distinct from the MERs' "liquid water once existed" (obs#4) and is not a re-stamp.
5. **Curiosity is NUCLEAR (MMRTG), not solar, and the APXS is "Alpha PARTICLE" (the same instrument class as the MERs).** Curiosity is powered by a radioisotope generator, the first nuclear-powered rover on another planet, distinct from the solar MERs; the Viking landers carried RTGs but were stationary landers, so the catalog scopes Curiosity as the first RTG-powered ROVER. Its APXS is the Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer, not Pathfinder's "Alpha Proton," and the catalog does not re-claim Pathfinder's mobile-rover rock-geochemistry first; Curiosity's instrument firsts are the onboard SAM and CheMin laboratories plus the ChemCam laser.
6. **The first onboard analytical laboratories are the distinct instrument first.** A drill delivers powdered rock to the CheMin X-ray diffractometer and the SAM gas-chemistry suite — the first rover to drill and ingest rock samples into onboard laboratories on another planet — together with the ChemCam laser (the first laser instrument on a planetary surface). The Mars Exploration Rovers (obs#4) were solar with arm-mounted contact instruments and a Rock Abrasion Tool but no sample-ingesting laboratory; Sojourner / Pathfinder (obs#3) had only the APXS on undisturbed surfaces.
7. **The longevity status is precise: still operating, approaching but NOT surpassing Opportunity's record.** Curiosity remains operational after roughly 14 years and is approaching Opportunity's ~14-year-136-day Mars surface-longevity record, but (as of 2026) has NOT yet surpassed it (Opportunity's mark, 2004–2019, still stands; Curiosity would not pass it until about 2027); the catalog never claims Curiosity is "the longest-operating Mars surface mission" or that it "surpassed Opportunity."
8. **NASA / U.S.-mission framing is an accuracy requirement here.** The Mars Science Laboratory was a wholly NASA / United States mission, developed and operated by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, with no foreign-partner caveat (the APXS was a Canadian Space Agency / MDA contribution); the rover was named by Clara Ma via a NASA student essay contest in 2009.
9. **A sustain references its predecessor exactly once, deliberately.** The only correct predecessor reference is a single nav-prev lineage note to v1.241 the Mars Exploration Rovers (Mars obs#4), plus lineage references to Mars Pathfinder (1.240, obs#3), Mariner 9 (1.239, obs#2), Viking (1.238, obs#1), and Mariner 4 (1.36, the first Mars flyby). The predecessors' anchors stay with them — 0 re-stamps.
10. **Clone source is the Mars Exploration Rovers (a NASA twin solar rover field-geology mission, Spirit and Opportunity, 2003–2019); Curiosity is a NASA nuclear habitability-assessment rover (Mars Science Laboratory, Atlas V 541, 2011–present).** The most structural replacement in the rewrite is removing ALL MER-specific content — "Spirit," "Opportunity," "MER-A," "MER-B," "the twin-rover framing," "Gusev Crater," "Meridiani Planum," "Eagle / Endurance / Victoria / Endeavour craters," "jarosite," "hematite blueberries," "goethite," "opaline silica," "Home Plate," "Columbia Hills," "Homestake," "the Athena payload name," "the Mössbauer spectrometer," "the airbag landing as the EDL," "the ~185 kg solar twin rovers," "the ~45 km / first-off-Earth-marathon endurance-distance records," "the first-in-situ-MINERAL-water-evidence anchor," and "the Wolverine + Common Juniper pairing" — and replacing with the Mars Science Laboratory and the rover Curiosity, the Clara Ma naming, the Atlas V 541 launch (26 November 2011) from SLC-41, Gale Crater and "Bradbury Landing" and the sky crane (6 August 2012), the MMRTG nuclear power, the car-sized ~899 kg rover, the 10-instrument payload (Mastcam, MAHLI, MARDI, ChemCam, APXS, CheMin, SAM, RAD, DAN, REMS) plus the drill, Yellowknife Bay and the habitable fluvio-lacustrine mudstone, the organic molecules and the methane, the "Hottah" streambed conglomerate, Mount Sharp / Aeolis Mons, the habitability goal, the still-operating status, NSSDCA/COSPAR 2011-070A, and the Brown Creeper + Cobra Lily pairing.
