**The Murmuration Phenomenon — Thousands Turning as One.** The **Dunlin murmuration** is one of the most spectacular natural phenomena in the Pacific Northwest. Winter Dunlin flocks on Puget Sound tidal flats can number 5,000 to 50,000+ individuals. The flocks form on mudflats during low tide and take to the air as coordinated masses. Each bird responds to the movements of its nearest 6-7 neighbors — there is no leader, and the coordination emerges from simple local rules applied simultaneously by thousands of individuals. The mathematical foundation is described in **Ballerini et al. 2008** (*PNAS*, "Interaction ruling animal collective behavior depends on topological rather than metric distance") and **Cavagna et al. 2010** (*PNAS*, scale-free correlations in starling flocks) — the topological-neighbor framework explains how coordination propagates across the flock at all scales. The result is a fluid, organic, pulsating shape that moves as one organism. The flock expands, contracts, splits, merges, elongates, compresses, twists, and turns. Shapes are unpredictable and mesmerizing: ribbons, spheres, pancakes, waterfalls, tornadoes. Shape transitions happen every fraction of a second. Dunlin in murmuration fly at **40-60 km/h**, with turns propagating through the flock as a wave moving faster than any individual bird — the turn wave travels at approximately **3x individual flight speed**, creating the illusion of a single organism composed of thousands of bodies.
