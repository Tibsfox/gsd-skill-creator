## Surprises

- **One satellite watches the air two ways at once — straight down and along the limb.** Aura reads the atmosphere with two complementary geometries: OMI looks straight down (nadir) across a wide ~2600 km swath, mapping the total column of ozone and the air-quality trace gases across the daylit globe each day, while MLS looks sideways along the limb — the thin bright edge of the atmosphere — sounding the vertical profile of the stratospheric composition. Two views of the same sky, one from above and one from the edge.
- **OMI reads ozone and the trace gases of air quality by the colours of the sky.** OMI is an ultraviolet–visible spectrometer (264–504 nm): it reads the total column of ozone and the air-quality trace gases — nitrogen dioxide, sulphur dioxide, formaldehyde, aerosols — and surface ultraviolet from the absorption signatures of those gases in the sunlight scattered back from the daylit Earth, turning the colours of the sky into a daily near-global map.
- **The same broad domain, two very different orbits and cadences.** Aura and its axis sibling TEMPO both observe atmospheric chemistry / air quality, but they read it very differently: Aura watches GLOBAL ozone and composition from LOW EARTH ORBIT, daily, while TEMPO watches HOURLY tropospheric air pollution over NORTH AMERICA from GEOSTATIONARY orbit. The same broad subject — the chemistry of the air — read from a polar low orbit in one case and from a fixed geostationary perch in the other.
- **A high-montane frog whose eggs sit in the sunlit pool under the shield of the sky.** The Cascades Frog (*Rana cascadae*), a high-elevation frog of the Pacific Northwest Cascades and Olympic Mountains, breeds in shallow, sunlit montane and subalpine pools and wet meadows; its exposed egg masses in clear high-altitude water make it sensitive to ultraviolet-B — the very radiation the ozone layer Aura watches screens out — a creature of the high bright air, native to the Cascades of Oregon and Washington and the Olympic Mountains (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A pink wet-meadow wildflower shaped like an elephant's head.** The Elephant's Head Lousewort (*Pedicularis groenlandica*), a pink-flowered wildflower of high Pacific Northwest wet meadows, fens, and pond margins, carries tiny flowers shaped like an elephant's head and trunk and grows in the same bright, open, high-altitude wet meadows where the Cascades Frog breeds — a true plant of the high bright meadow, native to PNW subalpine wet meadows (ecological description only; distinct from the other louseworts and the meadow heaths) — bound with the throughline of the high bright meadow under the shield of the sky.
