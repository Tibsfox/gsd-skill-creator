## Mission Overview

Aura is the obs#14 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's atmospheric-chemistry satellite, the third of the original Earth Observing System flagship trio (Terra, Aqua, Aura), the one that watches the air itself: the ozone layer that shields life from the Sun's ultraviolet, and the chemistry of the troposphere and stratosphere. ★ It is a NASA EOS mission managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center (GSFC), carrying four instruments: OMI (the Ozone Monitoring Instrument), a Dutch–Finnish (KNMI/FMI, with NIVR) nadir ultraviolet–visible spectrometer (264–504 nm) that maps ozone, nitrogen dioxide, sulphur dioxide, formaldehyde, aerosols, and surface ultraviolet across a ~2600 km swath for near-global daily coverage; MLS (the Microwave Limb Sounder), a JPL limb sounder of stratospheric composition (ozone, chlorine chemistry / ClO, water vapour); TES (the Tropospheric Emission Spectrometer), a JPL infrared spectrometer for tropospheric ozone and carbon monoxide; and HIRDLS (the High Resolution Dynamics Limb Sounder), a UK/US (Oxford / NERC + NCAR) infrared limb sounder. The satellite reads the air two ways at once: OMI looks straight down across a wide swath, mapping ozone and the trace gases of air quality across the daylit globe each day by ultraviolet–visible spectrometry, while MLS looks sideways along the limb, sounding the vertical composition of the stratosphere — ozone, the chlorine chemistry that drives ozone loss, and water vapour. The mission launches 15 July 2004 (10:01 UTC) on a Delta II 7920-10L from Vandenberg (SLC-2W), California, and reaches a sun-synchronous orbit at roughly 705 km altitude and ~98.2° inclination, joining the A-Train. The story is the Delta II launch from Vandenberg, the climb to the sun-synchronous A-Train orbit, OMI mapping the ozone and the trace gases across the daylit globe, MLS sounding the thin bright edge of the atmosphere for the stratospheric composition, and the two-decade record of the ozone layer and its slow recovery gathered from the A-Train. The science is central: OMI measures the total column of ozone and the air-quality trace gases (nitrogen dioxide, sulphur dioxide, formaldehyde, aerosols) and surface ultraviolet, while MLS measures the vertical profile of the stratospheric composition, and together they track the ozone layer that shields life from the Sun's ultraviolet — an essential part of the Earth system tied to atmospheric chemistry and the climate. ★ Two of the four instruments have ended: HIRDLS's chopper motor stalled 17 March 2008 (no science since), and TES was decommissioned 31 January 2018; those two individual instruments are described in the past, but OMI and MLS remain active. Aura's firsts are scoped carefully: OMI's daily-global, high-spatial-resolution mapping of ozone and air-quality trace gases by ultraviolet–visible spectrometry; MLS's microwave limb sounding of stratospheric composition; and the sustained two-decade record of the ozone layer and atmospheric composition from the A-Train. ★ The scope is load-bearing: ozone had been measured from space before Aura (TOMS on Nimbus-7, launched 1978), so it is NOT "the first to measure ozone from space"; OMI's advance is the daily-global high-resolution air-quality and ozone mapping, MLS's the stratospheric limb composition, and the value is the long continuous record. ★★★ Aura is OPERATING (launched 2004, OMI and MLS still active ~20+ years, expected to ~2028), so the catalog frames its whole story in the PRESENT (Aura orbits its sun-synchronous orbit, carries its instruments, measures and maps the ozone and the trace gases, sounds the stratospheric composition along the limb, and watches the ozone layer's slow recovery). ## Surprises

- **One satellite watches the air two ways at once — straight down and along the limb.** Aura reads the atmosphere with two complementary geometries: OMI looks straight down (nadir) across a wide ~2600 km swath, mapping the total column of ozone and the air-quality trace gases across the daylit globe each day, while MLS looks sideways along the limb — the thin bright edge of the atmosphere — sounding the vertical profile of the stratospheric composition. Two views of the same sky, one from above and one from the edge.
- **OMI reads ozone and the trace gases of air quality by the colours of the sky.** OMI is an ultraviolet–visible spectrometer (264–504 nm): it reads the total column of ozone and the air-quality trace gases — nitrogen dioxide, sulphur dioxide, formaldehyde, aerosols — and surface ultraviolet from the absorption signatures of those gases in the sunlight scattered back from the daylit Earth, turning the colours of the sky into a daily near-global map.
- **The same broad domain, two very different orbits and cadences.** Aura and its axis sibling TEMPO both observe atmospheric chemistry / air quality, but they read it very differently: Aura watches GLOBAL ozone and composition from LOW EARTH ORBIT, daily, while TEMPO watches HOURLY tropospheric air pollution over NORTH AMERICA from GEOSTATIONARY orbit. The same broad subject — the chemistry of the air — read from a polar low orbit in one case and from a fixed geostationary perch in the other.
- **A high-montane frog whose eggs sit in the sunlit pool under the shield of the sky.** The Cascades Frog (*Rana cascadae*), a high-elevation frog of the Pacific Northwest Cascades and Olympic Mountains, breeds in shallow, sunlit montane and subalpine pools and wet meadows; its exposed egg masses in clear high-altitude water make it sensitive to ultraviolet-B — the very radiation the ozone layer Aura watches screens out — a creature of the high bright air, native to the Cascades of Oregon and Washington and the Olympic Mountains (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A pink wet-meadow wildflower shaped like an elephant's head.** The Elephant's Head Lousewort (*Pedicularis groenlandica*), a pink-flowered wildflower of high Pacific Northwest wet meadows, fens, and pond margins, carries tiny flowers shaped like an elephant's head and trunk and grows in the same bright, open, high-altitude wet meadows where the Cascades Frog breeds — a true plant of the high bright meadow, native to PNW subalpine wet meadows (ecological description only; distinct from the other louseworts and the meadow heaths) — bound with the throughline of the high bright meadow under the shield of the sky.

## Dedication

v1.294 Aura is dedicated to the people at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, at KNMI and the Finnish Meteorological Institute, and at Oxford and the National Center for Atmospheric Research, who built and fly the satellite that watches the air itself. The teams who built four instruments to read the sky — one that looks straight down across a wide swath and maps the ozone and the trace gases of the air by the colours of sunlight scattered back from the daylit Earth, and one that looks sideways along the thin bright edge of the atmosphere and sounds the composition of the stratosphere — and tuned them so well that the whole globe's ozone is mapped each day and the recovery of the ozone layer can be watched year after year. The crews who carried it aloft on a Delta II from Vandenberg into its sun-synchronous orbit. The people who keep the watch over the shield of the air, two decades on, and the scientists who read the slow healing of the ozone layer under the Montreal Protocol. The satellite is still watching, and that record of the air belongs to them.
