## Summary

**Thunderpussy** arrives at E=7 -- the Seattle hard rock band formed in 2013, entirely female lineup, and the first all-female band in the engine's S36 sequence. Molly Sides (vocals), Whitney Petty (guitar), Leah Julius (bass), and Ruby Dunphy (drums) constitute a band that plays in the tradition of classic hard rock -- Led Zeppelin, Heart, early Aerosmith -- with an unapologetic commitment to volume and stage presence. Thunderpussy self-titled debut (2018) on Easy Eye Sound and *Velvet Rut* (2022) establish a sound that is guitar-forward, blues-rooted, and loud. They are not grunge, not garage -- they are straight-line hard rock from Seattle, picking up a thread that goes back through Heart (documented at degrees 234-235) and forward into a contemporary practice.

The engine has passed through all-male bands for the majority of its S36 sequence. The first all-female entry arriving at degree 248 is not a programmatic achievement -- it is the consequence of the Seattle music canon's documented composition. Thunderpussy earns the entry on the music alone: E=7 is accurate for a band that plays at volume with technical precision and no concession to the trend toward quieter production that dominated 2010s rock. Their live performances are consistently described as one of the best in the current Seattle scene. The historical notation -- first all-female band -- is accurate and belongs in the record.

On the soundscape side, **Hoh Rain on Maple** closes the hydrological cycle arc with the **FIRST METEOROLOGY entry in the engine's SPS history**. The Sound of Puget Sound track has documented fish, birds, mammals, invertebrates, and now abiotic landscape sounds -- but degree 248 marks the first time the SPS entry is a weather event rather than a landscape feature. Rain on a bigleaf maple leaf produces a **patter-drip** sound: the initial impact of the raindrop on the leaf surface, the secondary drip from the leaf edge to the ground, and the variation in timing that creates an irregular but dense percussion. In the Hoh Rain Forest, where annual precipitation exceeds 140 inches, this sound is nearly continuous for months at a time.

The bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*) is the dominant understory broadleaf tree in the Hoh Rain Forest and across the Olympic Peninsula. Its leaves can reach 12 inches across -- among the largest simple leaves of any North American tree -- and they support dense communities of epiphytic mosses and ferns. A Hoh maple in full moss cover, receiving rain, produces a layered sound: the rain on the outer moss surface, the rain on the bare leaf beneath, and the drip lines from the leaf edges and from the moss itself. The E=4 rating reflects the intimacy of the sound -- rain on a maple is not loud; it is pervasive, continuous, and close.
