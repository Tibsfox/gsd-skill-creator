## Summary

**Botch** arrives at E=8 -- the engine's maximum, tied by only a small number of entries in the full 360-degree sequence -- for the Tacoma post-hardcore quartet that released *We Are the Romans* in 1999 and permanently altered what a heavy guitar band could do with time signature, dissonance, and structural expectation. Botch formed in Tacoma in 1993 with Brian Cook (bass), Tim Latona (drums), David Knott (guitar), and Dave Verellen (vocals), released two full-length records -- *American Nervoso* (1998) on Excursion Records and *We Are the Romans* (1999) on Hydra Head Records -- and broke up in 2002 at the moment when their influence was just beginning to propagate forward through the underground. They reunited in 2022 and released *...And We Understood Reaching* (2023), which confirmed that the band's relationship to time and tension had not diminished.

*We Are the Romans* is the record. It is the post-hardcore record that every subsequent generation of heavy music fans discovers and measures other records against. "To Our Friends in the Great White North," "Man the Ramparts," "Frequency Ass Bandit," "Swimming the Channel vs. Driving It" -- these are the titles, and the music inside them is a sustained argument about what precision and aggression can do together. Botch's relationship to time signature is not mathematical in the way that is sometimes attributed to progressive music -- it is physical, bodily, felt before it is counted. The odd meters and the irregular phrase lengths in *We Are the Romans* do not feel like puzzles; they feel like attacks.

E=8 at degree 254 is a critical assignment for two reasons: it ties the engine maximum, and it marks **Botch's placement on Hydra Head Records**, the Boston-based independent that was the primary institutional force behind American post-hardcore and sludge in the late 1990s and 2000s. Hydra Head released Botch, Cave In, Isis, Pelican, Converge, and Boris, among others -- it was the label that held the genre together when the genre was being defined. Botch on Hydra Head is not incidental context; it is the specific institutional moment that made *We Are the Romans* the record it became.

On the soundscape side, **Fog Horn at Admiralty Inlet** arrives at E=3 -- and it is the **FIRST MARITIME SOUND** in the SPS track's history. Admiralty Inlet is the northern entrance to Puget Sound proper, the 11-mile-wide strait between Whidbey Island and the Kitsap Peninsula where the Sound connects to the Strait of Juan de Fuca. The fog horn at Admiralty Inlet -- the horn at Point Wilson Light, at the northeastern tip of the Olympic Peninsula, at the entrance to the inlet -- is one of the acoustic landmarks of Washington State maritime navigation. Its periodic moan is a signaling device: it fires in a fixed pattern, typically every 30 seconds, as a warning to vessels operating in reduced visibility. The sound carries for miles over open water. E=3 reflects the moderate-but-distinctive energy of this manufactured acoustic event: it is not natural, but it has become part of the Sound's acoustic identity over more than a century of operation.

The FIRST MARITIME entry in the SPS track marks a categorical expansion: the engine has documented rivers, waterfalls, rain, wind, tidal surges, and now enters the maritime register -- the sounds produced by human navigation infrastructure operating in the Pacific Northwest marine environment. The fog horn is the first of these. It will not be the last.
