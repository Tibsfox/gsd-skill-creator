When Jones plays The Triple Door, he is not discovering Seattle. He is visiting a city that was shaped, in part, by the same tradition he helped build on McLemore Avenue.

Booker T. Jones -- the architect of the Memphis Sound. Leader of Booker T. & the M.G.'s, the house band at Stax Records, the rhythm section that played on hundreds of sessions for Otis Redding, Sam & Dave, Albert King, Wilson Pickett, Carla Thomas, Eddie Floyd, The Staple Singers. "Green Onions" (1962), recorded in a single take during downtime between sessions, No. 3 on the Billboard Hot 100, No. 1 on the R&B chart, one of the most recognizable instrumentals in the history of American music. Jones was eighteen years old. The M.G.'s were two Black musicians (Jones and Al Jackson Jr.) and two white musicians (Steve Cropper and later Donald "Duck" Dunn), performing as an integrated band in 1962 Memphis, where Jim Crow was the law and lunch counters were segregated. "When you walked in the door at Stax, there was absolutely no color." Stax operated a studio where Black and white musicians worked side by side as equals, quietly, in a converted movie theater on McLemore Avenue, while the rest of Memphis enforced separation. Jones was a tenth-grade high school student playing baritone saxophone on Stax sessions. His classmate and best friend was Maurice White, who would go on to found Earth, Wind & Fire. His schoolmate David Porter would become Isaac Hayes's songwriting partner. Three teenagers in the same building at Booker T. Washington High School who would collectively reshape American popular music across multiple genres. Prodigy is an understatement.

Rock and Roll Hall of Fame (1992). Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award (2007). Grammy for Best Instrumental Album (*Potato Hole*, 2010). Producer of Bill Withers's *Just as I Am* (contains "Ain't No Sunshine") and Willie Nelson's *Stardust* (the biggest-selling album of Nelson's career -- a Memphis soul organist producing a country outlaw's pop standards album, recorded in ten days in Malibu). Multi-instrumentalist: Hammond B-3 organ, piano, guitar, saxophone, trombone, oboe, double bass. Bachelor of Music Education from Indiana University, senior recital on trombone. Not an organist who happens to play other instruments. A formally trained musician who happens to be most famous for the organ.

Of the classic M.G.'s, only Booker T. Jones survives. When he tours now, he carries the entire institutional memory of the band, the label, and the era. ### Part A: Booker T. Jones (Seattle Shows) -- THE TOURING ANCHOR (E=5 Institutional Tidal, R&B/Soul Returns Genre Stage 20, The Congregational Organ, The Gospel Swell, Stax Records Enters Label Ecosystem, First Non-Residential Artist, The Last M.G. Standing, Hammond B-3 Breaks Stratocaster Thread)

Full deep research covering Booker T. Jones as the first non-residential artist in the engine, the architect of the Memphis Sound, leader of Booker T. & the M.G.'s, Stax Records house band leader, multi-instrumentalist and producer, whose connection to Seattle is through repeated PNW performances across decades:

- **BOOKER TALIAFERRO JONES JR. -- BORN NOVEMBER 12, 1944, MEMPHIS, TENNESSEE, Soulsville Neighborhood, Father a Science Teacher, Prodigy on Multiple Instruments, Church Organ as Origin Point:** Grew up on Ethel Avenue in South Memphis in the neighborhood now known as Soulsville. Musically omnivorous from childhood -- learned saxophone, trombone, oboe, double bass, and piano at school, played organ at his church. The church organ is the origin point -- the Hammond B-3 that would define his career was first encountered in a house of worship. The gospel tradition that runs through all Southern soul music ran through Jones's hands before he was a teenager. At Booker T. Washington High School, classmate and best friend was Maurice White (founder of Earth, Wind & Fire), schoolmate David Porter (Isaac Hayes's songwriting partner). Three teenagers who would collectively reshape American popular music, all in the same building at the same time.

- **STAX RECORDS -- THE STUDIO ON McLEMORE AVENUE, 926 East McLemore, Converted Movie Theater, "When You Walked In the Door at Stax, There Was Absolutely No Color":** Jones's professional career began at sixteen in 1960 as a staff musician at Satellite Records (renamed Stax). Founded by siblings Jim Stewart and Estelle Axton (Stewart + Axton = Stax). Operated out of a converted movie theater whose sloped floor created unusual acoustics -- the room itself was an instrument. What made Stax different from Motown (assembly-line production) and Atlantic (New York sophistication): arrangements created on the studio floor, not pre-written on charts. Musicians listened to each other and built grooves collectively, in real time. The sound was rawer, more spontaneous, more gospel-rooted. And it was integrated. In 1962 Memphis, where Jim Crow was the law, Stax Records operated a studio where Black and white musicians worked side by side as equals. A daily practice conducted quietly while the rest of Memphis enforced separation.

- **BOOKER T. & THE M.G.'S -- THE BAND THAT BUILT THE SOUND, "Green Onions" (1962) Recorded in a Single Take During Session Downtime, No. 3 Pop / No. 1 R&B, Two Black Musicians and Two White Musicians in 1962 Memphis:** Summer 1962. Four musicians backing a session: seventeen-year-old keyboardist Jones, twenty-year-old guitarist Steve Cropper, bassist Lewie Steinberg, drummer Al Jackson Jr. During downtime, Jones noodled a bluesy organ riff. Jim Stewart told them to keep playing. Recorded in a single take. "Green Onions" -- one of the most recognizable instrumentals in American music history. An integrated band in 1962 Memphis. Audiences were segregated even when the band wasn't: "The black people thought it was a black band and the white people thought it was a white band." Records didn't come with photographs. The music was racially ambiguous. The integration was hidden in plain sight, audible but invisible.

- **THE HOUSE BAND: BUILDING OTHER PEOPLE'S MASTERPIECES -- Otis Redding, Sam & Dave, Albert King, Wilson Pickett, Carla Thomas, Eddie Floyd, The Staple Singers, Isaac Hayes:** The M.G.'s were the engine room of Stax Records. Not just a recording act -- the house band that provided the rhythmic and harmonic foundation for some of the most important soul recordings ever made. Cropper co-wrote "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay" with Otis Redding. Jones's Hammond B-3 was the harmonic glue. Jackson's drumming earned him the name "The Human Timekeeper." Duck Dunn's syncopated bass defined the bottom end of Memphis soul. The session-musician-as-architect paradigm: the M.G.'s co-created the sound but the credit went to the artists out front. Jones was an architect, not a frontman. He built the rooms that other people lived in.

- **THE CLASSIC LINEUP AND ITS INSTRUMENTS -- Jones (Hammond B-3), Cropper (Fender Telecaster), Dunn (Fender Precision Bass), Jackson (Drums "The Human Timekeeper"):** Duck Dunn replaced Lewie Steinberg in 1965. Dunn brought syncopated, locked-in grooves that meshed tightly with Jackson's drums. The classic four became one of the tightest rhythm sections in the history of recorded music. Their economy was legendary -- they played exactly what the song needed and nothing more. The spaces between the notes were as important as the notes themselves. Key M.G.'s recordings: "Green Onions" (1962), "Hip Hug-Her" (1967), "Soul Limbo" (1968, later BBC cricket theme), "Time Is Tight" (1969), "Mrs. Robinson" (1969, cross-genre instrumental cover), "Melting Pot" (1971, the last great M.G.'s track).

- **THE LOSSES -- Al Jackson Jr. (1975), Donald "Duck" Dunn (2012), Steve Cropper (2025), Jones the Last M.G. Standing:** Jackson shot and killed at thirty-nine, October 1, 1975. Dunn died on tour in Tokyo at seventy, May 13, 2012. Cropper died in Nashville at eighty-four, December 3, 2025. The attrition is total. Jones is the sole surviving member of the classic lineup. Every Hammond B-3 chord he plays at The Triple Door is a transmission from a world that no longer exists except through him. The last M.G. standing.

- **SOLO CAREER AND PRODUCTION -- Bill Withers "Just as I Am" (1971), Willie Nelson "Stardust" (1978), "Potato Hole" Grammy (2010), Memoir "Time Is Tight" (2019):** Production credits that span genres and decades. Produced Withers's debut (contains "Ain't No Sunshine"). Produced Nelson's *Stardust* in ten days in Malibu -- they were neighbors -- ten years on Billboard's country albums chart, Nelson's biggest seller. A Memphis soul organist producing a country outlaw's pop standards album. The genre boundary-crossing is total. Grammy for Best Instrumental Album for *Potato Hole* (2009, with the Drive-By Truckers and Neil Young) demonstrates Jones remained vital nearly fifty years after "Green Onions." Bachelor of Music Education from Indiana University, senior recital on trombone. The distance between a trombone recital at Indiana University and a Hammond B-3 session at Stax Records is the distance between academic music and the street, and Jones inhabits both. Memoir *Time Is Tight: My Life, Note by Note* (2019) provides first-person testimony about the daily reality of integration at Stax.

- **THE SEATTLE CONNECTION: THE TOURING ANCHOR -- The Triple Door, Kirkland Performance Center, Mount Baker Theatre, Decades of PNW Performances, The I-5 Corridor:** Documented performances: The Triple Door April 28-29, 2022; Kirkland Performance Center April 11, 2024; Triple Door October 3-4, 2025; Mount Baker Theatre Bellingham October 2, 2025. An intimate theater where 300 people sit at tables and listen. The house-band model inverted: the anonymous session musician is now the featured performer where every note is audible, every gesture visible. Touring pattern encompasses the full I-5 corridor. Typical setlist: "Green Onions," "Time Is Tight," "Hip Hug-Her," "Soul Limbo," "Melting Pot," solo material. A museum tour conducted by the artist who built the museum.

- **MEMPHIS SOUL AND SEATTLE SOUL: THE PARALLEL TRADITIONS -- Central District as Seattle's Soulsville, Jackson Street Corridor, Wheedle's Groove, KYAC, Stax Influence Flowing Through 45 RPM Singles:** Memphis and Seattle developed parallel Black music scenes in the mid-twentieth century. Seattle's Central District was the PNW's equivalent of Memphis's Beale Street. The Jackson Street corridor clubs of the 1940s-50s hosted jazz, blues, and R&B in a concentrated geographic zone. The *Wheedle's Groove* project (Light in the Attic Records, 2004) excavated Seattle's forgotten soul and funk scene -- Black on White Affair, Cold Bold and Together, the Overton Berry Trio, dozens of others who played CD clubs, recorded on local labels, heard on KYAC. The CD musicians of the 1960s-70s were listening to Stax records. The influence flowed through the same channels everywhere: 45 RPM singles, AM radio, jukebox play. But in Seattle, the influence landed in a neighborhood that already had its own musical infrastructure, clubs, radio station, audience. The Memphis sound enriched Seattle's sound. It did not replace it.

- **STAX IN THE ENGINE'S LABEL ECOSYSTEM -- Atlantic (30) and Stax (38) Connected Through Historical Distribution Deal 1960-1968:** Atlantic distributed Stax nationally from 1960 to 1968 under a deal negotiated by Jerry Wexler. When that deal ended -- badly, with Atlantic retaining all Stax masters -- Stax lost its back catalog. Ray Charles (degree 30) recorded for Atlantic. Booker T. Jones (degree 38) recorded for Stax. The two labels that defined the soul music era are now both in the engine, connected through the distribution deal that linked them historically.

- **THE TOURING ANCHOR -- THIRTY-NINTH ARTIST-CITY PATTERN, First Non-Residential Pattern, The Artist From Another City Whose Repeated Performances Create an Ongoing Gravitational Relationship:** The Touring Anchor does not relocate. - **THE CONGREGATIONAL ORGAN -- THIRTY-NINTH ACOUSTIC ELEMENT (Artist-Side), Hammond B-3 with Leslie Speaker, Gospel-Rooted, Warm Swirl, Breaks the Three-Degree Stratocaster Thread (35-37):** The Hammond B-3 organ as played by Booker T. Jones. Timbral roots in the Black church, translated into secular instrumental music without losing its spiritual resonance. The rotating Leslie speaker produces a warm, swirling tone -- the vibrato is physical, the sound literally moves through space. Jones's approach defined by economy and groove. He does not play fast. He does not play loud. He plays with precision. The congregational organ is the first keyboard-based element. The Stratocaster thread (35-36-37) is broken. Guitar yields to organ. Six strings yield to a rotating speaker cabinet. Blues-rock tradition yields to the soul tradition. A new timbral family enters.

- **THE GOSPEL SWELL -- THIRTY-EIGHTH BLUE NOTE DEPLOYMENT, The Blue Note Embedded in Organ Timbre, Leslie Speaker Doppler Wobble, Flatted Third Between Church and Club:** On the organ, the blue note is not bent through string tension or vocal inflection. It is approached through chord voicing and the Leslie speaker's physical rotation. Jones voices minor-to-major ambiguity in his chord choices. The Leslie adds a physical dimension: as the sound rotates, the pitch wobbles due to the Doppler effect, creating natural vibrato that makes every note slightly blue. The gospel swell is the blue note as it exists in the Black church -- the warm, communal wash of an organ chord hovering between sadness and joy, between the secular and the sacred. Structurally distinct from three consecutive guitar-based deployments (35-36-37: articulate sting, screaming bend, grunge bend). A parallel lineage, not a successor.

### Part B: Black-throated Gray Warbler -- THE DRAWL (Parulidae Returns After 18-Degree Gap, Sixth Parulidae Takes Family Lead, Setophaga Pair Contrast Yellow 16 vs BTG 38, Oak-Pine First Dry Woodland, Garry Oak Critically Endangered, Yellow Loral Spot, Quintet Closed -- New Chapter Opens, E=4 Deceleration After Hummingbird Trio)

Full deep research covering the Black-throated Gray Warbler as the sixth Parulidae, returning after the longest family absence in the engine (18 degrees), introducing the first dry woodland ecosystem, the drawl as the thirty-ninth acoustic element, and the *Setophaga* pair contrast with the Yellow Warbler:

- **SETOPHAGA NIGRESCENS -- "THE DARKENING MOTH-EATER," Townsend 1837, *Nigrescens* as Present Participle, a Verb Form Not an Adjective, the Bird That Is Becoming:** *Setophaga* from Ancient Greek *ses* (moth) + *phagos* (eater) = "moth-eater." *Nigrescens* from Latin *nigrescere* = "to become black" or "to darken." Not "black" (*niger*) but "blackening" (*nigrescens*) -- the present participle catches the bird mid-transformation. The species epithet describes an ongoing process rather than a static feature. The first warbler whose name contains motion. Compare: MacGillivray's (person), Wilson's (person), Yellow (physical description), Yellowthroat (morphology), Orange-crowned (concealment), Black-throated Gray (process). Formerly *Dendroica nigrescens* before the 2011 AOS merger of *Dendroica* into *Setophaga* based on molecular phylogenetics.

- **PARULIDAE RETURNS -- 18-DEGREE GAP, The Longest Family Absence Followed by a Return, Degrees 20-38, Eight Families Explored During Warbler Silence, Quintet Was a Chapter Not the Whole Book:** The last warbler sang at degree 20 -- the Orange-crowned Warbler's descending trill evaporating into silence. Between degree 20 and degree 38, the engine explored flickers, grosbeaks, tanagers, robins, swallows, waxwings, finches, crossbills, and hummingbirds. Eight families. Eighteen degrees. No other family has been gone this long and come back. Parulidae has approximately 115 species in North America. Five was never going to be the end. The quintet closed at degree 20. What opens at degree 38 is a broader Parulidae expansion beyond the quintet's structural completeness.

- **THE WARBLER SEXTET -- Five E=3 Wet-Side Warblers Plus One E=4 Dry-Side Warbler, Clean Break from Quintet:** MacGillivray's (14, churry-churry-sweet, narrative, E=3, understory thicket), Wilson's (15, chi-chi-chi-chi-chet, urgency, E=3, riparian willow), Yellow (16, sweet-sweet-sweeter, brightness, E=3, riparian willow), Common Yellowthroat (17, witchety-witchety, catchiness, E=3, freshwater marsh), Orange-crowned (20, trilling descending rattle, release, E=3, shrubby slope), Black-throated Gray (38, lazy buzzy wheeze, the drawl, E=4, oak-pine). The quintet was a wet-side E=3 phenomenon. The sixth warbler is a dry-side E=4 bird. The quintet sang with clean or thin tones. The Black-throated Gray buzzes with rough timbre. The break is clean: the sixth warbler is not quintet-plus-one. It is something else.

- **THE *SETOPHAGA* PAIR: DEGREES 16 AND 38 -- Same Genus, Opposite Aesthetics, The Highlighter Pen and the Ink Drawing:** Yellow Warbler (*S. petechia*) = brightest PNW warbler, vivid yellow, ascending crescendo, E=3, riparian. Black-throated Gray (*S. nigrescens*) = starkest PNW warbler, black-and-white, lazy buzzy wheeze, E=4, dry woodland. Twenty-two degrees apart. The genus *Setophaga* contains both the canvas filled with color and the canvas left bare with one brushstroke of warmth. The largest New World warbler genus (~34 species) now has two representatives. First *Setophaga* recurrence. There will certainly be more.

- **THE SOUND: LAZY BUZZY WHEEZE SONG -- *Weazy Weazy Weazy Wee-zy*, 4-5 Buzzy Phrases, Unhurried, the Laziest Warbler in the Engine:** Typically transcribed as *weazy weazy weazy wee-zy* or *zeedle zeedle zeedle zee*. First 3-4 phrases on the same pitch, final phrase rising or falling slightly. Duration 1.5-2.5 seconds. Tempo slow for a warbler. Timbre buzzy, husky, wheezy -- rough, insect-like quality. Not the clean pure-toned notes of the Yellow Warbler or the thin trill of the Orange-crowned. The song sits in the oak canopy like a summer afternoon. Calling note: a distinctive *tup* or *tchep*, flatter and duller than many warbler chips. The word "lazy" is not pejorative -- in open woodland with moderate ambient noise, a buzzy quality carries well through vegetation because buzz contains energy across a broader frequency band than a pure tone. The laziness is efficient.

- **OAK-PINE: THE ENGINE'S FIRST DRY WOODLAND -- Garry Oak (Only Native PNW Oak), Ponderosa Pine, Open Savanna, Rain Shadow Side, < 10% of Historical Range Remains:** Garry oak (*Quercus garryana*) ranges from southern British Columbia through Washington and Oregon to northern California. The PNW's only native oak species. Less than 10% of historical Garry oak woodland remains intact. Threats: fire suppression (Douglas fir overtops and shades out oaks), urban development, agricultural conversion, invasive species (Scotch broom, Himalayan blackberry). Oak-pine woodland is dry, sun-drenched, open canopy, sparse understory, bunchgrass and wildflowers. The rain shadow landscape. The PNW is defined in popular imagination by rain and dense conifers. But the region contains significant dry woodland, and the Black-throated Gray Warbler is one of its signature species. PNW distribution: south Puget Sound lowlands (Lewis-McChord, Mima Mounds), San Juan Islands, eastern Cascades foothills, Willamette Valley, southern Oregon. Locally common in appropriate habitat but uncommon in wet west-side forests.

- **PLUMAGE: THE INK DRAWING -- Black Crown, White Supercilium, Black Throat, Black Cheek Patch, Gray Back, White Wing Bars, and One Yellow Loral Spot:** Graphic contrast. No color except one small yellow patch between eye and bill -- the diagnostic field mark. The Yellow Warbler (16) was entirely yellow. The Black-throated Gray is entirely achromatic except for one dot. The genus pair maps the extremes of warbler color: maximum yellow (*S. petechia*) and minimal yellow (*S. nigrescens*, one small spot). Connects to the hidden crown of the Orange-crowned Warbler (degree 20) -- both challenge the assumption that a field mark should be conspicuous. Orange-crowned hides it. Black-throated Gray shrinks it. Two strategies of minimal color.

- **ECOLOGY: THE DRY WOODLAND SPECIALIST -- Mid-Canopy Gleaner, Unhurried Foraging, Short- to Medium-Distance Migrant to Western Mexico:** Forages by gleaning from leaves and branches at mid-canopy height (5-15 m). Method: systematic, hopping along branches, peering at leaf surfaces. Prey: insects, caterpillars, spiders. The foraging style matches the song: unhurried, methodical, deliberate. Nests in oaks or pines at 2-10 m, open cup of plant fibers. Short- to medium-distance migrant: breeding range from southern BC through California, wintering in western Mexico. Migration among the shortest of the engine's warblers -- shifts latitude within the same biome: dry woodland in summer, dry woodland in winter. Arrives PNW late April to mid-May, departs August-September.

- **CONSERVATION: LEAST CONCERN BUT HABITAT DECLINING -- The Bird Is Secure But Its Best Habitat Is Disappearing:** IUCN Least Concern. Population stable to slightly declining. The paradox: the species persists because it uses a range of dry woodland types, not just Garry oak. But in the PNW, loss of Garry oak woodland reduces available breeding habitat. Fire suppression, urban development, invasives, agricultural conversion. The conservation story is about habitat, not species. The Black-throated Gray Warbler is an indicator of oak woodland health. Where the warbler sings, the oaks are still standing.

- **CROSS-REFERENCES:** SPS-020 (Orange-crowned Warbler -- Parulidae returns, 18-degree gap, opposite in ecosystem wet slope vs dry woodland, tempo fast trill vs slow drawl, timbre thin vs buzzy, quintet closer vs sextet opener); SPS-016 (Yellow Warbler -- *Setophaga* pair, same genus opposite aesthetics, brightest warbler vs least colorful, ascending crescendo vs lazy buzzy wheeze, 22-degree gap between genus recurrences); SPS-014 (MacGillivray's Warbler -- first warbler vs sixth warbler, wet understory skulker vs dry canopy singer, Parulidae spans both); SPS-037 (Calliope Hummingbird -- order change Apodiformes to Passeriformes, three consecutive hummingbirds end, mechanical/threadlike sounds give way to vocal buzzy warbler, 3.1g to ~8g, mountain meadow to oak-pine); SPS-032 (Bohemian Waxwing -- buzz connection, both buzzy sounds, social buzz E=5 hundreds of birds vs solitary drawl E=4 one bird in an oak); SPS-033 (Cassin's Finch -- dry-side birds, montane ponderosa vs lowland oak-pine, engine's dry woodland belt spans from lowland oaks to mountain pines); SPS-034 (Red Crossbill -- canopy foragers, crossbill in conifers extracting seeds vs warbler in oaks gleaning insects, same elevation zone different foraging guilds).

### Parallel: Booker T. Jones x Black-throated Gray Warbler

The Part A/Part B resonance at degree 38 operates on four axes:
