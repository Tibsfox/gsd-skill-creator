The visiting dignitary and the darkening moth-eater. Degree 38 introduces the first artist in the engine who is explicitly framed as a visitor -- not born in Seattle, not raised in Seattle, not a resident. Booker T. Jones is from Memphis, Tennessee, born November 12, 1944, in the Soulsville neighborhood that would become the epicenter of Southern soul music. His connection to Seattle is gravitational, not biographical: repeated performances at The Triple Door, Kirkland Performance Center, and Mount Baker Theatre across decades of touring the Pacific Northwest corridor. The CSV reads "Booker T. Jones (Seattle shows)" -- the parenthetical is the tell. The engine has mapped thirty-seven degrees of Seattle-rooted or Seattle-adjacent artists. Degree 38 maps an artist whose tradition -- Memphis soul, Stax Records, the M.G.'s -- forms a direct ancestral line to the R&B and soul music that developed independently in Seattle's Central District. When Jones plays The Triple Door, he is not discovering Seattle. He is visiting a city that was shaped, in part, by the same tradition he helped build on McLemore Avenue.

Booker T. Jones -- the architect of the Memphis Sound. Leader of Booker T. & the M.G.'s, the house band at Stax Records, the rhythm section that played on hundreds of sessions for Otis Redding, Sam & Dave, Albert King, Wilson Pickett, Carla Thomas, Eddie Floyd, and The Staple Singers. "Green Onions" (1962), recorded in a single take during downtime between sessions at 926 East McLemore Avenue, No. 3 on the Billboard Hot 100, No. 1 on the R&B chart, one of the most recognizable instrumentals in the history of American music. Jones was seventeen years old. The M.G.'s were two Black musicians (Jones and drummer Al Jackson Jr.) and two white musicians (guitarist Steve Cropper and, from 1965, bassist Donald "Duck" Dunn replacing Lewie Steinberg), performing as an integrated band in 1962 Memphis, where Jim Crow was the law and lunch counters were segregated. "When you walked in the door at Stax, there was absolutely no color." Stax operated a studio where Black and white musicians worked side by side as equals, quietly, in a converted movie theater whose sloped floor created unusual acoustics that made the room itself an instrument, while the rest of Memphis enforced separation. Jones was still in tenth grade at Booker T. Washington High School, playing baritone saxophone on Stax sessions at sixteen. His classmate and best friend was Maurice White, who would go on to found Earth, Wind & Fire. His schoolmate David Porter would become Isaac Hayes's songwriting partner. Three teenagers in the same building who would collectively reshape American popular music across multiple genres. Prodigy is an understatement.

Rock and Roll Hall of Fame (1992). Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award (2007). Grammy for Best Instrumental Album (*Potato Hole*, 2010, with the Drive-By Truckers and Neil Young). Producer of Bill Withers's *Just as I Am* (1971, contains "Ain't No Sunshine") and Willie Nelson's *Stardust* (1978 -- ten days in Malibu, neighbors at the time, ten years on Billboard's country albums chart, the biggest-selling album of Nelson's career, a Memphis soul organist producing a country outlaw's pop standards album). Multi-instrumentalist: Hammond B-3 organ, piano, guitar, saxophone, trombone, oboe, double bass. Bachelor of Music Education from Indiana University, senior recital on trombone. Not an organist who happens to play other instruments. A formally trained musician who happens to be most famous for the organ. Memoir *Time Is Tight: My Life, Note by Note* (2019, Hachette). The losses of the classic lineup are total. Al Jackson Jr. shot and killed at his Memphis home at thirty-nine, October 1, 1975. Donald "Duck" Dunn died on tour in Tokyo at seventy, May 13, 2012. Steve Cropper died in Nashville at eighty-four, December 3, 2025. Of the classic M.G.'s, only Booker T. Jones survives. When he tours now, he carries the entire institutional memory of the band, the label, and the era. Every Hammond B-3 chord at The Triple Door is a transmission from a world that no longer exists except through him.

Meanwhile, in an oak-pine woodland on the dry side of the Cascades, where Garry oaks (*Quercus garryana*) stand in open savanna and the air smells of sun-warmed ponderosa pine bark, a bird drawn in ink on white paper sings a lazy, buzzy song from the canopy. *Weazy weazy weazy wee-zy.* The Black-throated Gray Warbler. *Setophaga nigrescens.* "The darkening moth-eater" -- *nigrescens* is not "black" (*niger*) but "blackening," a present participle catching the bird mid-transformation, as if the black is still spreading across the plumage. John Kirk Townsend described the species in 1837. Bold black-and-white striping -- black crown, white supercilium, black throat, black cheek patch, white malar stripe, gray back with black streaks, white wing bars. No color. Except: one small yellow loral spot between the eye and the bill. One dot of warmth on an otherwise achromatic bird. The ink drawing with a single brushstroke of gold. ## Part A: Booker T. Jones -- The Touring Anchor Enters the Engine

Full deep research covering Booker T. Jones as the engine's first non-residential artist, the architect of the Memphis Sound, the thirty-ninth artist-city pattern (The Touring Anchor), the congregational organ as the thirty-ninth acoustic element (artist-side), the gospel swell as the thirty-eighth blue note deployment, institutional tidal as the seventh E=5 mode, Stax Records entering the engine's label ecosystem with a direct historical tie to Atlantic Records (degree 30), R&B/Soul returning at Genre Stage 20 after two Blues Rock degrees, and the last M.G. standing.

- **Booker Taliaferro Jones Jr. -- born November 12, 1944, Memphis, Tennessee, the Soulsville prodigy who learned organ in church and found his career on McLemore Avenue.** Grew up on Ethel Avenue in South Memphis in the neighborhood now known as Soulsville. Musically omnivorous from childhood -- learned saxophone, trombone, oboe, double bass, and piano at school, played organ at his church. The church organ is the origin point; the Hammond B-3 that would define his career was first encountered in a house of worship. The gospel tradition that runs through all Southern soul music ran through Jones's hands before he was a teenager. At Booker T. Washington High School, classmate and best friend was Maurice White (founder of Earth, Wind & Fire); schoolmate David Porter would become Isaac Hayes's songwriting partner. Three teenagers in the same building who would collectively reshape American popular music across multiple genres.

- **Stax Records -- the studio on McLemore Avenue, 926 East McLemore, converted movie theater, "when you walked in the door at Stax, there was absolutely no color."** Jones's professional career began at sixteen in 1960 as a staff musician at Satellite Records (renamed Stax). Founded by siblings Jim Stewart and Estelle Axton (Stewart + Axton = Stax). Operated out of a converted movie theater whose sloped floor created unusual acoustics -- the room itself was an instrument. What made Stax different from Motown (assembly-line production) and Atlantic (New York sophistication): arrangements created on the studio floor, not pre-written on charts. Musicians listened to each other and built grooves collectively, in real time. The sound was rawer, more spontaneous, more gospel-rooted. And it was integrated. In 1962 Memphis, where Jim Crow was the law, Stax Records operated a studio where Black and white musicians worked side by side as equals, quietly, while the rest of Memphis enforced separation.

- **Booker T. & the M.G.'s -- the band that built the sound, "Green Onions" (1962) recorded in a single take, No. 3 pop / No. 1 R&B, two Black and two white musicians in 1962 Memphis.** Summer 1962. Four musicians backing a session: seventeen-year-old keyboardist Jones, twenty-year-old guitarist Steve Cropper, bassist Lewie Steinberg, drummer Al Jackson Jr. During downtime, Jones noodled a bluesy organ riff. Jim Stewart told them to keep playing. Recorded in a single take. "Green Onions" -- one of the most recognizable instrumentals in American music history. An integrated band in 1962 Memphis. Audiences were segregated even when the band wasn't: "The black people thought it was a black band and the white people thought it was a white band." Records didn't come with photographs. The music was racially ambiguous. The integration was hidden in plain sight, audible but invisible.

- **The house band: building other people's masterpieces -- Otis Redding, Sam & Dave, Albert King, Wilson Pickett, Carla Thomas, Eddie Floyd, The Staple Singers, Isaac Hayes.** The M.G.'s were the engine room of Stax Records. Not just a recording act -- the house band that provided the rhythmic and harmonic foundation for some of the most important soul recordings ever made. Cropper co-wrote "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay" with Otis Redding. Jones's Hammond B-3 was the harmonic glue. Jackson's drumming earned him the name "The Human Timekeeper." Duck Dunn's syncopated bass defined the bottom end of Memphis soul. The session-musician-as-architect paradigm: the M.G.'s co-created the sound but the credit went to the artists out front. Jones was an architect, not a frontman. He built the rooms that other people lived in.

- **The classic lineup and its instruments -- Jones (Hammond B-3), Cropper (Fender Telecaster), Dunn (Fender Precision Bass), Jackson (drums, "The Human Timekeeper").** Duck Dunn replaced Lewie Steinberg in 1965. Dunn brought syncopated, locked-in grooves that meshed tightly with Jackson's drums. The classic four became one of the tightest rhythm sections in the history of recorded music. Their economy was legendary -- they played exactly what the song needed and nothing more. The spaces between the notes were as important as the notes themselves. Key M.G.'s recordings: "Green Onions" (1962), "Hip Hug-Her" (1967), "Soul Limbo" (1968, later BBC cricket theme), "Time Is Tight" (1969), "Mrs. Robinson" (1969, cross-genre instrumental cover), "Melting Pot" (1971, the last great M.G.'s track).

- **The losses -- Al Jackson Jr. (1975), Donald "Duck" Dunn (2012), Steve Cropper (2025), Jones the last M.G. standing.** Jackson shot and killed at thirty-nine, October 1, 1975, at his Memphis home. Dunn died on tour in Tokyo at seventy, May 13, 2012. Cropper died in Nashville at eighty-four, December 3, 2025. The attrition is total. Jones is the sole surviving member of the classic lineup. Every Hammond B-3 chord he plays at The Triple Door is a transmission from a world that no longer exists except through him. The last M.G. standing.

- **Solo career and production -- Bill Withers's *Just as I Am* (1971), Willie Nelson's *Stardust* (1978), *Potato Hole* Grammy (2010), memoir *Time Is Tight* (2019).** Production credits that span genres and decades. Produced Withers's debut (contains "Ain't No Sunshine"). Produced Nelson's *Stardust* in ten days in Malibu -- they were neighbors -- ten years on Billboard's country albums chart, Nelson's biggest seller. A Memphis soul organist producing a country outlaw's pop standards album. The genre boundary-crossing is total. Grammy for Best Instrumental Album for *Potato Hole* (2009, with the Drive-By Truckers and Neil Young) demonstrates Jones remained vital nearly fifty years after "Green Onions." Bachelor of Music Education from Indiana University, senior recital on trombone. The distance between a trombone recital at Indiana University and a Hammond B-3 session at Stax Records is the distance between academic music and the street, and Jones inhabits both. Memoir *Time Is Tight: My Life, Note by Note* (2019, Hachette) provides first-person testimony about the daily reality of integration at Stax.

- **The Seattle connection: The Touring Anchor -- The Triple Door, Kirkland Performance Center, Mount Baker Theatre, decades of PNW performances along the I-5 corridor.** Documented performances: The Triple Door April 28-29, 2022; Kirkland Performance Center April 11, 2024; The Triple Door October 3-4, 2025; Mount Baker Theatre Bellingham October 2, 2025. The house-band model inverted: the anonymous session musician is now the featured performer where every note is audible, every gesture visible. Touring pattern encompasses the full I-5 corridor. Typical setlist: "Green Onions," "Time Is Tight," "Hip Hug-Her," "Soul Limbo," "Melting Pot," solo material. A museum tour conducted by the artist who built the museum.

- **Memphis soul and Seattle soul: parallel traditions -- Central District as Seattle's Soulsville, Jackson Street corridor, *Wheedle's Groove*, KYAC, Stax influence flowing through 45 RPM singles.** Memphis and Seattle developed parallel Black music scenes in the mid-twentieth century. Seattle's Central District was the PNW's equivalent of Memphis's Beale Street. The Jackson Street corridor clubs of the 1940s-50s hosted jazz, blues, and R&B in a concentrated geographic zone. The *Wheedle's Groove* project (Light in the Attic Records, 2004) excavated Seattle's forgotten soul and funk scene -- Black on White Affair, Cold Bold and Together, the Overton Berry Trio, dozens of others who played CD clubs, recorded on local labels, and were heard on KYAC. The CD musicians of the 1960s-70s were listening to Stax records. The influence flowed through the same channels everywhere: 45 RPM singles, AM radio, jukebox play. But in Seattle, the influence landed in a neighborhood that already had its own musical infrastructure, clubs, radio station, audience. The Memphis sound enriched Seattle's sound. It did not replace it.

The Touring Anchor validates the visitor as a legitimate engine participant. The Export (0, Quincy Jones) left Seattle. The Touring Anchor (38, Booker T. Jones) arrives. The engine's first thirty-nine degrees are now bracketed by departure and arrival.

- **The congregational organ -- thirty-ninth acoustic element (artist-side), Hammond B-3 with Leslie speaker, gospel-rooted, breaks the three-degree Stratocaster thread (35-37).** The Hammond B-3 organ as played by Booker T. Jones. Timbral roots in the Black church, translated into secular instrumental music without losing its spiritual resonance. The rotating Leslie speaker produces a warm, swirling tone -- the vibrato is physical, the sound literally moves through space. Jones's approach defined by economy and groove. He does not play fast. He does not play loud. He plays with precision. The congregational organ is the first keyboard-based element in the engine. The Stratocaster thread (35 Cray, 36 Hendrix, 37 Jones) is broken. Guitar yields to organ. Six strings yield to a rotating speaker cabinet. Blues-rock tradition yields to the soul tradition. A new timbral family enters.

- **The gospel swell -- thirty-eighth blue note deployment, the blue note embedded in organ timbre, Leslie speaker Doppler wobble, flatted third between church and club.** On the organ, the blue note is not bent through string tension or vocal inflection. It is approached through chord voicing and the Leslie speaker's physical rotation. Jones voices minor-to-major ambiguity in his chord choices. The Leslie adds a physical dimension: as the sound rotates, the pitch wobbles due to the Doppler effect, creating natural vibrato that makes every note slightly blue. The gospel swell is the blue note as it exists in the Black church -- the warm, communal wash of an organ chord hovering between sadness and joy, between the secular and the sacred. Structurally distinct from three consecutive guitar-based deployments (35-36-37: the articulate sting, the screaming bend, the grunge bend). A parallel lineage, not a successor.

## Part B: Black-throated Gray Warbler -- The Drawl

Full deep research covering the Black-throated Gray Warbler as the sixth Parulidae, returning after the longest family absence in the engine (eighteen degrees from Orange-crowned at degree 20 through Black-throated Gray at degree 38), introducing the first dry woodland ecosystem (oak-pine Garry oak savanna on the rain-shadow side of the Cascades), the drawl as the thirty-ninth acoustic element (species-side), the *Setophaga* pair contrast with the Yellow Warbler at degree 16, and Parulidae taking the family lead with six members.

- ***Setophaga nigrescens* -- "the darkening moth-eater," Townsend 1837, *nigrescens* as present participle, a verb form not an adjective, the bird that is becoming.** *Setophaga* from Ancient Greek *ses* (moth) + *phagos* (eater) = "moth-eater." *Nigrescens* from Latin *nigrescere* = "to become black" or "to darken." Not "black" (*niger*) but "blackening" (*nigrescens*) -- the present participle catches the bird mid-transformation. The species epithet describes an ongoing process rather than a static feature. The first warbler whose name contains motion. Compare: MacGillivray's (person), Wilson's (person), Yellow (physical description), Yellowthroat (morphology), Orange-crowned (concealment), Black-throated Gray (process). Formerly *Dendroica nigrescens* before the 2011 AOS merger of *Dendroica* into *Setophaga* based on molecular phylogenetics. Described by John Kirk Townsend in 1837 from specimens collected in the Columbia River region during the Wyeth expedition -- the same Townsend who gave his name to Townsend's Warbler (degree 39).

- **Parulidae returns -- 18-degree gap, the longest family absence followed by a return, degrees 20-38, eight families explored during warbler silence.** The last warbler sang at degree 20 -- the Orange-crowned Warbler's descending trill evaporating into silence. Between degree 20 and degree 38, the engine explored flickers (21-22), grosbeaks (23), tanagers (24), robins (25), swallows (26-30), waxwings (31-32), finches (33), crossbills (34), and hummingbirds (35-37). Eight families. Eighteen degrees. No other family has been gone this long and come back. Parulidae has approximately 115 species in North America. Five was never going to be the end. The quintet closed at degree 20. What opens at degree 38 is a broader Parulidae expansion beyond the quintet's structural completeness.

- **The warbler sextet -- five E=3 wet-side warblers plus one E=4 dry-side warbler, clean break from quintet.** MacGillivray's (14, *churry-churry-sweet*, narrative, E=3, understory thicket), Wilson's (15, *chi-chi-chi-chi-chet*, urgency, E=3, riparian willow), Yellow (16, *sweet-sweet-sweeter*, brightness, E=3, riparian willow), Common Yellowthroat (17, *witchety-witchety*, catchiness, E=3, freshwater marsh), Orange-crowned (20, descending trilling rattle, release, E=3, shrubby slope), Black-throated Gray (38, lazy buzzy wheeze, the drawl, E=4, oak-pine). The quintet was a wet-side E=3 phenomenon. The sixth warbler is a dry-side E=4 bird. The quintet sang with clean or thin tones. The Black-throated Gray buzzes with rough timbre. The break is clean: the sixth warbler is not quintet-plus-one. It is something else.

- **The *Setophaga* pair: degrees 16 and 38 -- same genus, opposite aesthetics, the highlighter pen and the ink drawing.** Yellow Warbler (*S. petechia*) = brightest PNW warbler, vivid yellow, ascending crescendo, E=3, riparian. Black-throated Gray (*S. nigrescens*) = starkest PNW warbler, black-and-white, lazy buzzy wheeze, E=4, dry woodland. Twenty-two degrees apart. The genus *Setophaga* contains both the canvas filled with color and the canvas left bare with one brushstroke of warmth. The largest New World warbler genus (~34 species) now has two representatives. First *Setophaga* recurrence. There will certainly be more (Townsend's at degree 39 is already named *S. townsendi*).

- **The sound: lazy buzzy wheeze song -- *weazy weazy weazy wee-zy*, 4-5 buzzy phrases, unhurried, the laziest warbler in the engine.** Typically transcribed as *weazy weazy weazy wee-zy* or *zeedle zeedle zeedle zee*. First 3-4 phrases on the same pitch, final phrase rising or falling slightly. Duration 1.5-2.5 seconds. Tempo slow for a warbler. Timbre buzzy, husky, wheezy -- rough, insect-like quality. Not the clean pure-toned notes of the Yellow Warbler or the thin trill of the Orange-crowned. The song sits in the oak canopy like a summer afternoon. Calling note: a distinctive *tup* or *tchep*, flatter and duller than many warbler chips. The word "lazy" is not pejorative -- in open woodland with moderate ambient noise, a buzzy quality carries well through vegetation because buzz contains energy across a broader frequency band than a pure tone. The laziness is efficient.

- **Oak-pine: the engine's first dry woodland -- Garry oak (only native PNW oak), ponderosa pine, open savanna, rain-shadow side, < 10% of historical range remains.** Garry oak (*Quercus garryana*) ranges from southern British Columbia through Washington and Oregon to northern California. The PNW's only native oak species. Less than 10% of historical Garry oak woodland remains intact. Threats: fire suppression (Douglas fir overtops and shades out oaks), urban development, agricultural conversion, invasive species (Scotch broom, Himalayan blackberry). Oak-pine woodland is dry, sun-drenched, open canopy, sparse understory, bunchgrass and wildflowers. The rain-shadow landscape. The PNW is defined in popular imagination by rain and dense conifers. But the region contains significant dry woodland, and the Black-throated Gray Warbler is one of its signature species. PNW distribution: south Puget Sound lowlands (Joint Base Lewis-McChord, Mima Mounds), San Juan Islands, eastern Cascades foothills, Willamette Valley, southern Oregon. Locally common in appropriate habitat but uncommon in wet west-side forests.

- **Plumage: the ink drawing -- black crown, white supercilium, black throat, black cheek patch, gray back, white wing bars, and one yellow loral spot.** Graphic contrast. No color except one small yellow patch between eye and bill -- the diagnostic field mark. The Yellow Warbler (16) was entirely yellow. The Black-throated Gray is entirely achromatic except for one dot. The genus pair maps the extremes of warbler color: maximum yellow (*S. petechia*) and minimal yellow (*S. nigrescens*, one small spot). Connects to the hidden crown of the Orange-crowned Warbler (degree 20) -- both challenge the assumption that a field mark should be conspicuous. Orange-crowned hides it. Black-throated Gray shrinks it. Two strategies of minimal color.

- **Ecology: the dry woodland specialist -- mid-canopy gleaner, unhurried foraging, short- to medium-distance migrant to western Mexico.** Forages by gleaning from leaves and branches at mid-canopy height (5-15 m). Method: systematic, hopping along branches, peering at leaf surfaces. Prey: insects, caterpillars, spiders. The foraging style matches the song: unhurried, methodical, deliberate. Nests in oaks or pines at 2-10 m, open cup of plant fibers. Short- to medium-distance migrant: breeding range from southern BC through California, wintering in western Mexico. Migration among the shortest of the engine's warblers -- shifts latitude within the same biome: dry woodland in summer, dry woodland in winter. Arrives PNW late April to mid-May, departs August-September.

- **Conservation: Least Concern but habitat declining -- the bird is secure but its best habitat is disappearing.** IUCN Least Concern. Population stable to slightly declining. The paradox: the species persists because it uses a range of dry woodland types, not just Garry oak. But in the PNW, loss of Garry oak woodland reduces available breeding habitat. Fire suppression, urban development, invasives, agricultural conversion. The conservation story is about habitat, not species. The Black-throated Gray Warbler is an indicator of oak woodland health. Where the warbler sings, the oaks are still standing.

- **The drawl -- thirty-ninth acoustic element (species-side), slow and buzzy, the warbler family's deceleration response to the hummingbird trio.** After the filament (degree 37) -- the Calliope Hummingbird's thinnest-wire signal that barely carries ten meters -- the Rufous, Anna's, and Calliope trio concludes. At degree 38 the engine reverts from Apodiformes (mechanical, aeromechanical, filament) to Passeriformes (vocal, buzzy, drawled), from mountain meadow to oak-pine, from 3 grams to 8 grams, from milliseconds to full seconds. The drawl is not just another warbler song. It is the engine's structural answer to the extremes of the hummingbird trio: what happens when a songbird deliberately takes its time, when the laziest pace is the most efficient pace, when a buzzy timbre does more work than a pure tone. Thirty-nine elements across thirty-nine degrees, one per degree without exception.

- **Parulidae takes the family lead with six members -- surpassing Hirundinidae and Turdidae (five each).** The largest songbird family in North America (~115 species) now has the most representatives in the engine. Six Parulidae: MacGillivray's (14), Wilson's (15), Yellow (16), Common Yellowthroat (17), Orange-crowned (20), Black-throated Gray (38). The family lead marks a structural threshold: the family that dominates North American songbird diversity now also dominates the engine's family distribution. Further Parulidae members are certain. The sextet is a staging point, not a ceiling.

- **The yellow loral spot as color theory in one dot -- minimal yellow, maximum information, the field mark as haiku.** Most warblers wear color. The Yellow Warbler is entirely yellow. The Common Yellowthroat flashes a yellow face behind a black mask. The Orange-crowned hides orange under gray. The Black-throated Gray commits to no-color except one small yellow spot. The loral spot is not a territorial flag and not a sexual ornament. It is a species identification mark for conspecifics and observers alike. The economy is total: one pixel of color on an otherwise achromatic canvas. The graphic designer's warbler.

- **The Rufous-Allen's and the sextet's clean break -- dry-side entry point, independent from the quintet's wet-side themes.** The quintet (14-17, 20) was unified by wet or mixed habitats (understory thicket, riparian willow, freshwater marsh, shrubby slope). The sextet member is dry-side only. The quintet was unified by E=3 clean tone. The sextet member is E=4 buzzy. The quintet was unified by song endings (resolution, urgency, brightness, catchiness, release). The sextet member is song pacing (unhurried, steady, drawn out). No category survives the transition from quintet to sextet. Parulidae is not a closed form. The sixth member starts a new chapter.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **Documentation abundance on Part A balanced the visitor-pattern novelty.** Booker T. Jones has sixty-plus years of documented performances, recordings, interviews, books, and archival material. Every claim in the research could be triangulated across multiple independent sources (Wikipedia, Stax Records, Memphis Music Hall of Fame, Encyclopedia.com, Britannica, NPR, The Bitter Southerner, Guitar World, Bandcamp Daily, Reverb News, Texas Monthly, Best Classic Bands, Grammy.com, Seattle Times, HistoryLink, setlist.fm, EverOut Seattle). The novelty of The Touring Anchor as the first non-residential artist-city pattern was supported by a deep documentary base, so the research budget could be spent on weaving the visitor pattern into the engine's existing taxonomy rather than on excavating basic facts.

- **The four Part A / Part B resonance axes surfaced from the data without forcing.** The engine did not impose the convergence of congregational organ and drawl, institutional memory and family return, touring anchor and summer migrant, or Stax and Parulidae as institutions that outlast individuals. The resonance was waiting to be recognized. Both degree-38 subjects are unhurried. Both carry something larger than themselves. Both are visitors. Both represent institutions with histories beyond any single session or season. The structural parallel emerged from the independent research and was validated by the writing rather than constructed by it.

- **Stax-Atlantic label relationship landed as a genuine cross-degree connection.** Ray Charles recorded for Atlantic (degree 30); Booker T. Jones recorded for Stax (degree 38); Atlantic distributed Stax from 1960 to 1968 under Jerry Wexler's deal, and when it ended Atlantic retained all Stax masters. The engine's label ecosystem now contains a direct historical tie between two degrees eight apart, with a specific mechanism (distribution) and a specific outcome (back-catalog loss). Future label-recurrence tracking has a template.

- **The Stratocaster-to-Hammond transition reframed the three-degree guitar thread as a closed set.** Degrees 35, 36, 37 all featured a Fender Stratocaster (Cray's clean, Hendrix's overdriven, Jones's grunge). Degree 38 replaced the guitar entirely with the Hammond B-3 organ through Leslie speaker. The thread closed. The keyboard-based family entered. The reframing was only possible because the three prior degrees had been recognized as a coherent sequence; the break at 38 gives the sequence its boundary.

- **Parulidae's eighteen-degree absence made the return feel earned rather than scheduled.** The engine did not visit a warbler between degrees 20 and 38. Eight families ran through the species side during the gap. The Black-throated Gray Warbler's arrival at degree 38 is a genuine re-entry, not a checkbox. The warbler sextet's structural break from the quintet is crisp precisely because the five-degree quintet sat closed for eighteen degrees before the sixth member reopened the family on new terms.

### What Could Be Better

- **The Touring Anchor pattern concentrates the visitor category onto a single exemplar without yet demonstrating how it scales.** Degree 38 establishes The Touring Anchor as the first non-residential pattern. Future visitor artists will need to be distinguishable from Jones on specific axes (origin city, touring frequency, venue type, audience relationship) before the pattern can carry a taxonomy. One data point is not yet a category. Next visitor artist should be analyzed with explicit comparison to Jones to test whether the pattern is a type or a singleton.

- **The Central District's role in Jones's Seattle connection is implied rather than documented.** The research establishes that Stax records influenced Seattle's Central District soul scene (degrees 32-34) and that Jones's contemporary tours play successor venues. The direct link -- specific CD artists citing Stax, specific CD clubs carrying M.G.'s singles, specific Jones setlists performed in CD venues -- was not excavated. Future CD-connected visitor degrees should capture the direct influence chain with named sources.

- **The Garry oak conservation story needs First Nations consultation the current research did not conduct.** Garry oak woodland is Coast Salish cultural territory, and indigenous fire practices were central to maintaining the savanna before European contact. The research documented the fire-suppression threat to modern Garry oak but did not engage with the Coast Salish knowledge systems that shaped the landscape. Future oak-pine degrees should include Duwamish, Puyallup, Nisqually, or Cowlitz Tribal Council consultation.

- **Gender gap in the artist-side coverage remains unaddressed.** Booker T. Jones is another male artist. The engine's artist-side distribution continues to skew male. The gender gap has been flagged as a structural lesson across prior degrees and remains uncorrected at degree 38.

- **The Rufous-Allen's-Townsend's complex for PNW *Setophaga* was referenced but not resolved.** The Black-throated Gray Warbler hybridizes with Townsend's Warbler (degree 39) in a narrow zone, and both overlap with the Hermit Warbler (not yet in the engine). The hybrid zone's genetics and field identification cues are active research topics in the PNW ornithology community. Degree 38 noted the hybridization but did not document the hybrid indices, the contact zones, or the behavioral cues. Future *Setophaga* degrees should engage the hybrid complex explicitly.

## Lessons Learned

- **The visitor validates the engine's geographic reach.** For thirty-seven degrees, the engine operated on an implicit assumption: artists belong to Seattle because they lived, were born, worked, or were based in Seattle. Degree 38 breaks that assumption. Booker T. Jones belongs to the Seattle 360 engine because he repeatedly performs in Seattle, because his tradition flows into Seattle's tradition, and because the relationship is real even though the residency never was. The engine is not a census of residents. It is a map of musical influences and presences. The visitor validates the gravitational model over the biographical model.

- **Keyboard replaces guitar as the primary degree-defining instrument when the thread demands it.** Three consecutive Stratocaster degrees (35-37) created a thread. Degree 38 required a different instrument to break the thread. The Hammond B-3 is that instrument. The engine's timbral evolution is not random; it follows the logic of thread and rupture. Track the primary instrument as a tracked axis alongside pitch, rhythm, and structure; the instrument change is itself a structural event.

- **Institutional energy (E=5 institutional tidal) foregrounds the collective over the individual.** Six prior E=5 modes (personal, scene-cumulative, collective, breakthrough, supernova, successor) all measured energy through artist-specific trajectories. Institutional tidal measures energy through the artist's affiliation with a larger body -- in Jones's case, Booker T. & the M.G.'s, Stax Records, and the Memphis soul tradition. Future institutional-tidal candidates should be identified through their relationship to a named institution rather than through individual career velocity.

- **Label connections across degrees are structural events, not coincidences.** Atlantic distributed Stax 1960-1968. Ray Charles (degree 30) recorded for Atlantic; Booker T. Jones (degree 38) recorded for Stax. The label connection is not a trivia note -- it encodes a historical relationship between the two most important soul labels and foreshadows how the engine's label ecosystem will densify as more artists enter. Mercury Records (three degrees: 31, 32, 35), Stax, Atlantic, Prestige, Tomato -- each recurrence should be tracked as a network edge.

- **The longest family absence followed by return is worth tracking explicitly.** Eighteen degrees between Orange-crowned Warbler (20) and Black-throated Gray Warbler (38). No family has been gone this long and come back. Future species-side tracking should include a "family gap" axis measured in degrees, because the gap's length correlates with the break's sharpness. Parulidae returns with E=4 instead of the quintet's E=3, a dry habitat instead of wet, a buzzy timbre instead of clean. The eighteen-degree gap allowed the return to reset the category.

- **Dry ecosystem entry changes the engine's ecological palette.** Thirty-eight degrees into the series, every habitat has been wet (riparian, freshwater marsh, estuary) or moist-temperate (mixed conifer, old-growth, mountain meadow). Oak-pine at degree 38 introduces the engine's first xeric biome. Future dry-side species (Lazuli Bunting, Sage Thrasher, Green-tailed Towhee, Brewer's Sparrow) should be scheduled to deepen the dry-woodland vocabulary before wet-side habitats saturate the species-side distribution.

- **The *Setophaga* pair confirms genus-level structural recurrence as an axis.** Yellow Warbler (16) and Black-throated Gray Warbler (38) share a genus and diverge on every other axis. Twenty-two degrees between appearances. Future genus-level recurrences (Townsend's Warbler at degree 39 makes three *Setophaga*) should be tracked as structural events because the same-genus-different-outcome pattern reveals the breadth of ecological and acoustic variation possible within a single clade.

- **Integration history belongs in the release notes, not just the artist's biography.** Booker T. & the M.G.'s were an integrated band in 1962 Memphis. The fact that audiences were segregated while the band was not is a cultural-context fact, not just a musician's biography. Future degrees where racial, cultural, or political history shaped the music should carry the context forward into the release notes so the engine's record reflects the conditions under which the music was made.

- **The laziest possible answer can be the most efficient.** The Black-throated Gray Warbler's drawl carries better through dappled oak canopy than a pure whistle would, because broadband buzz preserves more signal energy across the frequency bands attenuated by foliage. The drawl is not a compromise; it is an optimization. The engine should note efficiency-through-apparent-inefficiency as a biological strategy and look for analogous patterns in future dry-woodland species.

- **The engine's first non-residential artist and its first returning family arrived in the same rotation.** Degree 38 is a structural convergence: the first visitor artist (Booker T. Jones) and the first family re-entry (Parulidae) ship on the same day. Future convergences of "first X + return of Y" should be tracked as engine-level events because they expand the taxonomy in two directions simultaneously. The visitor pattern and the family-return pattern are independent axes that happened to intersect at degree 38.
