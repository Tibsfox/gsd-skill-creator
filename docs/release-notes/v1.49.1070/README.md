## Mission Overview

Voyager 2 is the obs#4 sustain of the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA deep-space probe whose defining achievement is the Grand Tour across all four giant planets and, uniquely, the only visits ever made to the ice giants Uranus and Neptune. It is managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) — the twin of Voyager 1, distinct from the Ames-managed Pioneer siblings. A three-axis-stabilized flyby probe with a large 3.7-m dish high-gain antenna and a scan platform of pointed instruments — not an orbiter or a lander — it launched on a Titan IIIE-Centaur from Cape Canaveral LC-41 on 20 August 1977 (before its twin Voyager 1, which launched 5 September 1977, but on a slower, longer trajectory, so Voyager 1 reached the planets first), powered by three MHW radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) fueled with plutonium-238 rather than solar panels, because sunlight is far too weak in the outer solar system, and it carries the Golden Record. Its payload included the imaging science subsystem (ISS), the infrared interferometer spectrometer (IRIS), the ultraviolet spectrometer (UVS), the photopolarimeter (PPS), the planetary radio astronomy (PRA) and plasma wave (PWS) instruments, the magnetometer (MAG), the plasma (PLS), the low-energy charged particle (LECP), and the cosmic ray subsystem (CRS). The story is the Grand Tour across the whole outer solar system: across the inner solar system on RTG power and on to Jupiter on 9 July 1979 — the fourth craft there — returning detailed imaging of the cloud bands, the Great Red Spot, the ring, and the Galilean moons, and using the Jupiter gravity assist to swing on to Saturn, reached on 25 August 1981 — the third craft there — where each giant planet's gravity flung the probe on to the next world. From Saturn the rare ~176-year planetary alignment carried Voyager 2 on to Uranus, reached on 24 January 1986 — the first and only spacecraft ever to visit the tilted ice giant — and then on to Neptune, reached on 25 August 1989 — the first and only spacecraft ever to visit the farthest giant — completing the only Grand Tour of all four giant planets. The scoping is load-bearing: the Jupiter (fourth) and Saturn (third) flybys are described, not anchored (the Pioneers and Voyager 1 came earlier); the Uranus and Neptune flybys are each the first AND only visits ever made; the all-four-giant-planets Grand Tour is the superlative; the Triton geysers are a science result (icy cryovolcanism), not "first active volcanism" (that is Voyager 1's Io); the heliopause crossing (5 November 2018, at about 119 AU) was the second into interstellar space after Voyager 1 (2012) and is described, not anchored; and the Golden Record is the richer audio-visual message, NOT a first (the Pioneer plaque, 1972). It continues to operate and transmit from interstellar space (as of 2026, using its trajectory-correction thrusters for attitude control, instruments being progressively powered down to conserve energy).
