## Surprises

- **A 1977 probe is still operating from interstellar space in 2026.** Voyager 2 continues to operate and transmit from interstellar space, nearly half a century after launch, using its trajectory-correction thrusters for attitude control as its main attitude thrusters are conserved, with its instruments being progressively powered down one by one to conserve its declining RTG power.
- **One spacecraft has reached Uranus and Neptune — and only one, only once.** Voyager 2 made the first and only flyby of Uranus (24 January 1986) and the first and only flyby of Neptune (25 August 1989); no mission has returned to either ice giant since, so these two single encounters remain the entire close-up record of both worlds.
- **One probe visited all four giant planets in a single Grand Tour.** Voyager 2 flew by Jupiter (1979), Saturn (1981), Uranus (1986), and Neptune (1989), using each giant's gravity to swing on to the next — the only spacecraft ever to do so, enabled by a rare ~176-year planetary alignment.
- **A moon erupting nitrogen at the cold edge of the planets.** At Neptune in 1989 Voyager 2 imaged the nitrogen geysers and retrograde orbit of the large moon Triton — icy cryovolcanism at one of the coldest places ever seen up close, a science result distinct from the silicate volcanism of Io.
- **A deep-diving seal of the cold North Pacific plunges far below where almost anything follows.** The Northern Elephant Seal (*Mirounga angustirostris*), a huge seal that breeds on California islands and coast and ranges far north across the cold North Pacific to forage (reaching the offshore waters of the Pacific Northwest), is among the deepest- and longest-diving of all air-breathing animals, plunging hundreds of metres (recorded beyond 1,500 m) into the cold, dark, deep-blue ocean (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Voyager 2 reaching alone into the far blue.
- **A deep-blue alpine wildflower holds the cold high places where few plants grow.** Mountain Bog Gentian (*Gentiana calycosa*), a deep-blue alpine wildflower of cold, high Pacific Northwest meadows, bogs, and streambanks (the Cascades and Olympics), opens its upright, vivid blue trumpet flowers in the short cold-mountain summer in places few plants reach (ecological description only) — a mirror of the deep cold blue of the far worlds reached by Voyager 2 alone.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A sustain KEEPS the predecessor's shared domain vocabulary — it does not remove it.** Unlike a rotation, this is a SUSTAIN (mode=continuation): Voyager 2 and the Voyager 1 / Pioneer siblings share almost all of their domain vocabulary, so the rewrite KEPT the shared outer-planets content (the outer solar system, deep space, the giant planets, the ice giants, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, RTG power, the flyby, the gravity assist, the Grand Tour, the escape trajectory, the heliosphere, interstellar space) and replaced only the predecessor-specific facts — Voyager 1 as the subject, the 5 September 1977 launch as "after Voyager 2 but on a faster trajectory," the discovery of active volcanism on Io, becoming the most distant human-made object in 1998, the first crossing of the heliopause in 2012, the Saturn–Titan slingshot out of the ecliptic, and the Black-footed Albatross + Puget Sound Gumweed pairing — with Voyager 2's: the JPL twin of Voyager 1, the 20 August 1977 Titan IIIE-Centaur launch (before Voyager 1 but slower), the Golden Record, the Jupiter and Saturn flybys (NOT firsts), the first and only flyby of Uranus, the first and only flyby of Neptune, the only Grand Tour of all four giant planets, the heliopause crossing second in 2018, the continued operation from interstellar space (2026), and the Northern Elephant Seal + Mountain Bog Gentian pairing.
2. **A sustain SUSTAINS the axis — the obs counter advances and there are prior siblings on the same axis.** Voyager 2 sustains the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis at obs#4 (the third consecutive sustain on the axis opened by Pioneer 10, sustained by Pioneer 11, and sustained by Voyager 1), so only the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION counter advances (obs#3 → obs#4) and the substrate-axis-rotation discipline holds UNCHANGED at obs#101 (no NEW rotation observed). The single deliberate backward reference is the nav-prev to the 1.252 Voyager 1 sibling, with a lineage thread to Pioneer 11 and Pioneer 10.
3. **The management is JPL, the twin of Voyager 1, NOT Ames — a correct intra-axis distinction.** Voyager 2 was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, the twin of Voyager 1, whereas the Pioneer siblings (obs#1/#2) were managed by Ames; this is a correct distinction within the shared axis, not a contradiction, and the catalog states JPL precisely throughout.
4. **Voyager 2 launched FIRST but reached the planets after Voyager 1 — never "the second Voyager."** Voyager 2 launched 20 August 1977, before Voyager 1 (5 September 1977), on a slower, longer trajectory, so Voyager 1 reached the planets first; the catalog states this precisely and never calls Voyager 2 "the second Voyager."
5. **The Uranus and Neptune results are each the first AND only visit ever made.** On 24 January 1986 Voyager 2 made the first and only flyby of Uranus, and on 25 August 1989 the first and only flyby of Neptune; the catalog scopes each as "the first and only spacecraft to visit," since no mission has returned to either ice giant, so the two single encounters remain the entire close-up record of both worlds.
6. **The Grand Tour is the superlative — the complete four-planet tour, not the individual Jupiter and Saturn flybys.** Voyager 2 is the only spacecraft to have visited all four giant planets, a unique Grand Tour enabled by a rare ~176-year alignment; the catalog holds the superlative on the complete four-planet tour, while noting that the Jupiter (fourth) and Saturn (third) flybys themselves are NOT firsts.
7. **The heliopause crossing was SECOND — described, not anchored.** Voyager 2 crossed the heliopause on 5 November 2018 (at about 119 AU), the second human-made object into interstellar space after Voyager 1 (2012); the catalog describes the crossing but does NOT anchor it, since the first crossing belongs to Voyager 1 (obs#3).
8. **0 re-stamps — Voyager 2 shares the predecessors' journey but not their firsts.** Voyager 2's Jupiter flyby is the FOURTH and its Saturn flyby the THIRD (the Pioneers and Voyager 1 came earlier); it does not re-stamp Voyager 1's Io volcanism, most-distant standing, or first heliopause crossing, nor the Pioneers' asteroid-belt traverse, escape trajectory, ETI plaque, F-ring discovery, or first detection of Saturn's magnetic field. Voyager 2's firsts are all at Uranus, at Neptune, and in the complete Grand Tour, yielding 0 predecessor re-stamps.
9. **The Triton geysers are a science result, not a volcanism anchor.** At Neptune (1989) Voyager 2 imaged the nitrogen geysers of Triton (icy cryovolcanism, a distinct phenomenon); the catalog frames this as a science result, NOT "first active volcanism beyond Earth" — that is Voyager 1's Io (obs#3).
10. **The Golden Record is the richer audio-visual message, NOT the first message to ETI.** The Pioneer plaque (1972) was the first physical message sent out of the solar system; the catalog describes the Golden Record as the richer audio-visual message Voyager 2 carries, not anchored as a "first."
11. **The mission is framed positively, in the present tense — Voyager 2 is still operating.** Voyager 2 continues to operate and transmit from interstellar space (as of 2026), using its trajectory-correction thrusters for attitude control, with its instruments being progressively powered down to conserve its declining RTG power; the catalog frames the mission positively in the present tense as the probe that completed the Grand Tour and went the widest, still answering home.
