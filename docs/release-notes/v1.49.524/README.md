## Summary

The twenty-fourth paired release and the first fresh dual-build -- the first degree where no pre-built NASA research.md existed, where both halves were constructed from catalog data and accumulated engine context. On October 3, 1962, at Launch Complex 14 at Cape Canaveral, Atlas 113-D ignited beneath Mercury spacecraft number 16. Its pilot had named it Sigma 7 -- Σ, the Greek summation symbol, the character that appears in every engineering equation where you add things up. Not Freedom, not Liberty Bell, not Friendship, not Aurora. The sum. Walter Marty Schirra Jr. was inside. He was thirty-nine years old, a Navy commander, an Annapolis graduate (class of 1945), a veteran of 90 combat missions in F-84E Thunderjets over Korea including an exchange tour with the 154th Fighter-Bomber Squadron where he shot down two MiG-15s while flying an Air Force airplane on loan from the Navy -- a fact that delighted his competitive sensibilities because it meant he had combat kills in another service's aircraft. He was the engineer of the Mercury Seven, the one who wanted to fly the mission exactly as designed. Not heroically. Not poetically. Not adventurously. Correctly.

NASA needed correct. Glenn's Friendship 7 had been complicated by the heat shield indicator scare. Carpenter's Aurora 7 had been operationally messy -- fuel consumption far above the flight plan, science experiments prioritized over engineering data, a 250-mile overshoot. After one flight that survived problems and one flight that created problems, NASA needed a flight that had no problems. They needed an engineering validation flight that proved the Mercury spacecraft and its pilot could execute a mission profile with precision, conserve resources methodically, and return to the planned recovery point on target. They got Schirra. Six orbits -- doubling the three-orbit missions. Nine hours and thirteen minutes. The key metric was hydrogen peroxide attitude control fuel: Glenn had landed with 61% remaining after three orbits; Carpenter had landed with 17% after three orbits; Schirra landed with 78% remaining after six orbits. He had twice as many orbits and four times Carpenter's fuel margin. He achieved this through drifting flight -- long periods of no active attitude control, letting the capsule tumble gently through space, using the automatic and manual systems only when the flight plan called for specific attitudes. The discipline of not touching the controls when the controls did not need to be touched. Splashdown was 4.5 miles from the recovery carrier USS Kearsarge -- close enough that Schirra could see the ship from his capsule window as he descended under the parachute. The precision was absolute. Every parameter within spec. The textbook flight.

Schirra chose the name Sigma because he was the kind of man who thought in symbols. Not literary symbols -- mathematical ones. The sum of all the work. The capsule was the summation of McDonnell Aircraft's engineering, of the Space Task Group's mission planning, of the worldwide tracking network's coordination, of the Atlas booster's gradually improving reliability, and of the five Mercury flights that preceded him. Glenn had proven the system could orbit. Carpenter had proven it could observe. Schirra proved it could operate -- routinely, efficiently, with the margins that would be required for longer missions. The Mercury program's penultimate flight was its most important for engineering: not the first orbit, not the science mission, but the demonstration that the machine worked as designed when flown as designed. The importance of boring. The precision flight that made Gemini possible because it proved Mercury was a production system, not a series of one-off adventures.
