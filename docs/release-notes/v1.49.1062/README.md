MAVEN also served as a key telecom relay for the surface rovers. It operated for about eleven years; its signal ended 6 December 2025 (while passing behind Mars) and NASA concluded the mission on 3 June 2026 — a long, successful mission that achieved its goals. ## Mission Overview

MAVEN is the obs#8 sustain of the MARS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA orbiter whose defining achievements are the first dedicated study of the Martian upper atmosphere and ionosphere, the first measurement of how Mars loses its atmosphere to space, and the first detection of persistent metal-ion layers in a planetary ionosphere beyond Earth. It was the second and final flown mission of the NASA Mars Scout Program (after Phoenix), with principal investigator Bruce Jakosky of the Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics (LASP) at the University of Colorado Boulder; the project was managed by NASA Goddard and the spacecraft was built by Lockheed Martin, with instruments from CU/LASP, the UC Berkeley Space Sciences Laboratory, and Goddard. An ORBITER — not a lander; it did not land or traverse — MAVEN carried a Particles & Fields package (SWIA, the Solar Wind Ion Analyzer; SWEA, the Solar Wind Electron Analyzer; STATIC, the SupraThermal And Thermal Ion Composition instrument; SEP, the Solar Energetic Particle instrument; MAG, the magnetometer; and LPW, the Langmuir Probe and Waves instrument), plus IUVS (the Imaging UltraViolet Spectrograph, a remote-sensing instrument) and NGIMS (the Neutral Gas and Ion Mass Spectrometer). It launched on an Atlas V 401 from Cape Canaveral SLC-41 on 18 November 2013 (18:28 UT) and entered Mars orbit on 21 September 2014 (10:24 p.m. EDT; about 02:24 UTC on 22 September 2014) on an elongated elliptical orbit (~4.5 hr period; periapsis ~150 km, apoapsis ~6,200 km) whose low point dips into the top of the atmosphere, with five Deep Dip campaigns lowering periapsis to ~125 km (about ten times denser) for roughly five days each to sample the well-mixed upper atmosphere directly. The science is its watch on the air: MAVEN measured how fast Mars loses gas to space — ions and neutral atoms (hydrogen, oxygen, and carbon-dioxide fragments) stripped away by the solar wind and by sunlight, the escape rate climbing sharply during solar storms — and, adding the loss over billions of years, concluded that escape to space removed the bulk of Mars's early, once-thicker atmosphere, the dominant reason the planet cooled and dried (present-day loss is commonly cited at roughly 2–3 kg/s). This explains where Mars's early air and water went; it complements, and does not contradict, the surface missions' evidence that Mars was once wet, and MAVEN did not "discover water." NGIMS detected persistent layers of metal ions (iron, magnesium, sodium) high in the ionosphere, delivered by the steady infall of interplanetary dust and meteoroids burning up in the atmosphere — the first such persistent metal-ion layers at a planet beyond Earth, distinct from the one-off, transient metal-ion enhancement during Comet Siding Spring's close pass in October 2014 (Earth has metal-ion layers of its own). IUVS mapped new types of Martian aurora — a deep diffuse aurora driven by solar energetic particles and a widespread dayside proton aurora; ESA's Mars Express (SPICAM) had earlier discovered discrete Martian aurora in 2004–2005, so MAVEN's diffuse and proton forms are new types rather than the first aurora at Mars. MAVEN also served as a key telecom relay for the rovers on the surface below it. It operated for about eleven years; its signal ended on 6 December 2025 and NASA concluded the mission on 3 June 2026 — a long, successful mission that answered where Mars's atmosphere went. ## Surprises

- **An orbiter measured how fast a planet loses its air to space for the first time.** MAVEN's particle instruments and mass spectrometer measured ions and neutral atoms (hydrogen, oxygen, and carbon-dioxide fragments) being stripped from the Martian upper atmosphere by the solar wind and by sunlight — the escape rate climbing sharply during solar storms — and, integrated over billions of years, the bulk loss of Mars's early atmosphere to space, the first direct quantification of how a planet loses its air.
- **The slow loss of the air explains where the water went.** By measuring atmospheric escape and adding it up over eons, MAVEN concluded that escape to space removed the bulk of Mars's early, once-thicker atmosphere — the dominant reason the planet cooled and dried — the "where did the water and air go" answer that complements the surface missions' evidence that Mars was once wet, without re-claiming the surface water findings.
- **Dust burning up high in the sky left persistent metal in the ionosphere.** MAVEN's NGIMS detected persistent layers of metal ions — iron, magnesium, and sodium — high in the Martian ionosphere, delivered by interplanetary dust and meteoroids burning up in the atmosphere; these were the first such persistent metal-ion layers detected at a planet beyond Earth, distinct from the one-off enhancement when Comet Siding Spring passed close to Mars in October 2014.
- **New types of aurora glow across the Martian sky.** MAVEN's ultraviolet spectrograph mapped new types of Martian aurora — a deep diffuse aurora driven by solar energetic particles and a widespread dayside proton aurora; ESA's Mars Express had earlier found discrete Martian aurora, so these diffuse and proton forms were new types rather than the first aurora at Mars.
- **A swallow lives its day borne aloft on the open air.** The Violet-green Swallow (*Tachycineta thalassina*), a small, iridescent green-and-violet aerial insectivore of the Pacific Northwest, forages on the wing high in the open air, spending most of the day aloft and rarely touching the ground (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of MAVEN keeping its watch on the air itself.
- **A milkweed gives its silk-borne seeds to the wind to be carried off.** The Showy Milkweed (*Asclepias speciosa*), a robust native milkweed of the dry interior West, releases seeds each tipped with a tuft of silky white filaments that catches the wind and carries the seed far off into the open air (ecological description only) — a mirror of the Martian air carried off to space.

## Dedication

v1.245 MAVEN is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's first mission to study the air of Mars and where it went. The Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics teams at the University of Colorado Boulder who led the science under Bruce Jakosky; the NASA Goddard teams who managed the project; the Lockheed Martin engineers who built and steered the orbiter to Mars; the University of California Berkeley and Goddard teams who built the particle instruments, the ultraviolet spectrograph, and the mass spectrometer; the navigators who fired the engines into an elongated elliptical orbit on a September night in 2014; and the scientists who, on each Deep Dip, dropped the orbiter into the top of the atmosphere to taste the gas directly. Year after year, a single craft circled high above the planet and watched the thin upper air being drawn off into space by the solar wind, reading the slow loss that turned a once-warm, wet world into the cold, dry one of today. It mapped new auroras, found metal carried in by falling dust, and relayed the words of the rovers below — a patient sentinel of the open air, until its successful work was done.
