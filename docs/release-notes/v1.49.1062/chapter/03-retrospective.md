## Surprises

- **An orbiter measured how fast a planet loses its air to space for the first time.** MAVEN's particle instruments and mass spectrometer measured ions and neutral atoms (hydrogen, oxygen, and carbon-dioxide fragments) being stripped from the Martian upper atmosphere by the solar wind and by sunlight — the escape rate climbing sharply during solar storms — and, integrated over billions of years, the bulk loss of Mars's early atmosphere to space, the first direct quantification of how a planet loses its air.
- **The slow loss of the air explains where the water went.** By measuring atmospheric escape and adding it up over eons, MAVEN concluded that escape to space removed the bulk of Mars's early, once-thicker atmosphere — the dominant reason the planet cooled and dried — the "where did the water and air go" answer that complements the surface missions' evidence that Mars was once wet, without re-claiming the surface water findings.
- **Dust burning up high in the sky left persistent metal in the ionosphere.** MAVEN's NGIMS detected persistent layers of metal ions — iron, magnesium, and sodium — high in the Martian ionosphere, delivered by interplanetary dust and meteoroids burning up in the atmosphere; these were the first such persistent metal-ion layers detected at a planet beyond Earth, distinct from the one-off enhancement when Comet Siding Spring passed close to Mars in October 2014.
- **New types of aurora glow across the Martian sky.** MAVEN's ultraviolet spectrograph mapped new types of Martian aurora — a deep diffuse aurora driven by solar energetic particles and a widespread dayside proton aurora; ESA's Mars Express had earlier found discrete Martian aurora, so these diffuse and proton forms were new types rather than the first aurora at Mars.
- **A swallow lives its day borne aloft on the open air.** The Violet-green Swallow (*Tachycineta thalassina*), a small, iridescent green-and-violet aerial insectivore of the Pacific Northwest, forages on the wing high in the open air, spending most of the day aloft and rarely touching the ground (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of MAVEN keeping its watch on the air itself.
- **A milkweed gives its silk-borne seeds to the wind to be carried off.** The Showy Milkweed (*Asclepias speciosa*), a robust native milkweed of the dry interior West, releases seeds each tipped with a tuft of silky white filaments that catches the wind and carries the seed far off into the open air (ecological description only) — a mirror of the Martian air carried off to space.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A sustain keeps the predecessor's axis vocabulary and replaces only the predecessor-specific content.** MAVEN shares the Mars-exploration vocabulary with the InSight predecessor (Mars, the red planet, the atmosphere, the upper atmosphere, the ionosphere, the solar wind, dust, in situ, planetary exploration, past water, climate) — that vocabulary is shared and correct. The rewrite removed only the InSight-specific content (the InSight name, SEIS, HP3 "the mole," RISE, CNES, DLR, Elysium Planitia, Vandenberg and the first-West-Coast-launch note, marsquakes, the deep-interior / seismometer / interior-structure anchors, the crust/mantle/liquid-core result, and the American Badger + Big Sagebrush pairing) and replaced it with MAVEN content.
2. **A sustain has no axis rotation to frame — and 1.244 was itself a sustain, so there is no rotation framing to remove.** The Mars axis opened at 1.238 (Viking, substrate-axis rotation #36); 1.239 through 1.245 are all SUSTAINS, so the clone source carries no rotation narrative, and at most a single distant note records that the Mars axis itself opened at 1.238 by rotating from the X-ray-astronomy axis (rotation #35, held complete at obs#15).
3. **MAVEN is an ORBITER, not a lander or rover — it varies the form from the recent surface missions.** Where the prior several Mars degrees were landers and rovers, MAVEN circled high above and read the upper atmosphere; the catalog never describes MAVEN landing or roving and never re-claims any surface first.
4. **The escape measurement EXPLAINS where the water and air went — it does not "discover water."** MAVEN's first quantification of atmospheric escape concluded that escape to space removed the bulk of the early atmosphere — the dominant cause of the wet-to-dry climate change — answering the surface missions' "Mars was once wet" question; the catalog never writes that MAVEN discovered water and never contradicts the Mars Exploration Rovers' mineral water evidence or Curiosity's habitability finding.
5. **The metal-ion first is PERSISTENT layers beyond Earth — distinct from the Comet Siding Spring one-off.** NGIMS found persistent meteoric metal-ion layers (iron, magnesium, sodium); the catalog keeps the word PERSISTENT to distinguish them from the transient enhancement during the October 2014 comet pass, and notes that Earth has its own metal-ion layers, so the first is scoped "beyond Earth."
6. **The aurora is NEW TYPES, never the first aurora at Mars.** IUVS characterized a deep diffuse aurora and a dayside proton aurora, but ESA's Mars Express/SPICAM discovered discrete Martian aurora earlier (2004–2005); the catalog frames MAVEN's aurora as new types and never writes "first aurora at Mars," and does not use the aurora as a canonical anchor.
7. **The mission's end is framed positively and mentioned once at low density.** MAVEN operated for about eleven years; its signal ended 6 December 2025 and NASA concluded the mission 3 June 2026. The catalog states this once and frames the mission positively as a landmark that achieved its goals, with no failure/loss vocabulary about the spacecraft — the "loss" the page features is the atmosphere escaping to space.
8. **NASA-with-university/industry-partners framing is an accuracy requirement here.** MAVEN was a NASA mission of the Mars Scout Program managed by NASA Goddard, with the spacecraft built by Lockheed Martin and instruments from CU/LASP (PI Bruce Jakosky), the UC Berkeley Space Sciences Laboratory, and Goddard — a NASA mission with major university and industry partners.
9. **A sustain references its predecessor exactly once, deliberately.** The only correct predecessor reference is a single nav-prev lineage note to v1.244 InSight (Mars obs#7), plus lineage references to Perseverance (1.243, obs#6), Curiosity (1.242, obs#5), the Mars Exploration Rovers (1.241, obs#4), Mars Pathfinder (1.240, obs#3), Mariner 9 (1.239, obs#2), Viking (1.238, obs#1), and Mariner 4 (1.36, the first Mars flyby). The predecessors' anchors stay with them — 0 re-stamps.
10. **Clone source is InSight (a NASA stationary solar-powered geophysics lander, Discovery, 2018–2022); MAVEN is a NASA upper-atmosphere orbiter (Mars Scout, 2013–2025).** The most structural replacement in the rewrite is removing ALL InSight-specific content — "InSight," "SEIS," "HP3," "the mole," "RISE," "CNES," "DLR," "Elysium Planitia," "Vandenberg," the first-West-Coast-launch note, "marsquakes," the deep-interior / seismometer / interior-structure anchors, the crust/mantle/liquid-core result, and the American Badger + Big Sagebrush pairing — and replacing it with MAVEN and the Mars Scout Program, Bruce Jakosky / LASP / CU Boulder, the Atlas V 401 launch from Cape Canaveral SLC-41 (18 November 2013), the 21/22 September 2014 Mars orbit insertion, the elongated elliptical orbit and the Deep Dip campaigns, the payload (Particles & Fields: SWIA/SWEA/STATIC/SEP/MAG/LPW; IUVS; NGIMS), the atmospheric-escape measurement and the wet-to-dry climate-loss conclusion, the persistent metal-ion layers, the new types of aurora, the relay role, the ~eleven-year mission concluded in 2026, NSSDCA/COSPAR 2013-063A, and the Violet-green Swallow + Showy Milkweed pairing.
