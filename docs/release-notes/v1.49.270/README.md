## Cheryl Waters -- The Voice That Opens the Door

Cheryl Waters has hosted the afternoon show on KEXP 90.3 FM since 1996 -- before it was KEXP. She joined **KCMU**, the University of Washington's college radio station, and was there when the station rebranded as KEXP in 2001 with a transformative gift from Paul Allen. She bridges both identities: the scrappy college station and the globally recognized independent music institution.

The KCMU-to-KEXP evolution is one of the most remarkable stories in American radio. KCMU was a college station -- volunteer DJs, limited signal, devoted listeners. The Paul Allen gift ($3.18 million, plus ongoing support) enabled the transformation: professional staff, upgraded signal, streaming infrastructure, the purpose-built studio at Seattle Center. The mission never changed -- independent music, artist-first programming, no commercial playlists -- but the scale changed dramatically. From thousands of local listeners to millions of global streamers. Cheryl Waters was present for the entire arc.

Her show is defined by **warmth and knowledge**. Waters has an encyclopedic command of independent music across genres and decades, and she deploys it with genuine enthusiasm rather than curatorial distance. The afternoon show is discovery radio -- the listener trusts Waters to play something they haven't heard, and she consistently delivers. In an era of algorithmic playlists, Waters represents the human curator: someone who listens to everything, selects with care, and presents with context.

E=3. The energy of radio hosting -- the voice, the curation, the between-song conversation. Not the showmanship of Pat O'Day's AM era (E=4) but the quieter authority of public radio. The energy is in the selection, not the performance. Waters lets the music carry the energy; her role is to frame it.

**Capitol Hill.** KEXP's studios are on Capitol Hill (at Seattle Center, technically Lower Queen Anne, but the station's cultural gravity is Capitol Hill). The station is a Capitol Hill institution in the same way that Sub Pop is -- physically located nearby, culturally inseparable from the neighborhood's identity as Seattle's music district.

The engine now has **five consecutive radio/media figures** (126-130): Nasty Nes Rodriguez (hip-hop), Jonathan Poneman (indie/label), Bruce Pavitt (indie/label), Pat O'Day (mainstream AM), Cheryl Waters (KEXP). Five figures spanning from 1959 to the present. From AM to FM to streaming. From mainstream to underground to global-independent. The full arc of Seattle radio.

## Cackling Goose -- The Split Species

The Cackling Goose (*Branta hutchinsii*) didn't officially exist as a species until **2004**, when the American Ornithologists' Union split it from the Canada Goose (*Branta canadensis*). The split formalized what field birders had long recognized: the smaller forms of "Canada Goose" were consistently different in size, bill shape, call, and breeding range.

The sound: a **higher-pitched cackling** -- shorter, more rapid, less resonant than the Canada Goose's deep honk. The pitch difference is the most reliable field mark for separating the two species at a distance. Where the Canada Goose honks, the Cackling Goose cackles -- a quicker, higher, more staccato vocalization.

E=4. Lower energy than the Canada Goose (E=5) because the Cackling Goose is physically smaller and produces a less powerful call. The higher pitch carries differently -- sharp and quick rather than deep and resonant.
