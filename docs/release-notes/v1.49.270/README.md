**Part B: Cackling Goose and the Goose Comparison Pair.** The **Cackling Goose** (*Branta hutchinsii*) is a small Arctic-breeding tundra goose whose recognition as a separate species from the **Canada Goose** dates only to **2004**, when the American Ornithologists' Union's 45th supplement to the Check-list (Banks et al. 2004) formalized what field birders had long observed — that the smallest forms of the former *Branta canadensis* were consistently different in size, bill shape, call, and breeding range from the larger forms. The split was grounded in molecular genetic studies of the late 1990s and early 2000s that revealed **distinct evolutionary lineages** rather than a smooth size gradient within one species. The species name honors **Thomas Hutchins**, the 18th-century naturalist colleague of John Richardson who first described the small tundra geese of Hudson Bay. Four subspecies were elevated to the new species: **B. h. minima** (Ridgway's, the smallest, Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta breeder wintering primarily in the Willamette Valley of Oregon), **B. h. leucopareia** (Aleutian, a former endangered species recovered through fox eradication on breeding islands, winters in California), **B. h. taverneri** (Taverner's, intermediate size, interior Alaska breeder, winters in Pacific states), and **B. h. hutchinsii** (Richardson's, Arctic Canada breeder, winters primarily in the southern Great Plains). The Cackling Goose is **purely migratory** — unlike the Canada Goose, which has massive resident (non-migratory) populations in the Pacific Northwest, every Cackling Goose in the PNW in winter bred in the Arctic and will return to the Arctic in spring. There are no resident Cackling Goose populations.

**The Higher Cackle as Acoustic Diagnostic.** The Cackling Goose's vocalization is the single most reliable field mark for separating it from the Canada Goose in mixed flocks. The call is **higher-pitched, more rapid, and squeakier** — a quick sharp "cack-cack-cack" or "yip-yip" distinctly higher and thinner than the Canada Goose's two-syllable "ah-HONK." Frequency range 400-2,000 Hz, with energy centered at 600-1,200 Hz — distinctly higher than the Canada Goose's 300-1,000 Hz energy center. The pitch difference is the fastest way to separate Cackling from Canada Goose at a distance. The higher pitch directly reflects the smaller body: smaller body means smaller syrinx means higher pitch. The Canada Goose weighs 2.6-6.5 kg; the Cackling Goose weighs 0.9-1.8 kg. In wintering flocks, the combined cackling creates a **rapid chattering texture** — higher, thinner, more agitated-sounding than the rolling honk wall of a Canada Goose flock. The acoustic character of a Cackling Goose flock is more "frantic" in quality. When mixed flocks contain both species (a common occurrence in Pacific Flyway wintering grounds), the two textures overlay each other, creating a complex bimodal soundscape where the higher cackling adds a treble layer to the bass honking.

## Part B: Cackling Goose — The Higher Cackle and the 2004 AOU Split Made Audible

- **Thirteenth consecutive Anatidae extends the all-time family-streak record.** The **Cackling Goose** (*Branta hutchinsii*) extends the **Anatidae** streak to **thirteen consecutive waterfowl** at degrees 118-130. The streak spans Harlequin Duck (118, E=5), Common Merganser (119, E=4), Hooded Merganser (120, E=4), Wood Duck (121, E=3), Bufflehead (122, E=3), Barrow's Goldeneye (123, E=3), Common Goldeneye (124, E=4), Greater Scaup (125, E=3), Canvasback (126, E=4), Tundra Swan (127, E=5), Trumpeter Swan (128, E=6), Canada Goose (129, E=5), Cackling Goose (130, E=4). Thirteen species. Six genera. Every major Anatidae subfamily present in the Pacific Northwest: mergansers, diving ducks, sea ducks, dabbling ducks, swans, and geese. The streak is now **four degrees beyond the Picidae nonet** (107-115, 9 consecutive) that previously held the all-time-prior family-streak record before this Anatidae run surpassed it.
- **Second *Branta* entry completes the goose comparison pair.** Degree 129 opened the genus ***Branta*** with the **Canada Goose** (large, deep honk, E=5). Degree 130 completes the **goose comparison pair** with the **Cackling Goose** (small, higher cackle, E=4). Same genus, adjacent degrees, one comparison pair. The 2004 AOU taxonomic split becomes an acoustic split. Deep versus high. Long versus short. Honk versus cackle. The engine pairs them the way taxonomy separated them. This is the **fifth comparison pair** in SPS, following Hairy/Downy Woodpecker (110-111), Barrow's/Common Goldeneye (123-124), Tundra/Trumpeter Swan (127-128), and now Canada/Cackling Goose (129-130).
- **The 2004 AOU split: one species becomes two.** Prior to 2004, all of the birds now classified as **Canada Goose** and **Cackling Goose** were considered one species (*Branta canadensis*) with up to 11 subspecies. The size range was enormous — the smallest subspecies barely larger than a Mallard, the largest among the heaviest flying birds in North America. **Molecular genetic studies** of the late 1990s and early 2000s revealed that the small forms (now Cackling Goose) and the large forms (now Canada Goose) represent **distinct evolutionary lineages** — not just a size gradient. A **bimodal size distribution** (two peaks rather than a smooth gradient) supported the two-species model. The **45th supplement to the AOU Check-list** (Banks et al. 2004) elevated the four smallest subspecies to the separate species *Branta hutchinsii* (Cackling Goose). The species name honors **Thomas Hutchins**, the 18th-century naturalist colleague of John Richardson who described the small tundra geese of Hudson Bay.
- **Four Cackling Goose subspecies cover distinct Arctic breeding regions.** *Branta hutchinsii* was recognized with four subspecies. **B. h. minima** (Ridgway's, the smallest) breeds on the **Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta** in western Alaska and winters primarily in the **Willamette Valley** of Oregon — the most common wintering Cackling Goose in the PNW. **B. h. leucopareia** (Aleutian) breeds on the **Aleutian Islands** and winters in California — a former endangered species recovered through fox eradication on breeding islands, parallel to the **Trumpeter Swan** recovery documented at degree 128. **B. h. taverneri** (Taverner's) breeds in interior Alaska, intermediate in size, and winters in Pacific states. **B. h. hutchinsii** (Richardson's) breeds in Arctic Canada and winters primarily in the southern Great Plains — less common in the PNW.
- **The higher-pitched cackle as acoustic diagnostic.** The Cackling Goose's call is **higher-pitched, more rapid, squeakier** — a quick sharp "cack-cack-cack" or "yip-yip" distinctly higher and thinner than the Canada Goose's two-syllable "ah-HONK." Frequency range 400-2,000 Hz with energy centered at 600-1,200 Hz — distinctly higher than the Canada Goose's 300-1,000 Hz energy center. The pitch difference is often the fastest way to separate Cackling from Canada Goose at a distance, especially in overhead flyovers or mixed-flock conditions where size judgment is difficult. The higher pitch directly reflects the smaller body: smaller body means smaller **syrinx** means higher pitch — the same body-size-to-pitch principle documented across the goose era.
- **Willamette Valley as primary PNW wintering ground.** The **Willamette Valley** in Oregon is the primary wintering ground for the smallest subspecies *B. h. minima*, which breeds on the Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta in western Alaska. **Tens of thousands** of these small geese winter in the agricultural fields of the Willamette Valley, feeding on waste grain, grass, and clover. This is the densest concentration of Cackling Geese in the Pacific Northwest. The valley's agricultural intensification and waste-grain abundance following the harvest creates the substrate that sustains the winter population. Cackling Geese also winter in the **Skagit Valley** of Washington, mixing with large concentrations of Canada Geese, Snow Geese, and Trumpeter/Tundra Swans — here Cackling Geese are outnumbered by Canada Geese but remain a distinct presence in agricultural fields.
- **Purely migratory in contrast to the partially resident Canada Goose.** Unlike the **Canada Goose**, which has massive resident (non-migratory) populations in the Pacific Northwest from extensive 20th-century introductions and urban adaptation, the **Cackling Goose** is **purely migratory**. Every Cackling Goose in the PNW in winter bred in the Arctic that summer and will return to the Arctic in spring. There are **no resident Cackling Goose populations**. The migratory absolutism reflects the species' breeding-range specialization — the Arctic tundra is the only breeding habitat, and there is no temperate-zone equivalent to sustain year-round presence. This migratory purity makes the Cackling Goose's seasonal appearance a more clearly-defined phenomenon than the Canada Goose's year-round resident mixing.
- **Aleutian Cackling Goose recovery parallels the Trumpeter Swan recovery.** The **Aleutian Cackling Goose** (*B. h. leucopareia*) was driven to endangered status by Arctic foxes introduced to the Aleutian Islands for fur farming in the 19th and 20th centuries. The foxes preyed on nesting geese, decimating the breeding population. **Fox eradication programs** on the Aleutian Islands in the 1980s-2000s enabled the population to recover, and the subspecies was removed from the endangered species list. This recovery parallels the **Trumpeter Swan** recovery documented at degree 128 (19th-century market-hunting near-extirpation, Red Rock Lakes NWR protection in the 20th century, reintroduction programs, population recovery). Both geese/swans demonstrate that Anatidae conservation interventions can succeed when causal pressures are removed.
- **The higher cackle as 131st species-side acoustic element.** The rapid squeaky "cack-cack-cack" or "yip-yip" at 400-2,000 Hz with energy centered at 600-1,200 Hz becomes **the higher cackle** — the 131st species-side acoustic element in the engine's accumulating catalog. Distinct from the Canada Goose's deep honk at degree 129 (200-2,500 Hz with energy centered low, E=5, brassy), distinct from the Trumpeter Swan's resonant bugling at degree 128 (low-frequency boom, E=6, deep), distinct from the Tundra Swan's melodious baying at degree 127 (high-whooping flock chorus, E=5, ethereal). The higher cackle occupies its own acoustic category: rapid squeaky flock-driven higher-pitched vocalization, appropriate for mixed-flock coordination and acoustic differentiation within the same genus.
- **Goose comparison pair as acoustic differentiation within genus-level unity.** The completed Canada/Cackling comparison pair documents that apparent genus-level unity (both *Branta*, both large waterfowl, both flock-forming winter visitors to the PNW) contains substantial species-level acoustic differentiation. **Canada Goose** at E=5 versus **Cackling Goose** at E=4. **Deep honk** versus **higher cackle**. **200-2,500 Hz low-centered** versus **400-2,000 Hz high-centered**. **Rolling honk wall** versus **rapid chattering texture**. **Two-syllable "ah-HONK"** versus **quick sharp "cack-cack-cack."** The acoustic differences mirror and exceed the size differences, and the completed pair becomes a reference for how same-genus species can differentiate acoustically to support sympatric winter coexistence in shared flocks and habitats.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Institutional memory is a structural asset that specific individuals embody.** Cheryl Waters' continuous presence across the 2001 KCMU-to-KEXP transition is the structural argument for why individual-human-continuity matters at institutions that undergo major transformations. Without a continuous on-air voice, the station would have risked losing its college-radio roots during the transition to professional independent public radio. Future entries documenting institutional transformations should identify the continuity-embodying individuals and treat their longevity as a first-class structural dimension rather than as biographical detail.
2. **Taxonomic splits are historical events with specific calendar dates and publishing authorities.** The **2004 AOU split** of *Branta canadensis* into *Branta canadensis* (Canada Goose) and *Branta hutchinsii* (Cackling Goose) is documented to a specific year, a specific publishing authority (Banks et al. in the 45th supplement to the AOU Check-list), and a specific genetic evidence base (molecular genetic studies of the late 1990s and early 2000s). Future taxonomic-split documentation should anchor to calendar dates, authority publications, and evidence bases rather than treating splits as ahistorical facts.
3. **Comparison pairs document same-category differentiation and reward adjacent-degree placement.** The Canada/Cackling Goose comparison pair (129-130) is the fifth comparison pair in SPS, and adjacent-degree placement allows direct acoustic and morphological contrast. Future comparison pairs should continue this adjacent-degree placement discipline, and the pattern of comparison pairs should be tracked as its own structural dimension alongside streak records and individual entries.
4. **Triads within longer arcs generate anticipatory structural weight.** The KEXP host trio (130-132: Waters, Richards, Mazzone) opens at degree 130 within the longer five-degree radio-figure arc (126-130). The trio structure creates anticipatory weight — future degrees have preordained structural roles — and the opener-middle-closer pattern becomes legible when a trio is recognized rather than just a sequence of related entries. Future sub-arc openings should be named as triads or tetrads when the structure supports it.
5. **Purely migratory species differ structurally from partially resident species even within the same genus.** The Cackling Goose (purely migratory) and Canada Goose (partially resident in PNW) share a genus but differ in migratory strategy, and the difference has ecological and observational consequences. Future genus-level comparisons should track migratory strategy as a first-class structural dimension alongside body size, habitat, and call.
6. **Subspecies-level documentation matters when the species contains distinct regional populations.** The Cackling Goose is not a single population but four subspecies (*minima*, *leucopareia*, *taverneri*, *hutchinsii*) with distinct breeding and wintering ranges. The aggregate species-level description obscures internal structure. Future polytypic species should be documented at subspecies level when the subspecies occupy distinct biogeographic ranges, and the within-species complexity should be treated as a structural dimension.
7. **Arctic-tundra breeders depend on compressed breeding-season ecology.** The Cackling Goose's Arctic tundra breeding requires quick pair formation, rapid nest building, tight incubation schedules, and compressed gosling rearing within the brief Arctic summer. This compressed ecology is itself a structural principle — it shapes the species' migration timing, population recovery rates, and vulnerability to climate change.
