## DJ B-Mello -- The Energy Surge

DJ B-Mello brings **hip-hop back to radio**. Nasty Nes Rodriguez (126) introduced hip-hop to the radio sequence as a pioneer -- the first hip-hop radio DJ in the Pacific Northwest, broadcasting from KCMU in the 1980s. Eight degrees later, B-Mello represents hip-hop's continued presence on independent radio, now through KEXP's contemporary programming.

E=5. This is the first E=5 since These Arms Are Snakes at degree 125 -- nine degrees of E=3-4 in between. The radio sequence has been deliberately restrained: radio hosts and label founders operate at lower energy than performing musicians. B-Mello breaks that pattern. A DJ performs. A DJ brings physical energy -- the scratching, the mixing, the crowd interaction. The "Hip-Hop/DJ" designation reflects both genre and craft.

**Capitol Hill, KEXP 90.3 FM.** B-Mello is the ninth consecutive radio/media figure (126-134) and the fifth consecutive KEXP figure (130-134). The KEXP sequence deepens: Waters (afternoon indie), Richards (morning emotional), Mazzone (world music), Cole (program director), B-Mello (hip-hop DJ). Five KEXP figures, five different roles, one station's breadth.

## Ross's Goose -- The Final Anatidae

The Ross's Goose (*Anser rossii*) is the Snow Goose's smaller cousin -- and the **last Anatidae in the engine**. Degree 134 closes a streak that began at degree 118 with the Harlequin Duck. Seventeen consecutive degrees. Seventeen species. The engine's definitive waterfowl survey ends here.

The sound: a **higher, more nasal call** than the Snow Goose. Where the Snow Goose (133) produces a shrill, dissonant flock call, the Ross's Goose is softer, higher-pitched, and more nasal. The comparison is immediate: in mixed flocks, the Ross's Goose sounds like a smaller, quieter version of the Snow Goose. Which is essentially what it is.

E=4. Lower energy than the Snow Goose (E=5). The size difference matters acoustically -- Ross's Goose is significantly smaller than Snow Goose, and the call reflects it. Less volume, less carrying power, higher pitch.

**Snow/Ross's comparison pair.** Degrees 133-134 present the classic birding identification challenge. Snow Geese and Ross's Geese winter in the same flocks, forage in the same fields, and at a distance look nearly identical. Birders learn to distinguish them by size (Ross's is smaller), bill shape (Ross's has a stubbier bill without the Snow Goose's "grinning patch"), and call (Ross's is higher and more nasal). The engine places them in consecutive degrees -- the comparison pair lives together in the progression as they live together in the field.
