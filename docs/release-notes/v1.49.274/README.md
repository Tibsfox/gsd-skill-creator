**Part B: Ross's Goose and the Anatidae Streak Closure.** The **Ross's Goose** (*Anser rossii*) is North America's smallest goose — body length 53-66 cm (21-26 inches), weight 1.0-1.6 kg (2.2-3.5 lbs), wingspan 113-123 cm — about **two-thirds the length and half the weight** of a Snow Goose. The size difference is dramatic, comparable to the Cackling/Canada Goose difference documented at degrees 130/129. The bill is the diagnostic field mark: **short, stubby, triangular**, distinctly shorter and stubbier than the Snow Goose's longer bill, and critically **lacking the black "grinning patch"** (the dark line along the bill's cutting edge) that is diagnostic of Snow Goose. The bill base may show **warty protuberances (caruncles)** in adult males — rough bluish-gray bumps unique to Ross's Goose. White morph Ross's Goose is entirely white with black wingtips, virtually identical to white morph Snow Goose except for size and bill. The **blue morph Ross's Goose** exists but is extremely rare, far rarer than the blue morph Snow Goose. Most Ross's Geese in the PNW are white morph. The head is rounder and more compact than Snow Goose, and the overall impression is of a cuter miniaturized Snow Goose. The species is named after **Bernard Rogan Ross** (1827-1874), a **Hudson's Bay Company** chief factor at Fort Resolution on Great Slave Lake in what is now the Northwest Territories of Canada. Ross collected natural history specimens and shipped them to the **Smithsonian Institution**, where ornithologist **John Cassin** described the goose as a new species in 1861. Ross never visited the birds' breeding grounds — the Arctic tundra nesting sites weren't documented until the 1930s. The species' primary breeding grounds are the **Queen Maud Gulf Migratory Bird Sanctuary** in the central Canadian Arctic, one of the most important waterfowl breeding areas in North America.

**The Higher Nasal Call as Acoustic Diagnostic.** The Ross's Goose vocalization is diagnostically different from the Snow Goose despite the species' similar appearance. The call is a **higher-pitched, nasal, squeaky** "kak" or short high grunt — weaker and squeakier than the Snow Goose's shrill forceful one-syllable "honk." Frequency range 800-5,000 Hz, higher than the Snow Goose's 600-4,000 Hz — the highest-pitched goose call in the engine. The upshift in frequency is consistent with the smaller body size (smaller syrinx = higher fundamental frequency). Volume is noticeably quieter than Snow Goose; the smaller body produces less acoustic power. In a mixed Snow/Ross's flock, the Ross's calls are often masked by the louder Snow Goose calls, but focused listening reveals the higher-pitched squeakier quality — like a **piccolo note within an orchestra's brass section**. Experienced birders listen for the pitch difference to locate Ross's Geese in mixed flocks, and the voice is often the first detection cue. A pure flock of Ross's Geese produces a higher-pitched thinner version of the Snow Goose flock sound — squeaky chattering rather than shrill roaring, thinner and more compact. The acoustic diagnostic value is high despite the weaker volume: in the field the voice finds the bird before the eyes do.

- **The higher nasal call as acoustic diagnostic.** The Ross's Goose vocalization is **diagnostically distinct** from the Snow Goose despite the species' similar plumage. The call is a higher-pitched, nasal, squeaky "kak" or short high grunt — **weaker and squeakier** than the Snow Goose's shrill forceful one-syllable "honk." Frequency range **800-5,000 Hz**, higher than the Snow Goose's 600-4,000 Hz — the highest-pitched goose call in the engine. The upshift in frequency is consistent with the smaller body size (smaller syrinx = higher fundamental frequency). Volume is noticeably quieter than Snow Goose; the smaller body produces less acoustic power. In a mixed Snow/Ross's flock, the Ross's calls are often masked by the louder Snow Goose calls, but focused listening reveals the higher-pitched squeakier quality — like a **piccolo note within an orchestra's brass section**. Experienced birders listen for the pitch difference to locate Ross's Geese in mixed flocks, and the voice is often the first detection cue. A pure flock of Ross's Geese produces a higher-pitched thinner version of the Snow Goose flock sound — squeaky chattering rather than shrill roaring. The difference is immediately apparent to anyone who has heard both.
- **North America's smallest goose: size, plumage, and field identification.** Ross's Goose is **North America's smallest goose** — body length 53-66 cm (21-26 inches), weight **1.0-1.6 kg** (2.2-3.5 lbs), wingspan 113-123 cm. **About two-thirds the length and half the weight of a Snow Goose.** This is a striking size difference, comparable to Cackling Goose versus Canada Goose at degrees 130/129. The bill is the diagnostic field mark: **short, stubby, triangular**, distinctly shorter and stubbier than the Snow Goose's longer bill, critically **lacking the black "grinning patch"** (dark line along the bill's cutting edge) that is diagnostic of Snow Goose. The bill base may show **warty protuberances (caruncles)** in adult males — rough bluish-gray bumps unique to Ross's Goose. Plumage is white with black wingtips, virtually identical to white morph Snow Goose except for size and bill. The **blue morph Ross's Goose** exists but is extremely rare — far rarer than the blue morph Snow Goose. Most Ross's Geese in the PNW are white morph. Head shape is rounder and more compact than Snow Goose, and the overall impression is of a **cuter, miniaturized** Snow Goose. In mixed flocks (the typical PNW encounter), look for: (1) significantly smaller size, (2) short stubby bill without grinning patch, (3) rounder head, (4) higher-pitched call. These four characters together are diagnostic.
- **Bernard Rogan Ross and the 1861 species description.** The species is named after **Bernard Rogan Ross** (1827-1874), a **Hudson's Bay Company** chief factor at **Fort Resolution** on **Great Slave Lake** in what is now the Northwest Territories of Canada. Ross collected natural history specimens during his HBC tenure and shipped them to the **Smithsonian Institution**, where ornithologist **John Cassin** described the goose as a new species in **1861**. Ross himself never visited the birds' breeding grounds — the Arctic tundra nesting sites weren't documented until the **1930s**, some seventy years after the species description. The honorific-naming convention reflects the 19th-century natural-history collection practice of HBC employees as specimen-collectors for Eastern institutions, and the Smithsonian-Cassin description pathway is typical of North American ornithological species descriptions of the mid-19th century.
- **Queen Maud Gulf Migratory Bird Sanctuary as primary breeding ground.** Ross's Goose breeds primarily in the **Queen Maud Gulf Migratory Bird Sanctuary** in the central Canadian Arctic — one of the most important waterfowl breeding areas in North America. The sanctuary supports major concentrations of Snow Goose and Ross's Goose during the Arctic summer breeding season. The growing Ross's Goose population, combined with Snow Goose overgrazing, raises active concerns about **habitat degradation** in this critical area — the same conservation-irony pattern documented at degree 133 for Snow Goose. The breeding-range specialization (Arctic tundra only, no temperate-zone equivalent) makes the species purely migratory — every Ross's Goose in the PNW in winter bred in the Arctic that summer and will return to the Arctic in spring. There are no resident Ross's Goose populations, unlike the Canada Goose's extensive resident populations in the PNW.
- **Skagit Valley and Willamette Valley winter presence: hiding in the Snow Goose flocks.** Ross's Goose in the PNW is primarily encountered **mixed in Snow Goose flocks** rather than forming large independent flocks. **Skagit Valley, Washington** hosts Ross's Geese among the massive Snow Goose concentrations — a careful scan of a Snow Goose flock may reveal dozens to a few hundred Ross's Geese among thousands of Snow Geese. **Willamette Valley, Oregon** agricultural fields host wintering Ross's Geese in mixed goose flocks. **Ridgefield NWR** and scattered sites throughout lowland western Washington and Oregon host scattered records. - **Population explosion from ~6,000 in 1950s to 1-2 million today.** Ross's Goose shares the **Snow Goose's conservation irony** in miniature. Historical rarity was genuine: the total population was estimated at only **6,000 birds in the 1950s**, and conservation measures and harvest restrictions were implemented. Population **explosion** followed — from roughly 6,000 in the 1950s to an estimated **1-2 million or more today**. The growth rate has been even more dramatic than the Snow Goose's in percentage terms. Unlike Snow Goose, Ross's Goose was genuinely rare within living memory — its recovery from ~6,000 to over a million is an extraordinary conservation success story *before* it became a potential ecological problem, and the species' arc encapsulates rarity → conservation → recovery → overshoot in a single lifetime.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Streak-closure events can register as structural completions rather than merely as terminations.** The Anatidae streak's closure at seventeen with the Ross's Goose is not a mere stopping point — it is the structural completion that the streak has been building toward since degree 118. All major subfamilies documented, both small-large comparison pairs within goose genera completed, energy-arc narrative (moderate opening, E=6 climax, descent, E=5 surge, E=4 coda) resolved. Future streak-closure events should be documented with this completion-rather-than-termination framing, naming what structural work the streak did and why the specific closure-degree is the correct endpoint.
2. **Energy-surge positioning matters — the streak's maximum register should arrive at the position where it generates maximum contrast.** B-Mello's E=5 at degree 134 arrives after four degrees of E=3-E=4 KEXP DJ streak entries (Waters 130, Richards 131, Mazzone 132, Cole 133). The surge-after-quiet structure generates more structural impact than the same E=5 would have at degree 130 (where it would have disrupted the infrastructure zone) or degree 132 (where it would have interrupted the curatorial-voice documentation). Future streak-closer positions should prefer maximum-register entries over baseline entries to send the streak out on its highest structural note.
3. **Bookend structures turn linear arcs into closed narratives with beginning-middle-end shape.** The nine-degree radio-axis arc (126-134) opens with Nasty Nes Rodriguez's hip-hop pioneering at 126 and closes with B-Mello's hip-hop contemporary-era at 134. The bookend turns an otherwise open-ended sequence of radio figures into a bounded narrative. Future multi-degree arcs should be designed with explicit bookends when the thematic material supports it, and the bookends should be named rather than left implicit.
4. **Comparison-pair mirror patterns can repeat within the same streak when body-size differentiation within genera is a recurring principle.** The Anatidae streak contains three internal comparison pairs: Tundra/Trumpeter Swan (127-128 within *Cygnus*), Canada/Cackling Goose (129-130 within *Branta*), and Snow/Ross's Goose (133-134 within *Anser*). The pattern repeats because body-size differentiation within genera is a consistent Anatidae principle. Future multi-entry family streaks should be examined for internal comparison-pair potential, and when the principle holds, the pairs should be placed adjacently for direct contrast.
5. **Historical-context honorific naming grounds species in their discovery pathway even when the name-giver never visited the species.** Bernard Rogan Ross never visited the Arctic tundra breeding grounds of the goose named for him — the Smithsonian shipment pathway from Fort Resolution, John Cassin's 1861 description, and the 1930s Arctic breeding-ground documentation are the full discovery arc. Future honorific-named species should be documented with the specific collection-and-description pathway rather than just the person-name, and gaps between description and biology (here, seventy years) should be noted as structurally significant.
6. **Population-explosion conservation-ironies repeat across closely related species under the same ecological drivers.** Ross's Goose exploded from ~6,000 in the 1950s to 1-2 million today under the same agricultural-subsidy pressures that exploded Snow Goose populations. The conservation-irony pattern repeats because the ecological driver is shared. Future closely-related species should be examined for parallel population-trajectory patterns, and when the patterns match, the common driver should be named as a structural principle rather than documented as coincidence.
7. **Genre-energy calibrations reflect host-craft demands rather than free-floating qualitative assignments.** B-Mello at E=5 is one notch above the other KEXP hosts (who operate at E=3-E=4) because hip-hop DJ performance demands kinetic energy that indie/world/infrastructure curation does not. The energy calibration reflects craft demands rather than host personality. Future radio/DJ entries should calibrate energy against genre-and-craft requirements rather than against host demeanor, and the E-value should be defensible as a craft-requirement registration.
8. **Return-to-opening-position events generate bookend-completion structural weight that linear arcs lack.** Hip-hop returns to the radio-axis arc at B-Mello (134) after opening at Nasty Nes (126). The return is the bookend-completion event that gives the arc its closed-narrative shape. Future long arcs should be designed with return-to-opening-position potential when the thematic material supports it, and the return events should be named as bookend-completions rather than merely as thread-continuities.
9. **Gentle-coda closures can end streaks more effectively than climactic closures.** The Anatidae streak closes at Ross's Goose E=4 rather than at the Trumpeter Swan's E=6 climax (degree 128). The E=4 gentle coda is the correct closure because the streak's climax has already been registered, and ending at maximum register would have been structurally wasteful. Future streak-closures should prefer gentle-coda closures after the climax has been registered, with the coda providing narrative closure rather than attempting to top the climax.
10. **Family-boundary transitions should be anticipated but not prematurely developed.** Degree 135's **Great Horned Owl** (Strigidae) opens a new family, a new order (Strigiformes), and a new ecological niche (apex nocturnal raptor). The transition is anticipated at degree 134's closure but not developed in detail because degree 135 deserves its own full degree of research. Future family-boundary transitions should be named as forthcoming structural events at the closing degree but deferred for full development at the opening degree.
