## Summary

**The Wailers** arrive at E=7 -- the Tacoma band, emphatically not the Jamaican reggae group, that recorded **"Tall Cool One" in 1959**, the **first Pacific Northwest single to chart nationally**. This distinction matters: before The Wailers charted, the Pacific Northwest existed at the margins of American popular music geography. After "Tall Cool One" reached the national charts, it was documented that this region could produce music that traveled beyond its own borders on its own terms. The Wailers are the opening entry in the Pacific Northwest rock narrative, and their placement at degree 242 -- deep in the Garage Rock Era, surrounded by The Sonics and The Kingsmen -- correctly situates them as the founding generation of a tradition that would run for six decades. The Wailers were from Tacoma, and their Tacoma identity is significant: Tacoma in the early 1960s was the center of the Pacific Northwest rock world, not Seattle. The Sonics (241) were Tacoma. The Wailers (242) were Tacoma. The myth of Seattle-as-origin is a later construction; the actual origin was south along the Sound, in a city with a working port, a working-class economy, and bands that played loud because the rooms they played in required it.

On the species side, **Cutthroat Trout** closes the Salmonidae arc with a **silent/dimple** at E=2 -- the final entry of the Silent Fish Trio and the last salmonid in the engine's dedicated run through the family. *Oncorhynchus clarkii* is the trout with the red slash marks under the jaw, the fish named by Lewis and Clark on the Corps of Discovery expedition, the fish that exists in more subspecies and local variants than any other Pacific salmonid. The "dimple" is the specific sound of a trout feeding at the surface -- a circular disturbance, a ring expanding outward, the minimum possible intervention in the water's surface. Cutthroat Trout close the Salmonidae arc at E=2 because that is what a feeding trout sounds like from a river bank: almost nothing, a suggestion of presence, a ring on the water. The Salmonidae arc ran six consecutive degrees (237-242) and documented the full range from the body-slap of a leaping Chinook at E=6 to the dimple of a surface-feeding Cutthroat at E=2. It closes here at degree 242.

- **Part B -- SPS:** Cutthroat Trout (silent/dimple, E=2) -- **SALMONIDAE ARC CLOSES**, Silent Fish Trio completes, Lewis and Clark's named trout, surface-feeding dimple
- **SALMONIDAE ARC CLOSES** -- six consecutive degrees (237-242) documented the core Pacific salmon and trout species of the Pacific Northwest: Chinook (E=6), Coho (E=6), Steelhead (E=5), Bull Trout (E=2), Dolly Varden (E=2), Cutthroat Trout (E=2); the arc moved from the largest, loudest, most culturally visible salmon species to the smallest, quietest, most interior trout species; it documented both the commercially fished species that built the PNW economy and the wilderness-indicator species that reveal the health of intact watersheds; the arc closes at degree 242 and will not reopen -- the next SPS entries will be outside the Salmonidae family entirely
- Tacoma as PNW rock origin -- the concentration of historically significant bands in Tacoma is not accidental; Tacoma in the late 1950s and early 1960s had the combination of factors that produce original music: a working port with connections to diverse musical influences, a working-class population that attended live music regularly, venues that paid for volume and energy, and a geographic position between Seattle and Portland that made it the natural hub of the regional circuit; The Wailers (1959) and The Sonics (1960) both emerged from this context within a year of each other, and both produced nationally significant music; the Seattle origin myth for PNW rock is a 1990s construction that the engine's chronological documentation systematically corrects
- Cutthroat Trout subspecies and Lewis and Clark -- *Oncorhynchus clarkii* includes more than a dozen recognized subspecies across the Pacific Northwest, Rocky Mountains, and Great Basin; the Coastal Cutthroat (*O. c. clarkii*) is the Puget Sound form, an anadromous and resident species that inhabits coastal streams and the nearshore Sound environment; the Westslope Cutthroat (*O. c. lewisi*) is the interior form; the cutthroat pattern -- the red or orange slash marks under the jaw -- is consistent across subspecies; Meriwether Lewis described the species in his journals on June 13, 1805, during the Corps of Discovery expedition; it is the trout that carries the history of western scientific documentation
- Silent Fish Trio complete -- Bull Trout (240, E=2), Dolly Varden (241, E=2), Cutthroat Trout (242, E=2) constitute three consecutive near-silent SPS entries while the S36 track ran at E=6, E=8, E=7; the three-degree cross-track divergence -- garage rock at or near the engine's maximum while wilderness trout barely register -- is the engine's definitive compositional statement about the relationship between human cultural intensity and ecological quietness
