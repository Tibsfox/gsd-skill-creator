## Mission Overview

Suzaku (ASTRO-EII) is the obs#15 continuation of the X-ray-astronomy axis and the broadband-sensitivity adjacent-sustain of the focusing-imaging branch — the JAXA/ISAS X-ray observatory (pre-launch ASTRO-EII, named Suzaku, the Vermilion Bird of the South, after launch), Japan's 5th cosmic X-ray astronomy satellite and the recovery of ASTRO-E, whose defining capability is the most sensitive simultaneous soft-to-hard broadband X-ray spectroscopy of its era — a focusing soft-X-ray imager (the XIS CCDs, fed by the thin-foil XRT) co-pointed with a collimated hard-X-ray detector (the HXD) on the lowest non-imaging hard-X-ray background then flown — plus the first X-ray micro-calorimeter (the XRS) carried to orbit. Launched 10 July 2005 (~12:30 JST) on an M-V (Mu-5, flight M-V-6) from Uchinoura Space Center, Japan, into a near-circular low Earth orbit (altitude ~570 km, inclination ~31°, period ~96 min; NSSDCA / COSPAR ID 2005-025A; SATCAT 28773; ~1,706 kg). The five co-aligned XRT telescopes use a conical-approximation (segmented conical) Wolter-I design with 175 nested gold-coated aluminum foils each (~155 µm aluminum substrate + epoxy coupling + replicated gold reflecting surface), GSFC-led (Serlemitsos foil lineage, co-developed with Nagoya University and ISAS): four XRT-I units at 4.75 m focal length feed the four XIS CCD cameras (XIS0–XIS3; MIT Lincoln Laboratory CCID41 devices, three front-illuminated plus one back-illuminated XIS1, the refined successors to ASCA's SIS), and one XRT-S unit at 4.50 m focal length feeds the XRS. The XRT band is ~0.2–12 keV with ~440 cm² at 1.5 keV (~250 cm² at 8 keV) per telescope — a larger soft-band area than ASCA (~350 at 1.5 keV) but not the largest (XMM-Newton owns the largest pointed effective area); the angular resolution is ~1.8–2.3 arcmin HPD (≈ ~2 arcmin; coarse foil-lineage, not sub-arcsecond). The XIS band is 0.2–12 keV over an 18×18 arcmin field with ~130 eV FWHM at 6 keV MODERATE spectral resolution; XIS2 was lost 9 November 2006 (an electrode-to-buried-channel charge short, suspected micrometeoroid), after which the instrument operated with three CCDs. The HXD (64 silicon PIN diodes plus 16 GSO/BGO phoswich well-counters inside 20 BGO anti-coincidence counters forming a well-type active shield) covers 10–600 keV (~142 cm² at 20 keV, ~273 cm² at 150 keV) as a collimated, non-imaging instrument. The XRS (32-channel micro-calorimeter, ~6 eV at 6 keV, band 0.3–12 keV, cooled to 60 mK by a four-stage chain: ADR + superfluid-helium cryostat + solid-neon dewar + a single-stage Stirling-cycle mechanical cooler) was the first X-ray micro-calorimeter flown to orbit; its liquid helium was lost to space 8 August 2005, about four weeks after launch, so it returned no celestial science. Communications became intermittent 1 June 2015 (attitude-control and battery degradation), mission completion was announced 26 August 2015, and operations formally ended 2 September 2015 after ~10 productive science years. **SUZAKU-FIRST-INSTANCE:** Suzaku (朱雀, the Vermilion Bird of the South; pre-launch name ASTRO-EII / ASTRO-E2) enters the catalog as Japan's 5th cosmic X-ray astronomy satellite (after Hakucho, Tenma, Ginga, ASCA), built and led by JAXA / ISAS (Japan) and the rebuild and recovery of ASTRO-E, which was lost in the M-V-4 launch anomaly of 10 February 2000 — the first instance, the obs#1 first — sustaining the X-ray-astronomy axis at obs#15. COSPAR 2005-025A; SATCAT 28773. Launched 10 July 2005 (~12:30 JST) on an M-V (Mu-5, flight M-V-6) from Uchinoura Space Center, Japan — a Japanese site, NOT a US site — into a near-circular low Earth orbit (altitude ~570 km, inclination ~31°, period ~96 min); ~1,706 kg, about four times the mass of ASCA. Payload: five co-aligned thin-foil X-Ray Telescopes (XRT) feeding the XIS (X-ray Imaging Spectrometer, four CCD cameras), the HXD (Hard X-ray Detector, collimated non-imaging), and the XRS (X-Ray Spectrometer, a micro-calorimeter). Suzaku is NOT a NASA mission — JAXA/ISAS led the mission, bus, launch, and operations; the NASA/US contribution is the five XRT foil telescopes (NASA/GSFC-led, Serlemitsos foil lineage co-developed with Nagoya University and ISAS), the XRS micro-calorimeter (detector array GSFC + University of Wisconsin; ADR and superfluid-helium dewar GSFC; solid-neon dewar and mechanical cooler Sumitomo Heavy Industries), and the XIS CCD sensors (US/MIT Lincoln Laboratory CCID41) plus a US Guest Observer Facility at GSFC — "GSFC-led foil optics, co-developed with Nagoya and ISAS," never "NASA built Suzaku," and never "GSFC built the XIS." Operated 2005–2015; communications intermittent from 1 June 2015, mission completion announced 26 August 2015, operations formally ended 2 September 2015 — a long successful mission ending naturally after ~10 science years, the longest-lived Japanese X-ray observatory.

**FIRST-ORBITAL-X-RAY-MICROCALORIMETER-FLOWN:** the XRS was the first X-ray micro-calorimeter spectrometer flown to orbit aboard an observatory (a 32-pixel micro-calorimeter array, ~6 eV resolution at 6 keV, operating at 60 mK behind a four-stage cooling chain: ADR + superfluid-helium cryostat + solid-neon dewar + a single-stage Stirling-cycle mechanical cooler; Kelley et al. 2007, PASJ 59, S77). The claim is a BOUNDED HARDWARE-FLIGHT first ONLY: the liquid helium was lost to space 8 August 2005, about four weeks after launch, via a thermal short between the helium and neon tanks, so the XRS returned NO celestial science observations. ASTRO-E's earlier XRS (2000) never reached a usable orbit; X-ray Quantum Calorimeter (XQC) payloads are suborbital sounding-rocket flights. The DELIVERED micro-calorimeter science form belongs to later missions: Hitomi/ASTRO-H SXS (2016) produced the first micro-calorimeter celestial spectroscopy before its loss, and XRISM/Resolve (X-ray-astronomy obs#7, 2023) established the first sustained, operational micro-calorimeter observatory. The qualifier is mandatory: NEVER "first micro-calorimeter spectroscopy" — only "first one flown to orbit."

**LOWEST-NON-IMAGING-HARD-X-RAY-BACKGROUND:** the HXD (64 silicon PIN diodes plus 16 GSO/BGO phoswich well-counters, surrounded by 20 BGO anti-coincidence counters forming a well-type active shield) achieved the lowest residual non-X-ray background among past NON-IMAGING hard-X-ray instruments, in the 15–70 keV and 150–500 keV bands (Kokubun et al. 2007, PASJ 59, S53; measured against RXTE-PCA, RXTE-HEXTE, and BeppoSAX-PDS), giving the most sensitive broadband hard-X-ray continuum and line spectroscopy of faint sources of its era. The qualifier "non-imaging" is mandatory: a focusing hard-X-ray telescope concentrates flux and reaches a lower background per resolution element. The HXD is COLLIMATED and NON-IMAGING — it does NOT own hard-X-ray focusing (NuSTAR, obs#2) and is NOT the first hard-X-ray detector (Ginga, HEAO-1 and earlier missions came before). The low, stable particle background of the ~570 km low Earth orbit is the direct enabler of the result.

**FIRST-ICM-TEMPERATURE-PROFILES-TO-THE-VIRIAL-RADIUS:** Suzaku's low, stable particle background (a direct consequence of the HXD active shield and the low-Earth orbit) enabled the first robust measurements of galaxy-cluster intracluster-medium (ICM) temperature, entropy, and abundance profiles out to approximately the virial radius — the cluster outskirts, Suzaku's signature science legacy (George et al. 2009, PKS 0745−191, MNRAS 395, 657, DOI 10.1111/j.1365-2966.2009.14547.x; and the broader Suzaku cluster-outskirts program). The scope is "first ROBUST ICM measurements TO THE VIRIAL RADIUS" — a science-result first enabled by the low background, NOT a survey first (eROSITA, obs#12) and NOT an imaging first. The faint, extended emission of the outer cluster is exactly the regime the low background opened, where prior instruments' particle background swamped the signal.

**X-RAY-ASTRONOMY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION:** The X-ray-astronomy axis Chandra opened at obs#1, NuSTAR extended at obs#2, RXTE/IXPE/NICER/Swift/XRISM continued at obs#3–7, Einstein continued as the focusing-imaging origin at obs#8, Uhuru continued as the survey/catalog origin at obs#9, HEAO-1 sustained as the deeper non-imaging survey-sustain at obs#10, ROSAT sustained as the first imaging all-sky survey at obs#11, eROSITA sustained as the first CCD-detector all-sky imaging survey at obs#12, XMM-Newton sustained as the largest-effective-area pointed focusing observatory at obs#13, and ASCA sustained as the first union of imaging optics with X-ray CCDs at obs#14 now sustains as an adjacent-sustain observation at obs#15 (axis-rotation #35 sustains, the 6th consecutive sustain) via Suzaku — the most sensitive simultaneous soft-to-hard broadband spectroscopy of its era (the soft XIS CCDs and the hard collimated HXD on the lowest non-imaging hard-X-ray background) plus the first X-ray micro-calorimeter flown to orbit, advancing the focusing-imaging branch from the imaging-telescope origin (Einstein, obs#8), the sub-arcsecond sharpness (Chandra, obs#1), the hard band (NuSTAR, obs#2), the largest collecting area (XMM-Newton, obs#13), and the first CCD imaging spectroscopy (ASCA, obs#14). Continuation by observable form, not by chronology: Suzaku (2005) advances the focusing-imaging branch Einstein (1978) originated and ASCA (1993) carried, placed at obs#15 by observable FORM and not by launch date — as Einstein (obs#8) and Uhuru (obs#9) precede the obs#1–7 missions in time; it is the direct successor to ASCA, extending the same Serlemitsos thin-foil lineage and adding the collimated hard-X-ray HXD and the XRS micro-calorimeter.

## Surprises

- **A JAXA/ISAS mission of Japan — not a NASA mission — flew the first X-ray micro-calorimeter to orbit and read the most sensitive broadband spectroscopy of its era.** Suzaku was built and operated by JAXA / ISAS (Japan's 5th X-ray satellite, the recovery of ASTRO-E) and launched on a Japanese M-V, with the NASA/US contribution confined to the GSFC-led XRT foil optics, the GSFC-led XRS micro-calorimeter, and the MIT-led XIS CCDs plus the GOF at GSFC; the XRS was the first X-ray micro-calorimeter flown to orbit, and the soft XIS plus the hard HXD read one source across two co-pointed bands at once on the lowest non-imaging hard-X-ray background then flown.

- **A deep, quiet detector well opened the faint hard-X-ray sky.** The HXD's well-type BGO active anti-coincidence shield rejected the particle background so thoroughly that it reached the lowest residual non-X-ray background among past non-imaging hard-X-ray instruments (15–70 & 150–500 keV; Kokubun et al. 2007), giving the most sensitive broadband hard-X-ray continuum and line spectroscopy of faint sources of its era — collimated and non-imaging, the qualifier kept.

## Dedication

v1.237 Suzaku is dedicated to the people who built and flew the satellite Japan named for the Vermilion Bird of the South — the JAXA/ISAS observatory that read one source across two co-pointed bands at once and carried the first X-ray micro-calorimeter to orbit, rebuilt and recovered after ASTRO-E was lost in 2000: the Institute of Space and Astronautical Science and JAXA teams in Japan who built, launched, and operated it. Yasuo Tanaka, Kazuhisa Mitsuda, and the project teams who led it; Peter Serlemitsos and the Goddard group who designed the thin-foil X-Ray Telescopes, with Nagoya University; the MIT Lincoln Laboratory and Kyoto teams behind the XIS cameras; Tadayuki Takahashi, Motohide Kokubun, and the HXD group who built the quiet, deep detector well; Richard Kelley, Caroline Stahle, the Goddard and Wisconsin teams, and Sumitomo Heavy Industries, who flew the micro-calorimeter and its cooling chain; Matt George and the cluster-outskirts observers who first read the intracluster medium to the virial radius; the Goddard Guest Observer Facility; and Riccardo Giacconi and Bruno Rossi, who founded the field with the 1962 rocket. From a low orbit, a soft band and a hard band were read together, in vermilion light.
