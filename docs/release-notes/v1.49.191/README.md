The cultural vocal learning dimension is what sets the Bewick's Wren apart from every other species in the engine. Young males settle on a territory in their first breeding season and memorize the songs of the males already holding adjacent territories. They copy these songs, building a repertoire of 9-22 distinct song types from the local acoustic environment rather than from inherited or innately programmed patterns. Because males learn from their neighbors, and their neighbors learned from *their* neighbors, songs change gradually across the landscape. A Bewick's Wren in Seattle sings somewhat differently from a Bewick's Wren in Portland. The differences accumulate with distance. This is genuine cultural transmission -- learned behavior passed horizontally rather than vertically. The Bewick's Wren is a cultural animal: its song is not genetically determined but socially learned. This stands in sharp contrast to the Pacific Wren (degree 1), whose intricate cascade warble has a much more fixed, innately structured quality. *Thryomanes* -- from Ancient Greek *thryon* ("reed") + *manes* ("madness, passion, frenzy"). The genus name means "reed madness" or "reed frenzy" -- an evocative name that captures the wren's restless, hyperactive energy as it works through tangles and brush. The word *manes* carries the same root as "mania" -- this is a bird driven by passionate, frenetic energy, constantly in motion, constantly investigating every crevice and tangle in its territory. *Thryomanes* is a monotypic genus -- the Bewick's Wren is its only species. Named by **Audubon** in 1827 for **Thomas Bewick** (1753-1828), the English wood engraver and naturalist whose detailed bird engravings in *A History of British Birds* (published 1797 and 1804) set a new standard for natural history illustration. Full translation: "Bewick's reed-frenzy bird" -- a name that honors an engraver and describes a temperament. Compare the Pacific Wren's *Troglodytes pacificus* ("Pacific cave diver") at degree 1, which emphasized the bird's habit of ducking into dark crevices. *Thryomanes* emphasizes the behavioral state -- the frenetic energy itself. Two wren genera, two naming philosophies: habitat (*Troglodytes*, the cave) versus temperament (*Thryomanes*, the frenzy).

## Part B: Bewick's Wren -- The Yard Song

Full deep research covering the Bewick's Wren as the second Troglodytidae, the yard bird, and the first species defined by cultural vocal learning:

- **Troglodytidae Returns After 54 Degrees -- Longest Family Return Gap.** Wrens last appeared at degree 1 (Pacific Wren). The 54-degree gap surpasses Paridae (41 degrees, 9 to 50) and Picidae (32 degrees, 22 to 54). Two wrens, two worlds: Pacific Wren = hidden, cryptic, old-growth, 100+ notes per bout, innate cascade. Bewick's Wren = conspicuous, bold, suburban, 10-25 notes per phrase, culturally learned repertoire. Same family, same body mass (8-12 g), opposite strategies. Cave diver (*Troglodytes*) vs. reed madness (*Thryomanes*).
- **Two Wrens, Two Worlds.** Pacific Wren (degree 1): genus *Troglodytes*, old-growth understory, cascade of 100+ notes, fixed repertoire, limited variation, E=2. Bewick's Wren (degree 55): genus *Thryomanes*, brushy suburban, variable repertoire of short phrases, 9-22 learned song types, strong geographic dialects, E=3. Cascade = complexity through density. Yard song = complexity through diversity and cultural transmission. The engine now has the full wren spectrum from wilderness to garden.
- **Cultural Vocal Learning -- First Species Defined by Social Song Learning.** Young males learn from neighboring territory holders, not parents. 9-22 distinct song types per male. Geographic dialects changing gradually with distance. Genuine cultural transmission -- horizontal (neighbor to neighbor) not vertical (parent to offspring). The first species in the engine whose primary acoustic identity is defined by social learning. Stands in sharp contrast to Pacific Wren's innately structured cascade.
- **The Yard Song -- Fifty-Sixth Acoustic Element (Species-Side).** Loud, melodious, variable song of whistles, warbles, buzzes, trills. Named for three resonances: (1) the habitat -- the yard bird, suburban edge soundtrack; (2) the cultural landscape -- song varies geographically through neighbor learning; (3) the contrast -- what happens when wrens leave old-growth and move into the garden. Differs from sap-well rhythm (54, arrhythmic percussion) and cascade (1, density). The yard song is diversity through cultural transmission.
- **Taxonomy: Thryomanes Bewickii -- Reed Madness for Thomas Bewick.** *Thryomanes* = "reed madness" (Greek *thryon* + *manes*). Monotypic genus. Named by Audubon 1827 for Thomas Bewick (1753-1828), English wood engraver. Full translation: "Bewick's reed-frenzy bird." One of the few N.A. birds named for a European naturalist whose fame rested on illustration. Order Passeriformes, Family Troglodytidae (~88 species, New World).
- **The Sound: Master Vocalist.** Frequency 2.0-8.0 kHz (peak 3.0-6.0 kHz). Duration ~2-4 seconds. Whistle-trill-finish three-part structure. Bright, clear, musical. Higher than Song Sparrow, louder than Pacific Wren. Delivered from exposed perches. Scold call: 2.5-6.0 kHz. Buzz/chatter: 3.0-7.0 kHz. Contact call: 3.0-5.0 kHz.
- **Plumage: The Bold Supercilium.** Brown above (PNW form richer), pale gray below. Bold white eyebrow (supercilium) -- defining field mark. Long tail, frequently cocked and flicked. 13-14 cm, 8-12 g. Slender decurved bill. Confusion species: House Wren (no supercilium), Pacific Wren (smaller, darker, shorter tail), Marsh Wren (cattail marshes).
- **Eastern Range Contraction and House Wren Competition.** Nearly disappeared from eastern N.A. Displaced by House Wren (*Troglodytes aedon*) through cavity competition -- entering nests, destroying eggs, usurping sites. Western populations stable. PNW stronghold. Opposite of Lesser Goldfinch expansion (degree 44). Engine now has both vectors.
- **Habitat: Suburban Edge.** Gardens (hedgerows, brush piles, tool sheds), thickets (blackberry, salal), open woodlands (Garry oak), streamside groves, brush piles. Common thread: ground-level structure. Needs brush, not old-growth. Year-round resident in western Washington. Largely sedentary.
- **Nesting: Cavity Opportunist.** Natural cavities and human structures (birdhouses, mailboxes, tin cans, old boots, dryer vents, coat pockets). Male builds dummy nests. 5-7 eggs. Incubation 14-16 days (female). Nestling 14-16 days (both parents). 1-2 broods.
- **Diet: Crevice Gleaner.** Insects: beetles, caterpillars, ants, spiders, bugs, flies, wasps. Gleaning bark, probing crevices, flipping leaf litter. Seeds/berries in winter. Occasional suet feeders. Restless, active, thorough.
- **E=3 Consecutive: Loud For Its Size.** Song carries across suburban territory. Two consecutive E=3 (54, 55). Species-side: E1:4, E2:14, E3:13, E4:22, E5:3. Quiet majority (31 of 56, 55.4%).
- **Passeriformes Resumes After Piciformes Interruption.** After one-degree Piciformes interruption (54). 48 of 56 (85.7%) Passeriformes.
- **CSV Substitution: Eleventh Consecutive Amphibian-Class Replacement.** Oregon Slender Salamander replaced. 11th consecutive amphibian replacement (45-55). Both year-round PNW lowland residents. Understory niche preserved.
- **Conservation: IUCN Least Concern, PNW Stronghold.** ~7-8 million breeding birds. Stable in western N.A. Protected under Migratory Bird Treaty Act 1918. Eastern decline: cautionary competitive displacement story.

## Lessons Learned

- **Folk zone yields distinct artists at every degree -- eleven paradigms, zero redundancy.** Eleven degrees of folk-adjacent research produced eleven distinct production paradigms. The engine's deep-research discipline scales to extended genre-family dominance without repetition. The folk zone is structurally deep, not superficially uniform.
- **Singer-Songwriter classification works analytically -- foregrounds mode of practice over genre of sound.** Naming the classification after the practitioner's self-conception (songwriter) rather than the audible genre (folk) is an analytical choice that produces cleaner classification. Future releases should consider mode-of-practice classification when the genre-of-sound category is ambiguous.
- **Infrastructure beneficiary pattern captures a real phenomenon -- E=2 careers exist through ecosystem support.** Small label plus community radio plus one influential endorsement plus a producer-friend plus neighboring-band session players plus a community venue -- no single element is load-bearing. The ecosystem is. Future artist-city pattern tracking should distinguish institutional careers (one big institution) from ecosystem careers (many small supports).
- **Cultural vocal learning is the engine's most unusual acoustic mechanism -- innate vs cultural identity is a new axis.** Previous species were classified by what they sound like. The Bewick's Wren adds classification by how they learn what they sound like. Future species entries should flag the innate-vs-learned dimension explicitly.
- **Longest family return gap creates strongest same-family contrast -- 54 degrees built enough context for maximum comparison.** The 54-degree gap between the Pacific Wren and the Bewick's Wren is the engine's largest family return gap. The contrast is correspondingly sharp: old-growth versus suburb, cascade versus yard song, innate versus cultural, hidden versus bold. Future family returns should be timed to maximize the accumulated context for contrast.
- **Parallel eleven-degree streaks (folk-adjacent + amphibian replacement) create structural resonance worth documenting.** Two independent streaks reaching eleven at the same degree is not coincidence; it is the engine's way of marking zone boundaries. Future degrees should watch for parallel streak convergence as a structural signal.
- **Best Part A/B resonances are discovered, not constructed -- community transmission emerged from material.** The community-transmission axis was not in the original research brief. It emerged from reading the Bewick's Wren cultural learning research alongside the Shelby Earl career arc. Future releases should hold resonance axes open until the research is complete.
- **Marine species gap is the engine's most critical structural debt at 56 degrees -- the water must speak.** The Sound of Puget Sound is a saltwater name and the engine has not documented a single saltwater species. This is a structural debt that compounds with every degree of delay. Future planning should enforce a marine species no later than degree 60.
