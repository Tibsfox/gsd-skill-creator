The cultural vocal learning dimension is what sets the Bewick's Wren apart from every other species in the engine. Young males settle on a territory in their first breeding season and memorize the songs of the males already holding adjacent territories. They copy these songs, building a repertoire of 9-22 distinct song types from the local acoustic environment rather than from inherited or innately programmed patterns. Because males learn from their neighbors, and their neighbors learned from *their* neighbors, songs change gradually across the landscape. A Bewick's Wren in Seattle sings somewhat differently from a Bewick's Wren in Portland. The differences accumulate with distance. This is genuine cultural transmission -- learned behavior passed horizontally rather than vertically. The Bewick's Wren is a cultural animal: its song is not genetically determined but socially learned. This stands in sharp contrast to the Pacific Wren (degree 1), whose intricate cascade warble has a much more fixed, innately structured quality. The cascade is complexity through density -- many notes per phrase. The yard song is complexity through diversity and cultural transmission -- many phrase types per bird, each learned from the social environment.

The eastern range contraction adds a conservation dimension to the engine's story. The Bewick's Wren has almost completely disappeared from eastern North America over the past century -- once breeding across the eastern United States from the Appalachians to the Midwest, now essentially extirpated from nearly all of its former eastern range. The primary driver appears to be competition with the House Wren (*Troglodytes aedon*), which expanded its range and population density throughout the eastern U.S. during the same period. House Wrens are aggressive cavity competitors -- they enter Bewick's Wren nests, destroy eggs, and usurp nest sites. The House Wren's expansion and the Bewick's Wren's contraction are mirror images. At degree 44, the Lesser Goldfinch documented range expansion -- a species gaining ground, pushing northward. *Thryomanes* -- from Ancient Greek *thryon* ("reed") + *manes* ("madness, passion, frenzy"). The genus name means "reed madness" or "reed frenzy" -- an evocative name that captures the wren's restless, hyperactive energy as it works through tangles and brush. The word *manes* carries the same root as "mania" -- this is a bird driven by passionate, frenetic energy, constantly in motion, constantly investigating every crevice and tangle in its territory. *Thryomanes* is a monotypic genus -- the Bewick's Wren is its only species. Named by Audubon in 1827 for Thomas Bewick (1753-1828), the English wood engraver and naturalist whose detailed bird engravings in *A History of British Birds* set a new standard for natural history illustration. Full translation: "Bewick's reed-frenzy bird" -- a name that honors an engraver and describes a temperament. Compare the Pacific Wren's *Troglodytes pacificus* ("Pacific cave diver") at degree 1, which emphasized the bird's habit of ducking into dark crevices. *Thryomanes* emphasizes the behavioral state -- the frenetic energy itself. Two wren genera, two naming philosophies: habitat (*Troglodytes*, the cave) versus temperament (*Thryomanes*, the frenzy).

### Part B: Bewick's Wren -- THE YARD SONG (TROGLODYTIDAE RETURNS After 54-Degree Absence Longest Family Return Gap, Two Wrens Two Worlds, Cultural Vocal Learning First in Engine, Geographic Dialects 9-22 Song Types, Eastern Range Contraction PNW Stronghold, Monotypic Genus Thryomanes Reed Madness, Passeriformes Resumes, E=3 Consecutive, 22 Families 56 Entries, Eleventh CSV Substitution)

Full deep research covering the Bewick's Wren as the second Troglodytidae, the yard bird, and the first species defined by cultural vocal learning:

- **TROGLODYTIDAE RETURNS AFTER 54 DEGREES -- Longest Family Return Gap:** Wrens last appeared at degree 1 (Pacific Wren). The 54-degree gap surpasses Paridae (41 degrees, 9 to 50) and Picidae (32 degrees, 22 to 54). Two wrens, two worlds: Pacific Wren = hidden, cryptic, old-growth, 100+ notes per bout, innate cascade. Bewick's Wren = conspicuous, bold, suburban, 10-25 notes per phrase, culturally learned repertoire. Same family, same body mass (8-12 g), opposite strategies. Cave diver (*Troglodytes*) vs. reed madness (*Thryomanes*).

- **TWO WRENS, TWO WORLDS:** Pacific Wren (degree 1): genus *Troglodytes*, old-growth understory, cascade of 100+ notes, fixed repertoire, limited variation, E=2. Bewick's Wren (degree 55): genus *Thryomanes*, brushy suburban, variable repertoire of short phrases, 9-22 learned song types, strong geographic dialects, E=3. Cascade = complexity through density. Yard song = complexity through diversity and cultural transmission. The engine now has the full wren spectrum from wilderness to garden.

- **CULTURAL VOCAL LEARNING -- First Species Defined by Social Song Learning:** Young males learn from neighboring territory holders, not parents. 9-22 distinct song types per male. Geographic dialects changing gradually with distance. Genuine cultural transmission -- horizontal (neighbor to neighbor) not vertical (parent to offspring). The first species in the engine whose primary acoustic identity is defined by social learning. Stands in sharp contrast to Pacific Wren's innately structured cascade.

- **THE YARD SONG -- FIFTY-SIXTH ACOUSTIC ELEMENT (Species-Side):** Loud, melodious, variable song of whistles, warbles, buzzes, trills. Named for three resonances: (1) the habitat -- the yard bird, suburban edge soundtrack; (2) the cultural landscape -- song varies geographically through neighbor learning; (3) the contrast -- what happens when wrens leave old-growth and move into the garden. Differs from sap-well rhythm (54, arrhythmic percussion) and cascade (1, density). The yard song is diversity through cultural transmission.

- **TAXONOMY: THRYOMANES BEWICKII:** *Thryomanes* = "reed madness" (Greek *thryon* + *manes*). Monotypic genus. Named by Audubon 1827 for Thomas Bewick (1753-1828), English wood engraver. Full translation: "Bewick's reed-frenzy bird." One of the few N.A. birds named for a European naturalist whose fame rested on illustration. Order Passeriformes, Family Troglodytidae (~88 species, New World).

- **THE SOUND: MASTER VOCALIST:** Frequency 2.0-8.0 kHz (peak 3.0-6.0 kHz). Duration ~2-4 seconds. Whistle-trill-finish three-part structure. Bright, clear, musical. Higher than Song Sparrow, louder than Pacific Wren. Delivered from exposed perches. Scold call: 2.5-6.0 kHz. Buzz/chatter: 3.0-7.0 kHz. Contact call: 3.0-5.0 kHz.

- **PLUMAGE: THE BOLD SUPERCILIUM:** Brown above (PNW form richer), pale gray below. Bold white eyebrow (supercilium) -- defining field mark. Long tail, frequently cocked and flicked. 13-14 cm, 8-12 g. Slender decurved bill. Confusion species: House Wren (no supercilium), Pacific Wren (smaller, darker, shorter tail), Marsh Wren (cattail marshes).

- **EASTERN RANGE CONTRACTION:** Nearly disappeared from eastern N.A. Displaced by House Wren (*Troglodytes aedon*) through cavity competition -- entering nests, destroying eggs, usurping sites. Western populations stable. PNW stronghold. Opposite of Lesser Goldfinch expansion (degree 44). Engine now has both vectors.

- **HABITAT: SUBURBAN EDGE:** Gardens (hedgerows, brush piles, tool sheds), thickets (blackberry, salal), open woodlands (Garry oak), streamside groves, brush piles. Common thread: ground-level structure. Needs brush, not old-growth. Year-round resident in western Washington. Largely sedentary.

- **NESTING: CAVITY OPPORTUNIST:** Natural cavities and human structures (birdhouses, mailboxes, tin cans, old boots, dryer vents, coat pockets). Male builds dummy nests. 5-7 eggs. Incubation 14-16 days (female). Nestling 14-16 days (both parents). 1-2 broods.

- **DIET: CREVICE GLEANER:** Insects: beetles, caterpillars, ants, spiders, bugs, flies, wasps. Gleaning bark, probing crevices, flipping leaf litter. Seeds/berries in winter. Occasional suet feeders. Restless, active, thorough.

- **E=3:** Loud for its size. Song carries across suburban territory. Two consecutive E=3 (54, 55). Species-side: E1:4, E2:14, E3:13, E4:22, E5:3. Quiet majority (31 of 56, 55.4%).

- **PASSERIFORMES RESUMES:** After one-degree Piciformes interruption (54). 48 of 56 (85.7%) Passeriformes.

- **FAMILY TRACKER:** 22 families, 56 entries. Parulidae leads at 10. Five families at 5. Three at 3. Troglodytidae rises to 2. Twelve singleton families. 54-degree return gap is longest in engine history.

- **CSV SUBSTITUTION:** Oregon Slender Salamander replaced. 11th consecutive amphibian replacement (45-55). Both year-round PNW lowland residents. Understory niche preserved.

- **CONSERVATION:** IUCN Least Concern. ~7-8 million breeding birds. Stable in western N.A. Protected under Migratory Bird Treaty Act 1918. Eastern decline: cautionary competitive displacement story.
