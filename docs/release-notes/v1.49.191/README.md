The **yard song** -- the 56th acoustic element on the species side -- is defined by cultural vocal learning as its primary acoustic identity. Young male Bewick's Wrens do not learn their songs from their fathers. They settle on a territory and memorize the songs of the males already holding adjacent territories. They copy their neighbors' songs, building a repertoire of 9-22 distinct song types from the local acoustic environment. Because males learn from neighbors, and neighbors learned from *their* neighbors, songs change gradually across the landscape. A Bewick's Wren in Seattle sings somewhat differently from a Bewick's Wren in Portland. A Bewick's Wren in Portland sings somewhat differently from a Bewick's Wren in Medford. The differences accumulate with distance. This is genuine cultural transmission -- learned behavior passed horizontally (neighbor to neighbor) rather than vertically (parent to offspring). The Bewick's Wren is a cultural animal: its song is not genetically determined but socially learned. Geographic dialects that drift across the landscape like folk songs drifting across geography.

The yard song differs from the cascade (degree 1) not in ambition but in strategy. The cascade overwhelms through density: 100+ notes compressed into a single 5-10 second burst, the most acoustically complex single song in the engine. The yard song overwhelms through diversity: 9-22 distinct song types per bird, each type a stereotyped pattern the male cycles through, the repertoire itself learned from the social environment. Density versus diversity. Innate complexity versus cultural richness. One wren compresses; the other collects. Both strategies achieve the same end: a vocal identity that dominates the acoustic space. The forest wren dominates with intensity. The yard bird dominates with variety.

The Bewick's Wren also carries one of the most dramatic range stories in North American ornithology. The species has **almost completely disappeared from eastern North America** over the past century, driven primarily by competition with the House Wren (*Troglodytes aedon*), which expanded its range and population density throughout the eastern U.S. during the same period. House Wrens are aggressive cavity competitors -- they enter Bewick's Wren nests, destroy eggs, and usurp nest sites. The House Wren expanded; the Bewick's Wren collapsed. In western North America, the species remains common and stable -- the Pacific Coast from British Columbia to Baja California is the stronghold. What was lost in the East persists here. And the loss is not only ecological. The eastern song dialects -- entire traditions of culturally transmitted vocal patterns passed from neighbor to neighbor across the Appalachians, the Midwest, the Gulf Coast -- were lost when the populations vanished. You cannot recover cultural traditions from genetic material. The school is closed. In the West, the school is still open: the folk tradition continues, the dialects persist, the neighbor-to-neighbor teaching carries on. But the eastern branch is silent.

- **THRYOMANES BEWICKII -- Etymology, Taxonomy, Monotypic Genus:** *Thryomanes* from Ancient Greek *thryon* (reed) + *manes* (madness, passion, frenzy). "Reed madness." The genus name captures the wren's restless, hyperactive, constantly-in-motion energy as it works through tangles and brush. *Manes* carries the same root as "mania." *Thryomanes* is a monotypic genus -- the Bewick's Wren is its only species. *bewickii* named by John James Audubon in 1827 for Thomas Bewick (1753-1828), the English wood engraver and naturalist, author of *A History of British Birds*. Bewick was a master of woodcut illustration whose detailed bird engravings set a new standard for natural history art. One of the few North American birds named for a European naturalist whose fame rested on illustration rather than field collection.

- **THE YARD SONG -- FIFTY-SIXTH ACOUSTIC ELEMENT (Species-Side), First Element Defined by Cultural Vocal Learning, Learned From Neighbors Not Parents, Geographic Dialects:** A loud, melodious, variable song of whistles, warbles, buzzes, and trills, delivered from an exposed perch in suburban and brushy habitats, learned from neighboring territory holders, and varying geographically through cultural transmission. Song characteristics: 2.0-8.0 kHz frequency range (peak 3.0-6.0 kHz), approximately 2-4 seconds duration, whistle-trill-finish three-part structure. Individual males hold 9-22 distinct song types, cycling through the repertoire in singing bouts. The yard song differs from the cascade (degree 1): the cascade is complexity through density (100+ notes per phrase), the yard song is complexity through diversity (9-22 distinct phrase types per bird). The yard song differs from the sap-well rhythm (degree 54): the sap-well rhythm is arrhythmic percussion (inherited behavior), the yard song is culturally transmitted melodic vocalization (learned behavior). Named "the yard song" for three resonances: (1) the habitat -- fence posts, brush piles, hedgerows, suburban gardens; (2) the cultural landscape -- songs vary geographically because taught by neighbors, creating acoustic dialects mirroring physical neighborhoods; (3) the contrast -- against the Pacific Wren's deep-forest cascade at degree 1, the yard song is what happens when wrens leave the old-growth and move into the garden. The song opens up. The darkness lifts. The bird sings from the fence post instead of the root tangle.

- **CULTURAL VOCAL LEARNING AND GEOGRAPHIC DIALECTS -- Songs Learned From Neighboring Territory Holders, Horizontal Cultural Transmission, Gradual Geographic Variation:** Young males do not learn from their fathers. They settle on a territory and memorize the songs of adjacent territory holders. The copying is wholesale -- the bird learns complete song types from the local acoustic environment. Songs change gradually across the landscape, producing continuous geographic dialects without sharp boundaries. Eastern vs western populations showed significantly different dialects before the eastern range contraction. The Bewick's Wren is a cultural animal: its song is not genetically determined but socially learned. This is the engine's clearest example of acoustic identity shaped primarily by social learning. Contrast with the White-crowned Sparrow (degree 48, "the dialect"): the sparrow shows sharper dialect boundaries between populations; the wren shows gradual, continuous dialect gradients. Both are cultural learners. Different dialect architectures: discrete zones (sparrow) vs continuous gradient (wren).

- **TWO WRENS, TWO WORLDS -- Pacific Wren (Degree 1) vs Bewick's Wren (Degree 55), Same Family Same Mass Opposite Everything Else:** Genus: *Troglodytes* (cave diver) vs *Thryomanes* (reed madness). Habitat: old-growth understory vs suburban brush. Visibility: hidden in cover vs conspicuous on exposed perch. Song: 100+ notes per cascade, innate structure, E=2 vs 9-22 learned song types, cultural transmission, E=3. Song learning: more innately structured vs learned from neighbors. Geographic variation: limited vs strong dialects. Repertoire: 1-3 types vs 9-22 types. Complexity strategy: density (many notes per phrase) vs diversity (many phrase types per bird). The Pacific Wren avoids human-modified landscapes. The Bewick's Wren thrives in them. The wrens bracket the engine to date: the forest interior at the beginning, the suburban edge at the current frontier. The most structurally complete species pair in the engine.

- **THE EASTERN RANGE CONTRACTION -- Near-Total Disappearance From Eastern North America, Competitive Displacement by House Wren, Opposite of Lesser Goldfinch Expansion at Degree 44:** Historical range: Pennsylvania to Virginia to Missouri to Kansas, south to the Gulf Coast. Current status: essentially extirpated from nearly all former eastern range. Remnant Ozark and Appalachian populations. Primary driver: competitive displacement by the House Wren (*Troglodytes aedon*) -- aggressive cavity competitors that enter nests, destroy eggs, usurp nest sites. Additional factors: habitat changes, cowbird parasitism. Western stability: common and stable throughout Pacific Coast from BC to Baja California. The engine now has both range vectors: expansion (Lesser Goldfinch, degree 44) and contraction (Bewick's Wren, degree 55). The eastern loss is also cultural: entire traditions of culturally transmitted song dialects were lost when populations vanished. Cultural traditions cannot be recovered from genetic material.

- **PLUMAGE: THE BOLD WHITE SUPERCILIUM -- Diagnostic Field Mark, Long-Tailed Active Wren, PNW Form Richer Brown:** Small (13-14 cm, 8-12 g), active, long-tailed wren. Brown above, pale gray below. Bold white supercilium (eyebrow stripe) extending from bill base past the eye -- the defining field mark. Long tail frequently cocked upward and flicked side to side -- a behavioral field mark. PNW form: richer brown above, grayer below. Confusion species separated: House Wren (plainer face, no bold supercilium), Pacific Wren (smaller, darker, shorter tail, deep forest), Marsh Wren (cattail marsh, not upland brush).

- **HABITAT: THE WREN OF THE SUBURBAN EDGE -- Gardens, Hedgerows, Brush Piles, Open Woodlands, Thickets:** Suburban gardens and yards (the yard bird par excellence), blackberry tangles, salal thickets, regenerating clearcuts, forest edges, Garry oak savanna, riparian corridors, brush piles and debris. The common thread: structure at ground level -- dense, tangled, complex vegetation or human-built structures providing crevices for foraging and nesting. Does not need old-growth forest. Needs brush. Nests in birdhouses, mailboxes, tin cans, old boots, tool shed shelves, garage rafters. Year-round resident in western Washington. Largely sedentary, non-migratory.
