## Mission Overview

Parker is the obs#1 opener of the HELIOPHYSICS-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's flagship heliophysics mission to "touch the Sun," whose defining records are the first fly-through of the Sun's corona, the closest approach to the Sun and the fastest human-made object ever, and its namesake honor for Eugene Parker. It is designed, built, and operated by the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory (JHU APL, Laurel MD) for NASA, developed under NASA's Living With a Star program and managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center — NOT STScI/IPAC/JPL. A Sun-grazing solar probe, it launched 12 August 2018 on a Delta IV Heavy (with a Star-48BV upper stage) from Cape Canaveral and uses seven Venus gravity assists over about seven years to progressively lower its perihelion toward the Sun across 24 planned close solar passes. Its carbon-composite Thermal Protection System — the heat shield — faces the Sun at about 1,370 °C (~2,500 °F) while the instruments behind it stay near room temperature; its four suites are FIELDS (electric and magnetic fields and waves), WISPR (the sole imager), SWEAP (solar-wind electrons, alphas, and protons), and IS☉IS (energetic particles). The story is the launch from Cape Canaveral, the seven Venus flybys each shrinking the orbit and dropping the perihelion sunward, the dive behind the shield into the corona, and the record-setting close, fast passes through the Sun's outer atmosphere. The science is central: Parker samples the solar wind at its source, measures the Sun's electric and magnetic fields and the energetic particles near the corona, images coronal streamers with WISPR, and maps the magnetic "switchbacks" that pervade the near-Sun solar wind. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: Parker is a SOLAR / heliophysics probe, NOT a space telescope, NOT an observatory looking outward, and re-stamps NO space-telescope-axis anchor; it is built and operated by JHU APL under NASA's Living With a Star program (Goddard), NOT STScI/IPAC/JPL; it is the first spacecraft to fly through the Sun's corona (it crossed below the Alfvén critical surface on 28 April 2021, announced 14 December 2021); at its record perihelion (24 December 2024) it is the closest object to the Sun (~6.1 million km from the surface) and the fastest human-made object ever (~692,000 km/h); and it is named for Eugene Parker (who predicted the solar wind in 1958), the first NASA mission named for a living person. ★★ Parker is STILL OPERATING in 2026 — it completed its 28th close solar pass on 8 June 2026 and continues an extended mission — so the catalog frames the mission in the PRESENT tense throughout (Parker flies, dives, samples, is the fastest and closest), in contrast with the FUTURE/planned-tense Roman page. ## Surprises

- **A spacecraft that flies through the Sun's outer atmosphere and survives.** Parker first crossed below the Alfvén critical surface into the corona on 28 April 2021 — the first spacecraft to fly through the Sun's corona, "touching the Sun" — sampling the solar wind at its source amid coronal streamers and magnetic "switchbacks," and it does so repeatedly, returning intact behind its heat shield each time.
- **A heat shield that holds the sunward face near 1,370 °C while the instruments stay near room temperature.** Parker's carbon-composite Thermal Protection System faces the Sun at about 1,370 °C (~2,500 °F) at closest approach, yet the four instrument suites riding in its shadow stay near room temperature — a thin disk of carbon-carbon foam and ceramic coating standing between the spacecraft and the full near-Sun solar flux.
- **Seven flybys of Venus to fall toward the Sun.** Parker uses seven Venus gravity assists over about seven years to progressively lower its perihelion toward the Sun across 24 planned close solar passes — it does not simply burn its way inward but trades orbital energy with Venus, flyby after flyby, to fall closer to the Sun than any spacecraft before it.

## Dedication

v1.266 Parker is dedicated to the people across NASA, the Goddard Space Flight Center, and the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory who designed, built, and now fly NASA's flagship mission to touch the Sun. The engineers who made a thin disk of carbon to stand between a spacecraft and the full heat of the Sun, holding its sunward face near fourteen hundred degrees while the instruments behind it ride near room temperature; the navigators who threaded seven flybys of Venus, each one trading a little speed to fall a little closer to the Sun; the teams who watch the probe cross the edge of the corona and sample the solar wind where it is born, amid the streamers and the switchbacks; and the scientists who read the fields and the particles from the fastest, closest place anything we have made has ever been. It is named for Eugene Parker, who in 1958 wrote down the solar wind before anyone had seen it, and who lived to watch his probe fly. They sent a small, shielded thing racing into the blaze, and it touched the Sun and came back to tell us.
