## Summary

**Jimi Hendrix** arrives at E=8 -- the engine's maximum, tied only by The Sonics (241) and, prospectively, by the entries ahead. James Marshall Hendrix was born on November 27, 1942, in Seattle, Washington, at King County Hospital. He grew up in the Central District, attended Garfield High School, and taught himself guitar on an acoustic with one string, then a secondhand Silvertone, before enlisting in the Army in 1961 to avoid a jail sentence. He was discharged in 1962 after breaking his ankle during a jump with the 101st Airborne, and spent the next several years working as a sideman on the chitlin circuit -- playing behind Little Richard, the Isley Brothers, and B.B. King -- before being discovered in a Greenwich Village club in 1966 by Chas Chandler of The Animals. Chandler brought Hendrix to London, assembled the Jimi Hendrix Experience with Noel Redding and Mitch Mitchell, and within eight months Hendrix had released "Hey Joe," "Purple Haze," and "The Wind Cries Mary." He played Monterey Pop in June 1967 and set his guitar on fire. He played Woodstock in August 1969 and ended the festival with a version of "The Star-Spangled Banner" that is still the definitive statement of what an electric guitar can do.

Hendrix is the greatest guitarist in the history of popular music. That is not a subjective claim made to fill a release note -- it is the consensus of every serious survey, poll, and critical evaluation that has been conducted since his death in 1970. He died on September 18, 1970, in London, at age 27. The engine assigns E=8 at degree 245 for the psychedelic rock phase of his career -- the Experience years, the Axis: Bold as Love and Electric Ladyland era, the period when he was reinventing what a rock trio could do with electricity, distortion, feedback, and left-hand technique. The E=8 is deserved and it is not inflated.

On the soundscape side, **River Current Over Boulders** marks a structural pivot that is as significant as any species entry the engine has made: **the FIRST ABIOTIC SPS entry in the engine's history**. From degree 1 through degree 244, every SPS entry has been a living organism -- a bird, a mammal, a fish, an invertebrate, an amphibian. At degree 245, the Sound of Puget Sound track shifts from biological species to the **acoustic landscape itself**: the rivers, waterfalls, rain, and wind that constitute the sonic substrate beneath every living thing the engine has documented. The river current over boulders -- the polyrhythmic sound of water moving through and around rock, breaking into competing rhythms at each obstruction -- registers at E=5. It is a complex sound: irregular, percussive, continuous, never the same twice. A boulder field in a river current produces multiple independent rhythmic streams that interact without resolving. It is polyrhythm without a composer.

The pairing of Hendrix with river current over boulders is not accidental. Hendrix plays with the same quality: multiple rhythmic streams in the same instrument, independent lines in the left and right hand, lead and rhythm simultaneously, the chord and the melody at once. The river does not know it is making music. Hendrix knew exactly what he was doing.
