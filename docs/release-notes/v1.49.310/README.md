**The Genus Numenius and the Curlew Lineage.** **Numenius** is the genus of large, long-billed, decurved-bill sandpipers — the curlew lineage — named from the Greek *noumenios* (new-moon bird), a reference to the crescent silhouette of the decurved bill. The genus contains approximately eight species globally, including the Whimbrel (*N. phaeopus*, trans-hemispheric), the Long-billed Curlew (*N. americanus*, North American grasslands-to-coast), the Eurasian Curlew (*N. arquata*, European coasts), the Bristle-thighed Curlew (*N. tahitiensis*, Alaska-to-Pacific-islands migrant), and the now-likely-extinct Eskimo Curlew (*N. borealis*) and Slender-billed Curlew (*N. tenuirostris*). The engine's entry at degree 170 opens the genus with the Whimbrel and carries forward at degree 171 with the Long-billed Curlew, establishing a two-degree curlew-bill run that documents the decurved-bill morphological adaptation across two Numenius species. **The Trans-Hemispheric Migration.** The Whimbrel is one of the longest-migrating shorebirds in the Western Hemisphere, undertaking a **trans-hemispheric migration** between **Arctic breeding grounds** in **Alaska** and **northern Canada** (and in the Eastern Hemisphere, **Eurasia**) and **wintering grounds** distributed across **South America**, **Africa**, **Southeast Asia**, and **Australia**. The American subspecies (*N. p. hudsonicus*, sometimes split as its own species) breeds on tundra wet meadows in Alaska and the Hudson Bay region and winters on the Pacific and Atlantic coasts of **South America** — Peru, Brazil, Argentina — as well as on Caribbean and Central American coasts. The migration route crosses **Pacific Northwest** coastal habitat during spring (northward) and fall (southward) passage, and **Puget Sound**, the **Washington coast**, and the **British Columbia** shoreline serve as stopover sites where Whimbrels pause to feed on mudflat invertebrates and replenish energy reserves before continuing the multi-thousand-kilometer journey. The migration is one of the great long-distance avian journeys: a bird breeding in Alaska in June may be feeding on a Peruvian beach in November, and the annual cycle threads three continents through one individual bird. **The Pacific Northwest Coastal Passage.** Whimbrels pass through Pacific Northwest coastal habitat during both spring and fall migration, with peak spring passage in late April through May and peak fall passage in July through September. Key Pacific Northwest stopover sites include the **Washington coast** mudflats at **Willapa Bay** and **Grays Harbor**, **Puget Sound** tidal flats (**Padilla Bay**, **Samish Bay**, and the **Skagit River** estuary), and **British Columbia** shorelines. The species is a regular but not abundant passage migrant — a birder walking a Pacific Northwest mudflat in May or August has a reasonable chance of encountering a small flock of Whimbrels probing the sand with their distinctive decurved bills, and the whistled call is often the first indicator of presence before visual identification. ## Part B: Whimbrel — The Third Scolopacidae and the First Curlew Bill

- **Third Scolopacidae in the Engine.** The sandpiper family reaches its third consecutive engine degree (168-170) with the Whimbrel, following the **Wilson's Snipe** at 168 (mechanical winnowing wing-feather display) and the **Dunlin flock** at 169 (collective contact-call wash during tidal-flat murmuration); three distinct acoustic strategies from one family.
- **Genus Numenius Opens.** The curlew lineage enters the engine at degree 170 — **Numenius** (Greek *noumenios*, new-moon bird, after the crescent-bill silhouette) contains approximately eight species globally, and the Whimbrel opens the two-degree curlew-bill run that continues at degree 171 with the **Long-billed Curlew** (*Numenius americanus*).
- **Decurved Curlew-Type Bill (8-9 cm).** The downward-curving 8-9 centimeter sharply-pointed bill is built for probing **fiddler crab** burrows and extracting marine worms, mollusks, and other tidal-flat invertebrates — the bill's curvature matches the shape of crab burrows and allows deeper extraction than a straight bill could manage, and the decurved morphology is the defining signature of the Numenius lineage.
- **Rapid Seven-Note Whistled Call.** The defining acoustic signature is a clear, evenly-spaced, whistled *pi-pi-pi-pi-pi-pi-pi* sequence delivered both in flight (as a migratory contact call) and on the ground (as a presence or alarm call), carrying over **mudflats** and **tidal shorelines** and standing as one of the most recognizable shorebird vocalizations of the Pacific Northwest coastline.
- **Trans-Hemispheric Migration (Alaska to South America).** American Whimbrels (*N. p. hudsonicus*) breed on **Arctic tundra wet meadows** in **Alaska** and the **Hudson Bay** region and winter along the Pacific and Atlantic coasts of **South America** (Peru, Brazil, Argentina), threading three continents through one bird's annual cycle and making the Whimbrel one of the longest-migrating shorebirds in the Western Hemisphere.
- **Pacific Northwest Coastal Passage.** The **Washington coast** (Willapa Bay, Grays Harbor), **Puget Sound** tidal flats (Padilla Bay, Samish Bay, Skagit River estuary), and **British Columbia** shoreline serve as spring and fall stopover sites where Whimbrels feed on mudflat invertebrates during peak passage (late April-May, July-September).
- **Cryptic Plumage and Striped Crown.** The 40-46 centimeter shorebird carries cryptic brown-and-buff plumage with a distinctive **striped crown** (dark lateral crown stripes contrasting with a pale median stripe) that distinguishes the Whimbrel from the similar Long-billed Curlew and from other sandpiper genera — the crown-stripe pattern is one of the primary field-identification markers alongside the decurved bill.
- **E=4 Clarity Calibration.** The E=4 energy calibration reflects the call's clarity and carry distance — audible across mudflat acoustics but not resonant at the crane-bugle scale, individual rather than flock-wash, unmistakable rather than cryptic — and reads as a mid-range shorebird acoustic calibration that complements the mechanical Snipe (168, E=4) and the collective Dunlin flock (169, E=3) in the current Scolopacidae run.
