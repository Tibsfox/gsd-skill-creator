## Jonathan Poneman -- Sub Pop Co-Founder #1

Jonathan Poneman is a KCMU DJ and co-founder of **Sub Pop Records**, the most structurally significant independent label in Pacific Northwest music history. Founded in 1988 with Bruce Pavitt (degree 128), Sub Pop began as a fanzine, became a cassette compilation series, and evolved into the label that signed Nirvana, Soundgarden, Mudhoney, TAD, Green River, and the first wave of what the world would call grunge.

But Sub Pop is not just grunge. The label's four-decade roster spans:

- **First wave (late 1980s):** Nirvana, Soundgarden, Mudhoney, TAD, Green River
- **Second wave (1990s-2000s):** The Postal Service, The Shins, Iron & Wine, Wolf Parade
- **Third wave (2008-present):** Fleet Foxes, Beach House, Father John Misty, Sleater-Kinney (later period), Clipping

Poneman's role: the business mind. Where Pavitt was the tastemaker and music writer, Poneman was the dealmaker, the evangelist, the one who convinced labels and distributors that Seattle had something worth distributing. Together they created the infrastructure that made grunge possible -- not by inventing the music, but by building the pipeline from the rehearsal space to the record store.

E=3. A radio personality and label co-founder. The energy is the energy of infrastructure -- phone calls, contracts, curation, advocacy. Lower than a performer's energy. But the leverage is enormous. E=3 at the individual level, E=10 at the ecosystem level.

**KCMU radio trio** -- second of three. The station pattern: Nasty Nes Rodriguez (126) brought hip-hop to Seattle radio. Poneman (127) used KCMU as a platform to champion the indie/punk scene that would become Sub Pop's roster. Pavitt (128) used KCMU and his Subterranean Pop fanzine to build the critical vocabulary for Seattle music. Three figures, one station, three different cultural functions: broadcasting, business, criticism.

## Tundra Swan -- The Record Breaks

The Tundra Swan (*Cygnus columbianus*) is a large migratory swan found in delta marshes, shallow lakes, and agricultural fields across the Pacific Northwest during migration. Smaller than the Trumpeter Swan (degree 128) but still a massive bird -- up to 21 pounds, 6-foot wingspan.

The sound: a **high, melodious baying** -- a clear, musical, bugle-like call, often heard from flocks in flight. The Tundra Swan's call is higher-pitched and more melodious than the Trumpeter Swan's deeper, more resonant bugling. When a flock calls together, the overlapping voices create a shimmering harmonic texture -- one of the most recognizable sounds of Pacific Northwest wetlands during migration.

E=5. The energy jumps sharply -- after five consecutive degrees at E=3 or E=4 on the species axis (122-126), the Tundra Swan's flock baying brings volume and presence back. A flock of swans calling in flight is not subtle. It carries across marshlands.

The Anatidae have now surpassed it.
