**The Rough-legged Hawk and the Hare-Footed Arctic Buteo.** **Rough-legged Hawk** (*Buteo lagopus*) is a member of **Order Accipitriformes / Family Accipitridae / Genus Buteo / species lagopus**. The species name is from Greek — *lagos* (hare) + *pous* (foot) — meaning "hare-footed," a reference to the feathered tarsi that extend all the way to the toes as an adaptation to arctic cold. The "rough" in the common name refers to the same feathered-leg feature, and the adaptation is taken further than the Ferruginous Hawk's partial feathering: the Rough-legged Hawk's feathering gives the appearance of wearing "leg warmers" when the bird is perched. **Four subspecies** are recognized; two occur in North America: *B. l. sanctijohannis* (the North American subspecies) and the Eurasian subspecies. Adult body mass ranges from 0.7 to 1.3 kg, wingspan reaches 52 inches (132 cm), and the wing shape is relatively long and narrow for a buteo — intermediate between a true buteo's broad wing and an accipiter's short wing. **Approximately 25-30%** of the North American population is dark morph (uniformly dark brown); the remainder is light morph with white head and upper breast, a dark belly band across the mid-abdomen, and a white tail with a dark terminal band. Both morphs winter in the PNW, and the white-banded tail is the key field mark distinguishing dark-morph Rough-legs from other dark-phase raptors.

**Arctic Breeding Context and the Lemming Cycle.** The Rough-legged Hawk breeds on **arctic and subarctic tundra** — open, treeless, dominated by sedges, mosses, lichens, and dwarf shrubs — and on the taiga-tundra boundary where trees become available for occasional nest sites. The bird is a **cliff-nester** by preference, using cliff faces and steep riverbanks as primary nest platforms. On the breeding grounds, the primary prey is **lemmings** (*Lemmus* and *Dicrostonyx* spp.) and **voles** (*Microtus* spp.), and the Rough-legged Hawk is one of the raptor species most tightly coupled to lemming population cycles. Lemming populations oscillate dramatically on approximately **3-4 year cycles**, from very high density (hundreds per hectare) to near-collapse and back, and the Rough-legged Hawk's breeding productivity tracks the cycle closely — high-lemming years produce multiple eggs and multiple fledged young, and years of lemming crashes produce fewer eggs and fewer fledglings. In years following high lemming productivity, the PNW and other southern wintering areas may receive **irruption influxes** of Rough-legged Hawks — more birds than typical years — and birders in Washington and Oregon learn to watch for irruption years by tracking northern lemming cycle reports. The engine's documentation of the lemming-cycle-coupled productivity at degree 230 establishes the **LEMMING SPECIALIST register** and the **IRRUPTION YEAR register** as first-instance SPS prey-specialization-vocabulary entries.

**Winter in the PNW Lowlands — The Skagit Flats and Beyond.** The Rough-legged Hawk arrives in the PNW lowlands in October-November, having migrated from arctic breeding grounds through the boreal zone, and departs in March-April to return north. The PNW winter residency period is approximately four to five months. The species uses open agricultural fields, estuarine grasslands, valley floors, and airport grass margins — the same open-country analogs that Ferruginous Hawks use when they winter in the lowlands — but Rough-legged Hawks are more commonly encountered in the lowland western PNW than Ferruginous Hawks, which are primarily eastern PNW residents. **Key winter locations** include the **Skagit Flats** (Skagit Valley, north of Seattle, one of the best Rough-legged Hawk watching locations in Washington, with the combination of open agricultural fields, estuarine grasslands near Padilla Bay, and high vole and mouse densities producing optimal winter habitat), the **Snohomish Valley**, the **Stillaguamish Flats**, and the **Nisqually Flats** near Olympia. ## Part B: Rough-legged Hawk — The Softest Raptor, the Arctic Visitor, and the Buteo Trilogy Closer

- **Species Baseline and Hare-Footed Arctic Designation:** **Rough-legged Hawk** (*Buteo lagopus*) is a member of **Order Accipitriformes / Family Accipitridae / Genus Buteo / species lagopus**. The species name is from Greek — *lagos* (hare) + *pous* (foot) — meaning "hare-footed," a reference to the feathered tarsi that extend all the way to the toes as an adaptation to arctic cold. The "rough" in the common name refers to the same feathered-leg feature, and the adaptation is taken further than the Ferruginous Hawk's partial feathering: the Rough-legged Hawk's feathering gives the appearance of wearing "leg warmers" when the bird is perched. Adult body mass ranges from 0.7 to 1.3 kg, wingspan reaches 52 inches (132 cm), and the wing shape is relatively long and narrow for a buteo — intermediate between a true buteo's broad wing and an accipiter's short wing. Four subspecies are recognized, two occurring in North America: *B. l. sanctijohannis* (the North American subspecies) and the Eurasian subspecies, with the North American subspecies accounting for the PNW winter population.
- **The Buteo Trilogy Closer Position:** Degree 230 closes the **BUTEO TRILOGY** — a three-degree window spanning **Red-tailed Hawk** at degree 228, **Ferruginous Hawk** at degree 229, and **Rough-legged Hawk** at degree 230 — documenting the Buteo genus at its full ecological range. The Red-tailed Hawk at degree 228 is the year-round generalist whose presence in nearly every habitat type across North America and whose raspy descending scream establish the cinematic raptor voice. The Ferruginous Hawk at degree 229 is the largest hawk in North America whose Columbia Basin breeding and rough alarm call establish the open-grassland-specialist register. The Rough-legged Hawk at degree 230 is the winter visitor whose Arctic tundra breeding and catlike mew at E=4 establish the softest-raptor-in-the-engine register. The trilogy closure at degree 230 completes the engine's documentation of the Buteo genus in the Pacific Northwest, and the trilogy structure (228-229-230) is a first-instance three-degree sub-arc within the broader eight-degree raptor arc.
- **The Catlike Mew at E=4:** The Rough-legged Hawk's primary call is a high, thin, descending mew — *peeeeah* or *keeaow* — with a quality often described as catlike. The call is drawn-out (0.5-1.5 seconds), descending in pitch, and slightly plaintive, operating in approximately 2,000-4,500 Hz. The sound lacks the roughness of the Ferruginous Hawk's kree-ah (229) and the power of the Red-tailed Hawk's keeeee-arrr (228), and it is the engine's quietest raptor sound across the 221-232 raptor arc. At E=4, the mew ties the Golden Eagle's high thin whistle at E=4 from degree 223 and is softer than every other raptor documented in Q3. The softness is context-appropriate: the bird is a winter visitor in someone else's landscape, not an aggressive territory holder. The Ferruginous Hawk is defending a breeding site with commanding open-country vocalization; the Rough-legged Hawk is here temporarily, and the call reflects that tentativeness. The softest-raptor designation establishes the **E=4 CATEGORY-OUTLIER** register in the engine's SPS amplitude-vocabulary at degree 230.
- **Arctic Tundra Breeding Ecology:** The Rough-legged Hawk breeds on **arctic and subarctic tundra** — open, treeless, dominated by sedges, mosses, lichens, and dwarf shrubs — and at the taiga-tundra boundary where trees become available for occasional nest sites. Nesting occurs on cliff faces, steep riverbanks, and in sparse tree crowns where trees are available, and the bird is a **cliff-nester** by preference. On the breeding grounds, the primary prey is **lemmings** (*Lemmus* and *Dicrostonyx* spp.) and **voles** (*Microtus* spp.), and the Rough-legged Hawk is one of the raptor species most tightly coupled to lemming population cycles. In years of lemming abundance, productivity is high — multiple eggs, multiple fledged young. In years of lemming crashes (approximately 3-4 year cycles), productivity drops dramatically — fewer eggs, fewer fledglings. The engine's entry of the Rough-legged Hawk at degree 230 documents the **CLIFF-NESTING BEHAVIOR register** and the **LEMMING SPECIALIST register** as first-instance SPS vocabulary additions.
- **Holarctic Range and the Circumpolar Distribution:** The Rough-legged Hawk is one of the few truly **holarctic raptors** — it breeds across the Arctic from North America through Eurasia (Scandinavia, Russia, Siberia) as the same species, not as sister species. The North American subspecies (*B. l. sanctijohannis*) and the Eurasian subspecies share specific status, and the circumpolar distribution is unusual among North American hawks and reflects the species' origin as a tundra specialist adapted to the pan-Arctic landscape. The **HOLARCTIC RANGE register** enters the engine's SPS biogeographic vocabulary at degree 230 as a first-instance entry, and the register is valuable for documenting species whose distribution extends beyond the North American continental boundary. The Rough-legged Hawk's circumpolar status contrasts with the Ferruginous Hawk's (229) limited-to-western-North-America distribution and the Red-tailed Hawk's (228) Americas-only range, and the contrast enriches the engine's biogeographic vocabulary with a first-instance holarctic entry.
- **PNW Winter Distribution and the Skagit Flats:** The Rough-legged Hawk arrives in the PNW lowlands in October-November, having migrated from arctic breeding grounds through the boreal zone, and departs in March-April to return north. The species uses open agricultural fields, estuarine grasslands, valley floors, and airport grass margins. **Key winter locations** include the **Skagit Flats** (Skagit Valley, north of Seattle, one of the best Rough-legged Hawk watching locations in Washington — the combination of open agricultural fields, estuarine grasslands near Padilla Bay, and high vole and mouse densities makes it optimal winter habitat), the **Snohomish Valley**, the **Stillaguamish Flats**, and the **Nisqually Flats** near Olympia. The Skagit Flats entry at degree 230 marks the first formal Skagit Audubon-documented raptor-winter location in the engine's SPS geographic vocabulary.
- **Hovering Behavior and Field Identification:** The Rough-legged Hawk is one of the North American buteos most frequently observed **hovering** — holding position against the wind with rapid wingbeats while scanning for prey below — and the behavior is well-adapted to windy, open terrain. A hovering Rough-leg in a Skagit Valley field is one of the PNW's characteristic winter raptor images. Field identification relies on several marks: the light morph shows a white head and upper breast, a dark belly band across the mid-abdomen, a white tail with a dark terminal band, and dark carpal (wrist) patches on the underwing. The dark morph is uniformly dark brown, and **approximately 25-30% of the North American population is dark morph** — proportionally more than most other polymorphic buteos. Both morphs winter in the PNW, and the white-banded tail is the key field mark distinguishing dark-morph Rough-legs from other dark-phase raptors.
- **Irruption Years and the Lemming-Cycle Coupling:** In years following high lemming productivity on the arctic breeding grounds, the PNW and other southern wintering areas may receive **irruption influxes** of Rough-legged Hawks — more birds than typical years. Birders in Washington and Oregon learn to watch for irruption years by tracking northern lemming cycle reports, and the irruption-year phenomenon is a documented PNW-winter signal of Arctic-ecosystem dynamics. The **IRRUPTION YEAR register** enters the engine's SPS prey-specialization-cycle vocabulary at degree 230, and the register documents a specific way in which Arctic ecological dynamics register in PNW winter raptor distributions. The lemming population cycle (3-4 years) is one of the best-known dynamics in Arctic ecology, and the Rough-legged Hawk's coupling to it makes the species a sentinel for high-latitude ecosystem state.

The family-boundary transition is structurally significant: Accipitridae (hawks, eagles, kites, harriers — broad-winged soaring raptors in general) hands off to Falconidae (falcons — pointed-winged fast-flying raptors whose tomial tooth on the upper mandible and hunting style mark them as structurally distinct). The engine's documentation of the last-Accipitridae-before-Falconidae position at degree 230 establishes the **FAMILY-BOUNDARY STRUCTURAL MARKER register** and sets up the boundary crossing at degree 231 as a documented transition rather than an unmarked one. The Peregrine Falcon (fastest animal on Earth) will open Falconidae with the most extreme flight-speed raptor documented by the engine, and the transition from the Rough-legged Hawk's soft mew to the Peregrine's sharp kak-kak-kak alarm will be both taxonomically and acoustically significant.
