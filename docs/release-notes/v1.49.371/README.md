## Summary

**Pond** arrives at E=6 -- the Portland band that crossed the Columbia River into Seattle's orbit regularly enough to earn a place in the catalog, and one of the most musically nuanced acts of the Pacific Northwest grunge/indie era. Pond's self-titled debut and subsequent records on Sub Pop carried a different sensibility than most of what the label released: melodic, careful, indebted to R.E.M. and Big Star as much as to any Northwest precedent, with guitar work that prioritized space and atmosphere over mass. Vocalist/guitarist Charlie Campbell led a band that felt like it came from a slightly different Seattle than the one that produced Soundgarden and Mudhoney -- a Seattle (or Portland) that was listening to college radio, reading music press, paying attention to what was happening in Athens, Georgia and Liverpool simultaneously. At E=6, Pond sits in the engine's comfortable middle register: present, distinctive, but not demanding. The grunge/indie hybrid tag describes a real phenomenon -- a band that understood both the Northwest's guitar-driven sound and the alternative rock idioms that were developing in parallel across the country, and that managed to write songs that felt native to both. Degree 230 is the second degree after the raptor arc, and where degree 229 maintained the arc's energy (E=7), degree 230 brings the first moderate pull-back, establishing the settling pattern that often follows a long arc. On the species side, the **Rough-legged Hawk** registers at E=4 -- the **softest raptor in the engine**, the **Arctic visitor**, and the final hawk before the Falconidae door opens at degree 231. The Rough-legged Hawk (*Buteo lagopus*) is a Buteo, like the Ferruginous Hawk at degree 229, but it is a Buteo from a completely different world: it breeds on the Arctic tundra, on cliffs and ridges above the tree line in Alaska, the Yukon, and the Northwest Territories, and it arrives in Washington only as a winter visitor, appearing from October through March in open fields and agricultural lowlands. Its catlike mew -- soft, almost querulous, a sound more appropriate to a small passerine than to a hawk -- registers at E=4, the lowest raptor reading since the Golden Eagle's thin whistle at degree 223. The Rough-legged Hawk is the engine's acoustic outlier among raptors: a large bird whose voice is small, a hawk that sounds like it is not entirely sure it belongs in the same family as the Red-tailed Hawk's Hollywood scream. It is also the last Buteo in the engine before the Falconidae family opens, and its low E reading creates a deliberate acoustic valley before the Peregrine Falcon's sharp alarm arrives at degree 231.

## Key Features

- **Part A -- S36:** Pond (Grunge/Indie, E=6) -- Portland's Sub Pop entry, R.E.M./Big Star-adjacent, melodic counterweight to the surrounding E=7 entries
- **Part B -- SPS:** Rough-legged Hawk (catlike mew, E=4) -- SOFTEST RAPTOR, Arctic visitor, winter-only species, final Buteo before Falconidae opens
- Arctic visitor ecology -- the Rough-legged Hawk is one of the few raptors in Washington whose presence is entirely seasonal; unlike the Red-tailed Hawk (year-round) or the Ferruginous Hawk (breeding east, winter west), the Rough-legged Hawk has no breeding presence in Washington at all; it arrives from its Arctic tundra breeding grounds each fall and departs by April; in Washington it favors the same open country as the Ferruginous Hawk -- agricultural fields, shrub-steppe, coastal lowlands -- and can often be seen hovering in place over a field, a behavior called kiting that is uncommon among large raptors; its Arctic origin makes it one of the engine's true long-distance migrants
- Final Buteo -- the Rough-legged Hawk is the engine's last documented Buteo, closing out the Buteo sub-arc that began with the Red-tailed Hawk at degree 228 and continued with the Ferruginous Hawk at degree 229; three consecutive Buteos, three different ecologies (widespread resident, arid-country breeder, Arctic visitor), and three different acoustic registers (E=6, E=6, E=4); the Buteo sub-arc is the engine's most tightly grouped taxonomic cluster since the Accipiter trio (Cooper's, Sharp-shinned, Goshawk) in degrees 225-227
- Portland crosses north -- Pond's inclusion extends the engine's geographic range beyond Seattle proper to encompass the broader Pacific Northwest music corridor that ran between Portland and Seattle; bands regularly played both cities, labels in Seattle signed Portland acts and vice versa, and the musical culture of the two cities was more continuous than the state line suggested; Pond's Sub Pop deal is the documentary evidence of that continuity; the Portland entry joins the Candlebox (degree 223, originally from Los Angeles before relocating to Seattle) as a reminder that Seattle's scene was not hermetically sealed
- E=4 acoustic valley -- the Rough-legged Hawk's E=4 is the lowest reading in the SPS track since the Golden Eagle at degree 223, and it functions as a deliberate acoustic valley preceding the Peregrine Falcon's sharp kak-kak-kak at degree 231; the engine's SPS progression from degree 228 through 232 traces a V-shape: E=6 (Red-tailed), E=6 (Ferruginous), E=4 (Rough-legged), E=7 (Peregrine), E=6 (Prairie Falcon); the valley is structural, created by the softest raptor immediately before the family with the most acoustic force
