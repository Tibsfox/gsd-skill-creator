**Pond** arrive at E=7 -- the Spokane-origin, later Portland-based grunge band formed in 1991 by Charlie Campbell (guitar), Randy Noles (vocals), Matt Stiteler (bass), and Chris Brady (drums), whose Sub Pop debut *Pond* (1993) stands as one of the label's overlooked masterworks of the grunge peak period: melodic, heavy, produced with a clarity that amplified rather than obscured the songs' emotional structure, and arriving at a moment when Sub Pop's catalog was being overshadowed by the major-label grunge that *Nevermind* had activated. Pond were not Nirvana and were not trying to be -- they were doing something quieter and more considered within the same sonic territory, a band whose songs used grunge's guitar weight as an emotional instrument rather than as a genre requirement, producing records that reward careful listening in a genre context that mostly rewarded volume.

**Cascade rock avalanche** arrives at E=8 on the SPS track -- the geophysical percussion event produced when a Cascade Range slope failure releases multi-ton boulders and accumulated rock debris in a cascading collapse, generating a sound that begins with the initial crack of the triggering rock break (transient, high-frequency), progresses through the rumbling acceleration of the mass in motion (low-frequency, building duration), and resolves in the final impact and settling (extended low-frequency reverberation). The Cascade rock avalanche is not a rare event -- the Cascades are an actively eroding volcanic and glacially modified mountain range, and slope failures occur throughout the range at the frequency that geology, freeze-thaw cycles, and snowmelt permit -- but the acoustic event when a large avalanche occurs is one of the Pacific Northwest's most dramatic naturally produced sounds: the initial crack carries kilometers, the rumble fills the mountain valleys, and the impact echoes off the opposing slopes for thirty seconds to two minutes after the primary event.
