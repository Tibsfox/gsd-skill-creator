## Part B: Cascade Rock Avalanche — Accelerating Multi-Ton Percussion at E=8, Geophysics Returns, the Self-Amplifying Cascade, the Summer Peak Season, and the Cliff-Face Release That Is a Scaled-Down Version of the May 18, 1980 Mt. St. Helens Eruption

- **The Cascade Rock Avalanche Is a Pure Mass-Movement Acoustic Event with No Biology and No Behavior.** The **Cascade Rock Avalanche** at E=8 documents a pure mass-movement acoustic event — no biology, no behavior, only gravity, fractured rock, and slope. The degree 322 entry marks **GEOPHYSICS RETURNS** in the post-Summit sequence after the Cascadia Subduction Current at degree 318 (E=5), and the E=8 calibration captures a local event rather than a plate-scale event: a cliff face releasing, not a plate boundary shifting. The mass involved is tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of kilograms, not cubic kilometers. The acoustic footprint is enormous in the immediate alpine environment but attenuates rapidly with distance, and E=8 captures the local acoustic intensity without overstating the large-scale geological significance.
- **The Initiation Phase: A Silent Free Fall Before the First Impact.** The initiating block falls freely from the cliff face in silence for approximately 3.2 seconds over 50 meters of free-fall height. During free fall, the block is silent — there is no friction surface to produce sound. Immediately before the block releases, sharp cracking sounds may be audible at close range — the fracture propagating through the final intact rock mass at 2,000-8,000 Hz, brief (0.1-0.5 seconds), preceding the main event by 0-5 seconds. The initiating event is often impossible to observe: a crack propagating at depth until the block moves. Initiation mechanisms include frost-wedging opening a fracture until structural support is lost, thermal expansion on a warm afternoon exceeding residual friction, seismic events (common in the Cascades near active volcanic centers), and water infiltration from snowmelt or summer thunderstorm reducing pore water pressure in fractures.
- **The First Impact: 450,000 Joules of Kinetic Energy in a Broadband Shock Wave.** The first impact of the rock mass on the slope below the cliff face is the acoustic event's most intense initial moment: a percussive impact that produces a broadband shock wave. A 1,000 kg block impacting at 30 m/s (terminal velocity for this size and density over 50 meters) delivers approximately 450,000 joules of kinetic energy at the moment of impact. This energy is rapidly converted into heat, deformation, and sound. The acoustic output is an impulsive broadband signal — percussive but with significant low-frequency content (50-200 Hz) from the shock wave and the momentum transferred to the slope, perceived as a thunder crack with a body-vibration low-frequency component. The high-frequency content (500-2,000 Hz) comes from the fragmentation of the impacting rock.
- **The Acceleration Phase: Self-Amplifying Multi-Ton Percussion.** After the first impact, the rock avalanche transitions from single-block fall to mass movement. The initial block shatters on impact and continues downslope; it is joined by rocks dislodged by the impact's vibration; additional blocks on the cliff face above, marginally stable, are released by the vibration. The mass grows and accelerates. This acceleration phase is the "multi-ton percussion" of the degree 322 description: not a single impact but a continuous, growing, accelerating cascade of impacts as increasing mass moves at increasing velocity down the slope. A fully developed rock avalanche is a self-amplifying acoustic event: the falling mass dislodges additional material, which increases the mass in motion, which increases the acoustic output, which produces additional vibration in the cliff face, which releases additional material. The acoustic envelope is growing throughout the acceleration phase — louder as more mass enters motion, broader in frequency as smaller fragments (higher frequency) join larger blocks (lower frequency). The full cascade acoustic profile covers 20-2,000 Hz.
- **Peak Amplitude at 100 Meters Exceeds 120 dB, Comparable to a Steller Sea Lion Bull Roar or Heavy Artillery.** Near the avalanche (within 100 meters), peak amplitude can exceed 120 dB — comparable to the acoustic maximum of a Steller Sea Lion bull roar at the degree 316 documentation or a heavy artillery discharge. At 500 meters, the event is still clearly audible at 90-100 dB against the alpine ambient (alpine environments are acoustically quiet when undisturbed). At 2 kilometers, the event is audible as a distinct rumble. Total event duration depends on cliff height and slope length: for a 100-meter cliff with 200 meters of active slope below, the total acoustic event is 30-90 seconds, with the initiating impact of 1-3 seconds, the full mass-movement acceleration of 10-30 seconds, and the deceleration tail of 20-60 seconds.
- **Glacial U-Shaped Valley Walls Extend Perceived Duration Through Acoustic-Chamber Reverberance.** In a Cascade alpine cirque or valley, the rock avalanche acoustic event is amplified by wall reflections. A cliff-face avalanche in a U-shaped glacial valley produces multiple reflections from the valley walls, extending the perceived duration and adding a diffuse reverberant quality to the direct signal. The mountain becomes a natural acoustic chamber. This reverberant quality is a defining characteristic of Cascade alpine avalanche acoustics that distinguishes them from avalanches in non-glacial topographies, and the glacial-valley acoustic-chamber effect is part of what the E=8 calibration captures at first-class resolution rather than only the primary mass-movement signal.
- **Summer Is the Peak Season for Cascade Rock Avalanches.** Rock avalanches in the Cascades are most frequent in summer for specific physical reasons. Freeze-thaw cycles throughout the previous winter have progressively opened existing fractures in the cliff face. The summer afternoon thermal cycle (cold night, warm day) produces daily expansion and contraction stress in surface rocks. The driest conditions of the year reduce water cohesion in debris that might otherwise hold unstable rock fragments in place. The summer Cascade cliff face is a loaded system, and the afternoon thermal maximum is the typical trigger time. The degree 322 event occurs on a cliff face 50-300 meters in height on the upper slopes of a Cascade peak or ridgeline, at elevation 4,000-8,000 feet, where steep rock is exposed above and below, and on a summer afternoon a fracture that has been widening through the winter and spring reaches a threshold and the block releases.
- **Volcanic and Non-Volcanic Cliff Types Both Produce Active Mass-Movement Sites.** Mt. Rainier is the most rock-avalanche-active major peak in Washington State, due to extensive hydrothermal alteration in its upper flanks where hydrothermal alteration replaces strong crystalline rock minerals (feldspar, pyroxene) with weaker clay minerals (smectite, kaolinite); documented events include the 2006 South Tahoma Glacier headwall event and the 2015 Sunset Amphitheater seismic record. The North Cascades produce rock avalanches on vertical granite walls of the Picket Range, the Cascade Crest near Glacier Peak, and the steep faces of the Liberty Bell group near Washington Pass; the 2003 Heather Hill rock avalanche near Cascade Pass was triggered by a summer afternoon thermal event. The Enchantments granite terrain above Leavenworth is an active rock-fall zone. At catastrophic scale, the May 18, 1980 Mt. St. Helens eruption was triggered by approximately 2.5 cubic kilometers of rock avalanche material (the largest recorded rock avalanche in North American history) releasing the pressurized magmatic system — the degree 322 cliff-face event is a scaled-down version of the same physical principle that catastrophically altered the Pacific Northwest landscape 46 years ago.
