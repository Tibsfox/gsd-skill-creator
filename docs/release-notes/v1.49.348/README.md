**The Porcupine Enters as First Erethizontidae.** The **North American Porcupine** (*Erethizon dorsatum*) arrives at degree 207 as the **first Erethizontidae** in the SPS engine, adding a new family-level taxonomic node to the species-vocabulary. The Erethizontidae family (New World porcupines) diverged from the Old World Hystricidae (Old World porcupines) approximately **30 million years ago**, and the two families now occupy separate evolutionary trajectories despite having convergently-evolved similar quill-based defensive systems. The North American Porcupine is the only Erethizontidae representative found in the United States and Canada, with distribution spanning Pacific Northwest forests (Olympic Peninsula, Cascade Range foothills, Coast Mountains, Northern Rockies), Eastern Canada, Northern United States, and Mexican high-country. Adult body mass ranges from 12 to 35 pounds, with males typically larger than females. **Approximately 30,000 Quills as Defensive Armor.** The Porcupine's defensive arsenal is staggering in scale. Approximately **30,000 quills** cover the animal's body, concentrated on the back, sides, and tail with the face and underside relatively quill-free. Each quill is 2 to 3 inches long, loosely attached to the body so that contact with a predator detaches the quill and embeds it in the predator's tissue, and tipped with 700 to 800 microscopic barbs that expand on contact with warm flesh and work the quill progressively deeper into the tissue across hours or days following the initial contact. Contrary to popular myth, Porcupines cannot throw or project their quills — the defensive register operates through physical-contact quill-detachment rather than through ranged projection. A single encounter between a Porcupine and a predator can embed hundreds of quills in the predator's face, mouth, or forelegs, with potentially-fatal consequences if the quills migrate toward vital organs or the predator cannot remove embedded quills to resume feeding. The quill-defense is therefore structurally distinct from acoustic-warning defenses (the Black Bear jaw-pop at 201, the Bighorn Sheep clash at 205) and from projectile-and-venom defenses (absent from the engine's Pacific Northwest mammalian register). Crucially, the quill-defense is **silent** — it produces no acoustic signature before, during, or after deployment. The Porcupine's acoustic register operates entirely separately from its defensive register.

**The Porcupine's Vocal Social Life Contradicts the Defensive Reputation.** Despite the fearsome quill-armor reputation, Porcupines are surprisingly vocal animals. The species' vocal repertoire includes moans, whines, shrieks, high-pitched cries, and a distinctive falsetto call, with vocalizations most active during mating season (**October-November**) when males and females engage in extended vocal exchanges that can continue for hours across forest-understory terrain. The vocalizations are most acoustically present at night, making Porcupines one of the more vocally active nocturnal mammals in Pacific Northwest forests. The **Porcupine moan** specifically — the vocalization documented at degree 207 at E=3 — is a extended-duration low-frequency vocalization produced during social encounters and pair-bonding interactions, functioning as a social-coordination and mate-attraction vocalization rather than as a threat display. The E=3 rating captures the moan's relatively low absolute amplitude and its occurrence in the forest-understory acoustic environment, where the vocalization competes with wind-through-trees, rain-on-fern-fronds, and the ambient sound of Pacific Northwest forest night. The vocal repertoire's existence is surprising given the popular image of Porcupines as silent, slow-moving, quill-covered defensive specialists, and the degree 207 entry documents the species' actual acoustic register accurately rather than reproducing the defensive-specialist stereotype.

## Part B: Porcupine — The First Erethizontidae Entry and the Pacific Northwest Forest-Floor Nocturnal Acoustic Register

- **Species Baseline and Taxonomic Identity:** **North American Porcupine** (*Erethizon dorsatum*) is a member of **Order Rodentia / Family Erethizontidae / Genus Erethizon / species dorsatum**, the only member of the family found in the United States and Canada, and the first Erethizontidae representative to enter the SPS engine. Adult body mass ranges from 12 to 35 pounds, with males typically larger than females, and the species' characteristic defensive arsenal of approximately 30,000 quills covers the back, sides, and tail while leaving the face and underside relatively quill-free. The species' life span in the wild extends 5-7 years typically, with captive specimens reaching 10-15 years, and the species is active year-round rather than hibernating despite Pacific Northwest forest-winter conditions.
- **Erethizontidae Family-Level Taxonomic Node Enters the Engine:** The **Erethizontidae family** (New World porcupines) enters the SPS engine at degree 207 as a new family-level taxonomic node, adding to the species-vocabulary after the Mountain Beaver's Aplodontidae debut at degree 206. Erethizontidae diverged from the Old World **Hystricidae** (Old World porcupines) approximately **30 million years ago**, with the two families now occupying separate evolutionary trajectories despite having convergently-evolved similar quill-based defensive systems. The North American Porcupine is the only Erethizontidae representative found in the United States and Canada; the family's Neotropical members (including the genera *Coendou* and *Chaetomys*) occupy Central and South American rainforest habitats rather than the temperate forest of the species' North American range. The family-level entry at degree 207 establishes a new branch in the engine's rodent taxonomic coverage.
- **Pacific Northwest Distribution Across Federal Conservation Units and Forest Ecosystems:** Porcupines are distributed across Pacific Northwest forests including the **Olympic Peninsula** (Olympic National Park), the **Cascade Range** (Mount Rainier National Park, North Cascades National Park, Mount Baker-Snoqualmie National Forest, Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forest), the **Coast Mountains** (Olympic National Forest, Pacific Coast foothills), and the **Northern Rockies** (Olympic Peninsula-adjacent Cascade foothills). The species is also distributed broadly across Eastern Canada, Northern United States, and Mexican high-country, but the Pacific Northwest populations provide the engine's primary species baseline. Habitat preferences include mature coniferous and mixed-coniferous forest with dense understory, access to deciduous trees for bark feeding, and rocky outcrops or large hollow trees for denning. Home range varies seasonally from approximately 10-50 acres in summer to more restricted winter ranges where dens concentrate activity.
- **Approximately 30,000 Quills as Passive-Contact Defensive Armor:** The Porcupine's defensive arsenal consists of approximately **30,000 quills** covering the back, sides, and tail. Each quill is 2 to 3 inches long, loosely attached to the body so that physical contact with a predator detaches the quill and embeds it in the predator's tissue, and tipped with 700 to 800 microscopic barbs that expand on contact with warm flesh. The barb expansion causes the quill to work progressively deeper into the tissue across hours or days following the initial contact, with potentially-fatal consequences if quills migrate toward vital organs or the predator cannot remove embedded quills. Contrary to popular myth, Porcupines **cannot throw or project their quills** — the defensive register operates through physical-contact quill-detachment rather than ranged projection. The defensive behavior sequence involves the Porcupine raising and rotating its body to present the quill-covered back and tail toward an approaching predator, then swinging the tail to contact the predator's face or forelegs. The quill-defense is structurally a passive-and-contact-dependent system.

The moan operates in the low-frequency register that propagates effectively through dense Pacific Northwest forest-floor acoustic environments, where higher-frequency vocalizations would be attenuated by canopy cover, wet-forest ground vegetation, and ambient rain-and-wind noise. The moan's extended duration supports detection against the persistent low-frequency background sound of Pacific Northwest forest night, and the vocalization's functional register is social-coordination-and-mate-attraction rather than threat-display or territory-announcement. The E=3 rating captures the moan's relatively low absolute amplitude and its adaptive amplitude-and-frequency trade-off against the specific acoustic environment rather than indicating general indifference to communication.

The vocal exchanges include moans, shrieks, whines, high-pitched cries, and a distinctive falsetto call, with the repertoire's breadth contradicting the popular image of Porcupines as silent, slow-moving, defensive-specialist animals. The mating-season vocal exchanges are most acoustically active at night, making Porcupines one of the more vocally active nocturnal mammals in Pacific Northwest forests during the October-November window. Mating-season vocal activity is reduced during non-mating months but does not disappear entirely; Porcupines maintain vocal communication throughout the year, with peak activity during the fall mating window and secondary activity during post-birth spring months when mothers and young communicate.
