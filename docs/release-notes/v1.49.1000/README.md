## Summary

v1.49.1000 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.190 to 1.191, adding CRRES — the Combined Release and Radiation Effects Satellite — as the obs#6 INTRA-AXIS continuation of the reopened near-Earth magnetosphere substrate-axis. The magnetosphere axis reopened at v995 FAST (obs#1, the in-situ high-cadence auroral-acceleration-region snapshot science), was carried at v996 DE-1 (obs#2, the first global auroral imaging), at v997 IMAGE (obs#3, the first dedicated global imaging of the entire inner magnetosphere), at v998 Polar (obs#4, the multi-wavelength auroral imaging and polar-cusp in-situ sampling), and at v999 AMPTE (obs#5, the in-situ ring-current ion composition and the artificial tracer releases), and at v1000 CRRES continues the axis at obs#6 with the comprehensive in-situ mapping of the radiation-belt environment. Where AMPTE sampled the ring-current ions directly to read their composition while releasing ion clouds that traced the plasma flow, CRRES swept repeatedly through the radiation belts on a geosynchronous-transfer orbit to map the proton and electron belt populations, discovered a second proton radiation belt formed by the great geomagnetic storm of March 1991, and measured the radiation environment's effect on microelectronics, adding the focused radiation-belt environment substrate to the imaging, in-situ, and composition foundations the axis already holds.

CRRES was a joint mission of NASA and the United States Air Force, with NASA Marshall Space Flight Center leading the NASA element and the Air Force Geophysics Laboratory providing the space-environment payload. The mission idea brought two purposes to the inner magnetosphere at once: a spacecraft carrying energetic-particle detectors would map the radiation-belt environment in detail, and a dedicated Microelectronics Package on the same spacecraft would measure how that environment affects electronic components. CRRES launched 1990-07-25 on an Atlas I vehicle from Cape Canaveral into a geosynchronous-transfer orbit, an elliptical path that carried the spacecraft from low altitude out through the heart of the radiation belts twice per revolution.

The signature of the mission is the comprehensive in-situ mapping of the radiation-belt environment. The energetic-particle detectors measured the proton and electron populations of the belts across radius, energy, and time as the transfer orbit swept the full radial structure of the inner magnetosphere over and over, building a detailed model of the belt environment that gave the focused energetic-particle counterpart to the ring-current ion composition picture the catalog already held.

The mapping campaign carried a landmark discovery: during the great geomagnetic storm of March 1991, CRRES observed the rapid formation of a new, second proton radiation belt at an unexpected location, capturing the creation of a long-lived belt feature directly. The observation transformed the understanding of how quickly the belt structure can reorganize, showing the belts as a dynamic feature that a single strong storm can reshape. Alongside the belt mapping, the Microelectronics Package recorded the space-radiation effects on electronic components in real time, the first systematic in-orbit measurement of its kind, and the dosimeters mapped the accumulated radiation dose along the orbit to produce dose maps of the inner-magnetosphere environment. Because the same spacecraft mapped the particle environment and measured its effect on the electronics and the accumulated dose, the data linked the environment directly to its consequences.

The science return joined the in-situ mapping of the radiation-belt environment to the measurement of its effect on technology. The belt populations were mapped directly across the inner magnetosphere, the storm-formed second proton belt was captured as it appeared, and the radiation effects and the accumulated dose were measured alongside, so CRRES read the environment and its consequences together. The early chemical-release canisters produced tracer clouds for plasma and ionosphere experiments alongside the radiation-belt mapping.

The axis-relevant substrate of CRRES at this degree is its continuation of the reopened near-Earth magnetosphere axis at obs#6 with the comprehensive in-situ mapping of the radiation-belt environment. CRRES sustains the MAGNETOSPHERE-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#6 cumulative (v995 FAST + v996 DE-1 + v997 IMAGE + v998 Polar + v999 AMPTE + v1000 CRRES), and it opens the INNER-MAGNETOSPHERE-ENERGETIC-PARTICLE-LINEAGE thread at obs#2 cumulative (v999 AMPTE ring-current ions + v1000 CRRES radiation-belt particles, CRRES supplying the focused belt-environment picture that complements the ring-current composition). As the obs#6 entry on the reopened axis, CRRES adds the radiation-belt environment and the microelectronics-effects substrates, and it sets the radiation-belt-environment anchor from which the reopened magnetosphere axis will grow. The forward continuation favors further substorm, reconnection, multi-spacecraft, and ionosphere-coupling missions.

CRRES is the first NASA and United States Air Force joint mission in the catalog, and the partnership records NASA Marshall leading the NASA element with the Air Force Geophysics Laboratory providing the space-environment payload. ## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis can be deepened by a focused environment map after composition sampling.** AMPTE sampled the ring-current ions to read their composition, and CRRES then mapped the radiation-belt environment in detail, showing that an axis can deepen by adding a focused environment map to the composition foundation, an obs#6 continuation that complements the composition sample with a belt-environment picture.
2. **A repeated-traversal orbit builds a time-resolved environment map.** The geosynchronous-transfer orbit carried CRRES through the belts twice per revolution, so the same region could be measured over and over as conditions changed, showing that a repeated-traversal orbit can turn a single spacecraft into a time-resolved survey of the inner magnetosphere.
3. **A single strong storm can build a new radiation belt rapidly.** CRRES captured the rapid formation of a new proton belt during the great geomagnetic storm of March 1991, showing that the belt structure is dynamic and can be reshaped quickly by a single storm, a result that reshaped the understanding of the radiation belts.
4. **Measuring the environment and its effect together links cause to consequence.** Because the same spacecraft mapped the particle environment and measured its effect on the electronics, CRRES tied the measured belt environment directly to its effect on technology, showing that measuring an environment and its consequence on the same platform links the two directly.
5. **A dose map turns a particle population into an engineering model.** The dosimeters mapped the accumulated dose alongside the particle measurements, showing that mapping the dose a population deposits turns a measured particle environment into a usable engineering model of the conditions a spacecraft encounters.
6. **A joint partnership can fly one mission across two organizations.** CRRES joined NASA and the United States Air Force in one mission, NASA Marshall leading the NASA element and the Air Force Geophysics Laboratory providing the payload, showing that a shared mission can distribute the science and the payload across two organizations while operating as a single coordinated whole.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across a distinct-palette mission.** Because CRRES reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.190 template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the radiation-belt environment mapping with a distinct proton-belt-amber / electron-belt-blue / dose-violet palette, making the distinct-palette mission a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The radiation belts are not a fixed background.** The belts were long thought of as a stable feature that varies slowly, but CRRES captured the rapid formation of a whole new proton belt during a single storm, so the catalog's first comprehensive belt-environment map also recorded the belts reorganizing in real time.
- **An enduring alpine mammal mirrors an enduring spacecraft.** The Mountain Goat, which endures and crosses the harshest exposed alpine terrain as a matter of course, enters as the endure-the-environment mirror to the mission that swept through and endured the intense radiation-belt environment, so the catalog pairs a high-exposure alpine mammal with a radiation-belt-environment mission.
- **A lichen literally draws a map.** The Map Lichen's growth pattern spreads across bare rock like a chart, so the catalog's radiation-belt-environment mapping mission is paired with a lichen that endures extreme exposure while its growth maps the surface it grows on.

## Dedication

v1.191 CRRES is dedicated to the team that built and flew the Combined Release and Radiation Effects Satellite: the CRRES science team who designed a mission to map the radiation-belt environment in situ while measuring its effect on technology; the Air Force Geophysics Laboratory space-environment team who built the energetic-particle detectors that mapped the proton and electron belts and captured the storm-formed second proton belt during the great geomagnetic storm of March 1991; the Microelectronics Package team who made the first in-orbit measurement of space-radiation effects on electronics; the dosimetry team who mapped the accumulated dose across the inner magnetosphere; and the NASA Marshall Space Flight Center team who led the NASA element of the joint mission. It is dedicated as well to the wider community of radiation-belt physicists and spacecraft engineers whose work CRRES advanced. CRRES launched in 1990 on a single vehicle, swept the belts on every revolution, and joined the radiation environment directly to its effect on technology.
