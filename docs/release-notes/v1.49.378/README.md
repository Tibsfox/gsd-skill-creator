## Summary

**Foo Fighters** arrive at E=7 -- the band that is, in the most literal structural sense, the post-Nirvana signal. Dave Grohl founded Foo Fighters in Seattle in 1994 immediately after Kurt Cobain's death, recording the first album entirely himself in a matter of weeks: all instruments, all vocals, every track. The act was simultaneously a grief mechanism and a proof of survival -- here is what happens when a drummer sits down and makes an entire album alone, in a home studio, without a plan for releasing it. That album leaked, spread, and became the debut. The Foo Fighters that followed were a proper band: Grohl plus Pat Smear, Nate Mendel, Taylor Hawkins, and Chris Shiflett across different configurations, but the founding identity -- Grohl as the engine, the band as an expression of what survives catastrophe -- never left. Post-grunge as a genre label is imprecise, but what it names is real: the bands that came after the collapse of the first-wave Seattle moment, that carried the melodic and emotional architecture of grunge into a slightly cleaner production context, without abandoning the underlying commitment to volume. Foo Fighters belong squarely to this category, and their arrival at E=7 -- two steps below the engine's maximum -- reflects a band that is loud and direct without hitting the ceiling that more aggressive predecessors reached. At degree 237, the engine stands at **65.8% complete**, moving through deep Quadrant III.

On the species side, the **Chinook Salmon** opens the **Salmonidae run** -- the engine's dedicated passage through Pacific salmon and trout species that will run from degree 237 through 242. *Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*, the King Salmon, is the largest Pacific salmon species, reaching weights above 100 pounds in exceptional individuals. Its **body-slap** sound -- the distinct crack of a large fish leaping and falling back against the surface of the water -- registers at E=6, the loudest sound in the Salmonidae arc. The Chinook is the salmon that defines the Pacific Northwest in the broadest cultural imagination: the one that built the canneries, the one that supported Coast Salish nations for thousands of years, the one that shaped the Columbia River's seasonal calendar. Its conservation status is complicated -- some populations are listed under the Endangered Species Act, others sustain commercial fisheries -- and its presence in the engine reflects both the ecological centrality of this species and the weight of what has been lost and what is at stake. The Foo Fighters / Chinook pairing is a degree of survivors: both carry the weight of what came before.
