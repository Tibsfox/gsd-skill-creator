## Summary

v1.49.1013 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.203 to 1.204, adding the Compton Gamma Ray Observatory (CGRO) — the second of NASA's four Great Observatories, the gamma-ray one — as the obs#1 first INSTANCE that OPENS the GAMMA-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis (axis-rotation #28 OPENED at obs#1). The infrared-astronomy axis sustained three observations from v1010 IRAS through v1012 WISE and reached a natural pair of forms, the all-sky survey and the deep pointed observatory; the forward continuation rotates the cadence to a different band of the spectrum, in gamma rays. Compton carried out the first comprehensive survey of the whole sky in gamma rays, the highest-energy light, from the most energetic phenomena in the universe, opening space gamma-ray astronomy as a full-sky science.

Compton was managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center and named for Arthur Holly Compton, the Nobel laureate whose study of how gamma rays scatter off electrons underlies gamma-ray detection. It reached low Earth orbit on 5 April 1991, carried aloft by the Space Shuttle Atlantis on mission STS-37, and at about seventeen tons it was the heaviest astrophysical payload flown to that time. The observatory carried four complementary instruments — BATSE, OSSE, COMPTEL, and EGRET — that together spanned photon energies across six decades, from roughly thirty thousand to thirty billion electron-volts.

The signature of the mission is the first comprehensive survey of the whole sky in gamma rays. The four instruments together covered the entire sphere across six decades of photon energy, so for the first time the most energetic sky was charted comprehensively, revealing a sky of the most energetic sources where lower-energy telescopes show little.

The first all-sky gamma-ray-burst monitor turned the bursts into a measured population. Compton's BATSE instrument watched the entire sky continuously for gamma-ray bursts, the brief, intense flashes of gamma rays that arrive from across the universe, and recorded several thousand of them, catching each one wherever and whenever it appeared.

The gamma-ray-burst isotropy result reshaped where the bursts are placed. BATSE found the bursts spread uniformly across the whole sky, with no concentration toward the plane of the Milky Way, strong evidence that they lie far beyond our galaxy at cosmological distances across the universe.

The first high-energy gamma-ray all-sky map gave the field its first full census of the most energetic sky. Compton's EGRET instrument produced the first map of the whole sky at the highest gamma-ray energies, charting active galaxies called blazars, gamma-ray-bright pulsars, and a diffuse gamma-ray background.

The axis-relevant substrate of Compton at this degree is its opening of the GAMMA-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#1, the first comprehensive full-sky gamma-ray survey. Compton opens the GAMMA-RAY-ASTRONOMY-AXIS thread at obs#1 NEW LOCKED, opens the GAMMA-RAY-ASTRONOMY-LINEAGE thread at obs#1 cumulative, and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#76 cumulative (axis-rotation #28 OPENED at obs#1). The forward continuation may favor a later gamma-ray observatory that sustains the axis at obs#2 — a dedicated burst-locating observatory or a large high-energy gamma-ray telescope — or a substrate-axis rotation #29 to an under-served axis.

Compton opens the gamma-ray way of seeing the sky on the catalog, and the mission records the Compton team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, the BATSE team at NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center, and the OSSE, COMPTEL, and EGRET teams. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.203 to 1.204, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#87 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A new band of the spectrum opens a new way of seeing the sky.** By surveying the whole sky in gamma rays, the highest-energy light, Compton revealed a sky of the most energetic sources where lower-energy telescopes show little, showing that each band of the spectrum opens a distinct view of the universe.
2. **A single observatory carrying complementary instruments spans a wide energy range.** By carrying four complementary instruments on one platform, Compton spanned six decades of photon energy from soft gamma rays to the very highest, showing that complementary instruments let one observatory study a source across a wide span.
3. **A continuous all-sky watch turns rare events into a measured population.** By watching the entire sky continuously, BATSE caught several thousand gamma-ray bursts, showing that a continuous all-sky monitor turns brief, unpredictable events into a large, well-measured population.
4. **A uniform distribution across the sky places sources beyond the galaxy.** By finding the bursts spread uniformly across the whole sky, BATSE placed them far beyond our galaxy at cosmological distances, showing that the spatial distribution of a population reveals where it lies.
5. **An axis opens at a substrate-form-distinct first observation.** By surveying the whole sky in gamma rays for the first time, Compton opened the gamma-ray-astronomy axis at obs#1, showing that an axis-opening rotation begins a new domain rather than extending an old one.
6. **The physics of detection ties an observatory to its name.** By building COMPTEL around the Compton-scattering geometry, the mission tied its name to the way it sees, showing how the underlying physics of detection shapes an instrument's design.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across a rotation.** Because Compton reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.203 WISE template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the gamma-ray survey while keeping the warm palette, making the axis-opening rotation a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The catalog's first gamma-ray survey is also its first all-sky-burst census.** Compton was built to survey the gamma-ray sky, and that same wide watch, through BATSE, became the first large census of the gamma-ray bursts, so the axis's first survey is also the mission that turned the bursts into a measured population.
- **A wide-sky aerial insectivore mirrors the survey of the whole gamma-ray sky.** The Common Nighthawk, the long-winged bird that patrols a wide expanse of the open sky and catches the brief, sudden event on the wing, enters as the wide-sky mirror to the all-sky gamma-ray-burst monitor, so the catalog pairs a bird that watches the open sky and catches the brief flash with an observatory that watched the whole sky for the brief flashes of gamma-ray bursts.
- **A pioneer wildflower of the most energetic events mirrors the survey of the most energetic sky.** Fireweed flourishes in the wake of the most energetic disturbances and returns its bright stand across a wide range, so the catalog's first gamma-ray survey is paired with a plant drawn to where the energy is greatest, the way Compton observed the most energetic sources in the universe.

A future revision could load encoded Compton data for a higher-fidelity rendering keyed to real observations.

v1.204 Compton is dedicated to the teams that built and flew NASA's gamma-ray survey of the whole sky: the Compton team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center who built and managed the observatory; the BATSE team at NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center who watched the entire sky for the gamma-ray bursts and found them spread uniformly across the sky; the OSSE team at the US Naval Research Laboratory who read the gamma-ray spectra; the COMPTEL team at the Max Planck Institute and the University of New Hampshire who imaged the medium-energy sky; the EGRET team at NASA Goddard and Stanford University who mapped the highest-energy sky; and the shuttle crew of mission STS-37 who carried the about-seventeen-ton observatory to orbit aboard Atlantis. It is dedicated as well to Arthur Holly Compton, whose study of how gamma rays scatter off electrons underlies gamma-ray detection. Compton surveyed the whole sky in gamma rays for the first time and mapped the most energetic sources known.
