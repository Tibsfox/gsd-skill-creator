## Surprises

- **The colour of the sea turned into the life within it.** SeaWiFS did not measure the life of the ocean directly — it measured light. The sensor read the water-leaving radiance in eight narrow bands, the colour the sea returns to space, and from the ratios of the blue and green bands (the OC4 algorithm) it recovered the chlorophyll-a concentration, and from the chlorophyll the phytoplankton biomass and the primary productivity. The greenness of the water, read from above, became a map of the living, growing ocean.
- **A NASA science mission flown on a satellite NASA did not own.** Rather than building and owning the spacecraft, NASA bought the data. SeaWiFS was NASA's first "data-buy" mission: the instrument was a NASA Goddard sensor, built by the Hughes Santa Barbara Research Center, but it flew aboard OrbView-2 / SeaStar, a satellite built and operated commercially by Orbital Sciences Corporation, and NASA purchased the science data record rather than the satellite — a new model for a NASA Earth-science mission.
- **The sensor that tilted to avoid the sun's reflection.** Looking down at the sunlit sea, an ocean-colour sensor risks being blinded by the sun glint, the mirror-bright reflection of the Sun off the water. SeaWiFS carried a tilt mechanism that could point its field of view +20°, 0°, or −20° along the orbit, steering the view away from the glint so the faint water-leaving radiance — the true colour of the sea — could be read cleanly.
- **A record that outlived the satellite.** SeaWiFS stopped collecting data in December 2010 and the mission was concluded in early 2011, but the climate-quality record it built lives on. The 1997–2010 ocean-colour record remains a benchmark for the long-term study of the living ocean, reprocessed with improved calibration and algorithms and distributed through NASA's Earthdata to this day — a retired mission whose data still works.
- **A PNW forage fish that lives on the bloom the colour reveals.** The Pacific Herring (*Clupea pallasii*), a small schooling forage fish of the PNW coastal sea, feeds on the zooplankton that graze the phytoplankton bloom and spawns on submerged nearshore vegetation — a silver fish of the productive coastal water, its food the bloom whose chlorophyll SeaWiFS read as the colour of the sea (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A slender green plant of the brackish coastal shallows.** The Widgeon Grass (*Ruppia maritima*), a slender green submerged aquatic flowering plant of PNW estuaries and brackish coastal lagoons, holds thin green stems and grass-like leaves in the sunlit shallow coastal water — a green photosynthetic plant of the living sea, bound with the throughline of the colour of the living sea.
