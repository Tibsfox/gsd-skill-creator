## Mission Overview

SeaWiFS is the obs#23 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's pioneering dedicated global ocean-colour mission, the Sea-viewing Wide Field-of-view Sensor that read the colour the sea returns to space and turned it into maps of the chlorophyll, the phytoplankton, and the productivity of the living ocean. ★ It was a NASA Goddard (GSFC) ocean-colour SENSOR; the instrument was built by the Hughes Santa Barbara Research Center (SBRC, later Raytheon), and NASA GSFC ran the SeaWiFS Project (calibration, processing, distribution). ★★ It flew aboard the OrbView-2 / SeaStar satellite, built and operated commercially by Orbital Sciences Corporation (ORBIMAGE), and it was NASA's FIRST "data-buy" mission — NASA purchased the science data rather than owning the satellite. SeaWiFS measured the water-leaving radiance of the sea in eight spectral bands (centres 412, 443, 490, 510, 555, 670, 765, 865 nm; visible + near-infrared); the band ratios (the OC4 algorithm) turned the colour of the water into the chlorophyll-a concentration → the phytoplankton biomass and the ocean primary productivity, the green of the living, productive sea read from above. The instrument gave ~1.1 km local-area coverage (LAC) / ~4.5 km global-area coverage (GAC), near-daily global coverage, and used a tilt mechanism (+20° / 0 / −20°) to steer the field of view away from the sun glint on the sea. The mission launched 1 August 1997 on an Orbital Sciences Pegasus XL, air-launched from the "Stargazer" L-1011 aircraft, aboard OrbView-2 / SeaStar, into a sun-synchronous orbit at ~705 km, inclination ~98.2°; science observations began ~18 September 1997. The story is the Pegasus XL air-launch from the "Stargazer" L-1011, the deployment of OrbView-2 / SeaStar into the sun-synchronous ~705 km orbit, the sensor sweeping its wide field of view across the sunlit sea, the eight bands measuring the water-leaving radiance, the tilt mechanism steering away from the glint, the band ratios of the OC4 algorithm turning the colour of the water into the chlorophyll-a concentration, and the chlorophyll-a mapped into the phytoplankton biomass and the primary productivity of the global ocean, near-daily, year after year, for more than thirteen years. The science is central: from the sun-synchronous polar orbit SeaWiFS measured the water-leaving radiance of the sea — the colour the ocean returns to space — and turned its eight bands, by the OC4 band-ratio algorithm, into the chlorophyll-a concentration, the phytoplankton biomass, and the primary productivity of the living ocean, near-daily and global. ★★★ SeaWiFS is RETIRED, so the catalog frames the whole MISSION in the PAST (SeaWiFS orbited the Earth, measured the water-leaving radiance, read the colour of the sea, and mapped the chlorophyll and the phytoplankton); it launched 1 August 1997, began science ~18 September 1997, and operated until data collection ended 11 December 2010, the mission formally concluded in February 2011 — the EXCEPTION being the climate-quality data record, which endures (the 1997–2010 record remains a benchmark and is still reprocessed and distributed today, present). SeaWiFS's firsts are scoped carefully: it was the pioneering dedicated, operational, global ocean-colour mission; the eight-band measurement of ocean chlorophyll and phytoplankton; and the first multi-year, climate-quality, continuous record of global ocean primary productivity (1997–2010). ★ The scope is load-bearing: ocean colour was measured from space before SeaWiFS — the Nimbus-7 Coastal Zone Color Scanner (CZCS, 1978–1986) was the proof-of-concept ocean-colour instrument — so SeaWiFS is NOT "the first ocean-colour" sensor; its distinction is being the pioneering DEDICATED, operational, global ocean-colour mission and the first multi-year climate-quality record of ocean primary productivity. ## Surprises

- **The colour of the sea turned into the life within it.** SeaWiFS did not measure the life of the ocean directly — it measured light. The sensor read the water-leaving radiance in eight narrow bands, the colour the sea returns to space, and from the ratios of the blue and green bands (the OC4 algorithm) it recovered the chlorophyll-a concentration, and from the chlorophyll the phytoplankton biomass and the primary productivity. The greenness of the water, read from above, became a map of the living, growing ocean.
- **A NASA science mission flown on a satellite NASA did not own.** Rather than building and owning the spacecraft, NASA bought the data. SeaWiFS was NASA's first "data-buy" mission: the instrument was a NASA Goddard sensor, built by the Hughes Santa Barbara Research Center, but it flew aboard OrbView-2 / SeaStar, a satellite built and operated commercially by Orbital Sciences Corporation, and NASA purchased the science data record rather than the satellite — a new model for a NASA Earth-science mission.
- **The sensor that tilted to avoid the sun's reflection.** Looking down at the sunlit sea, an ocean-colour sensor risks being blinded by the sun glint, the mirror-bright reflection of the Sun off the water. SeaWiFS carried a tilt mechanism that could point its field of view +20°, 0°, or −20° along the orbit, steering the view away from the glint so the faint water-leaving radiance — the true colour of the sea — could be read cleanly.
- **A record that outlived the satellite.** SeaWiFS stopped collecting data in December 2010 and the mission was concluded in early 2011, but the climate-quality record it built lives on. The 1997–2010 ocean-colour record remains a benchmark for the long-term study of the living ocean, reprocessed with improved calibration and algorithms and distributed through NASA's Earthdata to this day — a retired mission whose data still works.
- **A PNW forage fish that lives on the bloom the colour reveals.** The Pacific Herring (*Clupea pallasii*), a small schooling forage fish of the PNW coastal sea, feeds on the zooplankton that graze the phytoplankton bloom and spawns on submerged nearshore vegetation — a silver fish of the productive coastal water, its food the bloom whose chlorophyll SeaWiFS read as the colour of the sea (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A slender green plant of the brackish coastal shallows.** The Widgeon Grass (*Ruppia maritima*), a slender green submerged aquatic flowering plant of PNW estuaries and brackish coastal lagoons, holds thin green stems and grass-like leaves in the sunlit shallow coastal water — a green photosynthetic plant of the living sea, bound with the throughline of the colour of the living sea.

## Dedication

v1.303 SeaWiFS is dedicated to the people at NASA Goddard, the Hughes Santa Barbara Research Center, and Orbital Sciences Corporation, and across the ocean-colour science teams, who built and flew the sensor that read the colour of the living sea from space and turned the light the ocean returns into a map of the life within it. The scientists who learned to read the chlorophyll and the phytoplankton of the global ocean, who built the first long, climate-quality record of the productive sea, and who keep that record alive and reprocessed long after the satellite fell silent in 2010. The colour the living ocean returns to space was theirs to read, and the green of the productive sea belongs to them.
