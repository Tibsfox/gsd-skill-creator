- **The Green River Split of 1988 as the Grunge Era's Most Productive Dissolution.** Green River dissolved in 1988 along a fault line of commercial-direction disagreement. **Mark Arm** and **Steve Turner** wanted the noise-and-fuzz underground direction; **Jeff Ament** and **Stone Gossard** wanted the commercial rock direction. The disagreement was irreconcilable and the band split along the fault line. Arm and Turner, joined by **Matt Lukin** on bass (formerly the Melvins) and **Dan Peters** on drums, formed Mudhoney in 1988 and went immediately to Sub Pop Records. Ament and Gossard recruited **Andrew Wood** as vocalist and formed Mother Love Bone; Wood died of a heroin overdose in March 1990 before Mother Love Bone's debut album release; Ament and Gossard then recruited **Eddie Vedder** and formed Pearl Jam in 1990. The single 1988 dissolution produces the entire Seattle grunge genealogy that the engine will document across degrees 294 (Green River itself), 295 (Malfunkshun), 296 (Mother Love Bone), 309 (TAD), 310 (Mudhoney), 311 (Nirvana), and 314 (Pearl Jam). - **"Touch Me I'm Sick" (August 1988) Is the First Grunge Single and Sub Pop Catalog Number 18.** "Touch Me I'm Sick" (Sub Pop SP18, August 1988) is the document that defines grunge as a fully realized form. The guitar sound uses the Big Muff Pi fuzz pedal cranked to maximum through a distorted amplifier; the aesthetic position is "beautiful ugliness" rather than "powerful distortion." The lyric ("Touch me, I'm sick") is a statement of self-conscious contamination engaging with the damaged and the discarded, lineage traced to The Stooges' "I Wanna Be Your Dog" and "Loose" fully metabolized and made Pacific Northwest. The timing is mutually defining: the single released August 1988, the same month Sub Pop Records officially began operations as a label. "Touch Me I'm Sick" and Sub Pop launched simultaneously. The single is not just a debut record; it is the definition of the label's identity. In 1990, Mudhoney and **Nirvana** released a split single where Mudhoney covered Parliament/Funkadelic's "Maggot Brain" and Nirvana covered "Touch Me I'm Sick," and the exchange is a mutual statement of respect between the two most important Sub Pop bands of the pre-breakthrough moment. **Kurt Cobain**'s version of "Touch Me I'm Sick" is one of the best available documents of his engagement with the Sub Pop aesthetic.
- **Superfuzz Bigmuff (1988) and the Tool-Naming Transparency.** The *Superfuzz Bigmuff* EP (Sub Pop, 1988) is named explicitly after its tools: the Univox Super-Fuzz pedal and the Big Muff Pi fuzz pedal. The tool-naming transparency is a radical act — the record is what it says it is, which is the application of these specific tools at maximum commitment. No mystification. No artistic distance. The tools are the art. *Superfuzz Bigmuff* defined Sub Pop's sound for international audiences and exported the Seattle underground aesthetic beyond the regional scene. - **Mark Arm's Vocal Register and The Sonics Lineage as Sonic Determinants.** **Mark Arm**'s vocal delivery is the most distinctive in Seattle grunge: the sneer, the howl, the garage-punk inflection borrowed from **Iggy Pop** and **The Stooges** filtered through **The Sonics**' Pacific Northwest rawness. Arm's voice encodes the Seattle underground's relationship to its own tradition — knowing, irreverent, technically capable of more than it chooses to produce, committed to the abrasive register as a philosophical position rather than a stylistic limitation. ## Part B: Mixed Dawn Chorus Pacific Storm — Overlapping Multi-Species Calls in Driving Rain at E=8, FIRST MULTI-SPECIES MAXIMUM-ZONE ENTRY in the Engine's 360-Degree Run, Eight Primary Species of the Pacific Northwest Puget Sound Lowlands, April-June Spring Peak, Light-Triggered Species Ordering, Rain White-Noise Floor and Volume-Selection Mechanism, and the Community-Level Acoustic Architecture

- **Eight Primary Species and the Pacific Northwest Dawn Chorus Composition.** The eight primary species of the Pacific Northwest Puget Sound lowland spring dawn chorus constitute the community that produces the E=8 event. **American Robin** (*Turdus migratorius*) anchors the middle register with pure whistled phrases at 2-8 kHz and the distinctive "cheerily, cheeri-up, cheer-up" phrase structure that is one of the most recognizable bird calls in the Pacific Northwest; the Robin continues singing through moderate to heavy rain more consistently than most other species, and its territorial imperative is strong enough that weather rarely silences it. **Pacific Wren** (*Troglodytes pacificus*) is the most acoustically complex small bird in the Pacific Northwest, producing a song containing thirty-plus distinct syllable types in a single bout covering 5-9 kHz despite a body mass of only 8-10 grams, and the species is associated with the dark moist forest understory that Pacific storms create. **Song Sparrow** (*Melospiza melodia*) is the most individually variable singer in the dawn chorus with an eight-to-twenty-song repertoire per individual switched between continuously at 2-6 kHz. **Swainson's Thrush** (*Catharus ustulatus*) arrives in Puget Sound in April-May as a spring migrant and produces the rising spiral phrase structure that is among the most beautiful bird calls in the Western Hemisphere. **Black-capped Chickadee** (*Poecile atricapillus*) adds the fee-bee territorial whistle and the chick-a-dee-dee-dee alarm call whose number of "dee" notes increases with predator threat level. **Red-winged Blackbird** (*Agelaius phoeniceus*) contributes the konk-la-ree territorial call at 1-3 kHz fundamental as one of the lowest-frequency contributions to the chorus from wetland edges throughout Puget Sound. **Dark-eyed Junco** (*Junco hyemalis*) provides the musical trill at 5-8 kHz as a high-frequency contribution with both resident and migratory populations present in spring. **Common Raven** (*Corvus corax*) grounds the chorus with deep gurgling, yodeling, and clicking calls at 200-1,500 Hz as the lowest-frequency sub-bass anchor.
- **The April-June Spring Peak and the Light-Triggered Species Ordering.** The spring dawn chorus peaks in late April through June. The chorus is triggered primarily by light level, not temperature or weather. Individual species respond to specific light thresholds at dawn, which is why the order in which species begin singing is predictable and consistent: **American Robin** first (earliest light response), followed by thrushes, then sparrows and chickadees, then blackbirds, then wrens, then juncos. The sequence is determined by each species' light sensitivity threshold and is a community-level behavioral fact emerging from the shared evolutionary history of the eight species rather than from any individual species' calling choice. At peak spring, the chorus begins approximately forty-five minutes before sunrise and sustains for sixty to ninety minutes. During this window the maximum number of species are singing simultaneously before the full morning light causes individual birds to shift from territorial singing to foraging.
- **The Pacific Storm as Acoustic Medium and the White-Noise Floor.** A Pacific storm overlapping with the spring dawn chorus produces the E=8 event through several mutually reinforcing acoustic physics phenomena. Pacific low-pressure systems can arrive at any time, but spring storms (April-May) frequently arrive on diurnal cycles that overlap with dawn because the atmospheric dynamics that produce spring storm intensification overnight mean the storm's acoustic peak often coincides with the biological dawn chorus peak. Rain on alder and big-leaf maple canopy — the dominant deciduous trees of Puget Sound lowlands — produces a broadband white noise floor at 40-80 decibels depending on rain intensity. Wind through Sitka spruce and Douglas fir tops adds a continuous sub-bass drone at 50-200 Hz beneath the bird calls, and this component is felt before it is distinctly heard, providing the physical grounding of the E=8 event.
- **The Volume-Selection Mechanism and the Rain-Filtered Chorus Character.** The storm's acoustic floor raises the minimum audible volume for individual bird calls and functions as an involuntary volume-selection mechanism: birds that cannot be heard over the rain do not contribute to the E=8 calibration, and only species that call loudly enough to rise above the rain floor are included. The volume selection amplifies the relative presence of loud callers (Robin, Pacific Wren, Red-winged Blackbird) and reduces the contribution of softer callers. The net effect is a chorus that is simultaneously more uniform in apparent volume and richer in tonal character than a fair-weather chorus. The rain-filtered chorus is not a diminished version of the fair-weather chorus; it is a different acoustic event with its own structural signature, and the E=8 placement registers the rain-filtered variant at first-class resolution.
- **Humid Air Acoustic Carry and the 2-5 kHz Advantage Frequency Range.** Saturated air actually carries mid-frequency bird calls (2-5 kHz range) further than dry air in some conditions, because the humidity reduces the high-frequency air absorption that limits call carry distance in dry conditions. Some species may call more effectively in rain conditions at their key communication frequencies, and the Song Sparrow's 2-6 kHz range, the Robin's 2-8 kHz range, and the Red-winged Blackbird's 1-3 kHz range all partially overlap the humidity-advantage zone. The acoustic physics at the storm-chorus intersection produces an environment in which some species are acoustically advantaged relative to their fair-weather reception, and the advantage structures the storm-chorus's species composition and volume balance at the community level.
- **Individual Identity Preserved in Simultaneous Combination.** The multi-species dawn chorus storm's defining structural principle is that individual identity is preserved in simultaneous combination. You can hear the Robin's cheerily phrases and the Pacific Wren's complex trill separately within the storm chorus even through the rain. Each species' call retains its species-specific acoustic signature — its frequency range, its phrase structure, its rhythm — and the combined sound is not a blur of indistinguishable voices but a dense, layered architecture in which each voice is individually identifiable by an experienced listener. The preservation of individual identity beneath the combined noise is the dawn chorus community's defining structural principle and the direct SPS parallel to Mudhoney's two-guitar simultaneous fuzz where Arm's rhythm guitar and Turner's lead are separately identifiable within the combined mix.
- **The Ecosystem-Level Composite and the Geography-Produces-the-Voice Principle.** The Puget Sound lowland ecosystem produces the conditions under which the eight primary species can share the same acoustic time-window at spring peak. Alder and big-leaf maple deciduous cover mixed with Sitka spruce and Douglas fir conifer cover, wetland edges providing Red-winged Blackbird habitat, shoreline shrubs providing Song Sparrow habitat, dark moist forest understory providing Pacific Wren habitat, and urban-edge providing Black-capped Chickadee habitat together constitute the habitat mosaic that supports the community composition. The ecosystem-level documentation at first-class resolution is the SPS track's parallel to the neighborhood-level documentation at first-class resolution on the S36 track: geography produces the voice, and the voice is documented at the specific-geography register rather than at generic region-average register. The E=8 placement registers the Puget Sound lowland composite at first-class resolution.
