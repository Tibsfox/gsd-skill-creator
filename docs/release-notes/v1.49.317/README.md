**Pear-Shaped Eggs and the Evolution of the Cliff Ledge.** The Common Murre's **pyriform egg** — broad at one end, sharply pointed at the other — is one of the most studied eggs in all of ornithology, and its adaptive significance is a direct answer to the geometry of cliff-ledge nesting. When bumped, a pear-shaped egg rolls in a **tight circle** rather than rolling straight off the ledge; the pyriform shape converts linear momentum into circular motion, keeping the egg on the narrow rock platform where it was laid. A spherical egg would roll straight and fall; the Common Murre's egg does not. The shape has been studied by physicists, engineers, and ornithologists, and **Tim Birkhead**'s 2016 book *The Most Perfect Thing: Inside (and Outside) a Bird's Egg* (Bloomsbury) documents the historical ornithological debate and current evolutionary-biology consensus on the pyriform adaptation. The Common Murre's egg also shows **extraordinary color variation** — blue, green, white, brown, spotted, streaked, plain — within a single colony, and this variation may help parents identify their own egg in the densely packed colony where thousands of eggs sit side by side on the rock. The engine's documentation at degree 177 preserves the pear-shaped egg as a **morphological first** entering the engine's egg-morphology vocabulary, and the pyriform geometry joins the seasonal rhinoceros horn (degree 175) as a second structural-first anatomical feature within the Alcidae four-degree run.

**The Cliff-Leap Fledging and the Dramatic Life-History Event.** Perhaps the most dramatic moment in the Common Murre's life history is the **cliff-leap fledging**: when chicks are only **15 to 25 days old** and still completely flightless, they **leap from their cliff ledges** — sometimes 300 feet or more down to the sea below — without the ability to fly. The cliff-leap is one of the most dramatic life-history events in all of bird biology, and its survival is predicated on the father's post-leap shepherding of the chick through the vulnerable sea-swimming phase. ## Part B: Common Murre — The Fourth Alcidae and the Colony Roar

- **Fourth Alcidae in the Engine.** The Common Murre (*Uria aalge*) enters at degree 177 as the **FOURTH ALCIDAE** (auk family) in a remarkable four-degree auk-family streak (174-177); the four entries collectively demonstrate that a single seabird family contains dramatically different life-history registers across crevice-nesting, nocturnal-burrow, cliff-burrow, and dense-open-ledge strategies, and the four-degree streak stands as the engine's deepest documented seabird family.
- **Genus Uria Opens.** The **Uria** genus — containing the Common Murre (*Uria aalge*) and the Thick-billed Murre (*Uria lomvia*) — enters the engine at degree 177 as a structural first; the genus name Uria derives from the Greek *ouria* (a type of waterbird mentioned by Aristotle and Athenaeus) and extends the engine's Alcidae taxonomic coverage beyond the previously documented Cepphus (Guillemot), Cerorhinca (Auklet), and Fratercula (Puffin) genera.
- **Dense Open-Ledge Cliff Nesting.** The Common Murre nests directly on bare rock at extreme packing density — up to **20 pairs per square meter** on narrow cliff ledges — with birds standing shoulder-to-shoulder on ledges sometimes only a few centimeters wide; the dense open-ledge nesting strategy is the densest of any bird species globally, and the predator-deterrence mechanism is direct: there is no space for a predator to land.
- **Pear-Shaped Pyriform Egg.** The Common Murre's egg is strongly pyriform (pear-shaped) — broad at one end, sharply pointed at the other — and rolls in a **tight circle** rather than off the ledge when bumped; the anti-roll geometry converts linear momentum into circular motion and is one of the most studied egg morphologies in ornithology, documented comprehensively in **Tim Birkhead**'s 2016 *The Most Perfect Thing: Inside (and Outside) a Bird's Egg* (Bloomsbury).
- **Extraordinary Egg Color Variation.** Common Murre eggs show extraordinary variation in color and pattern — blue, green, white, brown, spotted, streaked, plain — within a single colony, and the variation may help parents identify their own egg in the densely packed colony where thousands of eggs sit side by side on bare rock; the egg-color-variation-for-parent-recognition hypothesis is the current ornithological consensus and enters the engine's egg-morphology vocabulary at degree 177.
- **Colony Roar as Collective-Sound E=5.** The Common Murre's growling guttural **arrr** call is moderate at the individual scale but multiplies by thousands into a **wall-of-sound colony roar** — one of the most immersive seabird soundscapes on Earth — continuous for the duration of the breeding season and audible from considerable distances at major colony sites; the colony roar enters the engine's acoustic-energy vocabulary as a new pathway to **collective-sound E=5** calibration distinct from individual-voice approaches.
- **Cliff-Leap Fledging and Post-Leap Father Shepherding.** Common Murre chicks leap from their natal cliff ledges at only **15 to 25 days old** while still completely flightless — sometimes 300+ feet down to the sea below — and the **male parent accompanies the chick at sea for weeks afterward**, feeding and protecting the flightless juvenile while it grows its flight feathers in the open ocean; the cliff-leap fledging enters the engine's life-history vocabulary as a dramatic structural first, and the father-chick post-leap sea relationship joins the engine's parental-investment vocabulary.
- **Penguin-of-the-North Diving-Seabird Register.** The Common Murre is sometimes called the "penguin of the Northern Hemisphere" — it stands upright on cliff ledges, displays a crisp black-and-white plumage pattern, and dives deep (100+ meters, 3+ minute breath-holds) using its wings for underwater propulsion — representing evolutionary convergence with Southern Hemisphere penguins as a specialized pursuit-diving seabird, and the "penguin-of-the-North" framing enters the engine's morphology vocabulary at degree 177 as the reference handle for pursuit-diving alcid documentation.
