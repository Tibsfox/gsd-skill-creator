## Summary

The twenty-fifth paired release and the Mercury program's farewell. On May 15, 1963, at 8:04 AM Eastern Standard Time, Atlas 130-D pushed Mercury spacecraft number 20 off Launch Complex 14 at Cape Canaveral. Inside was Leroy Gordon Cooper Jr., thirty-six years old, an Air Force major, the youngest of the Mercury Seven at selection, the most junior in rank, the most patient. While Glenn orbited and Carpenter experimented and Schirra engineered, Cooper trained and waited. He was the last of the original seven to fly solo, and the mission he got -- the longest, the most demanding, the one that required the most from both the machine and the man -- was the one best suited to his methodical, calm, unhurried temperament. He named the capsule Faith 7. He never elaborated much on why. But the choice was operational: after eight Mercury flights, each pushing the envelope further, the final mission required faith that the systems would hold for thirty-four hours, faith that the pilot could endure a full day in microgravity, and faith that a spacecraft design now five years old was adequate for an endurance flight that would push it to its absolute limits.

Twenty-two orbits. Thirty-four hours, nineteen minutes, forty-nine seconds. A full day and a half in a capsule measuring 1.89 meters at its widest -- a volume of 1.7 cubic meters, less than a telephone booth. Every system was a consumable: oxygen, lithium hydroxide for CO2 scrubbing, water for the heat exchanger, hydrogen peroxide for attitude control, battery power. The mission timeline was a countdown of consumables -- the spacecraft would operate until the first critical system ran out, and the pilot's job was to manage all of them simultaneously so that none ran out before splashdown. Cooper ate, slept (the first American to sleep in space -- seven hours, not continuous, but genuine sleep confirmed by biomedical telemetry), photographed (he reported seeing individual houses, roads, and vehicle tracks from orbit -- the human visual system performing beyond laboratory predictions when given a zero-scatter view from above the atmosphere), deployed a flashing beacon sphere for visual tracking experiments, and operated the first live television camera from an American spacecraft.

Then, on orbit 19, the systems began failing. The 0.05g indicator lit up falsely -- a sensor malfunction that would confuse the automatic control system during actual reentry. On orbit 20, the attitude reference gyroscopes drifted beyond usable range. The automatic stabilization system lost its ability to determine which way the capsule was pointing. On orbit 21, CO2 levels began rising -- the lithium hydroxide canisters approaching saturation after thirty hours of continuous absorption. The electrical system degraded: the main inverter showed intermittent failures, bus voltage fluctuated, instruments lost power and did not recover. Cascading failures -- not a single-point problem but the cumulative degradation of a spacecraft operating far beyond its design point. The ground controllers faced the question: end the mission early or trust Cooper to fly it home manually?

They chose Cooper. John Glenn, serving as CapCom in the Pacific tracking station, talked Cooper through the manual retrofire procedure. Cooper aligned the capsule using visual reference to the stars and the horizon -- no functioning gyroscopes, no automatic attitude control, just a pilot's eyes and the constellations. He fired the retrorockets manually, at the correct time, at the correct attitude. He flew the reentry by hand -- maintaining the correct bank angle through the communications blackout when the ionized plasma of reentry blocked all radio contact and he was alone with a failing spacecraft at 28,000 kilometers per hour. He splashed down 6 kilometers from the recovery carrier USS Kearsarge. The precision was remarkable -- better than Glenn's automated landing, comparable to Schirra's textbook, and achieved entirely by hand in a spacecraft whose automatic systems had died. The last Mercury astronaut proved what the program had always claimed: the pilot was not a passenger. The pilot was the backup system. When everything else failed, the man flew the machine home.
