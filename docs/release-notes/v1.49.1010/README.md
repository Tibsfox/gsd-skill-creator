## Summary

v1.49.1010 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.200 to 1.201, adding IRAS — the first spacecraft to survey the entire sky in infrared — as the obs#1 first INSTANCE that OPENS the INFRARED-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis (axis-rotation #27). The solar-observatory axis sustained five observations from v1005 OSO through v1009 Parker Solar Probe and reached a natural capstone with the in-situ flight through the corona; per the rotation discipline the catalog now rotates away from the solar axis and opens a new substrate-axis on a different way of seeing the sky — in infrared light, from a cryogenically cooled telescope above the atmosphere. IRAS opens space infrared astronomy, revealing a sky of dust, cool stars, and distant galaxies invisible to ordinary telescopes. The new axis begins where space infrared astronomy itself began.

IRAS was a joint mission of NASA, the Netherlands Agency for Aerospace Programs (NIVR), and the United Kingdom's Science and Engineering Research Council (SERC), with NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory leading the United States effort. The mission launched on 25 January 1983 aboard a Delta 3910 from Vandenberg Air Force Base and surveyed the sky for about ten months until its superfluid liquid-helium coolant was spent in November 1983, the planned span of a cryogenic mission. IRAS carried a 57-centimeter Ritchey-Chrétien telescope cooled by superfluid liquid helium to a few degrees above absolute zero, so the instrument's own thermal glow would not swamp the faint infrared signal, with detectors observing the sky's thermal emission at 12, 25, 60, and 100 micrometers in a Sun-synchronous orbit.

The signature of the mission is the first survey of the entire sky in infrared. Scanning the whole sky in strips from its Sun-synchronous orbit, IRAS turned infrared astronomy from a few pointed observations into a full survey and built a complete picture of the sky's infrared emission.

The mission gave the field its first comprehensive infrared census. From the survey scans IRAS compiled the IRAS Point Source Catalog of about 350,000 infrared sources, the reference infrared-sky census that served astronomers for decades, where no infrared source catalog had existed before.

The survey revealed a sky no one had seen. IRAS detected dusty debris disks around nearby stars such as Vega, Fomalhaut, and Beta Pictoris, the first observational evidence of the material from which other planetary systems form, the Vega phenomenon; it revealed the diffuse infrared cirrus, the wispy clouds of cool dust threading the interstellar medium; and it found the ultraluminous and starburst infrared galaxies, a dust band in the asteroid belt, and several new comets, including comet IRAS-Araki-Alcock.

The superfluid-helium-cooled telescope gave the mission its foundation. By chilling the telescope to a few degrees above absolute zero, IRAS quieted the instrument's own infrared glow so it could read the faint warmth of the sky, the cryogenic cooling form that made sensitive infrared observation from space possible.

The axis-relevant substrate of IRAS at this degree is its opening of the INFRARED-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#1, the new way of seeing the sky in infrared. IRAS opens the INFRARED-ASTRONOMY-AXIS thread at obs#1 NEW LOCKED, opens the INFRARED-ASTRONOMY-LINEAGE thread at obs#1 cumulative, and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#73 cumulative (axis-rotation #27 enacted at v1010). The forward continuation favors later infrared observatories that build on the all-sky-survey, cryogenic-telescope, and infrared-catalog foundations IRAS established.

IRAS opens the infrared way of seeing the sky on the catalog, and the mission records the IRAS team at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, the Netherlands and United Kingdom partner teams, and the infrared-astronomy science teams. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.200 to 1.201, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#84 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Cooling a telescope to the cold lets it read the warmth of the sky.** By chilling the telescope near absolute zero with superfluid liquid helium, IRAS quieted the instrument's own infrared glow, showing that a cryogenic telescope can detect the faint warmth of a sky invisible to ordinary telescopes.
2. **An all-sky survey turns a band into a map.** By scanning the whole sky in infrared from a Sun-synchronous orbit, IRAS turned infrared astronomy from a few pointed observations into a complete map, showing that a survey can open a whole new domain at once.
3. **A first census becomes a lasting reference.** By compiling the IRAS Point Source Catalog of about 350,000 sources, IRAS gave the field its first comprehensive infrared census, showing that a first catalog in a new band can serve as the reference for decades.
4. **An infrared excess reveals the material of forming planetary systems.** By finding far more infrared light around Vega than the star alone could produce, IRAS discovered the dusty debris disks, showing that an infrared survey can reveal the material from which other planetary systems form.
5. **A rotation can open a wholly new way of seeing.** By rotating away from the solar-observatory axis after its capstone, the catalog opened the infrared-astronomy axis with IRAS, showing that a substrate-axis rotation can carry the catalog to a distinct mission family and a new band of the sky.
6. **A planned cryogenic span is enough for a complete first survey.** By mapping the whole infrared sky in the roughly ten months until its helium coolant was spent, IRAS showed that a cryogenic mission's planned span can be enough to carry out a complete first survey and open a field.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across a distinct-palette mission.** Because IRAS reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.200 template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the infrared survey with a distinct infrared-warm / cirrus-violet / source-bright palette, making the distinct-palette mission a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The coldest instrument in the catalog reads the warmth of the sky.** IRAS chilled its telescope near absolute zero, yet that cold is exactly what let it read the warmth of the infrared sky, so the catalog's first infrared entry is the mission cooled to the cold to detect the heat.
- **A heat-sensing snake mirrors the survey that read the warmth of the sky.** The Western Rattlesnake, whose facial pit organs sense infrared, enters as the seeing-in-infrared mirror to the first infrared all-sky survey, so the catalog pairs a pit viper that reads warmth in the dark with a survey that read the warmth of a sky hidden to visible light.
- **A flower that blooms at the snow's edge mirrors the telescope chilled near absolute zero.** The Glacier Lily blooms at the very edge of the melting snow, warmth emerging where it is coldest, so the catalog's first infrared mission is paired with a subalpine lily that brings life out of the cold the way the cooled telescope read warmth from the cold.
