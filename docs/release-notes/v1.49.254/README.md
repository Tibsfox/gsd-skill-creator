## Part B: Three-toed Woodpecker — The Beetle-Kill Specialist and the Picidae Octet Record

- **The eighth consecutive Picidae and the all-time family streak record.** The **Three-toed Woodpecker** (*Picoides dorsalis*) extends the Picidae streak to **eight consecutive degrees** (107-114) at degree 114 — **BREAKS ALL-TIME RECORD**. - **Three toes not four — the diagnostic morphology.** The common name **Three-toed Woodpecker** refers to the species' diagnostic **three-toed zygodactyl foot** — two toes forward, one toe back (rather than the standard woodpecker two-forward-two-back). The reduction to three toes provides **enhanced grip on vertical substrate** at the cost of some climbing versatility, and it places the Three-toed in its own evolutionary lineage separate from the four-toed **Dryobates** (Hairy, Downy), **Dryocopus** (Pileated), and **Melanerpes** (Lewis's, Acorn) woodpeckers. The morphology is the most reliable visual field mark for the species when observed at close range, and it is why the species (together with the Black-backed Woodpecker, its close relative) remained in the genus **Picoides** after the 2015 taxonomic revision that split off the four-toed species into **Dryobates**.
- **Beetle-kill obligate — specialist on disturbed spruce-fir forest.** The **Three-toed Woodpecker** is a **beetle-kill obligate** — the species depends on coniferous forests that have recently experienced **bark beetle outbreaks** (particularly spruce beetle and mountain pine beetle) where dead and dying trees accumulate bark-beetle galleries. The woodpecker flakes off loose bark to expose the galleries and extracts the beetles and their larvae. Outside beetle-kill habitat, the species cannot sustain itself — it requires the specific ecological conditions of post-disturbance conifer forest. As beetle outbreaks have become more frequent and severe with climate change and historical fire suppression, Three-toed Woodpecker populations have concentrated in specific beetle-kill stands and can be locally abundant where outbreaks are severe.
- **The slow deliberate drum — 14-17 strikes per second.** The **Three-toed Woodpecker's** drum runs at approximately **14-17 strikes per second** — slower than the **Hairy's** ~25/sec and roughly matching the **Pileated's** 15-16/sec despite the much smaller body size. The drum is **methodical and deliberate** — individual strikes are more distinguishable than in the machine-gun drums of larger or faster species, and the overall effect is rhythmically distinct. Duration 0.5-1.5 seconds per roll. Fundamental frequency 100-200 Hz. The slow deliberate drum is the species' acoustic territory marker and contrasts with both the Pileated's resonant boom (low-frequency, long detection range) and the Hairy's sharp stutter (mid-frequency, moderate detection range).

Future taxonomy of engine states should distinguish **corridor** (continuous run), **saturation zone** (high-density recurrence with breathers), **cluster** (2-3 consecutive similar entries followed by shift), and **sprinkle** (isolated recurrences at low density). The vocabulary refinement will help future retrospective analysis.
10. **Catalog fidelity scales with catalog maturity and cadence discipline.** Fifty-four consecutive original CSV species (degrees 61-114) demonstrates that the engine's sequencing is robust at unprecedented depth. Future scaling should expect century-length CSV fidelity runs once the catalog reaches full maturity, and substitutions should be treated as exceptional events requiring explicit justification rather than as routine adjustments.
