The signature of this degree is the rapid-response transient form. The five X-ray observatories before it each stare or monitor: Chandra and NuSTAR focus deep images of chosen targets, RXTE and NICER time the variability of known sources, IXPE integrates the polarization of bright ones. None can catch a brief, unpredictable explosion anywhere on the sky and be on it within a minute. Swift is built around exactly that problem, and its three co-pointed instruments divide one job — detect, slew, capture — across three bands while a flight computer closes the loop autonomously with no human involved. ## Mission Overview

The Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory is the obs#6 continuation of the X-ray-astronomy axis and the substrate-form-distinct sustain by observing strategy — a wide-field-triggered, autonomously-slewing, three-band observatory that races to a fleeting cosmic explosion and reads its early multi-wavelength afterglow. A NASA Medium-Class Explorer (MIDEX-3, Explorer 84) selected 14 October 1999 and managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center with its Mission Operations Center at the Pennsylvania State University, it launched 20 November 2004 (17:16:01 UTC) aboard a Delta II 7320-10C (flight Delta 309) from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station, Space Launch Complex 17-A into a near-circular ~585–600-kilometer, 20.6-degree low-Earth orbit. It carries three co-pointed instruments driven by onboard burst software: the Burst Alert Telescope (BAT), a wide-field coded-aperture (coded-mask) gamma-ray imager with a CdZnTe detector plane of 32,768 elements that images and triggers over 15–150 keV across about 2 steradians and computes a burst position to 1–4 arcminutes onboard (lead Scott D. Barthelmy, NASA Goddard); the X-ray Telescope (XRT), a grazing-incidence Wolter Type-I focusing telescope of 12 nested gold-coated nickel mirror shells (the JET-X flight-spare optics) feeding an e2v CCD-22, working over 0.2–10 keV at a modest ~18-arcsecond resolution (lead David N. Burrows, Penn State); and the UV/Optical Telescope (UVOT), a 30-centimeter modified Ritchey-Chrétien telescope over ~170–650 nm through an eleven-position filter wheel onto a photon-counting microchannel-plate-intensified CCD (lead Peter W. A. Roming, Penn State and MSSL). A new BAT position triggers, with no ground command, an autonomous spacecraft slew (baseline under ~90 seconds; first demonstrated 17 January 2005) and a programmed XRT/UVOT sequence; for roughly 30 percent of bursts the narrow-field telescopes begin observing while the burst is still in progress, and alerts are distributed worldwide in seconds through the Gamma-ray Coordinates Network. The founding Principal Investigator was Dr. Neil Gehrels (Goddard), succeeded after his death in 2017 by S. Bradley Cenko; the mission was renamed in his honor on 10 January 2018. Designed for a two-year prime mission, the observatory has operated for more than twenty years — it achieved the first X-ray afterglow and first arcsecond localization of a short burst (GRB 050509B), set the highest-redshift burst record (GRB 090423, z≈8.2), and found the first relativistic jetted tidal disruption event (Swift J1644+57); and it SUSTAINS the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#6 (axis-rotation #35 sustains). Swift operates as a complete, fully successful mission — the rapid-response transient observatory that catches a cosmic explosion as it happens, across three bands.

## Structural firsts

**SWIFT-FIRST-INSTANCE:** Swift enters the catalog as the rapid-response transient observatory — the first instance, the obs#1 first — sustaining the X-ray-astronomy axis at obs#6.

**SHORT-GRB-X-RAY-AFTERGLOW-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The first detection of an X-ray afterglow, and the first arcsecond-scale localization, of a short-duration gamma-ray burst — GRB 050509B (9 May 2005), the XRT on target ~62 seconds after the trigger, refined to ~9 arcsec, host a luminous elliptical at z=0.225. The qualifier matters: long-burst afterglows had been routine since BeppoSAX in 1997, and the first short-burst OPTICAL afterglow is HETE-2's GRB 050709 (a different burst); the short-burst merger origin was established jointly by three 2005 bursts and proved definitively in 2017 by GW170817, so Swift is not credited alone with proving it.

**HIGHEST-REDSHIFT-GRB-FIRST-INSTANCE:** GRB 090423 (BAT trigger 23 April 2009) at redshift z≈8.2 — the most distant spectroscopically confirmed object known and the highest-redshift gamma-ray burst known at the time (2009), ~630 Myr after the Big Bang. The qualifier matters: the prior burst record was Swift's own GRB 050904 (z=6.295), and the record was later surpassed by the photometric GRB 090429B and by JWST-era galaxies beyond z>13; z≈8.2 (Tanvir) and z≈8.1 (Salvaterra) are the same burst — not "the most distant object ever."

**RELATIVISTIC-JETTED-TDE-FIRST-INSTANCE:** Swift J1644+57 (BAT trigger 28 March 2011) — the first identified relativistic, jetted tidal disruption event, a star torn apart by a supermassive black hole that launched a relativistic jet beamed toward Earth (Burrows 2011, Bloom 2011, Levan 2011, with the Zauderer radio companion). The "relativistic / jetted" qualifier matters because thermal, non-jetted tidal-disruption flares were known from the ROSAT era of the 1990s — Swift J1644+57 is the first jetted one, not "the first tidal disruption event ever."

**X-RAY-ASTRONOMY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION:** The X-ray-astronomy axis Chandra opened, NuSTAR extended, RXTE continued, IXPE continued, and NICER continued sustains as a substrate-form-distinct observation at obs#6 (axis-rotation #35 sustains) — Swift is the first multi-wavelength member of the axis, distinguished by observing strategy (the rapid, autonomous, panchromatic capture of a fleeting transient) rather than a new detector physics.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis can sustain on a new observing strategy, not just a new instrument.** The X-ray-astronomy axis sustains at obs#6 because Swift reads the high-energy sky in a way the five focused, timing, and polarimetry observatories could not — by catching a fleeting transient as it happens, autonomously, across three bands. The distinction is the observing strategy and the multi-wavelength reach, not a new detector class, so the cadence continues the axis as a substrate-form-distinct sustain rather than rotating away.
2. **A multi-wavelength mission can still belong to an X-ray axis.** Swift is the first multi-wavelength member of an otherwise X-ray-only axis. Its focusing X-ray telescope and its X-ray-afterglow and X-ray-transient science place it on the axis; the gamma-ray and UV/optical instruments are intrinsic to its form and are correct, not off-axis contamination.
3. **A first can hinge on the conjunction of its qualifiers.** Swift's defining first is not any phenomenon-first (Vela, BATSE, BeppoSAX, and HETE-2 hold those) — it is the rapid, autonomous, routine, multi-wavelength FORM, the conjunction of all four properties. The short-burst, distance-record, and tidal-disruption firsts each carry their own qualifiers (the X-ray afterglow of a SHORT burst; the most distant burst AT THE TIME; the first RELATIVISTIC, JETTED tidal disruption event).
4. **A focusing telescope on an axis of focusing telescopes still needs the resolution stated.** The XRT genuinely focuses X-rays, but at a modest ~18-arcsecond half-power diameter — about 36× coarser than Chandra's ~0.5 arcsec. Stating the modest resolution keeps "focusing" and "imaging" correct Swift content while never claiming Chandra's first-focusing or sub-arcsecond imaging.
5. **The rapid response opened three classes of transient.** Because the focusing telescope was on the burst within minutes, Swift settled the short-burst merger question (GRB 050509B and the old-population host), set the early-universe distance record (GRB 090423), and found a new kind of event (the relativistic jetted tidal disruption Swift J1644+57) — each a consequence of the autonomous slew getting the narrow-field telescopes on target while the event was still bright.
6. **A continuation keeps the predecessors' vocabulary and references them by name.** Because Swift sustains the X-ray-astronomy axis, the shared X-ray vocabulary is correct and kept, and Chandra (obs#1, soft/medium focusing), NuSTAR (obs#2, hard-band focusing), RXTE (obs#3, timing), IXPE (obs#4, polarimetry), and NICER (obs#5, soft-band timing) are referenced as the form complements — but Swift's own facts (the three co-pointed instruments, the autonomous slew, the early-afterglow era, the short-burst / distance-record / tidal-disruption science) and only its own five anchors are stamped; the predecessors' anchors and predecessor-specific hardware (NICER's X-ray Timing Instrument, concentrators, silicon drift detectors, SEXTANT, Gendreau and Arzoumanian, the ISS mounting; IXPE's Gas Pixel Detectors; RXTE's PCA/HEXTE; NuSTAR's deployable mast; Chandra's sub-arcsecond optics) stay with their missions.
7. **Accuracy guardrails guard against the stale-source and fabricated-citation classes of error.** Using the 20 November 2004 launch on a Delta II 7320-10C from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station SLC-17A (not the slipped 17 November date, not a Delta II Heavy, not Vandenberg, not "Space Force Station"); the descriptive (non-acronym) name and the 2018 rename in honor of Neil Gehrels (who died in 2017, a different year); the MIDEX-3 / Explorer 84 selection in 1999 at ~US$163M; the ~585–600 km design orbit; the BAT coded-mask imager (not focusing), the XRT JET-X flight-spare focusing optics with the XMM CCD heritage, and the UVOT eleven-position filter wheel; and only the verified references — keeps the facts load-bearing and correct.
8. **The operations status is a twenty-year body of work, not a live state.** A two-year prime baseline operated for more than twenty years, with a reaction-wheel failure in January 2022 and a gyroscope degradation in March 2024 kept distinct (and not conflated), and a 2025–2026 reduced-operations posture — described as the body of work rather than asserted as a present-tense "fully operational" or "shut down" status.

## Surprises

- **A wide-field trigger and a rapid, autonomous slew open a whole class of science.** Swift adds no sharper image and no new observable; by watching a sixth of the sky for a flash and then repointing its own narrow-field telescopes onto the new position within about a minute, with no human in the loop, it reads the early multi-wavelength afterglow of a cosmic explosion while it is still fading — and that single capability settled the short-burst merger question, set the early-universe distance record, and found the relativistic jetted tidal disruption event.
- **A short burst's host was an old elliptical galaxy.** GRB 050509B's X-ray afterglow sat at the edge of a luminous, non-star-forming elliptical at z=0.225 — an old stellar population with no massive young stars to collapse, which (with GRB 050724 and HETE-2's GRB 050709) made the case that short bursts come from compact-object mergers rather than the collapse that powers the long bursts, proved definitively in 2017 by GW170817.
- **A jumping spider already hunts the way Swift observes.** *Habronattus oregonensis* detects a fleeting target's motion with its wide-field secondary eyes, then rapidly swivels the movable retinae of its high-acuity, colour- and ultraviolet-sensitive principal eyes onto the target to examine it before the pounce — a wide-field motion trigger, a rapid autonomous reorientation, then a narrow-field, multi-wavelength examination, exactly Swift's BAT-trigger / autonomous-slew / XRT-and-UVOT pipeline.
- **A small annual fires its seeds the moment it is touched.** The Little Western Bittercress, the Pacific Northwest native called "shotweed," holds its ripe seed pods under tension and ballistically flings their seeds the instant a fleeting touch sets them off — an explosive, autonomous release triggered by a brief external event, mirror to Swift's rapid, self-actuated response to a transient trigger: a fleeting event firing an immediate response, the touch the burst and the seed launch the slew.

## Dedication

v1.228 Swift is dedicated to the people who built the observatory that races to a cosmic explosion and reads its light across three bands: Dr. Neil Gehrels, the founding Principal Investigator at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, who led the mission from inception and for whom it is now named, and S. Bradley Cenko, who carried it forward; the instrument leads Scott D. Barthelmy, who built the Burst Alert Telescope at Goddard, David N. Burrows, who led the X-ray Telescope at Penn State with Leicester and Brera, and Peter W. A. Roming, who led the UV/Optical Telescope at Penn State with the Mullard Space Science Laboratory; the short-burst and distance-record authors Nial Tanvir, Andrew Levan, Ruben Salvaterra, and Judith Racusin; the tidal-disruption authors David Burrows, Joshua Bloom, and Andrew Levan; and Riccardo Giacconi and Bruno Rossi, who founded X-ray astronomy with the 1962 rocket flight that discovered Scorpius X-1. From a low orbit, a wide-field eye caught the flash of an explosion, a spacecraft turned itself toward it within a minute, and two narrow-field telescopes read the fading light of the first minutes after a star died.
