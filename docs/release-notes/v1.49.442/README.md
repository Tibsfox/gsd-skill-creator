**Hovercraft** arrive at E=6 -- the Seattle noise rock outfit co-founded by Beth Liebling (later Orton) and Rachel Carns, a band that operated at the intersection of noise rock and experimental guitar work in the Sub Pop orbit without fully belonging to the Sub Pop aesthetic. Hovercraft's music was not grunge in the chord-and-hook sense but in the noise-and-energy sense: sustained drone, abrasive texture, the guitar as a source of sustained output rather than a vehicle for riff. In the context of the 301 sequence, Hovercraft arrives at E=6 as the final sixth of the engine opens -- the first degree past the five-sixths mark, the entry that begins the terminal arc of the 360-degree sequence.

The placement is deliberate. After Pond's quiet five-sixths mark at degree 300, the engine's final sixty degrees begin with noise rock -- a genre that operates at lower song-density than grunge but at high textural intensity. E=6 for Hovercraft reflects a band whose energy is sustained and abrasive but whose compositional approach is more expansive than compressed. The noise rock register is the right register for the opening of the final sixth: not a fanfare, not a spike to E=9, but the beginning of a sustained, textured terminal sequence that will escalate through the apex predator arc alongside.

**Roosevelt Elk bugle** arrives at E=8 on the SPS track -- *Cervus canadensis roosevelti*, the largest elk subspecies in North America, producing the signature bugle that defines the autumn rut in Pacific Northwest old-growth and edge habitat. The Roosevelt Elk bugle is one of the most recognizable large mammal vocalizations in the Pacific Northwest: a rising whistle that ascends over three to four seconds, breaks into a flute-like squeal at the peak, and resolves into a series of grunts. The bugle carries over one to two kilometers through forested terrain, a vocal advertisement of dominance, fitness, and territorial occupancy broadcast at first light in October across the Olympic Peninsula, the Cascade foothills, and the Coast Range.

E=8 for the Roosevelt Elk bugle is the correct assignment, and it opens the **APEX PREDATOR ARC** at the threshold. This is not the apex predator itself -- the elk is prey, the largest prey animal in the Pacific Northwest ecosystem, a two-pound animal measured in hundreds -- but the elk bugle is the sound that announces the apex predator arc's arrival. The bugling bull elk in the rut is at its most exposed, at its most audible, at its most visible. It is the acoustic signal that draws the cougar, the wolf, and the bear. The apex predator arc opens not with the predator's voice but with the prey's declaration that the predator's season has begun.
