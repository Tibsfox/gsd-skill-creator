## Summary

v1.49.1043 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.225 to 1.226, adding IXPE and SUSTAINING the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY axis at obs#4 — a SUBSTRATE-FORM-DISTINCT continuation rather than a new rotation. The axis opened at v1040 with the Chandra X-ray Observatory (substrate-axis rotation #35), the grazing-incidence telescope that brought soft and medium-band cosmic X-rays to a half-arcsecond focus, continued at v1041 with NuSTAR, the first telescope to *focus* the hard X-ray band (about 3–79 keV) on a deployable mast, and continued again at v1042 with RXTE, the first satellite dedicated to high-time-resolution X-ray timing. Chandra and NuSTAR read the focused image of the high-energy sky; RXTE read its rapid variability in time. IXPE reaches a fourth observation that is again substrate-form-distinct, and the new trade is wholly different from both: it reads neither the sharp picture nor the light curve but the *polarization* of the X-ray light — the angle and degree of its electric-field vector. Where the focusing telescopes answer *where* a photon came from and the timing observatory answers *when* it arrived, IXPE answers a third question the others cannot: how the source's light is *oriented and ordered*, which recovers the geometry of the emitting region and the structure of its magnetic field. The way of seeing extends from the sharp soft-band image (Chandra), to the focused hard-band image (NuSTAR), to the rapid light curve and power spectrum (RXTE), to the polarization vector field (IXPE) — the alignment of a magnetar's field, the radial-versus-toroidal field of a supernova remnant or pulsar wind nebula, the disk-versus-jet geometry of an accreting black hole. The distinction across this continuation is the observable, not the band or the resolution: IXPE's optics are moderate (~25-arcsecond focus, far coarser than Chandra) and its band is soft (2–8 keV), because its first is *polarimetry*, not sharpness or reach. The axis sustains at obs#4 (axis-rotation #35 sustains at obs#4) rather than capping after the focusing and timing observatories, and the forward cadence can continue to obs#5 — a later timing mission or a next-generation polarimeter — or rotate onward per the substrate-axis-rotation discipline.

IXPE reached orbit on 9 December 2021 (about 06:00 UTC) on a SpaceX Falcon 9 (Block 5, booster B1061) launched from Kennedy Space Center, Launch Complex 39A in Florida, into a near-circular low-Earth orbit at a nominal ~600-kilometer altitude (about 540 km achieved) and a near-equatorial ~0-degree inclination (orbital period ~90 minutes). The near-zero inclination is a deliberate, science-driven choice: an equatorial orbit minimizes passages through the South Atlantic Anomaly, holding the charged-particle background on the gas detectors low, and it keeps the observatory in frequent contact with the Italian Space Agency's equatorial Malindi ground station in Kenya. IXPE is a NASA Small Explorer (SMEX) mission in the Astrophysics Explorers Program — SMEX-14 / Explorer 97 — selected on 3 January 2017 and managed by NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center (MSFC), which also builds the mirror modules and hosts the Science Operations Center; it is a NASA–Italian Space Agency (ASI) partnership in which ASI, with INAF and INFN, provides the polarization-sensitive Gas Pixel Detectors (detector contractor OHB Italia), while the spacecraft was built by Ball Aerospace on its BCP-100 bus. The ~325–330-kilogram observatory was designed for a two-year baseline science mission and has since operated as an extended Guest-Observer facility. IXPE-FIRST-INSTANCE obs#1 NEW LOCKED at v1043.

The signature of IXPE at this degree is the observable it adds. Chandra, NuSTAR, and RXTE all record a photon's energy and arrival, and the two focusing telescopes also record its direction to form an image; none captures its polarization — the orientation of the oscillating electric field the light carries, set by the geometry and magnetic field of wherever it was made. IXPE is built around that one missing property. Its three telescopes feed imaging photoelectric polarimeters that exploit the photoelectric effect: when a 2–8 keV X-ray is absorbed in the detector gas, it ejects a photoelectron whose initial direction is preferentially aligned with the light's electric-field vector; the detector images the photoelectron's track and reconstructs its direction, and the statistical distribution of track angles over many photons yields the polarization degree (how aligned the field is) and angle (its direction). The mission is the first imaging X-ray polarimeter and the first observatory dedicated to X-ray polarimetry of many classes of cosmic source — the polarization counterpart to the focusing and timing observatories on the same axis. Polarimetry of cosmic X-rays did not begin here: a 1971 sounding-rocket experiment by Martin Weisskopf's group made the world's first such measurement, the OSO-8 satellite measured the Crab Nebula's polarization in the 1970s with a Bragg-crystal instrument, and the PolarLight CubeSat flew a small photoelectric polarimeter in 2018 — but all were non-imaging and limited to one or two of the brightest objects. IXPE's distinct firsts are *imaging* the orientation across a source, doing so with roughly two orders of magnitude better sensitivity that opened dozens of source classes, and being a *dedicated* observatory. IXPE carries three identical X-ray telescopes that share one deployable optical bench, all serving the polarization observable. The Mirror Module Assemblies (MMAs) are three flight mirror modules (a fourth was built as a spare), made in-house at NASA Marshall: each is a set of 24 concentrically nested Wolter-I grazing-incidence shells formed by electroformed-nickel replication, focusing 2–8 keV X-rays at a 4-meter focal length with a moderate ≤25-arcsecond half-power diameter — coarse next to Chandra's sub-arcsecond optics, because the mission trades imaging sharpness for an unobstructed beam onto a polarimeter. The Detector Units (DUs) are three identical Gas Pixel Detectors (GPDs), the Italian-built imaging photoelectric polarimeters at each focus (nine were produced and four selected, three flight plus a spare); each images photoelectron tracks in a dimethyl-ether gas cell (about 800 millibar over a ~1-centimeter absorption gap) read out by a custom CMOS ASIC of about 105,600 hexagonal pixels at 50-micrometer pitch, recording for every photon its position, energy, arrival time, and polarization angle. The Gas Electron Multipliers at the heart of the detectors were contributed by RIKEN in Japan, and Nagoya University supplied the mirror-module thermal shields. A 3.7-meter coilable boom (built by Northrop Grumman) extends after launch to set the focal distance between the mirror modules and the detectors — a shared spaceflight technology, not a first, since NuSTAR flew the first deployable-mast X-ray telescope. The observatory is three-axis stabilized and dithered in a Lissajous pattern so that the detectors' small instrumental modulation averages out while the source's true polarization is preserved.

Within weeks of first light IXPE returned orientations no instrument had reached. Its first target, the supernova remnant Cassiopeia A, gave the first X-ray polarization detection of a supernova remnant — a low average polarization (~1.8%) whose magnetic field ran surprisingly radial rather than the expected tangential, compressed geometry (Vink and colleagues, 2022). The mission then made the first detection of linearly polarized X-rays from a magnetar, the ultra-magnetized neutron star 4U 0142+61, where the polarization degree reached 13.5% ± 0.8% over the 2–8 keV band and the polarization angle swung by about 90 degrees across it — a signature pointing to two emission regions and to QED vacuum birefringence in the star's extreme field (Taverna and colleagues, 2022). It measured the first X-ray polarization of a stellar-mass black-hole binary, Cygnus X-1, finding an unexpectedly high 4.0% ± 0.2% signal aligned with the radio jet, which placed the X-ray-emitting corona in the plane of the accretion disk rather than above it (Krawczynski and colleagues, 2022). It mapped the Crab pulsar and nebula at ~20% polarization in a toroidal field (Bucciantini and colleagues, 2023), found the blazar Markarian 501 polarized at ~10% with the angle along the jet — evidence that a shock front accelerates the jet's particles (Liodakis and colleagues, 2022) — and later caught a rotating polarization angle in Markarian 421 (Di Gesu and colleagues, 2023) and the polarization of the brightest accreting neutron star, Scorpius X-1 (La Monaca and colleagues, 2024). ## Mission Overview

IXPE is the obs#4 continuation of the X-ray-astronomy axis and the form-distinct complement to the focusing and timing telescopes before it — the first imaging X-ray polarimeter and the first observatory dedicated to X-ray polarimetry of many cosmic source classes, reading the orientation and order of X-ray light rather than its image or its light curve. A NASA Small Explorer (SMEX-14 / Explorer 97) in the Astrophysics Explorers Program, selected 3 January 2017 and managed by NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center in partnership with the Italian Space Agency (ASI), it launched 9 December 2021 (about 06:00 UTC) on a SpaceX Falcon 9 (Block 5) from Kennedy Space Center, Launch Complex 39A, into a near-circular ~600-kilometer near-equatorial (~0-degree) low-Earth orbit chosen to suppress the particle background and to favor the equatorial Malindi downlinks. It carries three identical telescopes, each a Wolter-I Mirror Module Assembly on a 3.7-meter deployable boom feeding an imaging photoelectric Gas Pixel Detector — 24 nested electroformed-nickel shells per MMA at a 4-meter focal length and a moderate ≤25-arcsecond half-power diameter (founding PI Martin C. Weisskopf, retired 2022; current PI Philip Kaaret); the GPDs image photoelectron tracks in dimethyl ether (~800 mbar) on a ~105,600-pixel CMOS ASIC across 2–8 keV with microsecond GPS time-tagging (Italian INAF/INFN/OHB Italia detectors built around RIKEN Gas Electron Multipliers, Nagoya University thermal shields). The ~325–330-kilogram Ball Aerospace observatory was built for a two-year baseline and now operates in an extended Guest-Observer phase; it made the first X-ray polarization detections of a supernova remnant (Cassiopeia A), a magnetar (4U 0142+61), and a stellar-mass black-hole binary (Cygnus X-1), and mapped the Crab nebula and the blazars Markarian 501 and 421; and it SUSTAINS the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#4 (axis-rotation #35 sustains). IXPE operates as a complete, fully successful mission — the first dedicated window onto the polarization of the X-ray sky.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis sustains when the next observation reads the same kind of source through a wholly new observable.** The X-ray-astronomy axis sustains at obs#4 because IXPE keeps the subject — neutron stars, black holes, magnetars, supernova remnants, pulsar wind nebulae, and blazar jets in the high-energy sky — but reaches it through the polarization rather than the image or the light curve; the substrate-form distinctness is the observable read, so the cadence continues the axis rather than rotating away from it.
2. **A third question lives in the orientation of the light.** The focusing telescopes answer *where* a photon came from and the timing observatory answers *when* it arrived; IXPE answers *how the light is oriented and ordered*, recovering the geometry of the emitting region and the structure of its magnetic field — the property neither the focused image nor the rapid light curve carries.
3. **A first can hinge on the qualifier.** IXPE is the first *imaging* X-ray polarimeter and the first observatory *dedicated* to X-ray polarimetry, not the first to measure cosmic X-ray polarization — a 1971 rocket, OSO-8 (1975), and PolarLight (2018) came earlier; the distinct firsts are imaging the orientation across a source, far better sensitivity, and a dedicated observatory, so the framing carries the "imaging / dedicated / many source classes" qualifier.
4. **A moderate focus is the right trade for a polarimeter.** A sub-arcsecond optic is not needed when the goal is the polarization; IXPE trades imaging sharpness (≤25-arcsecond focus) for an unobstructed beam onto an imaging photoelectric Gas Pixel Detector, the trade the polarization observable demands.
5. **One observatory reads the orientation across many kinds of source.** The same three telescopes recovered the radial magnetic field of the supernova remnant Cassiopeia A, the ~90° angle swing of the magnetar 4U 0142+61, the jet-aligned corona of the black-hole binary Cygnus X-1, the toroidal field of the Crab nebula, the shock-accelerated jets of Markarian 501 and 421, and the polarization of the accreting neutron star Scorpius X-1.
6. **The polarization sees the geometry the image and the light curve cannot.** A radial-versus-toroidal magnetic field, a disk-plane-versus-corona-above geometry, the QED vacuum birefringence of a magnetar's extreme field — all are written into the orientation and order of the light, recoverable only by reading the polarization degree and angle; the fourth observation is what the first imaging X-ray polarimeter sees that the focusing and timing telescopes could not.
7. **A continuation keeps the predecessors' vocabulary and references them by name.** Because IXPE sustains the X-ray-astronomy axis, the shared X-ray vocabulary is correct and kept, and Chandra (obs#1, soft/medium focusing), NuSTAR (obs#2, hard-band focusing), and RXTE (obs#3, timing) are referenced as the form complements — but IXPE's own facts (the Wolter-I Mirror Module Assemblies, the photoelectric Gas Pixel Detectors, the Falcon 9 launch, the equatorial orbit, the polarization results) and only its own six anchors are stamped; the predecessors' anchors and predecessor-specific hardware stay with their missions.
8. **Accuracy guardrails guard against the stale-infobox and fabricated-citation classes of error.** Using a Falcon 9 from Kennedy Space Center LC-39A (not Pegasus, not Cape Canaveral/Vandenberg), a ~600 km near-equatorial orbit, ~325–330 kg mass, ~US$188 million total cost, a two-year baseline, a 3.7 m boom distinct from the 4 m focal length, three flight telescopes with 24 shells per MMA, ≤25-arcsec resolution, the Cas A full-ApJ citation and the distinct Taverna/Krawczynski Science DOIs, and only the verified references — keeps the facts load-bearing and correct.

## Surprises

- **You see a new property by reading the orientation of the light.** IXPE adds no sharper image and no faster clock; by reading the polarization degree and angle of every 2–8 keV photon through the photoelectric tracks in its Gas Pixel Detectors, the mission recovers the geometry and magnetic-field structure of a source that the sharpest focusing telescope and the fastest timing observatory on the same axis cannot carry — a third kind of question answered with a moderate-resolution focus.
- **The supernova remnant's field ran radial, not tangential.** Cassiopeia A's first X-ray polarization, the first ever measured for a supernova remnant, showed a low ~1.8% average degree whose magnetic field pointed surprisingly radial rather than the expected compressed, tangential geometry — an orientation the image alone never revealed (Vink et al., 2022).
- **A forest dragonfly already reads the plane of polarized light the way IXPE reads X-ray polarization.** The Blue-eyed Darner's enormous compound eyes are sensitive to the plane of polarized light, and it uses the horizontally polarized reflection off a water surface to recognize water and orient to it — reading a hidden directional property of light ordinary vision cannot see, exactly the orientation IXPE reads in the polarization angle and degree of the X-ray sky.
- **A subalpine lily's fiber bundle is the alignment IXPE measures as the polarization degree.** Beargrass leaves are bundles of highly aligned parallel fibers — prized as the strongest basketry fiber for exactly that ordered alignment — and the plant raises a densely ordered cylindrical raceme; a strongly aligned magnetic field gives a high polarization degree the way the tightly aligned fiber bundle is strong, and a disordered field gives a low one.

From an equatorial orbit, three telescopes read the orientation written into the high-energy light.
