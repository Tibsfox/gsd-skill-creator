## Surprises

- **You see a new property by reading the orientation of the light.** IXPE adds no sharper image and no faster clock; by reading the polarization degree and angle of every 2–8 keV photon through the photoelectric tracks in its Gas Pixel Detectors, the mission recovers the geometry and magnetic-field structure of a source that the sharpest focusing telescope and the fastest timing observatory on the same axis cannot carry — a third kind of question answered with a moderate-resolution focus.
- **The supernova remnant's field ran radial, not tangential.** Cassiopeia A's first X-ray polarization, the first ever measured for a supernova remnant, showed a low ~1.8% average degree whose magnetic field pointed surprisingly radial rather than the expected compressed, tangential geometry — an orientation the image alone never revealed (Vink et al., 2022).
- **A forest dragonfly already reads the plane of polarized light the way IXPE reads X-ray polarization.** The Blue-eyed Darner's enormous compound eyes are sensitive to the plane of polarized light, and it uses the horizontally polarized reflection off a water surface to recognize water and orient to it — reading a hidden directional property of light ordinary vision cannot see, exactly the orientation IXPE reads in the polarization angle and degree of the X-ray sky.
- **A subalpine lily's fiber bundle is the alignment IXPE measures as the polarization degree.** Beargrass leaves are bundles of highly aligned parallel fibers — prized as the strongest basketry fiber for exactly that ordered alignment — and the plant raises a densely ordered cylindrical raceme; a strongly aligned magnetic field gives a high polarization degree the way the tightly aligned fiber bundle is strong, and a disordered field gives a low one.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis sustains when the next observation reads the same kind of source through a wholly new observable.** The X-ray-astronomy axis sustains at obs#4 because IXPE keeps the subject — neutron stars, black holes, magnetars, supernova remnants, pulsar wind nebulae, and blazar jets in the high-energy sky — but reaches it through the polarization rather than the image or the light curve; the substrate-form distinctness is the observable read, so the cadence continues the axis rather than rotating away from it.
2. **A third question lives in the orientation of the light.** The focusing telescopes answer *where* a photon came from and the timing observatory answers *when* it arrived; IXPE answers *how the light is oriented and ordered*, recovering the geometry of the emitting region and the structure of its magnetic field — the property neither the focused image nor the rapid light curve carries.
3. **A first can hinge on the qualifier.** IXPE is the first *imaging* X-ray polarimeter and the first observatory *dedicated* to X-ray polarimetry, not the first to measure cosmic X-ray polarization — a 1971 rocket, OSO-8 (1975), and PolarLight (2018) came earlier; the distinct firsts are imaging the orientation across a source, far better sensitivity, and a dedicated observatory, so the framing carries the "imaging / dedicated / many source classes" qualifier.
4. **A moderate focus is the right trade for a polarimeter.** A sub-arcsecond optic is not needed when the goal is the polarization; IXPE trades imaging sharpness (≤25-arcsecond focus) for an unobstructed beam onto an imaging photoelectric Gas Pixel Detector, the trade the polarization observable demands.
5. **One observatory reads the orientation across many kinds of source.** The same three telescopes recovered the radial magnetic field of the supernova remnant Cassiopeia A, the ~90° angle swing of the magnetar 4U 0142+61, the jet-aligned corona of the black-hole binary Cygnus X-1, the toroidal field of the Crab nebula, the shock-accelerated jets of Markarian 501 and 421, and the polarization of the accreting neutron star Scorpius X-1.
6. **The polarization sees the geometry the image and the light curve cannot.** A radial-versus-toroidal magnetic field, a disk-plane-versus-corona-above geometry, the QED vacuum birefringence of a magnetar's extreme field — all are written into the orientation and order of the light, recoverable only by reading the polarization degree and angle; the fourth observation is what the first imaging X-ray polarimeter sees that the focusing and timing telescopes could not.
7. **A continuation keeps the predecessors' vocabulary and references them by name.** Because IXPE sustains the X-ray-astronomy axis, the shared X-ray vocabulary is correct and kept, and Chandra (obs#1, soft/medium focusing), NuSTAR (obs#2, hard-band focusing), and RXTE (obs#3, timing) are referenced as the form complements — but IXPE's own facts (the Wolter-I Mirror Module Assemblies, the photoelectric Gas Pixel Detectors, the Falcon 9 launch, the equatorial orbit, the polarization results) and only its own six anchors are stamped; the predecessors' anchors and predecessor-specific hardware stay with their missions.
8. **Accuracy guardrails guard against the stale-infobox and fabricated-citation classes of error.** Using a Falcon 9 from Kennedy Space Center LC-39A (not Pegasus, not Cape Canaveral/Vandenberg), a ~600 km near-equatorial orbit, ~325–330 kg mass, ~US$188 million total cost, a two-year baseline, a 3.7 m boom distinct from the 4 m focal length, three flight telescopes with 24 shells per MMA, ≤25-arcsec resolution, the Cas A full-ApJ citation and the distinct Taverna/Krawczynski Science DOIs, and only the verified references — keeps the facts load-bearing and correct.
