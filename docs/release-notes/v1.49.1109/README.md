## Mission Overview

SORCE is the obs#12 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's solar-irradiance Earth-science mission, the small satellite that watched the Sun itself rather than the Earth, carrying four instruments that measured the total and the spectral solar irradiance, the whole energy output of the Sun and how it was distributed across the spectrum. ★ It was built and operated by the Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics (LASP) at the University of Colorado Boulder; the spacecraft bus was a LEOStar-2 platform built by Orbital Sciences Corporation. The four instruments each took a part of the Sun's light: the TIM (Total Irradiance Monitor) measured the total solar irradiance (TSI), the Sun's whole energy gathered into a single number; the SIM (Spectral Irradiance Monitor) measured the spectral solar irradiance (SSI) across the visible and the near-infrared; the SOLSTICE (Solar Stellar Irradiance Comparison Experiment, two units) measured the ultraviolet SSI and calibrated it against the stars; and the XPS (XUV Photometer System) measured the X-ray and extreme-ultraviolet. Together they spread the Sun's light across its spectrum and counted its whole energy. The mission launched 25 January 2003 on a Pegasus XL rocket, air-launched from Orbital's L-1011 "Stargazer" aircraft over the Atlantic off Cape Canaveral, Florida, and reached a low Earth orbit at roughly 645 km altitude, ~40° inclination. The story is the air-launch from the L-1011, the climb to low orbit, the four instruments turning to the Sun, the total irradiance gathered into a single number day after day, the Sun's light spread into its spectrum from the extreme-ultraviolet through the visible to the near-infrared, and the long watch over the Sun's energy that ran for some seventeen years before it ended. The science was central: the TIM measured the total solar irradiance — the whole energy output of the Sun reaching the top of the atmosphere — and the SIM, SOLSTICE, and XPS measured how that energy was distributed across the spectrum, so that together they read both the total and the spectral solar irradiance, the energy INPUT that sets the energy budget of the whole climate system. SORCE's firsts were about accuracy, spectral coverage, and continuity: the TIM advanced the absolute accuracy and stability of total-solar-irradiance measurement; the SIM provided the first continuous daily measurement of the broad-spectrum spectral solar irradiance; and together the instruments built a long, continuous, climate-quality record of total and spectral solar irradiance. ★ The accuracy result is load-bearing: the TIM advanced the absolute accuracy and stability of TSI measurement — its lower value, ~1361 W/m², helped resolve a long-standing discrepancy in the absolute TSI scale, where older instruments had read erroneously high (~1365–1366) because of internal scattered light (TIM launch accuracy ~350 ppm, stability ~10 ppm/yr); it is NOT "the first TSI measurement" (Nimbus-7 ERB in 1978 and ACRIM on SMM in 1980 preceded). ★ The spectral result is equally load-bearing: the SIM gave the first continuous daily measurement of the broad-spectrum SSI, and the SIM, SOLSTICE, and XPS together covered the spectrum from the X-ray and extreme-ultraviolet through the visible to the near-infrared. ★ The continuity result is load-bearing: the long 2003–2020 daily TSI + SSI record is a continuous, climate-quality record. ★★★ SORCE is RETIRED (2003–2020), so the catalog frames its whole story in the PAST (SORCE launched 25 January 2003, reached low orbit, measured the total and spectral solar irradiance day after day, and ended in 2020 — the mission completed 25 February 2020, the planned Phase-F passivation, the TIM record carried on by TSIS-1 on the ISS). ## Dedication

v1.292 SORCE is dedicated to the people at the Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics at the University of Colorado Boulder, at NASA, and at Orbital Sciences Corporation, and to the engineers and scientists who built and flew the satellite that turned its back on the Earth to watch the Sun. The teams who built four instruments — one to gather the Sun's whole energy into a single number, three to spread its light across the spectrum from the X-ray and extreme-ultraviolet through the visible to the near-infrared — and tuned them so well that the total irradiance was read to a few parts in a million, settling a long-standing discrepancy about the absolute value of the Sun's energy. The crews who carried it aloft beneath the L-1011 over the Atlantic off Cape Canaveral and released it to fly its own rocket to orbit. The people who kept the daily watch for some seventeen years, far past the five it was designed for, and the scientists who built the long, climate-quality record of the energy that drives Earth's weather and climate, handing it on to TSIS-1. The instruments are quiet now, and that record of the Sun's gathered light belongs to them.
