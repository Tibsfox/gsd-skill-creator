## Surprises

- **One small satellite turned its back on the Earth to watch the Sun.** Where every axis sibling pointed its instruments down at the Earth, SORCE turned the other way and watched the Sun itself — measuring the total and spectral solar irradiance, the whole energy output of the Sun and how that energy was distributed across the spectrum, because that solar energy is the input that drives Earth's whole climate system.
- **Four instruments, each taking a part of the Sun's light.** SORCE carried the TIM, the SIM, the SOLSTICE, and the XPS. The TIM gathered the Sun's whole energy into a single number, the solar constant; the SIM measured the spectral irradiance across the visible and near-infrared; the SOLSTICE measured the ultraviolet and calibrated it against the stars; and the XPS measured the X-ray and extreme-ultraviolet — so that together they read both the total and the spectral solar irradiance.
- **The TIM's lower number resolved a long-standing discrepancy.** The TIM measured the total solar irradiance at ~1361 W/m² — lower than older instruments that had read ~1365–1366, erroneously high because of internal scattered light. By advancing the absolute accuracy and stability of the measurement (launch accuracy ~350 ppm, stability ~10 ppm/yr), SORCE's TIM helped resolve a long-standing discrepancy in the absolute TSI scale.
- **A freshwater turtle that hauls out to bask on the Sun's energy.** The Painted Turtle (*Chrysemys picta*; the PNW Western Painted Turtle, *C. p. bellii*), a freshwater turtle of Pacific Northwest ponds, slow streams, and lake margins, hauls out onto logs and banks to bask in the sun for hours — a solar-powered ectotherm warming its body and metabolism on the sun's energy, native to the Columbia Basin, the Puget and Willamette lowlands, and British Columbia — a creature that takes the gathered light of the Sun into its body, the living version of the energy SORCE measured at the source (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A meadow buttercup whose mirror-petals focus the sunlight.** The Western Buttercup (*Ranunculus occidentalis*), a glossy yellow spring wildflower of Pacific Northwest meadows and prairies, has smooth, mirror-like petals that reflect and focus sunlight — a real optical effect, the petal's epidermis plus an air layer acting as a tiny mirror that concentrates the sun's light and warmth onto the flower's centre and reflects ultraviolet to pollinators — native to OR/WA/BC/CA meadows (ecological description only; distinct from the marsh-marigolds and other Ranunculus relatives) — bound with the throughline of the gathered light of the Sun, the thing that focuses it.
