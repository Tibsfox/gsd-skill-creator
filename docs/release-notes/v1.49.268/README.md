**Part B: Trumpeter Swan and the Anatidae Streak's Third Consecutive Record Extension.** The **Trumpeter Swan** (*Cygnus buccinator*) is the largest native bird in North America. Adult males weigh up to **35 pounds**, with wingspans reaching **8 feet**. The species name *buccinator* means "trumpeter" in Latin, referring to the bird's horn-like call. The vocalization is a **deep, resonant bugling** — a low-pitched, powerful, horn-like call that carries across wetlands. **The Conservation Triumph: From 70 Birds to 60,000.** By the 1930s, fewer than **70 Trumpeter Swans** were known in the lower 48 states. Unregulated hunting, market shooting for skins and meat, the feather trade (for hats and fans), and habitat loss to drainage and agricultural conversion had driven North America's largest native bird to the edge of extinction across the contiguous United States. A small population persisted in remote areas of **Yellowstone National Park** and adjacent regions, and larger but still diminished populations survived in remote Alaska and northern Canada. Beginning in the 1930s and accelerating after World War II, coordinated recovery efforts — habitat protection (the **Red Rock Lakes National Wildlife Refuge** in Montana was established in 1935 specifically for Trumpeter Swan conservation), breeding and translocation programs (**The Trumpeter Swan Society** founded in 1968 coordinates multi-agency recovery work), hunting regulation (Trumpeter Swan hunting prohibited across most of the species' range), and wintering-ground protection (Pacific Northwest wintering populations protected through state wildlife management) — together produced recovery to over **60,000 Trumpeter Swans across North America** today. The recovery is one of the great conservation success stories of the twentieth century, and the species' restoration from regional near-extirpation to continental abundance is the structural context for the bird's arrival at degree 128. **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife** documents significant Trumpeter Swan wintering populations on **Skagit Valley** agricultural lands and **Puget Sound** wetlands, making the species a reliable presence in Pacific Northwest winter birding.

## Part A: Bruce Pavitt — The Taste Architect and the Sub Pop Co-Founder Diptych's Close

- **Olympia origins at The Evergreen State College.** **Pavitt** attended **The Evergreen State College** in Olympia, Washington from 1979 to 1983. Evergreen's narrative-evaluation curriculum (no traditional grades, written faculty evaluations, self-directed academic program) attracted students interested in interdisciplinary work — music, visual art, political theory, environmental studies, and media studies all intersected in the same student bodies. The college's radical pedagogy produced not just Pavitt but **Calvin Johnson** (K Records founder) and **Slim Moon** (Kill Rock Stars founder), making Evergreen the institutional substrate that produced three Olympia-rooted labels. Pavitt's Subterranean Pop fanzine, which he started while at Evergreen in 1980, grew directly out of the college's interdisciplinary environment.
- **Subterranean Pop fanzine as originating platform.** **Subterranean Pop** (the fanzine, 1980) reviewed and championed independent music across the United States. The format was region-by-region surveys of local scenes, reviews of small-run cassettes and seven-inch singles, interviews with label operators and band members. The fanzine's structural insight was that independent music was not a single scene but a network of regional scenes, each with its own characteristic artists, venues, and institutional supports. Pavitt traveled or corresponded with regional contacts across the country, and the fanzine compiled the resulting surveys into a national-scale picture of the independent-music underground. The "Sub Pop" name as a concept originated in the fanzine title before being applied to a cassette compilation series and finally to the record label.

The compilation format allowed Pavitt to document and distribute the regional-scene surveys that the fanzine had been compiling in print. **Sub Pop 100** (1986, LP) and **Sub Pop 200** (1988, three-LP box set) extended the compilation format and became the direct infrastructure from which Sub Pop Records the label emerged. The Sub Pop 200 box set included early tracks by Soundgarden, Mudhoney, Nirvana, TAD, and Beat Happening (K Records's flagship band), making the compilation a cross-label Pacific Northwest survey that established Pavitt's curatorial ear at regional scale before the label itself launched.

Adult males weigh up to **35 pounds** (15.9 kg), with wingspans reaching **8 feet** (2.4 m). Females are somewhat smaller but still larger than the Tundra Swan. The species' size is structurally related to its acoustic profile — larger body mass requires larger internal anatomy, and the elongated trachea coiled within the sternum that produces the deep resonant bugling is part of the scaling relationship between body size and vocal production. The Trumpeter Swan's size also structures its ecological role: the bird requires substantial wetland areas (several hundred acres minimum for breeding territory), abundant aquatic vegetation, and sheltered waters for nesting and molt.
- **Deep resonant bugling: tracheal anatomy as acoustic mechanism.** The Trumpeter Swan's vocalization is a **deep, resonant bugling** — a low-pitched, powerful, horn-like call that carries across wetlands. The mechanism is anatomical: the trachea is elongated and coiled within the sternum (the keel bone of the breast), functioning as a resonating chamber that amplifies and lowers the pitch. The Latin species name *buccinator* means "trumpeter," explicitly referencing the horn-like character of the call. The fundamental frequency is in the low hundreds of Hz (roughly 200-500 Hz for the main call), with the resonating chamber producing harmonic overtones that give the call its brass-instrument character. The vocalization is loud enough to carry multiple kilometers across open wetland and low enough in frequency to travel through vegetation without being substantially absorbed. **E=6** correctly reflects the carrying power and the sustained quality of the bugling.

- **Conservation triumph from fewer than 70 birds in the 1930s to over 60,000 today.** By the 1930s, fewer than **70 Trumpeter Swans** were known in the lower 48 states. Unregulated hunting, market shooting for skins and meat, the feather trade (for hats and fans), and habitat loss to drainage and agricultural conversion had driven North America's largest native bird to the edge of extinction across the contiguous United States. A small population persisted in remote areas of Yellowstone National Park and adjacent regions. Beginning in the 1930s and accelerating after World War II, coordinated recovery efforts — habitat protection (Red Rock Lakes National Wildlife Refuge established 1935), breeding and translocation programs (The Trumpeter Swan Society founded 1968), hunting regulation (Trumpeter Swan hunting prohibited across most of the species' range), and wintering-ground protection — together produced recovery to over **60,000 Trumpeter Swans across North America** today. The recovery is one of the great conservation success stories of the twentieth century.

The **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife** maintains winter survey data and protects key wintering areas through land-use planning and private-landowner partnerships. The **Skagit Valley** wintering grounds are one of the most reliable locations in North America for observing Trumpeter Swans, and the wintering population's persistence is a direct consequence of the multi-decade recovery effort combined with Pacific Northwest wintering-ground protection. The species' return to Pacific Northwest abundance after its twentieth-century near-extinction is part of the ecological and cultural narrative that makes the Trumpeter Swan a structurally significant species for this engine.
