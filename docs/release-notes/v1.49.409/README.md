## Summary

**The Sonics** arrive at E=8 -- the Tacoma garage rock and proto-punk band formed in 1960, one of the founding acts of American punk rock, and one of the most important bands in the entire Pacific Northwest music canon. The Sonics -- Gerry Roslie (vocals, keyboards), Larry Parypa (guitar), Andy Parypa (bass), Rob Lind (saxophone), and Bob Bennett (drums) -- recorded a body of work in 1964-1966 that defined a proto-punk aesthetic before the word punk had been attached to music: loud, distorted, raw, vocally aggressive, structurally simple in ways that were innovative rather than limited, and driven by a commitment to maximum energy that had no precedent in American pop music at the time. Their recordings for Etiquette Records -- *Here Are the Sonics* (1965), *Boom* (1966), and the singles between and around those albums -- are among the most prescient documents in American rock history. "The Witch" (1964), "Psycho" (1965), "Strychnine" (1965), "Have Love Will Travel" (1965) -- these are not period pieces. They are recordings that have aged forward rather than backward: they sound, to contemporary ears, more like 1977 punk or 1984 hardcore than like 1965 rock 'n' roll, and that is a document of how far ahead of their context the Sonics were operating.

E=8 for The Sonics ties the engine maximum. It is the correct assignment. Gerry Roslie's vocal delivery on "Psycho" is as aggressive as anything recorded in the S36 catalog at any energy level. The distorted organ, the overloaded guitar, the drumming that hits hard on every beat -- The Sonics were playing at maximum intensity in 1965, and that intensity has not been diminished by the sixty years that have passed. The engine documents them at the proto-punk entry point -- here, at degree 268 -- rather than at a later, more canonically recognized position because this is where they belong chronologically and stylistically in the Seattle 360 sequence.

The **Pacific Wren** (*Troglodytes pacificus*) -- formerly considered part of the Winter Wren complex, split into its own species in 2010 -- is among the most voluminous singers in the Pacific Northwest avifauna. A small, round, dark-brown bird weighing approximately 10 grams, the Pacific Wren occupies the dense underbrush and root tangles of old-growth and mature second-growth conifer forest, and it produces a song of extraordinary complexity and duration: a rapid, cascading sequence of high-frequency notes lasting up to seven seconds, comprising as many as 36 distinct sound elements, delivered at a rate that human hearing cannot parse in real time. The Pacific Wren chorus -- multiple males singing simultaneously in a territory boundary dispute or dawn chorus event -- produces an acoustic event of remarkable density: multiple streams of complex, rapid, high-frequency song, overlapping and interweaving, generating a wall of biological sound that fills the forest floor acoustic space completely.

E=5 is where Pacific Wren chorus belongs in the engine's acoustic record, and it is where it arrives.
