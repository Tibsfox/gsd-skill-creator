## Summary

**Helms Alee** arrives at E=7 -- the Seattle sludge rock trio of Ben Verellen (guitar, vocals), Dana James (bass, vocals), and Hozoji Matheson-Margullis (drums, vocals), founded in 2007 and operating out of the same Pacific Northwest heavy music lineage that produced the Melvins, Karp, and Botch before them. Helms Alee plays sludge rock: slow, heavy, layered, with tempos that prioritize mass over velocity and tones built for maximum low-end density. Their catalog -- *Night Terror* (2008), *Weatherhead* (2011), *Sleepwalking Sailors* (2014), *Noctiluca* (2018), *Keep This Be the Way* (2022) -- documents a band refining the sludge form across fifteen years of consistent output on Southern Lord Records. E=7 for sludge rock is an honest assignment: the genre's energy is not expressed through speed but through weight, and Helms Alee carry that weight at the high end of what their format permits.

Sludge rock enters the engine for the first time at degree 253. The genre is a Pacific Northwest specialty -- the Melvins invented it in Aberdeen in the mid-1980s, and the region has sustained the heaviest concentration of sludge practitioners anywhere in North America. Sludge's defining characteristics are its tempo (slow to very slow), its tuning (dropped, often to B or A), its production (thick, dense, wet), and its structural patience (songs that develop over eight to twelve minutes without apology). Helms Alee brings all of these into the engine at E=7 -- a score that acknowledges both the sustained intensity of the genre and the three-instrument density of a trio in which every member sings.

On the soundscape side, **Puget Sound Tidal Current** arrives at E=2 -- the subsonic pulse produced by the movement of water through the Sound's main basin, the passages between the islands, and the narrows that constrict flow at points like Tacoma Narrows, Rich Passage, and Colvos Passage. The tidal current of Puget Sound is not a sound most humans hear directly: its lowest-frequency components propagate below the threshold of human hearing, and its acoustic signature is most clearly detected by underwater hydrophones. What is audible -- to someone standing at a narrows point, watching the water -- is a broad low frequency hum, the sound of an enormous mass of water in organized movement. E=2 reflects the subtlety of this acoustic event: it is present, it is consistent, it is real, but it operates at the margins of human perception.

The pairing is appropriate. Helms Alee's sludge rock operates in frequencies that similarly challenge the limits of what a listener can track -- the low end of a sludge record lives in the 40-60Hz range, at the border between music and physical sensation. The tidal current of Puget Sound occupies the same physical register. Both are real, both are powerful, and both require a listener willing to pay attention to what is happening at the lower end.
