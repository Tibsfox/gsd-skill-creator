# v1.49.359 -- Degree 218: The Blood Brothers + Grizzly Bear jaw-pop

**Release Date:** 2026-04-02
**Type:** Seattle 360 / Sound of Puget Sound -- Degree 218 of 360

## Summary

**The Blood Brothers** arrives at E=8 -- the **HIGHEST S36 ENERGY EVER RECORDED**, and the first E=8 in the Seattle 360 engine's history. Jordan Blilie and Johnny Whitney's dual-vocal attack is one of the most extreme sounds ever produced in Seattle: two singers screaming simultaneously in different registers, backed by Cody Votolato's slashing guitar, Morgan Henderson's punishing bass, and Mark Gajadhar's relentless drums. On V2 Records and later Artist in Residence, albums like *...Burn, Piano Island, Burn* (2003) and *Crimes* (2004) are records that refuse to let the listener breathe -- every song is a sustained panic attack rendered in post-hardcore and art-punk, with lyrics that read like fever-dream poetry. The Blood Brothers emerged from **Shoreline**, bringing a new geographic node into the engine for the first time. At E=8, they are the only artist in 218 degrees to reach this energy level. On the species side, the **Grizzly Bear jaw-pop** registers at E=7 -- forming a **comparison pair** with the Grizzly Bear roar at degree 217. The jaw-pop is a close-range threat display: a rapid, percussive snapping of the jaws that produces a sharp cracking sound, often accompanied by huffing and ground-slapping. Where the roar is broadcast aggression (E=8, landscape-scale), the jaw-pop is intimate aggression (E=7, within-50-meters). Together, degrees 217-218 produce a combined S36+SPS energy of **E=15**, obliterating the previous combined record of E=14 set just one degree earlier.

- GRIZZLY BEAR COMPARISON PAIR (217-218) -- the roar (E=8) and jaw-pop (E=7) document the Grizzly Bear's full threat-display repertoire; the roar is long-range broadcast aggression; the jaw-pop is close-range, immediate-danger signaling; the energy drop from E=8 to E=7 is counterintuitive -- the jaw-pop is the more dangerous sound, because if you can hear it, you are too close

- Dual-vocal architecture -- The Blood Brothers' two-singer format (Blilie's deeper rasp, Whitney's higher shriek) creates a stereo field of human distress that has no peer in the Seattle canon; the two voices never harmonize in a traditional sense -- they collide, overlap, and interrupt each other, producing an acoustic chaos that mirrors the lyrics' surrealist imagery
