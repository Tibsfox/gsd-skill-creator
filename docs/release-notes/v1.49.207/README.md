## Lessons Learned

- **The three-degree Death Cab study reveals the chemistry of the collective.** Studying a band's members separately demonstrates that the collective sound is not simply the sum of its members. Gibbard's bare wire (E=2) and Walla's clean room (E=3) are different things that became Death Cab's settling sound (E=2) when combined. The whole exceeds the sum, and the parts examined separately reveal what the whole partially conceals. The engine gains analytical depth by documenting multiple perspectives on the same musical organism, and future bands with multiple notable members should be evaluated for whether a multi-perspective study is warranted.
- **The producer-as-artist concept fills a structural gap.** The people who shaped the Pacific Northwest sound from behind the console were present in the engine's records (at degrees 58 and 59) but absent from its degrees. Making the invisible visible: the cross-degree production credits reveal a web of connections beneath the visible artist relationships. The producer network is the engine's invisible infrastructure -- the people behind the records that other artists made, who shape what the engine names without themselves being named until they receive their own degrees.
- **The clean room as the first production-derived acoustic element extends the engine's vocabulary.** Every previous element described what the music sounds like. The clean room describes what the space around the music sounds like. Production is architecture, not decoration. The acoustic-element vocabulary now includes both performance-derived elements (the bare wire, the country thunder, the dusk) and production-derived elements (the clean room) -- and future producer-primary degrees should be evaluated against this distinction.
- **The Barsuk diaspora provides a template for understanding indie-ecosystem trajectories.** Three trajectories from one label (Death Cab to Atlantic, Gibbard to self-release, Walla to self-release) captures how artists navigate the indie ecosystem differently from the same starting point. Each trajectory is valid, each is different, and the diaspora pattern can be applied to future label clusters where multiple artists started together and ended in different institutional positions.
- **The Mustelidae quintet sets the definitive maximum for consecutive family entries.** Five works because each member introduces genuine novelty (different habitat, different size, different conservation status, different vocal character). But 7.4% of degrees on one family is the ceiling. Future same-family entries must be distributed across later degrees, not consecutive. The quintet is the upper bound that should not be exceeded; the prior quartet "non-negotiable" claim was premature, and the next quintet (if any) should be flagged as a structural event in advance, not retroactively.
- **Edge habitat as a concept extends the engine's ecological vocabulary.** From single-ecosystem designations (river, forest, canopy) to dual-ecosystem designations (the boundary between meadow AND forest). The ecotone is a new category that future species should be evaluated against. Edge species often have different conservation trajectories than interior species (the conservation paradox: edge habitat increases with fragmentation), and that distinction is now available for future taxonomic-state analysis.
- **The ermine transformation introduces the engine's first climate-change vulnerability indicator.** As PNW winters become milder and shorter, the white coat may become a liability (white on brown ground = visible). The mismatch between photoperiod trigger and actual snow cover is a documented conservation concern. The climate-vulnerability category should be tracked for future species, and species whose phenology depends on photoperiod should be flagged as potentially vulnerable to climate-driven mismatches.
- **The conservation paradox within the quintet is structurally instructive.** The same human activities that eliminated the Fisher and threatened the Marten CREATED more habitat for the Weasel. Edge habitat increases with forest fragmentation. Five members of one family, five conservation stories, from reintroduced (Fisher) to thriving (Weasel). The conservation-status distribution across same-family entries is itself a structural attribute and should be tracked.
- **Surplus killing appears twice in the quintet from different drivers.** Mink (64) kills from opportunity in prey-dense environments; Weasel (67) kills from metabolic necessity at 40-60% body weight daily. Same behavior, different underlying pressures. The distinction between opportunity-driven and necessity-driven surplus killing is analytically useful and should be tracked for future predator entries.
- **The *Mustela* anchor closes the quintet's taxonomic story at the foundational genus.** Five entries, five genera (*Lontra*, *Neogale*, *Martes*, *Pekania*, *Mustela*), ending at the root genus from which the entire family Mustelidae is named. The progression ends at the beginning -- the root genus reached at the end. The weasel IS the type animal. Future family-cluster sequences should be evaluated for whether they end at the family's foundational genus, and clusters that do should be flagged as structural events.
