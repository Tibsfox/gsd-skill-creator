- ***Sterna hirundo* Taxonomy and the Linnaeus 1758 Binomial Nomenclature.** The Common Tern is scientifically named *Sterna hirundo* — with *Sterna* as the genus name for most common terns (rooted in Old English *stearn* or Scandinavian/Germanic equivalents meaning "tern") and *hirundo* as the species name meaning "swallow" (from the Latin *hirundo* for the swallow bird family). The species was described by **Carl Linnaeus** in 1758 in *Systema Naturae*, Edition X, and the binomial nomenclature "swallow tern" captures the tern's flight character: the Common Tern flies like a swallow (buoyant, agile, with rapid wingbeats and hovering capacity) rather than like a gull (flat-winged soaring, mainly dynamic flight). The Common Tern is one of the most widely distributed terns in the world with breeding populations across North America, Europe, Asia, and in smaller populations into the Southern Hemisphere, making it one of the taxonomically best-documented Laridae species.
- **Pacific Northwest Distribution: Less Common than Atlantic Coast, Specific Colony Sites at East Sand Island and Dungeness Spit.** The Common Tern is less common in the Pacific Northwest than on the Atlantic coast — where the species breeds extensively along the entire coastline from Maine to Georgia — but Pacific Northwest populations exist at specific colony sites. Washington State Common Tern colonies include **East Sand Island** at the mouth of the **Columbia River** (one of the most significant managed Common Tern colony sites in the Pacific Northwest), **Dungeness Spit** in Clallam County (the five-mile-long sand spit that is one of the longest natural sand spits in the United States and that supports mixed colonial waterbird nesting), and occasional interior lake-island sites. The Common Tern is a migrant and breeding bird in the Pacific Northwest rather than a year-round resident, with populations arriving in May for the breeding season and departing for South American wintering grounds by September. The species' Pacific Northwest population is smaller than the Atlantic population, but the colonial nesting at East Sand Island produces one of the PNW's most significant tern colonies and supports first-class documentation.
- **The Keerr Call at 1,000-6,000 Hz: Sharp, High-Pitched, Emphatic Colony Vocalization.** The Common Tern's primary alarm and contact call at the colony is a sharp, emphatic "keerr" or "kee-arr" vocalization produced at a frequency range of approximately 1,000-6,000 Hz with the fundamental frequency concentrated at approximately 2,000-4,000 Hz. The keerr call is substantially higher-pitched than any of the three preceding Laridae entries' primary calls: the Glaucous-winged Gull's mew at 800-1,500 Hz fundamental, the Herring Gull's alarm at 800-4,000 Hz, and the Western Gull's colony calls at similar gull-register frequencies. The keerr is structurally distinct from the gull calls not only in pitch but in duration and repetition pattern: the call is a short, emphatic, two-syllable vocalization that is produced at high rates in colony contexts and that carries a penetrating quality at the higher-frequency range. The keerr call is the Sterninae sub-family's acoustic signature and the arc-closing register that establishes the tern as acoustically distinct from the gulls.
- **Sandy Spit Colonial Nesting: Flat Exposed Substrates, Shallow Scrape Ground Nesting.** Common Terns nest colonially on flat, exposed, low-profile substrates — sandy spits at river mouths and coastal inlets, barrier beaches, gravel bars, and lake islands. The nesting habitat is exposed and open, with no cliff face or elevated rocky island to provide visual advantage or acoustic reflectance, and the colony's defense is behavioral (dive-bombing alarm, vocal mobbing) rather than topographic. The nest itself is a shallow scrape in sand or gravel with minimal construction, and the exposure means the colony is vulnerable to flooding (storm surge, river floods), predation, and human disturbance. Documented Pacific Northwest colony sites include East Sand Island (managed multi-species colony with Common, Caspian, and Forster's Terns), Dungeness Spit, and occasional inland sites. Colony size ranges from a few dozen pairs to several hundred pairs at established sites, with East Sand Island supporting one of the larger mixed-tern colonies in the region.
- **Dive-Bombing Alarm: Kinetic-Plus-Acoustic Defense Unique in the Laridae Arc.** The Common Tern's most dramatic colony alarm behavior is dive-bombing — a bird that detects a threat (human intruder, potential predator) will fly at the intruder, calling sharply with the keerr call, and strike or narrowly miss the intruder's head with its bill. At an active colony, multiple birds dive-bomb simultaneously from different angles, creating a kinetic-plus-acoustic alarm response that is absent in the three preceding Laridae arc entries. The gull entries document vocal alarm and colony-density acoustic responses (Glaucous-winged Gull mew and alarm, Herring Gull alarm, Western Gull colony chorus) but do not document physical dive-bombing at the colony level. The island supports mixed-species colonial nesting with **Common Tern** (*Sterna hirundo*), **Caspian Tern** (*Hydroprogne caspia*), and **Forster's Tern** (*Sterna forsteri*) plus other colonial waterbirds including cormorants and pelicans. The Caspian Tern population at East Sand Island has been the subject of significant management efforts related to juvenile Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*) smolt predation — the Caspian Terns historically preyed on outmigrating Chinook smolts in the Columbia River estuary at levels sufficient to affect listed Chinook populations, and management efforts have worked to reduce the Caspian Tern population at East Sand Island while maintaining the Common Tern and Forster's Tern populations.
