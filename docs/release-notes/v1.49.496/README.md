**Solger** arrive at E=10 -- the Seattle hardcore band formed in 1981 by Wade Tonkin (vocals), Jeff Barton (guitar), Sam Bent (bass), and Nick Dyer (drums), releasing the EP *Solger* (1982, Engram Records) as the complete document of one of the Pacific Northwest's earliest and most fully committed hardcore bands -- a band that operated at the genre's first-generation intensity, before the crossover developments that would shape The Accused (degree 351) and before the hardcore-adjacent developments that would produce the Seattle scene's second and third waves. Solger's hardcore is not genre-adjacent or genre-synthesizing: it is hardcore in its pure first-generation form, the template that the Pacific Northwest's 1980s scene developed in parallel with Black Flag in Los Angeles, Dead Kennedys in San Francisco, Minor Threat in Washington D.C., and Bad Brains in D.C. -- the simultaneous national development of an approach to punk rock that prioritized velocity, political directness, and physical commitment over the melodic and commercial considerations that the mainstream rock tradition prioritized.

Solger's recordings are sparse in the way that first-generation hardcore recordings are necessarily sparse: recorded with the equipment available to a Seattle band in 1982, produced with the budget available to a band releasing on a local label, documented in the condition that the Pacific Northwest underground's physical infrastructure of the early 1980s made possible. The sonic result is a document whose roughness is the document -- the undifferentiated guitar distortion, the drums in the acoustic environment of the recording space rather than the processed isolation of studio production, Tonkin's vocal approach at the maximum volume and velocity his instrument supports. Solger's EP is seven minutes of material that establishes the Pacific Northwest hardcore position at its foundational moment: not the genre's ceiling in terms of compositional sophistication (that ceiling belongs to the bands that came later) but the genre's founding document in the region, the recording that established that Seattle could and would participate in the national hardcore conversation at the same intensity as the tradition's founding cities.

**Stonefly emergence full river** arrives at E=10 on the SPS track -- the Pacific Northwest's most dramatic invertebrate acoustic event, and one of the most visually and acoustically overwhelming biological phenomena the region's river ecology produces: a mass stonefly (*Plecoptera*) emergence event on a major Cascade or Olympic river, when millions of adult stoneflies leave the water simultaneously after their aquatic nymph stage and fill the river valley in a density that makes flight through the emergence cloud impossible and that produces a specific acoustic environment as the combined wingbeats of millions of insects constitute a low-frequency broadband sound mass across the river valley. The Pacific Northwest's salmonfly (*Pteronarcys californica*) and other large stonefly species produce the most dramatic emergence events of any aquatic insect in the region: individual salmonflies reach 50mm in body length and wingspans of 80mm, producing wingbeat sounds audible individually at close range; in mass emergence, the combined sound of millions of individuals constitutes an acoustic environment.

The full-river emergence adds the river's own acoustic contribution: the high-water-flow sound of a Cascade river in late spring (when most major stonefly emergences occur, triggered by water temperature reaching the species-specific threshold) as the acoustic backdrop against which the emergence cloud rises; the birds responding to the emergence (fish-eating birds, aerial foragers, riparian-specialist songbirds all responding to the protein windfall of a mass emergence) adding the avian alarm and foraging layer; and the fish feeding at the surface (trout and salmon rising in numbers during mass emergence, their surface strikes audible at the river's margins) adding the water-strike acoustic element. The full-river emergence is the river ecosystem's maximum biological output event -- the moment when the aquatic insect biomass that has been accumulating through the winter and spring converts from invisible aquatic nymph to visible, audible aerial adult, transforming the river valley's acoustic environment in a matter of hours.
