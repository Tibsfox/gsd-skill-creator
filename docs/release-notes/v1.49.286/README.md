**THEESatisfaction and the Genre-Bending Duo.** **THEESatisfaction** is the duo of **Stasia Irons** (MC, performing solo as **SassyBlack**) and **Catherine Harris-White** (singer, known as **Cat**), active from approximately 2008 to 2018 with the core documented period running 2008-2016 when the duo released their two Sub Pop albums. The duo operated at the intersection of multiple genres simultaneously — hip-hop, R&B, neo-soul, funk, psychedelia, electronic, and Afrofuturist art music — and this multi-genre fluency distinguishes them from any previous S36 engine entry. Where **Common Market** (degree 145) is conscious duo rap with dense multisyllabic lyricism and layered boom-bap production, where **Digable Planets** (degree 144 via Ishmael Butler) brings jazz-rap polish, and where **Shabazz Palaces** (degree 103 through the same Ishmael Butler) pushes experimental abstract hip-hop, THEESatisfaction refuses to settle into any single genre register. A single track might begin as hip-hop, dissolve into ambient texture, and resolve as neo-soul. The album-level structure of both *awE naturalE* and *EarthEE* is explicitly one of dissolution — individual tracks blur into one another and genre labels become useless descriptors. This structural genre-defiance is not evasion but a positive aesthetic choice that connects to the Afrofuturist tradition of reimagining Black musical futures outside inherited categorical constraints.

**Part A: THEESatisfaction — The Records, Sub Pop, and Afrofuturist Black Femme Art.** The original entry at degree 146 is organized around three documentary anchors: the **two Sub Pop records** (*awE naturalE* 2012 and *EarthEE* 2015), the **Sub Pop label expansion** (second hip-hop act after Shabazz Palaces), and the **Afrofuturist Black femme aesthetic-political practice**. *awE naturalE* (2012) is the Sub Pop debut, and its deliberate capitalization in the title reflects THEESatisfaction's playful relationship with language and naming (the band name itself features unconventional capitalization). The album is a collage of hip-hop beats, R&B vocals, psychedelic textures, and experimental production that moves fluidly between genres rather than settling into any one. Critical reception from Pitchfork, The Stranger, KEXP, and other outlets recognized the album as something genuinely new — not hip-hop, not R&B, not electronic, but all of these simultaneously. Ishmael Butler's appearance on the album cemented the creative connection between the two Sub Pop acts. *EarthEE* (2015) is the sophomore, more confident, more expansive, and more explicitly Afrofuturist. The album leaned into cosmic imagery, technological metaphor, and reimagined Black futures — connecting THEESatisfaction to the Afrofuturist tradition that extends from Sun Ra's Arkestra through Parliament-Funkadelic's cosmic mythology through Janelle Monae's ArchAndroid narratives and through Shabazz Palaces' *Black Up* (2011) and *Lese Majesty* (2014). *EarthEE* pushed even further from genre boundaries, with tracks blending hip-hop, house, ambient, soul, and noise in ways that make genre labels unusable. **Sub Pop Records**, founded in 1988 by **Bruce Pavitt** and **Jonathan Poneman** as the quintessential grunge / indie rock label (Nirvana, Mudhoney, Soundgarden's early work), began signing hip-hop and R&B artists in the 2010s with Ishmael Butler's Shabazz Palaces as the first bridge. **The Duo's Chemistry and Black Femme Identity.** The duo's power derived from the contrast and complementarity between Stasia's rapping and Cat's singing. **Stasia Irons** brought the hip-hop dimension — rhythmic vocal delivery, lyrical density, and a rapper's sense of timing and flow. **Catherine Harris-White** brought the R&B and soul dimension — melodic vocals, harmonies, and a singer's sensitivity to texture and emotion. In any given track, the two would weave in and out of each other, sometimes overlapping, sometimes alternating, sometimes merging into a single polyphonic voice. The contrast was also tonal: Stasia's delivery was more angular, rhythmic, and assertive; Cat's was more curved, melodic, and enveloping. This created structural tension and resolution within individual songs that neither half could achieve alone. Both members were Black women, and their art was explicitly grounded in Black femme experience — joy, struggle, sexuality, community, creativity, resistance — in ways that made THEESatisfaction the first engine entry to document a Black femme artistic partnership as primary subject. The aesthetic-political practice of refusing to compartmentalize Blackness, femmeness, and artistic ambition into separate categories parallels the musical practice of refusing to compartmentalize hip-hop, R&B, and experimental electronic into separate genres.

**Part B: The Common Nighthawk — The Aerial Specialist and the Booming Dive.** The **Common Nighthawk** (*Chordeiles minor*) enters the engine at degree 146 as the **second Caprimulgidae** following the Common Poorwill's degree-145 entry, and together the two species establish the nightjar family's full ecological range. Where the **Common Poorwill** is a ground-sitter whose E=3 hypnotic-repetition "poor-will" call represents the family's maximum-energy-conservation pole (torpor, sit-and-wait hunting, rocky-ground nesting, hibernation through winter), the **Common Nighthawk** is an aerial specialist whose E=5 dual-mode signature represents the family's maximum-energy-expenditure pole (continuous flight, sustained aerial display, migrating to South America rather than hibernating). The two species are adjacent-degree entries, but the ecological contrast is extreme: the Poorwill conserves energy by shutting down, while the Nighthawk expends energy by never stopping. The Nighthawk hunts by **continuous aerial hawking** — flying back and forth through insect swarms with its enormous gape opened wide to scoop up moths, beetles, and flying ants. The bird eats on the wing, drinks on the wing by skimming water surfaces (like a swallow), and only lands to nest or roost during the day. The most active period is twilight — dusk and dawn — rather than full darkness, making the species technically **crepuscular** rather than strictly nocturnal despite its "nighthawk" name.

**The Booming Dive Mechanics.** The Common Nighthawk's most distinctive behavior is the **booming dive**, a masterpiece of aerodynamic sound production that stands as one of the most dramatic aerial displays of any North American bird. The male Nighthawk climbs to altitude (typically 20-50 meters above ground) using strong, measured wingbeats. At the top of the climb, the bird briefly stalls or hovers, orienting itself for the dive. This momentary pause creates dramatic tension for any observer. The bird then tips into a steep dive (often nearly vertical), folding its wings partially and accelerating under gravity. The dive can reach speeds estimated at **60-100 km/h**. At the bottom of the dive (typically 5-10 meters above ground), the bird abruptly spreads its wings and pulls out. The rush of air through the outer primary feathers — which are stiff and specially shaped for this purpose — creates a deep, resonant **"VROOM"** or **"BOOM"**. The sound is produced by air vibrating the primary feathers, not by the voice. The frequency is approximately **100-200 Hz** — very low, almost subsonic — which is why it carries so far and is felt in the chest as much as heard. The boom can be audible at **500+ meters** in still conditions. The physics is similar in principle to how a reed instrument produces sound, but using feathers instead of a reed and airflow from the dive instead of blown air. This **first non-vocal mechanical sound** on the species axis opens the aerodynamic-vibration register, and future species with similar mechanical acoustic signatures (wing-snapping, rattling, whirring) can now be legibly documented within the register that degree 146 establishes.

**The "Peent!" Call and Multi-Modal Acoustic Signature.** Alongside the booming dive, the Common Nighthawk produces a distinctive nasal vocal **"peent!"** call, given in flight as the bird hawks insects over open territory. The call is buzzy, electric, and carries clearly at several hundred meters over open ground. It has a slightly ascending tonal quality and a characteristic nasal timbre that distinguishes it from any other aerial insectivore in the Pacific Northwest. The peent is the Nighthawk's primary vocal signature, and it is produced continuously during active hunting bouts. The combination of the nasal vocal peent with the mechanical aerodynamic boom makes the Common Nighthawk the engine's **first multi-modal acoustic signature** on the species axis. Every prior species entry has been documented through a single acoustic mode — vocal calls, songs, hoots, or tooting. The Nighthawk introduces the precedent that a species can combine vocal and non-vocal sound production as part of a single integrated acoustic identity. On a summer evening, a displaying male produces a rhythmic soundscape of "peent... peent... peent..." punctuated by periodic **BOOMs** that dominate the acoustic landscape of the territory. The E=5 rating calibrates the multi-modal aerial-display register: loud vocal amplitude (peent clearly audible at hundreds of meters) plus the dramatic mechanical BOOM (audible at 500+ meters as a low-frequency vibration felt in the chest) plus the visual spectacle of the steep dive plus the sustained performance (dozens of dives in a single twilight session) plus the territorial and courtship persistence throughout the breeding season.

## Part B: Common Nighthawk — The Booming Dive, Second Caprimulgidae, and the First Multi-Modal Acoustic Signature

- **The Common Nighthawk enters as the second Caprimulgidae establishing the family-contrast-pair pattern.** The **Common Nighthawk** (*Chordeiles minor*) enters the engine at degree 146 as the **second Caprimulgidae** following the **Common Poorwill** (*Phalaenoptilus nuttallii*) at degree 145, and the two species together establish the nightjar family's full ecological range in adjacent degrees. - **The booming dive produces the first non-vocal mechanical sound on the species axis.** The Common Nighthawk's most distinctive behavior is the **booming dive**, a masterpiece of aerodynamic sound production that stands as one of the most dramatic aerial displays of any North American bird. At the bottom of a steep dive from 20-50 meters altitude (ending at 5-10 meters above ground), the male Nighthawk abruptly spreads its wings and pulls out, and the rush of air through the outer primary feathers — which are stiff and specially shaped for this purpose — creates a deep, resonant **"VROOM"** or **"BOOM"** at approximately 100-200 Hz. The sound is produced by air vibrating the primary feathers, not by the voice. This is the **first non-vocal mechanical sound** documented on the species axis, and its introduction at degree 146 opens the **aerodynamic-vibration register** as a legitimate first-class acoustic register alongside the established vocal register. Future species with similar mechanical acoustic signatures (Ruffed Grouse drumming, Mourning Dove wing whistles, Broad-tailed Hummingbird wing trills, manakin wing-snaps if documented) can now be legibly cataloged within the register that degree 146 establishes, and the precedent extends the species axis's acoustic vocabulary beyond the vocal-only framework that had constrained the prior 145 entries.
- **The multi-modal acoustic signature combines vocal peent with aerodynamic BOOM.** Alongside the booming dive, the Common Nighthawk produces a distinctive nasal vocal **"peent!"** call given in flight as the bird hawks insects over open territory. The call is buzzy, electric, and carries clearly at several hundred meters with a slightly ascending tonal quality and characteristic nasal timbre. The combination of the **nasal vocal peent** with the **mechanical aerodynamic BOOM** makes the Common Nighthawk the engine's **first multi-modal acoustic signature** on the species axis. Every prior species entry has been documented through a single acoustic mode — vocal calls, songs, hoots, or tooting. The Nighthawk introduces the precedent that a species can combine vocal and non-vocal sound production as part of a single integrated acoustic identity. On a summer evening, a displaying male produces a rhythmic soundscape of "peent... peent... peent..." punctuated by periodic **BOOMs** that dominate the acoustic landscape. The E=5 rating reflects the combined impact of the dual-mode signature — loud vocal peent (audible at hundreds of meters), dramatic mechanical BOOM (audible at 500+ meters as low-frequency vibration felt in the chest), visual spectacle of the steep dive, and sustained performance across dozens of dives per twilight session.
- **The aerial-specialist strategy contrasts sharply with the Common Poorwill's ground-sitter strategy.** Where the **Common Poorwill** conserves energy through torpor, sit-and-wait hunting, ground-sitting, and rocky-ground nesting with hibernation through winter, the **Common Nighthawk** expends energy through **continuous aerial hawking**, sustained aerial display, and **migration to South America** when insects disappear. The contrast is extreme: the Poorwill shuts down, the Nighthawk never stops. The Nighthawk hunts by continuous aerial hawking — flying back and forth through insect swarms with its enormous gape opened wide to scoop up moths, beetles, and flying ants. The bird **eats on the wing**, **drinks on the wing** by skimming water surfaces like a swallow, and only lands to nest or roost during the day. The most active period is twilight (dusk and dawn) rather than full darkness, making the species technically **crepuscular** rather than strictly nocturnal despite the "nighthawk" name. The adjacent-degree contrast pair (Poorwill at 145, Nighthawk at 146) demonstrates the family's ecological range in two consecutive entries rather than spreading the contrast across many degrees.
- **Urban rooftop nesting introduces the anthropogenic-habitat register.** One of the Common Nighthawk's most distinctive adaptations is **urban rooftop nesting** on flat gravel-covered commercial rooftops. The gravel mimics the natural gravel bars and rocky ground where the species originally nested, and the eggs (laid directly on the gravel with no nest structure) are camouflaged against the substrate. This adaptation initially expanded the species' range into cities and made urban environments viable breeding habitat, but it has also proved vulnerable as gravel rooftops are replaced by rubberized membrane roofs that do not provide nesting substrate. The urban-rooftop-nesting register introduces the **anthropogenic-habitat register** to the species axis, opening documentation of species that adopt human-built environments as primary habitat. Future species with similar anthropogenic-habitat adaptations (Chimney Swifts nesting in chimneys, Peregrine Falcons nesting on skyscrapers, Black-crowned Night-Herons nesting on urban trees) can now be legibly cataloged within the register that degree 146 establishes.

The folk name captures the bird's dual nature — powerful sound (bull) plus agile flight (bat) — in a way that the formal common name "Common Nighthawk" obscures. - **The first vertical-airspace habitat opens the species axis's spatial register.** The Common Nighthawk's habitat is the **open sky over diverse substrates** — urban rooftops, gravel bars, open fields, forest clearings — which makes the species the first engine entry whose primary habitat is **vertical airspace** rather than horizontal terrestrial surface. Unlike the prior 145 species entries whose habitats are forests, shrublands, wetlands, or coastal zones (all conceived as terrestrial or intertidal surfaces), the Nighthawk's primary habitat is the open column of air where aerial insects fly. The species relates to terrestrial surfaces only for nesting (gravel substrate) and roosting (lengthwise along branches). This vertical-airspace habitat register is a first on the species axis and opens documentation of aerial-specialist species whose primary life activities happen above rather than on the ground.
- **E=5 multi-modal aerial-display energy calibrates the spectacular register.** The Common Nighthawk registers at **E=5**, the highest energy rating among the first two Caprimulgidae entries and a significant step up from the Common Poorwill's E=3 hypnotic-repetition rating at degree 145. The E=5 rating is defensible through the **multi-modal acoustic output** (vocal peent plus mechanical BOOM), the **spectacular aerial display** (one of the most dramatic of any North American bird), the **substantial amplitude** (peent at several hundred meters, BOOM at 500+ meters as low-frequency vibration), the **sustained performance** (dozens of dives per twilight session), and the **landscape presence** (the displaying Nighthawk dominates the acoustic landscape of its territory during peak breeding). The E=5 placement matches the aerial-display-energy tier established by the Short-eared Owl at degree 140 (bark + wing-clap, E=5) and contrasts with both the Poorwill's E=3 hypnotic-repetition (moderate amplitude, repetitive, no visual spectacle) and the Flammulated Owl's E=2 near-inaudibility (degree 144). The match with THEESatisfaction's E=5 on the artist axis produces the rare adjacent-energy pairing at degree 146.
- **The population decline introduces conservation-concern status to the species axis.** The Common Nighthawk's population has **declined significantly across North America**, documented through the **Breeding Bird Survey** data showing consistent downward trends since the 1960s and through the **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW)** Species of Greatest Conservation Need listing. The decline has multiple drivers: urban rooftop nesting, while an adaptation that initially expanded the species' range, has proved vulnerable as gravel rooftops are replaced by rubberized membrane roofs that do not provide nesting substrate. Agricultural pesticide use has reduced the flying-insect prey base. Light pollution may disrupt aerial insect-hunting patterns. The population-decline documentation introduces the **conservation-concern status register** to the species axis documentation, and future species entries with documented population declines will be noted within this register. The Common Nighthawk's SGCN status documents it as one of the first engine-axis species with explicit conservation-concern framing.
- **Robert M. Brigham provides the modern primary-source scientific authority for Caprimulgidae.** **Robert M. Brigham**, the avian-ecology researcher whose studies of Caprimulgidae span decades, provides the primary-source scientific documentation anchoring both degree 145 and degree 146 species coverage. His studies — **Brigham et al. 2011** on Nighthawk populations and behavior, **Brigham 1992** on Poorwill torpor, **Csada & Brigham 1992** on nocturnal insect hunting — represent sustained focus on the nightjar family across decades and make him the singular modern authority on Pacific Northwest Caprimulgidae ecology. His work bridges the **Jaeger**-discovery era of 1946-1949 to the contemporary population-trend documentation of the 2010s, and his documentation of both species' life histories, vocalizations, and population trends provides the foundation on which the engine's Caprimulgidae documentation rests. **Edward S. ## Lessons Learned

1. **Family introductions with contrasting representative species benefit from adjacent-degree pairing.** The Caprimulgidae family's introduction at degrees 145-146 through the Common Poorwill (ground-sitter, E=3) and Common Nighthawk (aerial specialist, E=5) adjacent-degree pair establishes the family's full ecological range in two consecutive entries rather than spreading the contrast across many degrees. Future family introductions should adopt this pattern when the family has representative extremes that benefit from simultaneous documentation, and the adjacent-degree pairing should be considered the default rather than the slower representative-single-entry pattern used for some earlier families.
2. **Non-vocal mechanical sounds deserve first-class acoustic register status rather than being treated as incidental.** The Common Nighthawk's aerodynamic BOOM, produced by air vibrating spread primary feathers during a steep dive, is the first non-vocal mechanical sound on the species axis, and documenting it as opening the **aerodynamic-vibration register** (rather than as an incidental acoustic detail of the booming dive behavior) preserves the analytical weight of the introduction.
