**"Spirit in the Sky" — The Four-Bar Fuzz-Guitar Introduction and the 1970 Chart Run.** "Spirit in the Sky" was recorded in 1969 and released on **Reprise Records** in late 1969, entering the **Billboard Hot 100** in early 1970. The recording reached **#3** on the chart during its 1970 run and sold approximately two million copies in its initial American release cycle. The recording's structural signature is the **four-bar fuzz-guitar introduction** — a D-chord-based figure with a chromatic walk-up (D to D#/Eb to E in the bass or implied in the guitar movement) repeated four bars, produced using a **Maestro Fuzz-Tone** or similar early fuzz-pedal device that introduced a dense, low-midrange-heavy, overdriven timbre to the electric-guitar signal. The fuzz-pedal technology on the recording was not new: the **Maestro Fuzz-Tone** had been introduced in 1962 as the first commercially available fuzz pedal; the **Fuzz Face** (1966, associated with **Jimi Hendrix**) and the **Tone Bender** (1965, associated with **Jimmy Page** and **Jeff Beck**) were the other primary fuzz units of the 1960s era; and the fuzz effect had been in rock use for several years before the Greenbaum recording. The specific fuzz-tone application on "Spirit in the Sky" was commercially effective — dense enough to carry the guitar forward in the mix, warm enough to avoid the harsh brittleness that other fuzz applications produced, and simple enough in compositional shape to be memorable on first hearing. The rhythm section on the recording — drums, bass, and tambourine — was minimal, and the arrangement's production contrast between the dense fuzz-guitar low-midrange and the bright tambourine high-frequency shimmer produced a full-spectrum arrangement from minimal elements. The producer of record was **Erik Jacobsen**, whose earlier production work with **The Lovin' Spoonful** established his command of pop-rock arrangement. **In parallel**, the Olympic Peninsula Wind Storm's acoustic environment is a full-spectrum multi-event arrangement driven by a natural meteorological forcing agent rather than a studio production decision — aeolian tones from many obstacle sizes at many simultaneous frequencies, tree-fall events producing characteristic crack-whomp-crash-secondary sequences, topographic amplification at ridge saddles and notches, continuous roaring background from bulk turbulent air movement — all combining into a natural full-spectrum acoustic environment comparable in compositional density to a studio production but produced by atmospheric physics rather than by human studio craft. **The same** full-spectrum-arrangement-from-simple-elements organizing principle connects both halves: "Spirit in the Sky"'s minimal instrumentation producing full-spectrum arrangement through specific production contrasts, and the Olympic ridge wind storm's simple forcing agent (Pacific frontal pressure gradient) producing full-spectrum acoustic environment through obstacle-interaction physics. **Each side** demonstrates that acoustic density is not a function of element count but of element interaction, and the **paired coordinate** converts the individual full-spectrum observations into a multiplicative structural statement about how the engine's acoustic vocabulary tracks arrangement-density across both human-production and natural-physics domains.

Exposed ridgetops act as eolian galleries: every obstacle on the ridge produces its aeolian tone, and the density of obstacles (tree snags, rock outcrops, ridge features) creates the maximum acoustic complexity. - **Tree-Fall Event Sequences — Crack-Whomp-Crash-Secondary:** Major ridge wind storms produce tree-fall events with characteristic acoustic sequences. A large **Sitka spruce** or **Douglas fir** falling in a storm produces not a single sound but a sequence: the initial **crack** at the base as the wood fails, the **whomp** of the crown hitting other trees, the **crash** of the whole tree hitting the ground, and the **secondary** sounds of branches breaking in the aftermath. The sequence unfolds across 2-5 seconds and is audible over the wind at distances of hundreds of meters. The tree-fall event is one of the more dramatic episodic sounds in the engine's SPS documentation, and its crack-whomp-crash-secondary signature is the specific forensic acoustic identifier that experienced listeners use to recognize the event against the continuous wind-storm background. Sitka spruce's shallow-root morphology — constrained by waterlogged soil and dense-competition lateral-root limitation — explains why Olympic blowdowns feature large uprooted trees rather than broken snags.
