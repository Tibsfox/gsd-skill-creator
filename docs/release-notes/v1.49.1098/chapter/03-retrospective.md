## Surprises

- **OCI samples more than 200 wavelengths to read ocean colour as a continuous spectrum.** The Ocean Color Instrument samples a continuous spectrum from the UV through the near-IR — continuous ~340–895 nm at ~5 nm spectral resolution / 2.5 nm sampling, plus seven discrete SWIR bands (940/1038/1250/1378/1615/2130/2260 nm) — more than 200 wavelengths in all, with ~2-day global coverage at ~1.2 km, the first global hyperspectral ocean-colour observations and the largest Earth-observing instrument Goddard has built in-house.
- **The hyperspectral data reveal which phytoplankton communities are present, not just how much.** Where the heritage discrete-multiband radiometers measured total chlorophyll, OCI's continuous spectrum lets scientists identify phytoplankton community composition — the different types and functional groups present — at global scale, the identification of phytoplankton community composition from space.
- **Two multi-angle polarimeters fly together to read aerosols and clouds.** SPEXone (high polarimetric accuracy, 5 viewing angles, ~385–770 nm, built by SRON) and HARP2 (up to ~60 viewing angles, 4 wavelengths ~441/549/669/873 nm, built by UMBC) fly together and complement each other, giving the advanced multi-angle polarimetry of aerosols and clouds (size, composition, amount) and enabling the study of aerosol–ocean interactions, the ocean and the atmosphere read as one coupled system.
- **A small iridescent sea slug that wears the ocean's colour in the sunlit shallows.** The Opalescent Nudibranch (*Hermissenda crassicornis*), a small, brilliantly iridescent aeolid nudibranch of Pacific Northwest tide pools, eelgrass beds, docks, and floats, shows vivid structural and pigmentary colour — a translucent body banded with electric-blue lines and tipped with orange/white cerata — in the sunlit shallows: a mirror of PACE reading the colour and light of the living ocean (behavioral / ecological description only; a sea slug, distinct from the terrestrial Banana Slug).
- **A brown alga that harvests sunlight along the bright rocky shore.** The Rockweed (*Fucus distichus*), a Pacific Northwest intertidal brown alga of rocky shores, photosynthesizes in the bright sunlit zone of the upper-middle shore — forked olive-brown fronds tipped with swollen yellowish gamete-bearing receptacles (no air bladders) — forming a foundational seaweed canopy over the bare rock (ecological description only) — bound with the throughline of the colour and light of the living ocean, harvesting the same sunlight PACE reads across the full spectrum.
