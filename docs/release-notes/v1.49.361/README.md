**The Extraordinary Climbing Ability and the Rotating Rear Ankles.** The Ringtail's most distinctive anatomical feature beyond the banded tail is its **rotating rear ankle joints** — hind-limb flexibility that allows the species to descend vertical rock faces headfirst, a capability shared with very few other mammals (squirrels, margays, certain arboreal primates). **The "Miner's Cat" Historical Practice and the Rare Mutualistic-Coexistence Record.** The Ringtail's historical cultural connection to Pacific Northwest and Western human communities is documented through the "**miner's cat**" practice — the nineteenth-century frontier custom of keeping tame ringtails as rodent-controlling mousers in Western mining camps. The practice exploited the Ringtail's natural rodent-predation specialization (the species is an effective hunter of mice, voles, and small rodents) to provide rodent control in camps where domestic cats were difficult to maintain. The "miner's cat" nickname persists in regional folklore and historical records, although the practice itself declined as the mining era ended and formal wildlife-protection statutes increasingly prohibited the keeping of native carnivores as pets.
