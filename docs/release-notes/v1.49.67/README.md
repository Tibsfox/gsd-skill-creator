## Summary

The 67th Research project and the third radio station entry. WLF (100.7 The Wolf / KKWF Seattle) traces 78 years of a single frequency — 100.7 FM — through nine call signs, seven format eras, and the Audacy bankruptcy that made it the first major-market country station to survive a Chapter 11 restructuring. Six modules map the complete arc: the CBS affiliate origins, the accidental country monopoly when KMPS (94.1) was eliminated by its own owner in 2011, the Morning Wolfpack franchise (CMA and ACM award-winning), the Entercom-to-Audacy-to-bankruptcy ownership chain, the military community engagement built around JBLM (Joint Base Lewis-McChord), and the industry context including AI-generated country music and genre fusion.

The Audacy bankruptcy story is the business centerpiece: the company that acquired CBS Radio for $5.1 billion went Chapter 11 in January 2024, emerged six months later, and sold most of its stations — but kept KKWF in the Seattle cluster because the frequency was too valuable to abandon. The "frequency as real estate" framework: the electromagnetic spectrum position at 100.7 MHz on Tiger Mountain has been continuously broadcasting since 1947 regardless of who holds the license.

WLF completes a three-station radio cluster with C89 (student/electronic), KSM (border/classic rock), and now WLF (commercial/country). Three stations, three market positions, three ownership models, one shared medium. The radio sub-cluster continues with KPZ (KPLZ pop heritage), K8S (Kubernetes — breaking the radio sequence), RBH (PNW Radio Broadcast History umbrella), and KUB (KUBE 93.3 hip-hop).

Named "The Wolf" — the station's current brand identity, chosen because wolves are native to the Pacific Northwest and the name projects strength in a competitive market. The irony: the wolf brand has survived longer than most of its predecessors on the frequency.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

1. **The bankruptcy module documents a real infrastructure event.** Audacy's Chapter 11 is not ancient history — it happened in 2024. The module documents specific mechanics: debt-to-equity conversion, station divestitures, the FCC vote that enabled the concentration that preceded the bankruptcy. This is infrastructure failure analysis applied to broadcast media.

2. **The military community module reveals a differentiated engagement model.** Operation K9 Companion ($400K, 16 dogs) is not a marketing stunt — it's a multi-year, measurable community program with specific outcomes. The JBLM connection grounds the station in a specific geographic and demographic reality. Country radio's military audience is structural, not coincidental.

3. **The AI country music section captures an inflection point.** By documenting Breaking Rust and Cain Walker (AI-generated country acts) alongside Beyonce's *Cowboy Carter* (genre boundary event), Module 06 captures 2024-2026 as the moment when country radio's relationship to authenticity was fundamentally challenged.

### What Could Be Better

1. **The 78-year frequency history is compressed.** Nine call signs and seven format eras in 267 lines means some eras get only a paragraph. A future deep dive into the pre-Wolf era (1947-2010) would strengthen the historical foundation.

### Lessons Learned

1. **Frequencies are infrastructure; formats are applications.** 100.7 MHz on Tiger Mountain has been broadcasting since 1947. The formats (CBS, soft rock, country) are applications running on that infrastructure. The frequency persists; the applications rotate. The same framework applies to FoxFiber: the physical infrastructure persists while the services running on it evolve.

2. **Corporate bankruptcy doesn't destroy community infrastructure.** KKWF survived Audacy's Chapter 11 because the frequency was too valuable and the community engagement too deep. The lesson: infrastructure with genuine community roots survives financial disruption. Infrastructure without community roots doesn't.
