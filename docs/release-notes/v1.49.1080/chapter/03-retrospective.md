## Surprises

- **A whole space telescope surveys nearly the entire sky, sector by sector.** TESS divides the sky into 26 sectors, each 24°×96°, and steps its four wide-field cameras around the sky to cover ~85% of it in the two-year prime mission (cumulative coverage exceeds 95% with the extended missions) — a wide, systematic survey of nearly the whole sky, exactly the opposite of Kepler's still stare at one field, and exactly what finding transiting planets around the nearest, brightest stars requires.
- **The four cameras stack into a single tall 24°×96° strip.** TESS carries four wide-field CCD cameras, each covering 24°×24°, stacked into one tall 24°×96° observation strip — a column of sky reaching from near the ecliptic pole down toward the ecliptic — and steps that strip sector by sector around the sky, a simple, wide geometry that tiles nearly the whole sky in a year per hemisphere.
- **TESS flies in a stable orbit in 2:1 resonance with the Moon.** TESS reaches a novel high-Earth elliptical orbit in 2:1 resonance with the Moon (the "P/2" orbit, ~13.7-day period) via a lunar gravity assist — swinging out toward the Moon's distance and back, a stable, low-radiation, thermally quiet platform that gives the long, uninterrupted view a wide survey needs (though not the first lunar-resonant orbit; NASA's IBEX used a similar P/3 orbit earlier).
- **TESS hunts the bright, nearby stars so its planets can be followed up.** Where Kepler stared at one distant field, TESS surveys the ~200,000 nearest and brightest stars, so the transiting planets it finds — thousands of TOIs and hundreds of confirmed worlds, including π Mensae c and LHS 3844 b — are the best targets for radial-velocity mass measurement and atmospheric characterization, and many feed JWST follow-up.
- **A broad-winged hawk hovers and quarters the whole open field, scanning section after section.** The Rough-legged Hawk (*Buteo lagopus*), an Arctic-breeding Buteo that winters across the open country of the Pacific Northwest, hovers on beating wings and methodically quarters wide-open ground, scanning section after section for the small movements of prey (behavioral / ecological natural history only, distinguished from the Red-tailed and Ferruginous Hawks) — a patient, systematic wide survey of the whole open country, a mirror of the sky TESS sweeps sector by sector.
- **A near-ubiquitous wildflower spreads its flat clusters of many small flowers across the whole open land.** Common Yarrow (*Achillea millefolium*), one of the most widespread wildflowers of the Pacific Northwest and beyond, opens flat-topped clusters of many small white flower heads above finely divided, feathery "thousand-leaf" foliage across open fields, meadows, dry slopes, and roadsides nearly everywhere (ecological description only, distinguished from other Achillea and from white umbellifers such as Queen Anne's lace) — a plant that covers the whole open landscape, a mirror of the whole open sky TESS surveys.
