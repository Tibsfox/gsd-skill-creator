The signature of ICESat at this degree is the laser pointed straight down. The spacecraft carried a single instrument, the Geoscience Laser Altimeter System (GLAS) — a diode-pumped, frequency-doubled, Q-switched, nadir-viewing Nd:YAG laser firing 40 pulses a second, each about 4 nanoseconds long and roughly 0.1 joule in energy, at two wavelengths: 1064 nanometers (infrared) timed the round trip to the surface for altimetry, while 532 nanometers (green) profiled the vertical distribution of clouds and aerosols. Each pulse lit a ground spot about 70 meters across, and at 40 hertz the spots stepped along the track about 170 meters apart. The instrument timed each pulse's round trip — range equals the speed of light multiplied by the travel time, halved — and, combined with precise orbit determination, that range became the elevation of the surface beneath every footprint, accurate to about 15 centimeters in a single shot over the ice and far better for change recovered by averaging many passes. GLAS carried three identical lasers (designated Laser 1, 2, and 3) for redundancy; Laser 1 was activated on 20 February 2003 and operated about 36 days of data before its output fell, the cause traced to the pump laser diode arrays in the transmitter. To protect the remaining transmitters and stretch the elevation record across years, the team adopted a campaign-based operating strategy: rather than running continuously, GLAS observed in roughly month-long campaigns about three times a year, each campaign re-occupying the same ground tracks so elevation could be compared season to season and year to year. The orbit itself was tuned for this — an 8-day exact-repeat track during commissioning, then the science orbit settled into a 91-day exact-repeat pattern (with a 33-day near-repeat sub-cycle) so each campaign retraced the earlier passes. ## Mission Overview

ICESat is the downward-pointed laser altimeter of the geodesy / satellite-laser-ranging axis — a benchmark mission of NASA's Earth Observing System managed by Goddard Space Flight Center: a single spacecraft launched 12 January 2003 (Pacific local; 13 January 2003 UTC) from Vandenberg Air Force Base, California aboard a Boeing Delta II (7320-10) into a near-circular near-polar orbit at about 94.0 degrees inclination and roughly 600 kilometers altitude; it carried one instrument, the Geoscience Laser Altimeter System (GLAS), a nadir-viewing Nd:YAG laser firing 40 pulses a second at 1064 nanometers for surface altimetry and 532 nanometers for clouds and aerosols, each pulse lighting a 70-meter footprint stepping along the track about 170 meters apart; GLAS carried three lasers run in roughly month-long campaigns about three times a year along a 91-day repeat track; the science was led by H. Jay Zwally (GSFC) with Bob E. Schutz (University of Texas at Austin Center for Space Research) leading the GLAS science team and James B. Abshire (GSFC) as instrument scientist; the mission measured ice-sheet elevation and its change to read the present-day balance of Greenland and Antarctica, made the first space-based sea-ice freeboard measurements, and mapped cloud heights, canopy height, and land topography; and it SUSTAINED the GEODESY / SATELLITE-LASER-RANGING substrate-axis at obs#4 via substrate-form-distinct continuation. ICESat operated as a complete, wholly successful mission — about seven years against a three-to-five-year design — and its elevation record continues unbroken through Operation IceBridge and ICESat-2.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis sustains when the next observation is substrate-form-distinct.** The geodesy axis continued to obs#4 because ICESat changed where the ranging substrate points (downward at the surface rather than between two craft or up from the ground), the quantity (the height of the ice rather than its mass or a static frame), and the science return (direct elevation and its change rather than a weighed gravity field) while keeping the root idea — showing again that substrate-form-distinct continuation is a deliberate test of distinctness, not a repeat.
2. **A laser pointed down can read the Earth directly.** Timing the round trip of a single pulse to the surface and combining the range with precise orbit gives the elevation beneath every footprint — a direct height where GRACE infers mass from gravity; the downward altimeter reads what the surface is doing without an intervening model of the field.
3. **Repeat surveys turn elevation into change.** Each campaign re-occupied the same ground tracks, so the difference between passes is the change in the surface height — the stack of repeat surveys, season to season and year to year, is the signal, the same shift from a single map to a time series that GRACE made for gravity.
4. **A redundancy contingency can become the operating cadence.** When Laser 1's output fell after about 36 days, the team adopted month-long campaigns about three times a year to protect the remaining lasers and stretch the record across years — turning a hardware contingency into the mission's defining rhythm of repeat surveys.
5. **One instrument can read many surfaces.** The single GLAS altimeter, pointed down, read the height of the ice, the freeboard of the sea ice, the tops of the clouds at 532 nanometers, the crowns of the forests, and the shape of the land — one downward beam yielding a wide family of measurements.
6. **The two readings of the same change meet.** GRACE weighs the moving mass of the ice from its gravity; ICESat measures the height of that same ice directly; over the same Greenland and Antarctica the mass-balance and elevation-change records cross-check one another — independent ways of seeing the same change.
7. **Bridge and successor keep a record continuous.** Operation IceBridge flew airborne lasers and radars from 2009 to 2019 to span the gap, and ICESat-2 continued the elevation record from 2018 — so the about-seven-year ICESat mission became part of a continuous multi-decade history of the height of the ice.
8. **Combined-mission figures stay attributed to both missions.** The 200 / 118 gigatons-a-year Greenland / Antarctica values are the combined ICESat + ICESat-2 record across 2003–2019; the ICESat-era Greenland figure is about 170 gigatons a year — keeping the two attributions distinct guards against the recent fabricated-citation class of error.

## Surprises

- **The whole measurement rests on a few nanoseconds of timing.** A 4-nanosecond pulse, 40 times a second, timed on its round trip to the surface and back, gives elevation to about 15 centimeters in a single shot over the ice — the height of the ice sheet read from the travel time of light.
- **A migrating bat already ranges the dark the way ICESat ranges the ice.** The Hoary Bat emits a pulse and times the returning echo to read distance, building a picture of the dark from the round-trip travel time of its own signal — exactly the laser altimeter's emit-pulse / time-the-return / turn-it-into-distance way of seeing, carried aloft.
- **A red alga is the living skin of the surface the laser ranges to.** Snow Algae blooms red as summer warms the snowfield, its pigment darkening the surface and quickening the melt, so the alga lives on the exact ice surface ICESat's laser ranges to — reddening with the same seasonal melt the altimeter measures as a lowering of the ice.
