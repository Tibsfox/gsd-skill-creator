## Summary

The 44th Research project and the first entry in the conventions sub-cluster. NWC (Norwescon) maps 48 years of the Pacific Northwest's premier science fiction and fantasy convention — running continuously since 1978 at the DoubleTree by Hilton SeaTac. Eight modules trace the full arc: founding vision, the Guest of Honor tradition as thesis statement, the panel room as public discourse, the hotel as social architecture, community that spans decades, the broader PNW convention ecosystem, conventions as Rosetta Stones for cross-domain translation, and the program book as verification artifact.

Norwescon is a fixed node. Same hotel, same weekend, same community, year after year for nearly half a century. That permanence is the convention's defining feature — the DoubleTree becomes a temporary city where writers meet scientists meet artists meet cosplayers, and the hallway conversations matter more than the scheduled programming. The literary SF tradition runs deep: Norwescon's writing workshop has launched careers, its Guest of Honor invitations are curatorial statements about what the genre values, and its volunteer infrastructure is a masterclass in community self-governance.

NWC connects to LNV (Larry Niven as past Guest of Honor), GRV/PJM (Seattle creative community), BRC (convention as temporary community infrastructure), FFA (masquerade and costume tradition), and the Rosetta Stone framework. It pairs with WCN (Westercon) as fixed node to traveling signal — together they document both architectures of community infrastructure.

Named "Room 101" — every convention has that one panel room where the deep conversations happen. Also an Orwell nod that fits a convention built on asking hard questions about technology, society, and imagination.

### Module Highlights

- **Module 02 "The Invitation"** (193 lines) treats the Guest of Honor selection as editorial curation. Who a convention invites is a statement about what the genre values this year — the invitation IS the thesis statement. Norwescon's GoH list reads as a timeline of what mattered in PNW speculative fiction across five decades.
- **Module 03 "The Panel Room"** is the largest module (257 lines) and the conceptual core. Panels are structured discourse — a conversation the community decided was worth having in public, with people chosen because they have something to say. The format is older than the internet but structurally identical to what the internet keeps trying to reinvent.
- **Module 04 "The DoubleTree"** (178 lines) maps the hotel as social architecture. The convention doesn't happen IN the hotel — the hotel BECOMES the convention. Hallways become chance-encounter spaces, the lobby becomes a living room, room parties become the real programming after midnight. Physical space shapes community behavior.
- **Module 05 "The People Who Come Back"** (235 lines) maps the social graph. Conventions are reunions for people who see each other once a year and pick up conversations mid-sentence. The community IS the convention; the programming is scaffolding.
- **Module 06 "Not the Only Con in Town"** (203 lines) places Norwescon in the broader PNW convention ecosystem. The same geographic isolation that created the Seattle sound and the PNW timber industry created the convention culture. Emerald City Comic Con, PAX, Sakura-Con — Norwescon is the literary root from which a constellation of conventions grew.
- **Module 07 "Convention as Rosetta Stone"** connects to the Research series framework. A convention is literally a cross-domain translation space — writers, scientists, artists, gamers, costumers speaking different creative languages in the same hotel for a weekend. The parallel to the hub (each project is a panel, the hub is the programming grid) is structural, not metaphorical.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

1. **Combined research+build pipeline.** Single Opus agent handled web research, document writing, 8 module creation, scaffold, and hub updates in one pass. No handoff between research and build agents — eliminated the wait time between phases.

2. **Convention-as-Rosetta-Stone (Module 07) is the conceptual payoff.** The Research series has been building toward the idea that every domain is a translation of the same underlying patterns. A convention is the physical proof — people who speak different creative languages meet in one space and find common ground. The module makes explicit what the hub has been doing implicitly.

3. **The conventions sub-cluster begins.** NWC is the first of three — the fixed node. WCN (Westercon, the traveling convention) and SMF (SMOFcon, the meta-layer) complete the stack. Three conventions, three architectural patterns, one community. Planning the cluster as a sequence rather than a batch means each one can reference the last.

### Lessons Learned

1. **The PNW creative ecosystem keeps revealing new layers.** Green River to Pearl Jam to Weyerhaeuser to Norwescon. Music, timber, conventions — all shaped by the same geographic isolation and DIY ethic. The research series maps the bioregion not just ecologically but culturally. Every project added makes the network more coherent.

2. **Forty-eight years of continuity is its own argument.** Norwescon has outlasted publishers, bookstores, magazines, and entire subgenres. The convention persists because the community persists. Documenting WHY it persists (Module 05's social graph, Module 04's hotel-as-architecture) matters more than documenting what happens there. Infrastructure outlasts content.
