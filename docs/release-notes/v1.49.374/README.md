# v1.49.374 -- Degree 233: Love Battery (return) + Merlin

**Release Date:** 2026-04-03
**Type:** Seattle 360 / Sound of Puget Sound -- Degree 233 of 360

## Summary

**Love Battery** returns at E=6 -- the **second appearance** of the U-District psychedelic rock band in the Seattle 360 engine, the first repeat entry since the engine entered the 200s, and a signal that the catalog has grown deep enough that returning to a significant band with a different aspect of their work is now part of the engine's vocabulary. Love Battery first appeared at degree 221 (E=5), opening the raptor arc with the band's textural, shoegaze-adjacent Sub Pop sound. Now at degree 233, the return entry documents a different face of the same band -- a slightly elevated E reading (from 5 to 6) suggesting that the engine is drawing on a harder or more forward moment in Love Battery's catalog, perhaps the more direct material that appeared on later records as the shoegaze influence was balanced against the band's rock instincts. The return is structurally significant: Love Battery at degree 221 opened the raptor arc, and Love Battery at degree 233 appears in the middle of the Falconidae arc, twelve degrees later, in a context where the engine has moved through eight consecutive raptors and is now tracking through the falcon family. Ron Nine's guitar work was always about sustain and overlay, about the way sounds could accumulate without collapsing, and its return here functions as a reference point -- the engine is not a linear sequence but a field, and figures can recur. The twelve-degree interval is long enough that the return registers as a development rather than a repetition. On the species side, the **Merlin** arrives at E=5 -- the small, fast, ferocious falcon of the northern forests. *Falco columbarius* is the third Falconidae in three consecutive degrees, and it is a bird that overturns expectations about size and aggression. The Merlin is compact -- smaller than a Kestrel in wingspan, similar in size to a Kestrel in body -- but it hunts shorebirds, swallows, and waxwings that are often its own size or larger. It flies in a direct, purposeful, low-level pursuit mode that is entirely unlike the high soaring of eagles or the towering stoops of Peregrines. Its *ki-ki-ki* chatter is rapid and continuous when the bird is agitated -- a cascade of sharp notes that communicates urgency without the Peregrine's mechanical authority. In Washington, the Merlin is a uncommon breeder in the eastern forests and a regular winter visitor to the Puget Sound lowlands, where it appears in flocks of shorebirds and starlings as a pursuit predator.

- Merlin ecology -- in Washington the Merlin breeds in the montane forests of the eastern Cascades and the northeastern highlands, nesting in old corvid nests in conifers; it is also a regular coastal winter visitor, appearing along the Sound's beaches, mudflats, and estuary edges where shorebird and small bird concentrations give it hunting opportunities; Merlins are known to follow mixed shorebird flocks for extended periods, picking off individuals; their speed and directness in flight -- none of the Peregrine's dramatic altitude, just low, fast pursuit -- makes them difficult to follow visually
- Hunting by size inversion -- the Merlin's prey selection inverts the expected size relationship between predator and prey; a bird of 5-7 inches (wing-folded) regularly attacks dunlin, Western Sandpipers, and European Starlings that are comparable in size; this is not rare opportunism but a consistent strategy; the Merlin's success depends on surprise, persistence, and flight agility rather than the mass advantage that larger raptors rely on; the engine documents this as a contrast to the Peregrine's speed advantage and the eagles' size advantage -- the Merlin wins by not accepting the terms of fair fight
- Third consecutive Falconidae -- degrees 231, 232, and 233 document three consecutive falcon species: Peregrine, Prairie, Merlin; each is a different size class (large, medium-large, small), a different primary habitat (coastal/urban, arid interior, boreal forest), and a different hunting strategy (vertical stoop, horizontal pursuit, low direct chase); the Falconidae arc is using its four degrees to document the full range of the family's ecological diversity in Washington before it closes at degree 234
