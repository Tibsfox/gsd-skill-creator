Degree 74 introduces **Poor Moon** -- a band whose significance is inseparable from its relationship to **Fleet Foxes** (degree 53). The side project is a fundamentally different kind of artistic entity from a solo artist or an independent band. A side project exists in the shadow of its parent act. ### STRUCTURAL EVENT: ONE-ALBUM BAND -- Second Defunct/Ended Artist

### THE REVERB WASH -- 75th Acoustic Element

The seventy-fifth acoustic element is **the reverb wash** -- the dreamy, reverb-heavy atmospheric quality where instruments and voices dissolve into spacious, psychedelic haze. In Poor Moon's music, reverb is not an effect applied to the sound but the **environment** the sound inhabits.

The reverb wash is distinct from the layered loop (degree 72, Briana Marela). The layered loop multiplies a single voice through technology -- the voice layered upon itself, building density. The reverb wash dissolves sounds into atmosphere -- the voice and instruments spreading outward, losing their sharp edges, becoming part of a larger sonic environment. The layered loop **accumulates**. The reverb wash **disperses**.

The reverb wash is distinct from the communal harmony (degree 53, Fleet Foxes). This distinction is particularly meaningful given that Poor Moon's members are Fleet Foxes members. The communal harmony is built by many voices singing together in precise harmonic alignment -- a compositional technique. The reverb wash is built by electronic processing -- reverb pedals, studio effects, the technology that makes a small room sound like a cathedral. Composition versus production. Harmony versus atmosphere.

The reverb wash is distinct from the hush (degree 68, Sam Beam). The hush is silence and quietness -- the confessional whisper. The reverb wash is not silent -- it is full, atmospheric, enveloping. ### THE AMERICAN PIKA: CHARISMATIC ALPINE SENTINEL

The American Pika is one of the most charismatic alpine species in the Pacific Northwest -- a small, round, hay-gathering mammal that lives in rocky talus slopes at high elevations. About the size of a tennis ball with ears, the Pika is the embodiment of alpine cuteness: big round ears, no visible tail, dense fur, quick movements across boulders, and a piercing "eenk" bark that echoes across alpine basins.

But the Pika's charisma carries a darker ecological narrative. The American Pika is a **climate change indicator** -- one of the species most directly threatened by warming temperatures. Pikas are exquisitely sensitive to heat: they can die from heat stress at temperatures as low as 25.5 degrees C (78 degrees F) when exposed for just a few hours. As temperatures warm, Pika populations are **retreating upslope**, and some low-elevation populations have already gone extinct. The Pika is the canary in the alpine coal mine.

### TAXONOMY: *Ochotona princeps*

- **Ochotona** -- from the Mongolian *ogdoi* or *ochodona*, the local name for pikas in Central Asia. A linguistic echo reaching from alpine Mongolia to the alpine Pacific Northwest.
- **princeps** -- Latin for "chief" or "first," the first American pika described scientifically, by John Richardson in 1828 from Canadian Rockies specimens.
- **Common names:** American Pika, rock rabbit (historical), cony/coney (biblical/archaic), whistling hare (descriptive). "Pika" from Tungusic *piika*.

Classification:
- **Class:** Mammalia (13th mammalian entry; 11th mammalian species)
- **Order:** Lagomorpha -- SECOND LAGOMORPHA after Snowshoe Hare (73, Leporidae). Back-to-back Lagomorpha (73-74) parallels back-to-back Anatidae (58-59). The Pika is NOT a rodent -- Lagomorpha diverged from Rodentia approximately 80-90 million years ago.
- **Family:** Ochotonidae -- **NEW FAMILY (34th)**. The pika family. Single genus (*Ochotona*), approximately 30 species. Only two North American species: *O. princeps* (American Pika) and *O. collaris* (Collared Pika, Alaska/Yukon). Diverged from Leporidae approximately 35-40 million years ago.
- **Species:** *O. princeps* -- distributed from central British Columbia south through the Cascades, Rockies, Sierra Nevada, and Great Basin ranges.

### THE SOUND: HIGH SHARP "EENK" BARK -- 75th Acoustic Element

The American Pika's acoustic contribution is its defining characteristic -- the sharp, piercing "eenk" bark that echoes across alpine talus slopes:

**The bark:** A short, loud, high-pitched call -- approximately 0.1-0.2 seconds in duration, centered at approximately 3,000-10,000 Hz. The call is variously transcribed as "eenk," "eek," "enk," or "meep." Audible at distances of 200-300 meters across open alpine terrain. The Pika sits on a prominent boulder, body erect, and delivers the bark with its whole body.

**Two call types:**
1. **Short call (alarm):** The single sharp "eenk" -- an alarm call given when a predator is detected. Given singly or in series of 2-5 calls.
2. **Long call (territorial/mating):** A longer, more complex vocalization of 1-3 seconds. Used for territorial advertisement and mate attraction.

Why "eenk" is the 75th acoustic element:
1. **Alpine acoustic.** The highest-elevation sound in the engine. All previous acoustic elements from lower elevations.
2. **E=4 -- the loudest mammalian call relative to body size.** Weighs 120-185 grams but audible at 300 meters.
3. **Alarm function.** Second alarm-calling lagomorph (after Hare's foot thump, 73). The hare thumps. The Pika shrieks.
4. **Alpine echo.** The talus functions as an acoustic amplifier -- gaps between boulders as resonant chambers.

### CLIMATE CHANGE INDICATOR: THE CANARY IN THE ALPINE COAL MINE

The American Pika's conservation story is fundamentally different from any previous species:

- **Lethal temperature:** 25.5 degrees C (78 degrees F) when exposed for extended periods. Dense fur and high metabolic rate make pikas extremely vulnerable to warming.
- **No effective cooling:** Do not pant effectively, do not sweat. Primary strategy: retreat to cool talus interior.
- **Elevation constraint:** The "escalator to extinction" -- when temperatures warm, pikas retreat upslope. Mountain summits are finite. The habitable zone shrinks from below.
- **Population losses:** Great Basin populations documented lost from lower-elevation sites. Range contracting from lower and southern margins.
- **Conservation status:** Petitioned for ESA listing in 2010, declined. IUCN Least Concern but notes climate vulnerability. Washington monitors Pika as climate indicator.

### HAY-GATHERING: THE PIKA AS ALPINE FARMER

The Pika's most remarkable behavior -- **hay-gathering** -- is unique among PNW mammals:

Beginning in mid-summer, the Pika cuts fresh vegetation (grasses, sedges, wildflowers), carries cuttings in its mouth to specific locations, lays them out to dry in the sun -- literally making hay. Dried vegetation is moved into the talus interior for storage. **Haypiles** of 15-30 kg -- more than **100 times** the Pika's own body weight.

The Pika does NOT hibernate. Active year-round beneath snowpack, feeding on stored hay through 6-8 months of alpine winter. Selective forager -- preferentially caches high-caloric plants, sometimes caches mildly toxic plants whose toxins break down during drying (natural preservation).

### PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: THE ALPINE TENNIS BALL

- **Length:** 162-216 mm head and body. No visible tail (vestigial, ~9-12 mm).
- **Weight:** 120-185 grams. Smallest lagomorph in the engine.
- **Ears:** Short, rounded, 20-26 mm (unlike long hare/rabbit ears -- minimizes heat loss).
- **Fur:** Dense, grayish-brown to cinnamon-brown. Two molts per year. Primarily insulative for alpine cold.
- **Feet:** Fur-soled for insulation and traction on rock. No digging claws (uses natural talus spaces).
- **Locomotion:** Short hops and quick dashes across boulders. Maximum ~15 km/h in short bursts. Rarely ventures more than a few meters from talus edge.

### TALUS SLOPE HABITAT: THE HIGHEST ELEVATION IN THE ENGINE

- **Talus:** Broken rock fields at high elevations. Gaps and interstitial spaces form a three-dimensional habitat.
- **Elevation:** In Washington, 1,500-3,000+ meters -- the highest elevation of any species in the engine.
- **Temperature:** Alpine talus maintains cool internal temperatures even in summer. Interstitial spaces function as natural refrigeration.
- **Vegetation:** Talus bordered by alpine meadows providing the Pika's food supply. The talus-meadow interface is the critical habitat boundary.

### BEHAVIORAL ECOLOGY

- **Territorial, solitary.** Territories of 400-700 square meters on talus slopes. Marked by scent (cheek glands) and vocalization.
- **Diurnal.** Unlike recent nocturnal mammals (opossum, raccoon, skunk, fox). Most active during cooler parts of day (bimodal: early morning, late afternoon).
- **Year-round activity.** Does NOT hibernate. Active beneath snowpack in winter.
- **Predators:** Short-tailed Weasel (67, can follow Pika into talus), American Marten (65), raptors, coyotes.
- **Reproduction:** Spring breeding. Litter of 2-6. Young must establish territory and build haypile before first winter.
- **Lifespan:** 3-6 years. Highest mortality in first winter (insufficient haypile = starvation).

## Lessons Learned

1. The side project is a new and analytically productive category in the engine's artist-city taxonomy. It introduces the concept of dual membership -- the artist who exists simultaneously in two bands, two aesthetics, two creative identities. 4. The one-album band / side project intersection reveals how band duration is shaped by context. Primary acts end by choice or attrition. Side projects end when the primary act reclaims its members. Poor Moon's end was gravitational, not volitional
5. The American Pika's climate change indicator status is the engine's most important conservation entry since the Spotted Owl (degree 4). The Pika cannot be saved by local action. This is the first species whose conservation requires planetary-scale intervention
