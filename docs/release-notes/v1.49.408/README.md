**The Oso Slide on March 22, 2014 — The Sequence of Events.** The Oso landslide occurred at approximately **10:37 AM** on Saturday **March 22, 2014**. The hillside above the North Fork Stillaguamish River valley near the community of **Oso** in **Snohomish County, Washington** failed after weeks of above-average rainfall had saturated the slope. Approximately 7.6 million cubic yards of earth, clay, and debris mobilized suddenly and moved down the slope. The slide crossed the North Fork Stillaguamish River, blocking the river's flow and creating an upstream impoundment that threatened additional flooding. The slide mass continued across the valley floor and into the rural neighborhood of **Steelhead Haven** on the south side of the valley, traveling at high speed across the valley floor and destroying or burying approximately fifty homes. Forty-three people died in the slide, including several members of the **Stillaguamish Tribe of Indians**. Rescue operations began immediately and continued for weeks; the last victim was recovered approximately seven weeks after the event. The Washington State Patrol, Snohomish County Sheriff's Office, Department of Natural Resources, United States Geological Survey, American Red Cross, and a large volunteer response from surrounding communities worked in the slide debris during the recovery period. ## Part B: Mudslide Oso — The Geophysics Arc Close at the North Fork Stillaguamish River on March 22, 2014

- **Category Baseline and Geophysics Arc Closing Position:** **Mudslide Oso** is the **seventh of seven** entries in the geophysics arc that opened at degree 261 (**Volcanic Tremor Rainier**, E=5) and closed at the current degree 267. The arc final amplitude profile — 5, 7, 5, 2, 6, 7, 8 — traces an arc-shaped progression that dips to its lowest amplitude at the submarine canyon (264, E=2) and rises to its highest amplitude at the Oso mudslide closing degree. The arc-closing position is structurally deliberate: the arc opened with the continuous low-energy vibration of a living volcano and closes with the high-energy catastrophic event that killed 43 people, honoring both the continuous low-energy processes and the rare high-energy catastrophic events that the Pacific Northwest geophysics register includes.
- **The Date and Time: Saturday, March 22, 2014, approximately 10:37 AM:** The Oso landslide occurred at approximately **10:37 AM** on **Saturday, March 22, 2014**. The specific date and time are anchored in multiple official records including the **United States Geological Survey**'s post-event reports, the **Snohomish County Sheriff's Office**'s emergency dispatch records, the **Washington State Patrol**'s traffic-incident logs, and eyewitness accounts documented in the subsequent official review. The Saturday-morning timing placed more residents at home than a weekday-morning timing would have, and the specific 10:37 AM start time is the mechanism by which the arrival of first emergency response can be precisely sequenced.
- **The Location: North Fork Stillaguamish River Valley, Snohomish County, Washington:** The slide occurred in the **North Fork Stillaguamish River** valley near the community of **Oso** in **Snohomish County, Washington**, approximately four miles east of the community of Oso itself and in the broader rural corridor that connects **Arlington** (to the west) and **Darrington** (to the east) via **State Route 530**. The valley floor is the geographic context in which the **Stillaguamish Tribe of Indians** holds treaty-reserved fishing rights on the river, and it is the location of the rural neighborhood of **Steelhead Haven** on the south side of the valley where the great majority of the 43 deaths occurred.
- **The Hazel Landslide Site and the Documented History of Previous Failures:** The hillside that failed is known as the **Hazel landslide site** and had a documented history of previous failures dating to the 1930s through 2006. Geologists had identified the slope as unstable, and previous smaller-scale slides had occurred at the site across the twentieth century. The 2014 event exceeded previous events by orders of magnitude in scale and runout: where previous slides had been contained within the near-slope area, the 2014 slide mobilized approximately 7.6 million cubic yards of material and traveled across the valley floor to inundate Steelhead Haven on the opposite side of the North Fork Stillaguamish River.
- **The Slide Mechanism: 7.6 Million Cubic Yards of Saturated Earth in Rapid Motion:** The primary slide mechanism was the rapid mobilization of approximately **7.6 million cubic yards** of saturated earth, clay, and debris from the Hazel landslide site. Weeks of above-average rainfall in February and March 2014 had saturated the slope beyond its stability threshold. The slide mobilized suddenly and traveled at high speed across the valley floor. The crossing of the North Fork Stillaguamish River blocked the river's flow and created an upstream impoundment that required controlled breach operations over several weeks to restore normal flow. The slide mass's runout across the valley into Steelhead Haven destroyed or buried approximately fifty homes. The 7.6-million-cubic-yard volume is documented in **USGS Open-File Report 2014-1170** and subsequent scientific publications.
- **The 43 Deaths and the Stillaguamish Tribe Community Members:** The slide killed 43 people, including several members of the **Stillaguamish Tribe of Indians**. Rescue operations began immediately and continued for weeks; the last victim was recovered approximately seven weeks after the event. The 43 fatalities make the Oso slide the deadliest single landslide event in **United States** recorded history — a ranking verified against the full United States landslide fatality record. The names of the 43 dead belong to their families, to Snohomish County, to the Stillaguamish Tribe of Indians, and to the communities of Oso, Darrington, Arlington, and the broader North Fork Stillaguamish River valley whose ongoing memorial work continues to honor them.
- **The Emergency Response and the Recovery Period:** Rescue and recovery operations continued for weeks after the slide. The **Snohomish County Sheriff's Office**, the **Washington State Patrol**, the **Washington State Department of Natural Resources**, the **United States Geological Survey**, the **American Red Cross**, the **United States Army Corps of Engineers**, the **Federal Emergency Management Agency**, and a large volunteer response from surrounding communities worked in the slide debris during the recovery period. The North Fork Stillaguamish River flow was partially restored through controlled breach operations over several weeks. The recovery effort's scale, duration, and volunteer participation were themselves structural features of the event documentation.
- **The USGS Open-File Report 2014-1170 and Post-Event Scientific Review:** The **United States Geological Survey** published **Open-File Report 2014-1170** and subsequent scientific publications documenting the Oso slide's mechanism, volume, and runout characteristics. The post-event scientific review established the 7.6-million-cubic-yard volume figure, the approximately 10:37 AM start time, the documented history of previous failures at the Hazel site, and the rainfall-saturation pre-conditions that led to the failure. The USGS documentation is the primary scientific reference for every subsequent analysis of the event and is the documentary anchor for the engine's degree 267 Part B record.
- **The Acoustic Signature: Deep Rumble at E=8 and the Low-Frequency Bass Propagation:** The acoustic signature of the Oso mudslide is a deep, sustained, bass-frequency rumble — the sound of saturated earth in rapid motion, the low-frequency propagation of millions of cubic yards of material crossing a valley floor at high speed. It is not the sharp crack of a rockslide (degree 266) or the infrasonic tremor of a volcano (degree 261). It is a low, massive, prolonged sound. Survivors reported hearing the slide before it reached them but not long enough to act on the warning. E=8 reflects the slide's peak acoustic energy at full mobilization, appropriate to the arc's closing amplitude position and to the physical magnitude of a 7.6-million-cubic-yard mass in rapid downslope motion.
- **Washington State Landslide Hazard Mapping Response:** The Oso event prompted substantial revisions to **Washington State** landslide hazard mapping and geologic review processes. The **Department of Natural Resources** expanded its landslide inventory mapping, the **University of Washington** Department of Earth and Space Sciences expanded its landslide research program, and the state legislature passed legislation affecting rural land-use regulations in high-hazard zones. The post-event policy response is not a part of the acoustic profile itself but is part of the event's structural documentation because the policy response continues the event's presence in Washington State's geophysical-governance record.

The engine's vocabulary can hold both in equivalent documentary weight because the peak-of-register structure is the shared structural feature. The lesson is that peak-of-register reach is a generalizable documentary concept that applies across radically different subject registers.
3. **Specific quantitative anchors make claims falsifiable and verifiable in ways that qualitative descriptions do not.** The four-million-sold RIAA quadruple-platinum certification and the 43-death Oso fatality count are specific, verifiable, public-record quantitative anchors that convert qualitative claims (commercial peak, deadliest landslide) into falsifiable statements. Future degrees should continue to anchor qualitative claims in specific quantitative figures where such figures exist.
4. **Native-community relationships to the geographic space of a degree's subject deserve explicit documentary presence rather than background-context treatment.** The Stillaguamish Tribe of Indians' treaty-reserved fishing rights on the North Fork Stillaguamish River, the tribe's loss of community members in the slide, and the tribe's pre-slide and post-slide relationship to the valley are structural features of the Oso event that cannot be treated as background context. The engine's documentation should continue to bring Native-community relationships into explicit documentary presence at degrees where the degree's subject is geographically anchored in Native territory.
5. **Commercial window registers should be opened at specific peak-occupant degrees rather than at general-context degrees.** The post-grunge commercial window of 1993-1995 is a specific structural feature of Pacific Northwest rock commerce, and the engine's decision to open the register at the Candlebox degree — where the window's peak occupant sits — anchors the register in a specific peak-commercial event. Commercial window registers opened at diffuse general-context degrees lose the specificity that makes the register falsifiable and applicable to other windows.
6. **Label infrastructure registers (labels, imprints, A&R decisions) should be opened at specific signings rather than at general-context mentions.** Madonna's Maverick Records imprint enters the engine at the Candlebox 1993 signing because that specific signing is where the imprint had a specific effect on the Pacific Northwest commercial rock story. Label-infrastructure registers opened at diffuse Madonna-adjacent or Maverick-adjacent degrees would lose the specificity that makes the register verifiable and applicable to other label signings.
7. **Documentary seriousness about high-consequence events requires separating the human-weight register from the mechanistic register.** The Oso slide's documentation separates the 43 deaths (handled in the dedication and the Part B bullets about the Stillaguamish Tribe) from the slide mechanism (handled in the Part B bullets about the 7.6 million cubic yards, the Hazel landslide site, and the USGS Open-File Report). The separation lets the documentation carry documentary seriousness without converting the human weight into spectacle and without letting the mechanistic documentation become insensitive to the human consequences.
8. **Arc amplitude profiles have shapes that are worth recording even when the shape analysis is deferred to a future degree.** The geophysics arc's final amplitude profile — 5, 7, 5, 2, 6, 7, 8 — is an arc-shaped progression with a mid-arc low point and an arc-close high point. Recording the profile at the arc-closing degree lets subsequent survey or retrospective degrees analyze the shape as a structural object. Amplitude profiles that are not recorded cannot be analyzed later, so the discipline of recording the profile at the arc close is a forward-investment documentary practice.
9. **Survivor accounts and the "warning that came too late" framing belong to the acoustic record because they are themselves acoustic-temporal facts.** Survivors of the Oso slide reported hearing the slide before it reached them but not long enough to act on the warning. This is both a human testimony and an acoustic-temporal fact: the low-frequency bass rumble of the slide propagated faster than the slide's leading mass, creating a short warning interval that was insufficient for action. The acoustic-temporal fact is itself part of the documentary record of the slide's acoustic profile and is not separable from the broader documentation.
10. **Multi-decade band trajectories require compression discipline when the commercial peak is the documentary anchor.** Candlebox's multi-decade 1990-to-present trajectory is compressed in the Part A record because the 1993 commercial peak is the degree's documentary anchor. The compression is a deliberate editorial choice that prioritizes the commercial-peak register over the full-trajectory register. Future survey degrees can address the full-trajectory register for bands where the compression loses important structural information.
