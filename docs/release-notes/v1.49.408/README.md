On the soundscape side, **Mudslide Oso** arrives at E=8 -- the deep rumble acoustic profile of the Oso mudslide of March 22, 2014, one of the deadliest natural disasters in Washington State history.

On the morning of March 22, 2014, a hillside above the North Fork Stillaguamish River valley near the community of Oso, in Snohomish County, failed. Approximately 7.6 million cubic yards of earth, clay, and debris -- saturated by weeks of above-average rainfall -- mobilized suddenly and swept across the river and into the community of Steelhead Haven, a neighborhood of rural homes on the south side of the valley. The slide traveled at high speed across the valley floor, destroyed or buried approximately 50 homes, blocked the North Fork Stillaguamish River, and killed 43 people. It remains the deadliest single landslide event in United States recorded history.

The acoustic signature of a mudslide at the scale of the Oso event is a deep, sustained rumble: the bass-frequency propagation of an enormous mass of saturated material moving rapidly downslope and across the valley floor. It is not the sharp crack of a rockslide or the infrasonic tremor of a volcano. It is a low, massive, prolonged sound -- a sound that those who survived reported hearing before the slide reached them, but not long enough to act on. E=8 is the correct assignment: the Oso slide at its moment of full mobilization was among the most energetic geophysical events in the modern Pacific Northwest record, and its acoustic profile -- the deep rumble that preceded and accompanied the movement of 7.6 million cubic yards of earth -- registers at the high end of the geophysics arc.
