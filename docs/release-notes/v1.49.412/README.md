## Part A: Solger — Earliest Seattle Hardcore, Paul Solger as Founding Figure, Independent Production, and the Pre-Infrastructure Origin of the Capitol Hill Scene

- **Solger as the Chronological Beginning of Seattle Hardcore Punk:** **Solger** formed in approximately 1980 — the same year as or slightly before **The Fartz** — and represents the chronological beginning of Seattle's hardcore scene. The precise chronology of Seattle's earliest hardcore bands is not always clear from commercial documentation (there was almost none), but archival evidence from fanzines, show flyers, and participants' recollections places Solger at the beginning. The engine's documentation at degree 271 treats the "earliest" claim carefully — "among the earliest, possibly the earliest" — rather than asserting absolute priority, consistent with the available archival record.
- **Paul Solger as the Band's Namesake and Guitar Architect:** The band is named for its guitarist **Paul Solger**, who is the founding figure around whom the band formed. Paul Solger's guitar playing and artistic vision for what Seattle hardcore should sound like are the organizing principle of the Solger material. The band-as-person nomenclature is characteristic of the DIY era: the band is identified with the individual whose artistic conception drives it, and that identification survives the band's dissolution when Paul Solger reappears in subsequent Capitol Hill configurations.
- **Steve Verwolf on Vocals and the Classic Configuration:** **Steve Verwolf** appears as vocalist on the Solger material. Verwolf also appears in The Fartz configuration documented at degree 270, providing a second cross-membership link between the two bands beyond the Paul Solger connection. The Verwolf vocal style — shouted, precise in rhythm, confrontational in content — is characteristic of early American hardcore vocal architecture and positions Solger within the broader vocal tradition being established in parallel at Los Angeles (**Black Flag**, **Dead Kennedys**), Washington D.C. (**Minor Threat**, **Bad Brains**), and Boston (**Gang Green**, **SSD**) in the same 1980-1981 period.
- **Independent Self-Release as the Label Context:** Solger's recordings were self-released — there was no label connection of any kind. **Alternative Tentacles** (Jello Biafra's label that released The Fartz material) was not involved; **Subterranean Records** (San Francisco) was not involved; **Sub Pop** did not yet exist (it was founded in 1986, six years after Solger's active period). The material was distributed through the punk underground network: fanzines (**Maximum Rocknroll**, **Flipside**), mail order, touring circuits, and hand-to-hand exchange. The absence of a label is not failure — it is the authentic condition of the earliest participants in a scene that had not yet produced its own infrastructure.
- **Maximum-Energy E=8 Amplitude with Zero Production Accommodation:** Solger at E=8 is a specific type of maximum energy — rawer, less polished, more immediately physical than the arena-filling hard rock amplitude of **Heart** (degree 269) or the Alternative-Tentacles-produced hardcore of **The Fartz** (degree 270). Solger's recordings are lo-fi even by hardcore standards of 1980-1981: the production values are minimal and the point was to capture the live energy rather than achieve studio quality. The E=8 register applies not because the recordings are technically loud but because the musical energy is maximal by design — velocity, aggression, downstroke-driven power chords, short song durations, no verse-chorus-verse-bridge structure.
- **Capitol Hill as the Geographic Center of the 1980-1981 Scene:** The **Capitol Hill** neighborhood of **Seattle** was the geographic center of the early hardcore scene. Rental halls, basements, fire stations, and the occasional sympathetic bar served as venues before purpose-built alternative music infrastructure existed. **The Metropolis** was one of the more established early venues. The scene was small in population — dozens of participants rather than hundreds — and the cross-membership of musicians like Paul Solger, **Duff McKagan** (later of **Guns N' Roses**, then in The Fartz), and **Blaine Cook** across multiple bands is a map of a community rather than a catalog of individual projects.
- **Paul Solger Cross-Membership as Biographical Link to Degree 270:** Paul Solger appears as bassist on The Fartz material documented at degree 270 and as guitarist on the Solger material documented at degree 271. The same musician, two adjacent engine degrees, two adjacent historical moments. The cross-reference is one of the engine's cleanest biographical links and preserves the small-community structure of early Seattle hardcore in the engine's record. Future Seattle Hardcore arc documentation (272 The U-Men, 273, 274) should maintain the same granularity of cross-membership tracking where the archival evidence supports it.
- **Parallel Development with the First Wave of American Hardcore:** Solger's 1980-1981 emergence places them in the first wave of American hardcore alongside West Coast centers (**Los Angeles**: Black Flag, Germs, Adolescents, Circle Jerks; **San Francisco**: Dead Kennedys, MDC, Flipper; **Portland**: **The Wipers**) and East Coast centers (**Washington D.C.**: Minor Threat, Bad Brains, **Dischord Records**; **Boston**: Gang Green, SSD; **New York**: Agnostic Front, Reagan Youth). Solger was not responding to Black Flag or the Dead Kennedys — they were developing simultaneously, in parallel, driven by the same musical and social impulse. The parallel-development observation is structurally productive because it distinguishes the earliest Seattle acts from later bands that were more clearly responding to prior California or East Coast models.
- **Recordings That Barely Survived as Primary Evidence:** The difficulty of finding Solger recordings is itself meaningful. It tells us something real about the conditions under which early Seattle hardcore existed: no recording budget, no professional documentation, no commercial release system. The music happened; some of it was captured on cheap magnetic tape; some of it survives; much of it does not. The engine's documentation at degree 271 treats the archival scarcity as first-class evidence of the pre-infrastructure condition rather than as a deficiency in the documentation.

## Part B: SRKW Whistles and Clicks — Short-Range Social Communication, Echolocation as Sensory Infrastructure, and the Three-System Acoustic Portrait Completed

- **Whistles as the Short-Range Social Communication System:** **SRKW whistles** are tonally pure (single-frequency) or slightly frequency-modulated vocalizations — smooth, musical sounds that contrast with the more complex frequency contours of the discrete calls documented at degree 270. They are produced by the same phonic-lip structures in the nasal region that produce the discrete calls and the echolocation clicks. The whistle system operates at short range — typically within a few body lengths — and is primarily affiliative: used between individuals in close proximity to maintain social bonds, coordinate movement within a subgroup, and facilitate mother-calf communication.
- **Whistle Frequency Range and Mid-Band Peak:** SRKW whistles typically range from approximately **1,000 Hz to 18,000 Hz**, with peak energy in the **3,000-12,000 Hz** mid-frequency band where the SRKW vocal apparatus produces them most efficiently. The frequency range overlaps substantially with human hearing, making SRKW whistles accessible to unamplified human listeners under appropriate acoustic conditions. The mid-frequency peak contrasts with the ultrasonic peak of the echolocation click system and allows whistles to be simultaneously audible to humans and functional for close-range orca communication.
- **Signature Whistles as Individual Acoustic Identity:** Individual Southern Residents develop **signature whistles** — individually distinctive whistle patterns that function as acoustic identity markers. The concept was established more firmly in bottlenose dolphins through the work of **Peter Tyack** at **Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution** but has been documented in killer whales as well. A calf's signature whistle develops through interaction with its mother and pod mates: the pattern is not genetically encoded but culturally transmitted through social interaction. The signature whistle system is the individual-level complement to the pod-level discrete call system documented at degree 270 — pod identity and individual identity are both acoustic and both culturally transmitted.
- **Echolocation Clicks as Broadband Sensory Pulses:** **SRKW echolocation clicks** are broadband pulses spanning from approximately **10,000 Hz to 80,000+ Hz**, with peak energy typically in the **20,000-60,000 Hz** range — well above the 20,000 Hz upper limit of human hearing. The clicks are produced by air movement through the phonic lips (also called "monkey lips" or "museau de singe") in the nasal region, focused through the **melon** (the oil-filled structure in the forehead) into a forward-directed directional beam, and received back through the highly sensitive lower jaw (the mandible, which contains acoustic fat that conducts sound to the middle ear). The frequency is high because shorter wavelengths resolve smaller objects more precisely.
- **Click-Rate Acceleration During Prey Approach:** During salmon-hunting, the SRKW produces rapid click trains directed toward prey. The click rate increases as the distance to prey decreases — a phenomenon also observed in bats during insect interception. Near prey, click rates can exceed **500 clicks per second**. The click-rate acceleration provides the information-density necessary for precise prey location during the final approach. The behavioral parallel with bat echolocation is one of the clearer examples of convergent evolution in biosonar systems, and the documentation at degree 271 treats the acceleration profile as first-class acoustic evidence rather than as a supplementary observation.
- **Salmon-Sized Object Detection at 100-200m:** The SRKW echolocation system can detect a salmon-sized object at ranges of **100-200 meters or more** under optimal conditions. The system discriminates **Chinook salmon** from other salmonid species (by body size and swim-bladder acoustic signature), discriminates fish size (relevant to prey selection), and discriminates fish orientation (facing toward or away from the orca, relevant to interception strategy). The information-density available to the SRKW through echolocation is substantially richer than the two-bit "is there a fish there" inference the phrase "biosonar" might suggest — it is a full three-dimensional sensory system with species-level and individual-level discrimination.
- **The Three-System Portrait Completed Across Two Adjacent Degrees:** The SRKW acoustic portrait is completed across degrees 270-271 through three functionally distinct systems: **discrete calls** (degree 270, long-range social, stereotyped, pod-specific, culturally transmitted), **whistles** (degree 271, short-range social, flexible, individually variable, signature-developing), and **echolocation clicks** (degree 271, sensory, broadband, prey-detection-and-navigation). No other species in the SPS track has received two consecutive entries for portrait completion, and the three-system documentation is the first of its kind in the engine. The cross-sync with the Solger side mirrors the completeness-through-continuation theme on the hardcore track.
- **The San Juan Channel and Lime Kiln Point as Research Infrastructure:** The **San Juan Channel** — between **San Juan Island** and **Lopez Island** — connects **Haro Strait** to the west with the **Strait of Juan de Fuca** to the south, and its deep tidal waters concentrate the **Chinook salmon** that SRKW depend on during summer foraging. **Lime Kiln Point State Park** ("Whale Watch Park") on San Juan Island provides public shore-based whale-watching and operates hydrophones that allow visitors to hear SRKW vocalizations in real time. **Ken Balcomb**'s **Center for Whale Research** (1976-present), based on San Juan Island, conducts the longest-running cetacean population study in the world and provides the ~73-75 individual figure on which SRKW population assessments rest.
- **Boat Noise as Direct Mechanistic Foraging Threat:** Vessel noise in the Salish Sea raises ambient acoustic levels and forces the Southern Residents to increase click amplitude (the **Lombard effect** applied to echolocation), potentially reducing the effective range of their sonar. If echolocation range is reduced by noise, the Southern Residents must expend more energy searching for Chinook salmon during an already nutritionally stressful period. The boat-noise threat is not abstract — it directly impairs the sensory system used to find food. The documentation at degree 271 makes the acoustic-disturbance threat mechanistically legible in physics terms rather than in generalized conservation-concern terms, and that mechanistic framing is a first-class element of the entry rather than a supplementary observation.

Future multi-degree species treatments should follow the same warrant-by-actual-acoustic-complexity principle rather than applying the two-degree treatment uniformly to every species.
