## Mission Overview

Pioneer 10 is the obs#1 opener of the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA deep-space probe whose defining achievements are the first mission to the outer solar system, the first traverse of the asteroid belt, the first flyby of Jupiter, the first placement on a solar-system escape trajectory, and the first physical message to extraterrestrial intelligence sent out of the solar system. It is managed by NASA's Ames Research Center and built by TRW (NOT JPL). A spin-stabilized flyby probe with a large dish high-gain antenna — not an orbiter or a lander — it launched on an Atlas-Centaur (Atlas SLV-3C / Centaur-D with a Star-37E kick stage) from Cape Canaveral LC-36A on 2 March 1972 (3 March 1972 01:49 UTC), powered by four SNAP-19 radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) fueled with plutonium-238 (~160 W at launch, ~140 W at Jupiter) rather than solar panels, because sunlight is far too weak in the outer solar system. Its payload included a magnetometer, plasma and charged-particle detectors, cosmic-ray and trapped-radiation instruments, the Imaging Photopolarimeter (IPP) that built spin-scan images of Jupiter, an infrared radiometer, and meteoroid and asteroid-dust detectors. The story is the long outbound voyage: across the inner solar system on RTG power, through the main asteroid belt (entered 15 July 1972, emerged 15 February 1973) with far fewer particle impacts than feared — the first craft to cross it and show the belt was safely navigable — and on to the first flyby of Jupiter (closest approach 3 December 1973 US local / 4 December 1973 02:26 UT, about 130,000 km from the planet), returning the first close-up data on the giant planet's atmosphere, its moons, and its intense radiation belts and magnetosphere as it dove through the magnetosphere and whipped around on a gravity assist. That assist placed Pioneer 10 on a solar-system escape trajectory — the first spacecraft set on a path to leave the solar system — carrying the Pioneer plaque outward. The scoping is load-bearing: the Jupiter flyby is the first spacecraft to fly by / reach Jupiter (Pioneer 11 came in December 1974 and Voyager 1 & 2 in 1979); the escape claim is the first placed on a solar-system escape trajectory / first to reach solar escape velocity — never "most distant" (Voyager 1 overtook Pioneer 10 on 17 February 1998) and never "first to leave the solar system / cross the heliopause" (Voyager 1, 2012). The RTG power is engineering identity, not a "first" anchor (earlier near-Earth/lunar missions carried RTGs). Routine contact ended 31 March 1997; the last successful data contact was about 27 April 2002; the last very faint signal was received 23 January 2003 from about 12.2 billion km, and the spacecraft is now inert (past tense). Pioneer 10 OPENS the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION substrate-axis at obs#1 (substrate-axis rotation #37). NSSDCA/COSPAR 1972-012A.

## Dedication

v1.250 Pioneer 10 is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's first probe to the outer solar system and opened the road to the giant planets. The Ames Research Center team who managed the mission; the TRW engineers who raised the spin-stabilized probe with its great dish antenna and its nuclear power supply; the launch team who lit the Atlas-Centaur off Cape Canaveral in March 1972; the navigators who threaded the spacecraft through the asteroid belt and bent its path around Jupiter; and the scientists behind the magnetometer, the particle and radiation detectors, the imaging photopolarimeter, and the dust counters. They sent a small craft farther than any had gone — through the belt of scattered rock that crews had feared, past the banded giant and its fierce belts of radiation, and onto a path out of the solar system — and bolted to its struts a plaque meant for whoever might one day find it. For thirty years the probe answered, fainter and fainter, from the dark edge of the system, until the last whisper came home in January 2003. The pathfinder went quiet, far out in the open country it had opened for everyone who followed.
