## Surprises

- **A 1972 probe crossed the asteroid belt safely.** Pioneer 10 was the first spacecraft to fly through the main asteroid belt (15 July 1972 – 15 February 1973), encountering far fewer dust and particle impacts than mission planners had feared — settling a real pre-flight question and showing the belt was safely navigable, opening the route to the outer solar system.
- **A probe reached Jupiter for the first time and survived its radiation belts.** Pioneer 10 made the first flyby of Jupiter on 4 December 1973 (UT) at about 130,000 km from the planet, returning the first close-up data on the giant planet's atmosphere, its moons, and its intense radiation belts and magnetosphere as it dove through the magnetosphere and whipped around on a gravity assist.
- **A single gravity assist set a spacecraft on a path out of the solar system.** The Jupiter flyby accelerated Pioneer 10 past solar escape velocity, making it the first spacecraft placed on a trajectory to leave the solar system — the first of the handful of artifacts ever set on an escape path (scoped carefully: Voyager 1, launched 1977 and faster, later overtook it to become the most distant human-made object).
- **A probe carried a message for extraterrestrial intelligence outward.** Pioneer 10 carries the Pioneer plaque — a gold-anodized aluminum plaque designed by Carl Sagan and Frank Drake and illustrated by Linda Salzman Sagan — the first physical message intended for extraterrestrial intelligence placed on a trajectory out of the solar system, preceding the Voyager Golden Record of 1977.
- **A shorebird crosses open country and sea to reach the far coast.** The Marbled Godwit (*Limosa fedoa*), a large, long-billed shorebird that breeds in interior North America, undertakes long seasonal migrations to winter and stage in substantial numbers on the outer coast and estuaries of the Pacific Northwest — a long-distance traveler of open country, marshes, and far shores (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Pioneer 10 voyaging out across the solar system to the far frontier.
- **A coastal grass pioneers the open, shifting frontier of the dunes.** American Dunegrass / Dune Wildrye (*Leymus mollis*), a robust, rhizomatous native grass of Pacific Northwest beaches and foredunes, is among the first plants to colonize and stabilize bare, shifting sand at the frontier of the land — its spreading rhizomes binding new ground and opening the way for other dune plants (ecological description only) — a mirror of Pioneer 10 opening the way to the outer planets.
