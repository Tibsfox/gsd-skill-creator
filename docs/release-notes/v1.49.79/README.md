## Summary

The 79th Research project and the most contemplative entry in the series. RLS (Resonant Landscapes) maps the intersection of sound, place, and meaning across five PNW landscapes — from Douglas Hollis's aeolian sculptures at NOAA's Seattle campus through the glacial silence of Spider Meadow, the storm acoustics of Rialto Beach, the archipelago ecology of the San Juan Islands, and the sacred absence of Sitka's Tlingit homelands connected to Matthiessen's Crystal Monastery.

Five modules trace the territory: sound and sculpture (Hollis's wind instruments, the Soundgarden sculpture park, Yamasaki's Pacific Science Center arches), Glacier Peak Wilderness (Spider Gap crossing, Image Lake reflections, wildflower ecology, mining history), Olympic Peninsula coastal trails (Rialto Beach, Ozette archaeological site with 55,000 Makah artifacts, Cascadia Subduction Zone edge), Islands and the Salish Sea (Mount Constitution, Moran State Park, Coast Salish reef net fishing, orca presence, Deception Pass), and Northern Journeys (Sitka/Tlingit history, Battle of Sitka, Tongass rainforest, NAGPRA repatriation, Matthiessen's Snow Leopard as contemplative framework).

RLS extends the Ecology cluster alongside COL, CAS, and ECO but adds a dimension none of them carry: the acoustic layer. Every landscape documented in the series has a soundscape — RLS is the first study to map sound as a primary characteristic of place rather than an incidental one.

Named "The Listening Trail" — because every trail in the PNW has a frequency if you stop to hear it. The aeolian sculptures make it explicit; the forests have always done it implicitly.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

1. **The acoustic layer adds a new dimension to PNW ecology.** COL mapped the Columbia Valley's geology, CAS mapped the Cascade Range's volcanology, ECO mapped living systems. RLS maps the soundscape — the frequency of wind through Douglas fir, the storm acoustics at Rialto Beach, the silence of Spider Meadow. Sound was always there; this study makes it visible.

2. **The Matthiessen connection in Module 05 provides philosophical depth.** The Snow Leopard's Crystal Monastery — where Matthiessen journeyed to see a sacred animal and found the sacred in its absence — provides a framework for understanding contemplative landscapes. The PNW has its own Crystal Monasteries: Spider Meadow, the Tongass, the San Juan Islands at dawn.

### Lessons Learned

1. **Landscapes have frequencies.** This is not metaphor — it is physics. Wind through conifer needles, waves against sea stacks, glacial meltwater over stone. Every landscape documented in the Research series has an acoustic signature. RLS is the first study to map it.
