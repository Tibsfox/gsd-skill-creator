The module that matters most is Module 01 (Sound and Sculpture): it takes a specific object — Douglas Hollis's *A Sound Garden* on the NOAA Western Regional Center campus at Sand Point — and reveals it as the Rosetta stone for reading every other landscape in the Research series as an acoustic phenomenon first and a visual phenomenon second. Everything else follows from that reframing. Glacier Peak (Module 02), the Olympic coast (Module 03), the Salish Sea (Module 04), and the Sitka-to-Crystal-Monastery arc (Module 05) are each downstream of the central claim that a place's soundscape is not metaphorical — wind through conifer needles, waves against sea stacks, glacial meltwater over stone are physical acoustic signatures, and the Research series has been documenting them without naming them until now.

**Anchor artifact named.** **The aeolian sculpture on the NOAA campus is the project's load-bearing artifact and the release names it as such.** Hollis's *A Sound Garden*, built 1982–1983, consists of twelve 21-foot steel towers topped with organ pipes attached to weathervanes; when wind rotates the vanes and pushes air through the pipes, the installation emits continuously shifting overlapping tones that never repeat. On calm days the sculpture is silent. On windy days it is audible across hundreds of meters. Its name — "A Sound Garden" — is the direct etymological source of the Seattle band Soundgarden, which Chris Cornell and Kim Thayil named after the sculpture after visiting it in the mid-1980s. The Research project treats that naming lineage as structural: the band drew its name from an aeolian instrument on a federal ocean-research campus in the city whose sound the world came to know as grunge, and the chain of acoustic-cultural references — Hollis, NOAA, Magnuson Park, Lake Washington's resonant surface, the Soundgarden memorial circle, Yamasaki's Pacific Science Center arches, the 1962 World's Fair, Seattle's musical geography — is the axis Module 01 walks. Every subsequent module inherits the method: identify the land's acoustic signature, walk the cultural forms that responded to it, document the physical and acoustic and human layers as one braid.

**Five-module traverse.** **The five-module structure maps an acoustic-layered traverse, not a checklist of parks.** Module 01 (Sound and Sculpture) anchors the project to the Seattle urban armature through Hollis's aeolian work, the Soundgarden sculpture park, Yamasaki's Gothic-modernist arches at the Pacific Science Center, and the 1962 World's Fair that reorganized the city's cultural geography. Module 02 (Glacier Peak Wilderness) takes the acoustic method into the alpine: Spider Meadow at dawn, Spider Gap crossing, Image Lake's reflection principle (the landscape sees itself), the wildflower ecology of high alpine meadows, the mining history that left cabin ruins and prospect pits across the wilderness, and the silence that is not silence but the absence of human noise. Module 03 (Olympic Peninsula Coastal Trails) walks Rialto Beach storm acoustics, the Hole-in-the-Wall sea arch, the Ozette archaeological site with its 55,000 Makah artifacts recovered after the 1970 mudslide exposure, the Cascadia Subduction Zone edge that produces the Orphan Tsunami of 1700, and the shipwreck acoustics along what sailors called the Graveyard of the Pacific. Module 04 (Islands and the Salish Sea) maps Mount Constitution's CCC stone tower modeled after Caucasus watchtowers, Moran State Park, Coast Salish reef net fishing practice, orca vocalization and the Southern Resident population, and Deception Pass as the acoustic gate between Whidbey Island and Fidalgo. Module 05 (Northern Journeys and Contemplation) extends the reach to Sitka, the 1802 and 1804 battles that dispossessed the Tlingit, the Tongass National Forest as North America's largest temperate rainforest, NAGPRA repatriation as acoustic and cultural return, and Peter Matthiessen's *The Snow Leopard* as the contemplative framework — "Have you seen the snow leopard?" "No! Isn't that wonderful!" — that the PNW's own Crystal Monasteries (Spider Meadow, the Tongass, the San Juan Islands at dawn) rhyme with.

**Ecology-cluster quartet completed.** **The acoustic layer adds a dimension none of the prior Ecology-cluster releases carried, and the release names the prior cluster explicitly.** COL (Columbia Valley) mapped geology at the continental-plate scale. CAS (Cascade Range) mapped volcanology at the arc-magmatism scale. ECO (PNW Ecology) mapped living systems at the biome scale. Each was a layered atlas of a landscape type, each was indispensable, and none of them mapped the soundscape as a primary axis. RLS completes the cluster with the dimension that was always present but never foregrounded: the frequency signature of a place. This is a genuine discovery, not a rhetorical flourish. Wind through Douglas fir needles produces a broadband hiss that peaks around 200–500 Hz depending on needle density and wind speed; waves against basalt sea stacks at Rialto produce percussive sub-200 Hz pulses whose interval encodes swell period; glacial meltwater over granite at Image Lake produces narrow-band tonal signatures that vary with flow; Tongass old-growth hemlock-spruce understory is acoustically unlike second-growth Douglas fir stand because the canopy scattering geometry differs. Every landscape has a frequency signature, and the signature is legible to any observer who stops to listen. The Research series is now equipped to cite acoustic-ecology claims across all future landscape projects because RLS establishes the method and the vocabulary.

**Matthiessen frame adopted.** **The Matthiessen connection in Module 05 is the project's philosophical spine, and the PNW has its own Crystal Monasteries.** Peter Matthiessen's 1978 *The Snow Leopard* narrates a journey into the Crystal Monastery region of Dolpo in northwestern Nepal, traveling with the zoologist George Schaller in search of the titular animal. Matthiessen never sees the snow leopard. The book's philosophical argument — that the most profound encounter is with what is not there, that sacred presence can be registered as sacred absence — is the framework Module 05 uses to read the PNW's contemplative landscapes. Spider Meadow at dawn is a Crystal Monastery: the silence is the phenomenon. The Tongass old-growth interior is a Crystal Monastery: the cathedral acoustics of 500-year hemlock is the phenomenon. The San Juan Islands from Mount Constitution at first light is a Crystal Monastery: the spatial-acoustic openness of the Salish Sea is the phenomenon. The Sitka Sound shoreline after the Battle of Sitka is a Crystal Monastery: the absence of the Tlingit clans that were forced from the site is the phenomenon, and the acoustic register of that absence is what the research module names and foregrounds. Naming the parallel as structural — Matthiessen's Dolpo and the PNW's contemplative landscapes as the same genre of place — is the release's single most editorial decision, and it is load-bearing for every future Research project that wants to engage sacred absence as a landscape phenomenon.

**The five-module traverse connects Seattle urban acoustic art to Tlingit homelands across 800 miles of coastline.** The geographic arc is deliberate: Module 01 begins at Sand Point on Lake Washington within Seattle's city limits, Module 02 moves east into the Glacier Peak Wilderness of the North Cascades, Module 03 moves west to the Olympic Peninsula and the open Pacific, Module 04 moves north into the Salish Sea archipelago, and Module 05 moves further north along the Inside Passage to Sitka and the Tongass and the comparative reach to Matthiessen's Dolpo. **The Ozette archaeological site in Module 03 is the release's hidden treasure and the acoustic-archaeology case study.** In 1970 a mudslide near Cape Alava on the Olympic coast exposed the remains of a Makah village that had been buried and preserved for roughly 500 years — Ozette. The recovery effort, conducted over 11 years by the Makah Tribe and Washington State University, recovered 55,000 artifacts, including baskets, cedar boxes, halibut hooks, whaling gear, and structural elements of longhouses, preserved by the anaerobic mud conditions. The artifacts are housed at the Makah Cultural and Research Center in Neah Bay. The acoustic-archaeology claim Module 03 makes is structural rather than speculative: a coastal village site's acoustic environment — surf pattern, wind pattern, swell period against offshore stacks — is part of the site's material culture, because the village's daily rhythms, song traditions, and oral-historical memory all entrain to the acoustic environment. Documenting the site acoustically is part of documenting it historically. The NAGPRA-repatriation framework that Module 05 picks up connects forward: the return of material culture must be accompanied by the return of acoustic context, because separating a drum from its coastline is separating two halves of a single artifact.

## Part A: Sound as Primary Characteristic of Place

- **Hollis Origin:** Douglas R. Hollis (b. 1948) studied at the University of Michigan and California College of Arts and Crafts; *A Sound Garden* (1982–1983) at the NOAA Western Regional Center on Lake Washington's northwest shore was the commission that established his national reputation in aeolian sculpture.
- **The Twelve Towers:** twelve 21-foot galvanized-steel tower structures, each topped with an organ pipe attached to a weathervane; wind rotates the vane, pushes through the pipe, produces overlapping tonal wails that shift continuously and never repeat.
- **Soundgarden Naming Lineage:** Chris Cornell and Kim Thayil named the Seattle band Soundgarden after visiting the sculpture in the mid-1980s; the naming is the direct etymological bridge between an aeolian instrument on a federal ocean-research campus and the sound the world came to call grunge.
- **Yamasaki's Pacific Science Center:** Minoru Yamasaki's Gothic-modernist arches at the 1962 World's Fair site form the acoustic-architectural counterpart to Hollis — arches that reshape the urban acoustic environment by diffusing and reflecting sound across Seattle Center's plazas.
- **Lake Washington as Resonant Surface:** the lake's enclosed basin produces a distinctive low-frequency reflectance that the aeolian sculpture exploits; wind patterns off the lake determine the sculpture's audibility schedule more than any calendar rhythm does.
- **Magnuson Park Context:** the sculpture sits on a former naval-air-station site (Sand Point Naval Station, decommissioned 1970s) now managed as Magnuson Park; the site's layered history — indigenous Duwamish land, Navy base, federal campus, public park — is part of the sculpture's situated meaning.
- **NOAA Art Walk:** the campus hosts a self-guided art walk that cross-references the aeolian sculpture with other public-art installations; the walk formalizes the acoustic-visual method RLS then scales across the Pacific Northwest.
- **Aeolian Tradition:** the tradition extends from the ancient Aeolian harp through Athanasius Kircher's 17th-century wind-harp designs through 20th-century sound art — Hollis's work is one node in a long sound-art genealogy, not a Seattle-specific novelty.
- **The Resonance Thread:** Module 01 names the through-line explicitly: the wind moves through twelve steel pipes and produces something that is not quite music and not quite weather, and the space between is the Research project's central metaphor for all five modules.
- **Smithsonian Connections:** *A Sound Garden* is documented in the Smithsonian's Save Outdoor Sculpture! (SOS!) survey; the documentation produces a cross-reference frame that the RLS page architecture inherits.

## Part B: Landscape as Acoustic Archive

- **Spider Meadow Silence:** Spider Meadow at dawn is the acoustic anchor of Module 02; the silence is not an absence of sound but the absence of human noise, which produces its own acoustic signature — wind through needles, distant meltwater, the subsonic ambient noise of the alpine.
- **Rialto Beach Storm Acoustics:** Pacific Ocean swell hitting basalt sea stacks at Rialto produces percussive sub-200 Hz pulses whose interval encodes swell period; Hole-in-the-Wall sea arch adds a whistle resonance when wind runs through it at the right angle.
- **Ozette Acoustic Archaeology:** the Ozette site's 55,000 recovered artifacts (baskets, halibut hooks, whaling gear, cedar boxes, longhouse structural elements) carry their acoustic context with them; the acoustic environment of Cape Alava is part of the site's material culture, not a backdrop to it.
- **Deception Pass Gate:** the pass between Whidbey and Fidalgo islands acts as an acoustic gate where tidal flow produces characteristic audible turbulence; the bridge above adds a human-made counterpoint — this is where the Salish Sea's rhythm becomes auditioned.
- **Southern Resident Orca Vocalization:** the L, J, and K pods produce distinctive vocal dialects documented by Center for Whale Research and NOAA researchers; orca acoustic presence in the Salish Sea is both a conservation metric and a landscape-identity marker.
- **Coast Salish Reef Net Fishing:** the Lummi and other Coast Salish peoples practiced reef net fishing at specific acoustic-tidal conjunctions; the practice is itself a form of acoustic reading of the sea, encoded in oral tradition and in the specific geography of anchor sites.
- **Tongass Cathedral Acoustics:** the old-growth hemlock-spruce interior of the Tongass produces cathedral-like acoustic reflection patterns; 500-year canopy scattering geometry is unlike second-growth Douglas fir stand acoustics, and the difference is diagnostic for forest age.
- **Sitka Sound as Contemplative Landscape:** the shoreline where Russian and Tlingit forces fought in 1802 and 1804 is now read through sacred-absence poetics; the acoustic register of Tlingit clan displacement is what Module 05 names and foregrounds.
- **Matthiessen's Framework:** *The Snow Leopard* (1978) narrates a journey through Dolpo in search of an animal never seen; the book's philosophical argument — that the most profound encounter is with what is not there — becomes the reading frame for the PNW's own Crystal Monasteries.
- **NAGPRA and Acoustic Return:** the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act of 1990 frames the return of material culture; Module 05 extends the frame by arguing that acoustic context must return with objects, because separating a drum from its coastline severs two halves of a single artifact.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **The acoustic layer adds a new dimension to PNW ecology, and the release names the prior cluster explicitly.** COL mapped the Columbia Valley's geology at the continental-plate scale, CAS mapped the Cascade Range's volcanology at the arc-magmatism scale, ECO mapped living systems at the biome scale. RLS maps the soundscape — the frequency of wind through Douglas fir, the storm acoustics at Rialto Beach, the silence of Spider Meadow, the cathedral acoustics of the Tongass. Sound was always there; this study makes it visible. Future Research projects on landscapes inherit the acoustic vocabulary and can cite RLS as the canonical acoustic-method reference.
- **The Matthiessen connection in Module 05 provides philosophical depth without forcing the frame.** *The Snow Leopard*'s Crystal Monastery — where Matthiessen journeyed to see a sacred animal and found the sacred in its absence — provides a framework for understanding contemplative landscapes. The PNW has its own Crystal Monasteries: Spider Meadow at dawn, the Tongass interior, the San Juan Islands from Mount Constitution at first light, Sitka Sound after the battle. Naming the parallel as structural — the same genre of sacred-absence place across continents — is the module's central editorial decision and it lands without feeling like borrowed depth.
- **The Soundgarden naming lineage closes an acoustic-cultural loop the series could not close before.** Chris Cornell and Kim Thayil named the band after Hollis's sculpture; the sculpture sits on a federal ocean-research campus; the campus sits on a former Navy base; the former Navy base sits on ancestral Duwamish land; and the sound the band made went on to define a genre. The release makes the loop visible in a single Module 01 walk, and the loop becomes citable infrastructure for future Seattle-cultural Research projects.
- **The three-commit atomic-content discipline continues to pay off.** Content commit `f5f3ad92c` + docs commit `325102db2` + merge commit `abb805527` means the v1.49.78..v1.49.79 window contains exactly one meaningful state transition. ### What Could Be Better

- **The acoustic-physics claims are illustrative rather than instrumented.** The release names specific frequency ranges (200–500 Hz for Douglas fir wind hiss, sub-200 Hz for Rialto surf pulses, narrow-band tonal signatures for Image Lake meltwater) but does not cite acoustic-ecology field measurements for those ranges. The claims are plausible and consistent with the general acoustics literature, but a follow-on project could commission field recordings with calibrated microphones and spectrogram analysis to ground the numbers against real data rather than against reasonable estimation.
- **The international-comparison thread with Matthiessen's Dolpo is gestured at but not walked at depth.** Module 05 uses Matthiessen as contemplative framework but does not systematically compare the PNW's Crystal Monasteries to Dolpo's crystal-monastery region at the ecological, cultural, or acoustic level. A future companion project could walk the comparison with specificity — Himalayan alpine versus North Cascades alpine, Tibetan Buddhist contemplative tradition versus Tlingit clan tradition, snow leopard presence/absence versus Southern Resident orca population — and the comparison would strengthen the theoretical frame the release introduces.
- **The Ozette acoustic-archaeology claim is structurally argued but not yet collaboratively developed with the Makah Tribe.** The module's argument that acoustic context must return with material artifacts is the release's most ethically load-bearing claim, but the argument is made externally; the release does not document tribal-collaboration-or-review of the argument. A future revision should engage the Makah Cultural and Research Center directly to verify, refine, or correct the framing before it propagates as uncontested method.

### What Needs Improvement

- **The Southern Resident orca section could be longer given the population crisis.** Module 04 treats orca vocalization as landscape-identity marker but does not walk the Southern Resident population decline (from 98 individuals in 1995 to below 75 in recent census counts) with the depth the crisis warrants. A follow-on revision or companion module should treat the acoustic-population link explicitly — vessel noise as acoustic stressor, Chinook salmon acoustic ecology, the Duwamish cleanup as acoustic-habitat restoration — because the crisis is ongoing and the acoustic frame is directly relevant to conservation policy.
- **The Tongass coverage is proportionally light given the forest's continental importance.** Module 05 treats the Tongass as contextual backdrop to Sitka but the Tongass itself — 16.7 million acres, largest temperate rainforest in North America, active management-plan conflicts — deserves module-level treatment. A future Research project could take the Tongass as primary subject with the same acoustic-ecology method RLS establishes, and the forest would carry the method forward at the scale it genuinely occupies.
- **The Cascadia-subduction-zone acoustic story is hinted at but not made seismological.** Module 03 names the 1700 Orphan Tsunami and the Cascadia edge but does not develop the acoustic-seismic connection — ultralow-frequency ground motion as precursor, infrasound coupling between ocean swell and continental shelf, the specific earthquake-acoustic signatures that would inform PNW preparedness. A future collaboration with the Pacific Northwest Seismic Network could close that gap and make the acoustic frame directly citable in hazard literature.

## Lessons Learned

- **Landscapes have frequencies, and acoustic ecology is a landscape-primary discipline.** This is not metaphor — it is physics. Wind through conifer needles, waves against sea stacks, glacial meltwater over stone, cathedral-acoustic reflection in old-growth understory. Every landscape documented in the Research series has an acoustic signature, and the signature is legible to any observer who stops to listen. RLS is the first study to map sound as a primary characteristic of place rather than an incidental one, and every future landscape project should carry an acoustic layer by default.
- **Naming prior-cluster completion explicitly strengthens the series.** COL, CAS, ECO, and RLS together constitute a completed Ecology-cluster quartet — geology, volcanology, biology, acoustics. Naming the quartet explicitly in the release gives the reader a map of where the series has been and implicitly sketches where it is going. Future Research projects should name their cluster context whenever a new dimension completes a prior cluster; the metadata-as-narrative discipline is how the series accumulates into architecture rather than stays an anthology.
- **A single object can anchor a five-module traverse if the object carries enough semantic load.** Hollis's *A Sound Garden* is a twelve-tower sculpture on a federal research campus; it does not obviously warrant a five-module PNW atlas. It carries the load because its naming, location, material, aeolian tradition, and cultural-reception history each open an entire research surface. Future Research projects should test candidate anchor-objects against the five-module test: can this object support five 300-line modules through independent research surfaces, without repetition, with the object remaining present in each? If yes, the project is viable; if no, the anchor needs revision.
- **Sacred-absence poetics is a rigorous frame, not a romantic one.** Matthiessen did not see the snow leopard and the book is not about the disappointment of not seeing — it is about the epistemology of sacred presence registered as sacred absence. A future Research project on any contemplative-landscape subject should reach for sacred-absence poetics first and ornate sacredness second, because absence-as-phenomenon is more specific than presence-as-beauty.
- **Acoustic-archaeology is an emerging method and the Research series can establish it.** The Ozette argument — that acoustic context must return with material artifacts — is a method claim, not just a Research observation. The method is consistent with NAGPRA's intent but extends NAGPRA into an acoustic register that the legislation did not originally contemplate. Future archaeological-heritage projects in the Research series should carry the acoustic-archaeology method forward and, where possible, engage originating tribes as collaborators in developing the method.
