# Retrospective — v1.49.79

## What Worked

1. **The acoustic layer adds a new dimension to PNW ecology.** COL mapped the Columbia Valley's geology, CAS mapped the Cascade Range's volcanology, ECO mapped living systems. RLS maps the soundscape — the frequency of wind through Douglas fir, the storm acoustics at Rialto Beach, the silence of Spider Meadow. Sound was always there; this study makes it visible.

2. **The Matthiessen connection in Module 05 provides philosophical depth.** The Snow Leopard's Crystal Monastery — where Matthiessen journeyed to see a sacred animal and found the sacred in its absence — provides a framework for understanding contemplative landscapes. The PNW has its own Crystal Monasteries: Spider Meadow, the Tongass, the San Juan Islands at dawn.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Landscapes have frequencies.** This is not metaphor — it is physics. Wind through conifer needles, waves against sea stacks, glacial meltwater over stone. Every landscape documented in the Research series has an acoustic signature. RLS is the first study to map it.

---

> *Every landscape has a frequency. The aeolian sculptures make it audible. The forests have always made it real. The question is whether you stop long enough to hear it.*
