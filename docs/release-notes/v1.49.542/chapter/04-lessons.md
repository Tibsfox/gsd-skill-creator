1. **The nine-degree E≥5 streak breaks.**
   Degrees 32-40 were nine consecutive degrees at E≥5, the longest sustained high-energy sequence in the engine. Degree 41 drops to E=4. The energy is not diminished -- Smith's gift was extraordinary -- but it operated through a different mechanism: interior, quiet, persistent, largely unheard by the mainstream. E=4 registers interior energy.

3. **The endurance record doubles.**
   Gemini 5 (degree 40) proved eight days. Gemini 7 (degree 41) proves fourteen. The endurance ceiling doubles in a single degree. The data shows cardiovascular deconditioning plateaus after 4-5 days -- a fourteen-day mission is not dramatically worse than an eight-day one.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #1996_

4. **Lovell's arc begins.**
   Jim Lovell makes his first spaceflight on Gemini 7. He will fly Gemini 12, command Apollo 8 (first lunar orbit), and command Apollo 13 (the famous "successful failure"). His Gemini 7 endurance experience -- 14 days in a cramped capsule, managing systems, conserving resources -- directly applied to the four-day lifeboat survival in Apollo 13's Lunar Module.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #1997_

5. **The G5C suit innovation.**
   The lightweight pressure suit -- 30% lighter, detachable helmet, partially removable -- was essential to the 14-day mission's medical success. NASA approved alternating unsuited periods: one astronaut could remove his suit while the other remained suited. A calculated risk that prevented skin breakdown, circulatory compromise, and psychological degradation.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #1998_

6. **The dual-mission model.**
   Gemini 7 as both endurance flight and rendezvous target eliminated the need for a separate Agena target vehicle -- the one that had exploded for the original Gemini 6. Using an already-orbiting crewed spacecraft was an improvisation that became the plan.

8. **Endurance plateaus before it breaks.**
   Gemini 7's cardiovascular deconditioning data showed that degradation was steepest in the first 4-5 days and plateaued thereafter. The plateau was the deliverable: it meant Apollo could be cleared for 8-12 day missions without additional endurance testing. The engine documents plateau-before-breaking as a general property of biological and operational systems under sustained stress, and future degrees will look for plateau signals as indicators of mission-clearance-readiness rather than continuing degradation as a sign of systems failing.

18. **The G5C suit engineering deserved more depth.**
   The lightweight G5C pressure suit -- approximately thirty percent lighter than the standard Gemini G4C, with a detachable helmet and partial-removal capability -- was the enabling technology for the fourteen-day mission's medical success. The release notes described the G5C at a summary level but did not trace the engineering decisions (zippered cover layer replacing the standard layered closure, reduced joint-ring count, simplified wrist disconnects) that made the weight reduction achievable. A future Pass 3 could anchor to the NASA Crew Systems Division documentation.

19. **The McDonnell Aircraft versus North American Aviation organizational contrast.**

The institutional-handoff story merits a dedicated paragraph.
