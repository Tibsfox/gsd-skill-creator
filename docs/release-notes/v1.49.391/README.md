## Summary

**Ayron Jones** arrives at E=7 -- the Seattle blues rock guitarist and vocalist who was born and raised in the Central District and represents the most direct living continuation of the Jimi Hendrix lineage in contemporary Seattle music. Jones is from the same neighborhood Hendrix grew up in. He attended the same cultural milieu. His guitar work is in the same tradition: blues-rooted, technically demanding, loud. The comparison is not gratuitous -- Jones himself has engaged with it directly in interviews, accepting the lineage without making it his entire identity. His 2021 album *Child of the State* on Big Noise / Atlantic Records is the statement of record: a mix of blues rock and contemporary production that reaches a mainstream audience without abandoning the guitar-forward, high-volume foundation.

Jones's career trajectory is significant for what it documents about Seattle: the Central District has been a historically Black neighborhood since the 1940s, when redlining and restrictive covenants confined Seattle's Black residents to that area; Jimi Hendrix grew up there in the 1950s; Ayron Jones grew up there in the 1990s and 2000s; the neighborhood has been substantially gentrified since then; the music that came from that geography spans five decades and represents a continuous cultural tradition that the city's development patterns have materially threatened. Hendrix at degree 245 and Jones at degree 250 are five degrees apart. The lineage is documented.

On the soundscape side, **Wind Old-Growth Fir** enters -- the **harmonic** sound of wind moving through a stand of old-growth Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), which is the dominant conifer of the interior Pacific Northwest west of the Cascades. Where the Sitka spruce wind entry (degree 249) documented the coastal forest, the old-growth fir entry documents the interior forest -- the forest type that dominated the Cascade foothills and the western slopes before commercial logging removed nearly all of it in the 20th century. Old-growth Douglas fir can reach heights of 300 feet and ages of 500-800 years. The wind sound through old-growth fir is a **harmonic** sound: the multiple canopy layers and the deep structure of a centuries-old stand produce a harmonic resonance that is distinctly different from second-growth forest, where the uniform age class and the absence of structural complexity produces a flatter, less harmonically rich wind sound.

The E=5 rating for wind old-growth fir is one step above wind Sitka spruce (E=4) because old-growth fir stands are typically taller, more structurally complex, and capable of producing a fuller harmonic range. The old-growth fir wind sound is one of the Pacific Northwest's defining acoustic signatures -- and it is now one of the rarest, because less than 10% of the original old-growth Douglas fir forest in Washington and Oregon remains.
