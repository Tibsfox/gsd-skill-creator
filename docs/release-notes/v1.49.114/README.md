# v1.49.114 "The Television Era"

**Released:** 2026-03-28
**Code:** TVH
**Series:** PNW Research Series (#114 of 167)

## Summary

A comprehensive deep-research project tracing the full arc of American broadcast television from Philo Farnsworth's 1920s image dissector through PNW station history, FCC licensing law, the cable/MTV explosion, and the ATSC 3.0 streamer bridge. The project grounds the entire Broadcasting cluster in regulatory and technical reality, connecting spectrum allocation and 47 CFR Parts 73/74 to the practical question of how a creator with a 50-watt UHF transmitter can serve 20,000 households today.

### Research Modules

1. **M-A: Television History** — Year-by-year annotated timeline of US broadcast TV from Farnsworth/Zworykin patents through ATSC 3.0
2. **M-B: PNW Broadcasting Heritage** — Station-by-station history: KING-TV (1948), KOMO's 1929 experiment, KCTS educational TV, Portland/Spokane markets
3. **M-C: Technical Standards** — Mechanical scanning through NTSC, VHF/UHF, all-channel mandate, ATSC digital transition, 5G Broadcast
4. **M-D: FCC Licensing & Legal** — Communications Act of 1934, 47 CFR Parts 73/74, station classes, LPTV protection, Class A conversion
5. **M-E: Cable Era & Streaming** — CATV origins through HBO satellite, CNN/ESPN/MTV, TCI consolidation, cord-cutting acceleration
6. **M-F: Streamer Bridge** — PEG access television, LPTV community origination, ATSC 3.0 Run3TV platform, operational model for internet-to-broadcast convergence

## Retrospective

### What Worked
- The three-track parallel structure (History+Tech, PNW+FCC, Culture+Bridge) cleanly separates technical, regulatory, and cultural dimensions
- Fleet activation profile with 17-20 roles matches the breadth of a 100+ year domain spanning engineering, law, and culture
- The Streamer Bridge module synthesizes everything into a concrete operational pathway, preventing the research from being purely historical

### What Could Be Better
- International broadcast standards (PAL, SECAM, DVB-T2) are mentioned but not deeply covered — a future extension could map the global standards landscape
- The pre-electronic era (mechanical television experiments before Farnsworth) could warrant its own focused treatment

## Lessons Learned

- FCC regulatory structure has not kept pace with convergence — LPTV licenses designed for 1982 technology are now the cheapest path to IP-native broadcast via ATSC 3.0
- The 1929 KOMO experiment in Seattle predates the commonly cited "birth of television" narrative by a decade, illustrating how regional innovation often precedes national recognition
- The signal always finds its way home: the arc from antenna to cable to streaming to ATSC 3.0 is fundamentally circular, returning broadcast to over-the-air delivery with internet-native capabilities
