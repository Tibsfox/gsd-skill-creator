## Summary

**Gas Huffer** returns at E=7 -- the Seattle garage rock band making their second appearance in the engine, arriving here in the geophysics arc after their earlier entry in the main sequence. Gas Huffer formed in Seattle in 1989 around Tom Price (guitar), Matt Wright (vocals), Joe Newton (bass), and Pete Lentini (drums) -- a lineup that defined a particular strand of Pacific Northwest garage rock: fast, irreverent, technically accomplished under the noise, and possessed of a sense of humor that distinguished them from the earnest alternative bands surrounding them. Their catalog on Empty Records and then Epitaph documents a band that played garage rock at a time when garage rock was not commercially fashionable, and played it with conviction and craft. Their return at degree 262 acknowledges both the durability of their contribution to the Pacific Northwest rock catalog and the specific energy profile of their work -- E=7 in the return context reflects the sustained density of a catalog that, heard cumulatively, sits at the high end of what the garage rock format can produce. A return appearance in the engine is unusual; it is assigned here because the geophysics arc needed a specific energy profile at degree 262, and because Gas Huffer's sound -- the propulsive, relentless quality of their best work -- is the correct acoustic accompaniment to the event the SPS track documents at this degree.

The return context also allows the engine to acknowledge what has accumulated since their first appearance: the full Gas Huffer catalog in retrospect, the context of where garage rock from Seattle ended up in the years after their peak, and the specific contribution of Tom Price -- whose work as a guitarist across multiple Seattle bands (U-Men, Gas Huffer, plus later collaborations) documents a career of unusual consistency in the Pacific Northwest rock record.

On the soundscape side, **Lahar Simulation Osceola** arrives at E=7 -- the ground vibration profile produced by a lahar: a volcanic mudflow, a mixture of volcanic debris, water, and rock moving through a river valley at speeds of up to 40 miles per hour. The Osceola Mudflow is the reference event: approximately 5,600 years ago, a massive lahar from Mount Rainier traveled 70 miles down the Puyallup River valley, reaching what is now Puget Sound and depositing volcanic debris across a broad alluvial fan that underlies much of the modern Puyallup, Orting, and Fife area. The Osceola Mudflow was not a small event -- it was one of the largest lahars in the Holocene record of the Cascades, and the land it created is now home to hundreds of thousands of people who live in the Puyallup River valley flood hazard zone. Lahar simulation ground vibration -- the modeled acoustic signature of a similar event, derived from seismic records of historic lahars on other Cascade volcanoes -- arrives at E=7 because a lahar is not subtle: it produces sustained, broad-spectrum ground vibration that can be heard and felt at considerable distances. E=7 is the correct assignment: not the explosive instantaneous intensity of a rockslide or an earthquake, but the sustained, massive, rolling vibration of an enormous mass of material in continuous rapid motion.

The pairing at degree 262 is the most geologically urgent in the engine's history. The Osceola Mudflow buried the area that is now Orting, Washington -- a city of approximately 8,000 people that sits directly in the Puyallup River lahar inundation zone, 35 miles downstream from Mount Rainier. The Puyallup River valley lahar warning system includes sirens and evacuation protocols, and the question of whether the population of the valley can be evacuated in the window a future lahar would provide is an active and ongoing public safety concern. The engine does not document this hazard to generate alarm; it documents it because the acoustic profile of this landscape -- the ground vibration of a lahar simulation, the infrasonic tremor of the volcano producing the lahars -- is as real as the bird songs and waterfalls the engine has documented, and it deserves the same honest treatment.
