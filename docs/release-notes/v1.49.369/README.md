## Summary

**Gas Huffer** arrives at E=7 -- the band that brought the Fremont neighborhood into the Seattle 360 catalog for the first time, and brought it in loudly. Tom Price (guitar), Matt Wright (bass), Joe Newton (drums), and Pete Demela (vocals) made punk/grunge on Epitaph Records and later Estrus Records through the 1990s, operating at a volume and velocity that owed as much to the Ramones and the Stooges as to anything Seattle had produced internally. Gas Huffer is the **first Fremont band** in the engine -- Fremont, the self-proclaimed "Center of the Universe," the neighborhood north of Lake Union where the Fremont Troll lives under the Aurora Bridge, where the Lenin statue stands at the center of a roundabout, where the summer solstice parade involves naked cyclists decorated in paint and the neighborhood absorbs this as ordinary Tuesday-level news. Fremont's band scene was always slightly different from Capitol Hill's -- more working-class, more punk-aligned, less invested in the art-rock and alternative-rock aesthetics that dominated the Hill -- and Gas Huffer is that difference embodied. At E=7, the band is the highest-rated entry in the raptor arc's musical track alongside Temple of the Dog (degree 225), which is fitting: Gas Huffer is the closer, the band that ends the arc with energy rather than reflection. Punk/grunge at E=7 is a statement -- a refusal of the introspection that characterized many of the preceding entries (Brad, Truly, Love Battery, Mad Season). The arc that opened with Love Battery's psychedelic dissolution now closes with Gas Huffer's full-throttle attack. On the species side, the **Red-tailed Hawk** registers at E=6 and delivers the **RAPTOR ARC'S CLOSING STATEMENT** -- eight consecutive raptor degrees, and the arc ends with the bird whose voice has been misassigned for a century. *Buteo jamaicensis* is the most common and most widely distributed hawk in North America, found in every habitat from arctic tundra to tropical rainforest, from downtown Seattle to the Palouse wheat fields. It is the bird that farmers see on fence posts, that highway drivers see on utility poles, that urban residents see circling above parking lots -- the hawk that most people have looked at most often without knowing its name. And its vocalization -- the raspy, descending *kreeee* that every Hollywood sound designer has assigned to the Bald Eagle -- is the most consequential audio misattribution in American natural history. Every movie eagle, every news broadcast cutaway to a soaring raptor, every patriotic advertisement featuring the national bird's cry: the sound is the Red-tailed Hawk. The Bald Eagle's actual voice (degrees 221-222: thin *kee-kee-kee*, piercing calls) is the real voice of the national symbol. The Red-tailed Hawk is the voice of the mythology. The **HOLLYWOOD CALL ARC** is complete: degree 221 planted the correction, degree 228 delivers the source. The engine closes the raptor arc with the most common hawk in North America, the most misrepresented bird in American culture, and the most important acoustic correction it has made.
