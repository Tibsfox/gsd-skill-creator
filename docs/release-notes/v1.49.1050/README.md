## Mission Overview

ROSAT (Röntgensatellit) is the obs#11 continuation of the X-ray-astronomy axis and the next-adjacent-sustain of the survey/catalog form — the German-led (DLR/MPE, PI Joachim Trümper) X-ray and extreme-ultraviolet astronomy mission that performed the first all-sky X-ray survey with imaging/focusing optics, surveying ~96% of the sky with a Wolter Type I telescope feeding a position-sensitive proportional counter and simultaneously conducting the first all-sky EUV survey. Launched 1 June 1990 on a NASA Delta II (6920-10) from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station, SLC-17A, Florida, into a near-circular ~580-kilometer, ~52.9-degree low-Earth orbit (NSSDCA / COSPAR ID 1990-049A; SATCAT 20638; spacecraft mass ~2426 kg; period ~96 minutes). It carried three instruments: the PSPC (Position Sensitive Proportional Counter, MPE, Germany, ~2° FOV, ~25 arcsec resolution, 0.1–2.4 keV), the HRI (High Resolution Imager, NASA/SAO, ~5 arcsec PSF, 0.1–2.4 keV — the explicit NASA hardware contribution), and the WFC (Wide Field Camera, UK/University of Leicester consortium, S1 ~60–140 Å, S2 ~110–200 Å). The XRT Wolter Type I optics used four nested paraboloid-hyperboloid mirror shells built by Carl Zeiss (Germany), ~84 cm aperture, ~240 cm focal length, effective PSF ~5 arcsec in flight. ROSAT was 3-axis stabilized (NOT spin-stabilized — it could both scan and point, unlike HEAO-1's spin-only mode). The spacecraft was built by Dornier (German aerospace), not TRW. The survey phase (RASS, 30 July 1990 – 25 January 1991) delivered the 1RXS catalog of ~124,730 sources (RASS-BSC 18,811 + FSC 105,924) and simultaneously the WFC EUV survey (BSC 383 / 2RE 479 sources). Pointed observations ran 1991 – February 1999 (~9,000+ targets). Science operations ended 12 February 1999; reentry 23 October 2011 over the Bay of Bengal. ROSAT SUSTAINS the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#11 (axis-rotation #35 sustains).

## Structural firsts

**ROSAT-FIRST-INSTANCE:** ROSAT (Röntgensatellit) enters the catalog as the German-led (DLR/MPE, PI Joachim Trümper) X-ray and extreme-ultraviolet astronomy mission — the first instance, the obs#1 first — sustaining the X-ray-astronomy axis at obs#11. Three-national architecture: German MPE providing the X-ray telescope (Wolter Type I, Carl Zeiss mirrors, ~84 cm aperture); NASA providing the HRI focal-plane instrument (SAO, microchannel plate, ~5 arcsec PSF) and the Delta II launch; and the UK University of Leicester consortium providing the WFC (EUV, S1/S2 bands). ROSAT is NOT a NASA mission; it is German-led with NASA and UK as contributors. It performed the RASS (30 July 1990 – 25 January 1991, first imaging all-sky X-ray survey), the WFC EUV all-sky survey (first EUV all-sky survey, simultaneously), and then ~9,000+ pointed observations. Science operations ended February 1999; reentry 23 October 2011.

**IMAGING-ALL-SKY-X-RAY-SURVEY-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The ROSAT All-Sky Survey (RASS) was the FIRST all-sky X-ray survey conducted with a focusing/imaging (Wolter Type I) telescope, producing the first imaged, position-accurate all-sky X-ray source catalog. Three-way primacy partition — Uhuru (obs#9, 1970): first all-sky survey (non-imaging collimated counters); Einstein/HEAO-2 (obs#8, 1978): first imaging X-ray telescope in space (pointed-only, ~5,000 observations, did NOT survey the full sky); ROSAT (obs#11, 1990): first all-sky survey performed WITH focusing/imaging Wolter optics — three distinct and complementary firsts. The published 1RXS catalog lists ~124,730 sources (RASS-BSC 18,811 + FSC 105,924); the 2RXS reprocessed catalog (Boller et al. 2016, DOI: 10.1051/0004-6361/201525648) = ~135,118; headline ">150,000" = raw detections, NOT the published total. Depth: ~150× HEAO-1's 842-source A-1 catalog, ~20× all prior satellites combined.

**EXTREME-ULTRAVIOLET-ALL-SKY-SURVEY-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The WFC (Wide Field Camera, UK/University of Leicester / SERC consortium, PI Ken Pounds) performed the FIRST all-sky survey in the extreme ultraviolet, running simultaneously with the RASS (30 July 1990 – 25 January 1991, ~96% sky coverage) in two bands: S1 ~60–140 Å (~90–206 eV) and S2 ~110–200 Å (~62–110 eV). The original published catalog is the WFC Bright Source Catalogue (BSC, 383 sources — Pounds et al. 1993, MNRAS 260, 77; DOI: 10.1093/mnras/260.1.77); the 1995 reprocessed 2RE catalogue has 479 sources (Pye et al. 1995, MNRAS 274, 1165). Two qualifiers: Apollo-Soyuz EUVT (1975) detected only ~4 EUV sources in pointed observations — NOT a survey, does NOT pre-empt the first-survey claim; NASA's EUVE (June 1992) = the second EUV all-sky survey. EUV credit = UK WFC team, NOT NASA.

**COMETARY-X-RAY-EMISSION-DISCOVERY-FIRST-INSTANCE:** ROSAT made the first recognized/reported detection of X-ray and EUV emission from a comet: Comet C/1996 B2 (Hyakutake), 1996 March 26–28, observed with the ROSAT HRI (simultaneous EUV imaging by the WFC). Discovery paper: Lisse et al. 1996, Science 274(5285), 205–209 (DOI: 10.1126/science.274.5285.205). X-ray brightness was ~100× brighter than the most optimistic predictions; emission formed a crescent-shaped region on the sunward side of the nucleus. No comet had been detected in X-rays by Einstein, EXOSAT, or any prior mission. The SWCX mechanism was explained by Cravens (1997, GRL; DOI: 10.1029/96GL03780). Qualifier: RASS (1990-91) physically recorded X-ray photons from four fainter comets that were only recognized retrospectively after Hyakutake (Dennerl et al. 1997, Science 277, 1625; DOI: 10.1126/science.277.5332.1625) — all archival photons are still ROSAT's; "first recognized/reported" = Hyakutake 1996.

**THERMALLY-EMITTING-RADIO-QUIET-ISOLATED-NEUTRON-STAR-CLASS-DISCOVERY-FIRST-INSTANCE:** ROSAT discovered all seven members of the X-ray Dim Isolated Neutron Stars (XDINS), the "Magnificent Seven" — thermally-emitting, radio-quiet isolated neutron stars. Prototype: RX J1856.5-3754, identified in archival 1992 ROSAT PSPC data by Walter, Wolk & Neuhäuser 1996, Nature 379, 233–235 (DOI: 10.1038/379233a0). Class: young (~1–4 Myr), nearby (<500 pc), purely thermally emitting (~57 eV blackbody for RX J1856), radio-QUIET, no magnetospheric non-thermal component; all seven are ROSAT discoveries. Critical qualifier: Einstein Observatory (1978–1981) detected soft X-ray emission from isolated RADIO-LOUD rotation-powered pulsars (B0656+14, B1055-52) — so "isolated neutron stars in X-rays" generically predates ROSAT; the anchor scopes precisely to the thermally-emitting/radio-quiet/XDINS class.

**X-RAY-ASTRONOMY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION:** The X-ray-astronomy axis Chandra opened at obs#1, NuSTAR extended at obs#2, RXTE/IXPE/NICER/Swift/XRISM continued at obs#3–7, Einstein continued as the focusing-imaging origin at obs#8, Uhuru continued as the survey/catalog origin at obs#9, and HEAO-1 sustained as the deeper non-imaging survey-sustain at obs#10 now sustains as a next-adjacent-sustain observation at obs#11 (axis-rotation #35 sustains) via ROSAT — the first imaging all-sky survey, elevating the survey/catalog branch from non-imaging collimated counters to imaging Wolter optics. Continuation by observable form, not by chronology: ROSAT (1990) sustains the survey/catalog branch Uhuru (1970) originated and HEAO-1 (1977) deepened, advancing it to the imaging tier.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An imaging all-sky survey is a distinct observable form from a pointed imaging telescope.** Einstein/HEAO-2 (obs#8) was the first imaging X-ray telescope — it observed ~5,000 pointed targets with arcsecond-class optics. ROSAT (obs#11) added the survey dimension: scanning the whole sky with those same Wolter Type I optics. The two firsts are complementary and must never be conflated.
2. **The three-way partition (Uhuru/Einstein/ROSAT) must be stated explicitly in every survey-first claim.** Stating "first all-sky survey" (without "with imaging optics") incorrectly re-stamps Uhuru's first. Stating "first imaging telescope" incorrectly re-stamps Einstein's first. The correct scope for ROSAT is always "first all-sky survey WITH imaging optics."
3. **German-led framing is an accuracy requirement, not a qualifier.** ROSAT is not a NASA-with-German-instruments mission; it is a German-led mission with specific NASA and UK contributions. Every identification of ROSAT must open with "German-led (DLR/MPE)" before naming the contributors.
4. **PSF values require the electrostatic-shield-halo context.** The "~2 arcsec" figure circulates in the literature as a mirror-core specification; the ~5 arcsec effective PSF is the flight value. Cite ~5 arcsec for any in-flight imaging performance claim.
5. **EUV catalog version disambiguation prevents a recurring error.** 383 (1993 BSC) and 479 (1995 2RE) are two distinct catalog editions of the same WFC survey. Citing "479 sources" alone loses the original-survey publication. Cite both with dates at every occurrence.
6. **XDINS anchor scope must encode radio-quiet.** "Isolated neutron stars in X-rays" pre-dates ROSAT by a decade (Einstein radio-loud pulsars). The correct scope is "thermally-emitting, radio-quiet isolated neutron stars (XDINS / Magnificent Seven)."
7. **Soft-CXB and hard-CXB resolution belong to different missions.** ROSAT resolved ~70–80% of the soft (0.5–2 keV) CXB. Chandra resolved the hard CXB. Einstein holds the first-instance for CXB source resolution. Conflating the three is a recurring error class.
8. **A continuation keeps predecessors' shared vocabulary and references them by name.** The X-ray-astronomy vocabulary (Wolter optics, Scorpius X-1, Giacconi and Rossi, neutron star, AGN, galaxy cluster, SNR, intracluster medium, keV, effective area, etc.) is correct and kept. Chandra through HEAO-1 are referenced as the form complements. Only predecessor-specific hardware and anchors stay with their missions.
9. **Ops-end / reentry separation is load-bearing for ROSAT.** Science closed February 1999; reentry occurred 23 October 2011 — a 12-year gap. The two events must never be collapsed into one.
10. **Clone source is HEAO-1 (non-imaging); ROSAT is imaging.** The most structural replacement in the rewrite is removing ALL "non-imaging," "collimated counters," "spin-stabilized," "TRW," "MSFC," "Atlas-Centaur/LC-36B," "A-1/A-2/A-3/A-4 instruments," and "842 sources" language and replacing with ROSAT's imaging Wolter optics, 3-axis stabilization, Dornier/DLR/MPE, Delta II/SLC-17A, PSPC/HRI/WFC instruments, and 1RXS ~124,730 sources.

## Surprises

- **A German-led mission — not a NASA mission — conducted the definitive early imaging X-ray survey.** ROSAT was funded and managed by Germany (DLR/DARA + MPE), with NASA contributing the HRI and Delta II and the UK contributing the WFC — a tri-national architecture that produced the deepest soft X-ray all-sky catalog of its era: ~124,730 sources vs HEAO-1's 842, a factor of ~150 improvement, through the addition of focusing optics to the survey strategy.
- **The first EUV all-sky survey came as a bonus instrument on the X-ray telescope.** The WFC was co-aligned with the XRT and surveyed the same ~96% of the sky in the extreme ultraviolet simultaneously with the RASS — two all-sky surveys in two wavebands running in parallel on one spacecraft, opening the EUV spectral window almost simultaneously with the X-ray survey.
- **Comets emit X-rays at levels no model had predicted.** Comet C/1996 B2 (Hyakutake) was ~100× brighter in X-rays than the most optimistic pre-observation calculations, and formed a clear crescent-shaped emission region on the sunward side — pointing directly to the solar-wind charge-exchange mechanism (SWCX) now recognized as a major contributor to the soft X-ray sky background.
- **A whole new class of neutron stars waited hidden in archival ROSAT data.** RX J1856.5-3754 was detected in 1992 PSPC data but identified as a thermally-emitting, radio-quiet isolated neutron star only in 1996 — pointing to a class of six more that had also been captured in ROSAT observations, collectively the "Magnificent Seven" XDINS, whose thermal emission reveals neutron-star cooling physics unavailable to radio or optical telescopes.
- **A hawk that surveys entire territories with the sharpest eyes in the sky.** The Red-tailed Hawk (*Buteo jamaicensis*) soars above its entire territory on thermals, two foveae per eye giving it both monocular field coverage and binocular precision focus at distance — a survey-and-focus architecture that mirrors ROSAT's all-sky survey with imaging optics: wide sweep, sharp positional precision, and the ability to target individual sources for deep follow-up.
- **A lichen built from three partners surveys its whole substrate through integration.** The Lungwort Lichen (*Lobaria pulmonaria*) is not a plant and not a single organism — it is a tripartite symbiosis (fungus + green alga + cyanobacterium) whose lobed surface maps and harvests moisture and light from every part of the tree it colonizes, mirroring the way ROSAT's three-nation instrument suite (German XRT + NASA HRI + UK WFC) harvested the whole X-ray and EUV sky through integrated coverage.

### What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived survey data.** The imaging-survey shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual 1RXS catalog source positions on the celestial sphere. A future revision could render the real ~124,730-source RASS positions (publicly available from HEASARC) for a higher-fidelity view of the imaged sky.
- **The ROSAT diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the 3-axis stabilized spacecraft with the XRT baffle tube, solar panels, and star-tracker assembly.
- **The WFC EUV survey is described but not computed.** The S1 / S2 EUV band configuration, the transmission curves, and the survey strategy are described but not rendered as a computed sky map; a future ship could integrate the published ROSAT WFC BSC positions into the EUV shader mode for a historically accurate EUV sky view.
- **The soft CXB resolved fraction is described rather than demonstrated.** The ~70–80% soft-band CXB resolution is cited (Hasinger et al. 1998) but not demonstrated as a model; a future ship could render the unresolved-to-resolved CXB fraction as a shader animation.

## Dedication

v1.233 ROSAT is dedicated to the people who built and flew the Röntgensatellit — the observatory that looked at the soft X-ray sky and saw it for the first time in focused images: Joachim Trümper at the Max-Planck-Institut für extraterrestrische Physik, who served as principal investigator and drove the mission from concept through operations; the PSPC team at MPE, who built the position-sensitive proportional counter that turned the RASS into a catalog of positions rather than count rates; Günter Hasinger and his collaborators, who resolved the soft cosmic X-ray background into the discrete sources that had always been there; Stephen Murray and the SAO team, who built the HRI that delivered the finest ROSAT images; Ken Pounds and the University of Leicester consortium, who built the Wide Field Camera and opened the extreme-ultraviolet sky simultaneously; and Riccardo Giacconi and Bruno Rossi, who founded the field with the 1962 rocket. From a focused telescope scanning the whole sky, the X-ray universe finally came into focus — 124,730 sources at their true positions, the sky mapped in soft light.
