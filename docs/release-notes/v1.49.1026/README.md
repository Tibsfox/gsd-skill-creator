Gravity Probe B is a NASA mission managed by NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center, with the experiment designed and built by Stanford University and Lockheed Martin; the principal investigator was Francis Everitt of Stanford. The spacecraft reached space on 20 April 2004 aboard a Boeing Delta II 7920-10C rocket from Vandenberg Air Force Base, California, into a near-circular polar orbit at about 640 kilometers altitude, and returned science data through its cryogen lifetime until the liquid-helium dewar was exhausted in 2005; the final analysis was published in 2011. Its COSPAR/NSSDC ID is 2004-014A. The experiment carried four London-moment gyroscopes — fused-quartz spheres each coated with a thin superconducting niobium film, each about the size of a ping-pong ball and machined round to within about forty atomic layers, the most perfectly spherical objects ever manufactured — cooled near absolute zero in a superfluid-liquid-helium dewar, with SQUID magnetometers reading the spin-axis orientation of each rotor from the faint London-moment magnetic field of the spinning superconductor, and a reference telescope locked on the guide star IM Pegasi to give a fixed celestial reference. The spacecraft flew drag-free, controlling its position around one gyroscope used as a free-floating proof mass so that only gravity acted on the experiment. A quiet, successful precision-physics experiment, Gravity Probe B confirmed both predictions of general relativity.

The signature of Gravity Probe B at this degree is the direct orbital test of general relativity — the measured geodetic effect and frame-dragging — opening the FUNDAMENTAL-PHYSICS axis at obs#1. The experiment measured the geodetic drift at about 6,602 milliarcseconds per year (general relativity predicts about 6,606), confirming it to better than one percent, and the frame-dragging drift at about 37 milliarcseconds per year (general relativity predicts about 39), consistent with the theory, and from those two angles turned Einstein's geometry of gravity into a measured laboratory result in orbit.

The geodetic effect turned spacetime curvature into a measured angle. The mass of the Earth curves the local geometry of spacetime, and that curvature tilts the spin axis of an orbiting gyroscope by a tiny, steady amount in the orbital plane. General relativity predicts about 6,606 milliarcseconds per year; Gravity Probe B measured a geodetic drift of about 6,602 milliarcseconds per year (−6,601.8 ± 18.3), confirming the prediction to better than one percent.

Frame-dragging — the Lense-Thirring effect — read the faint sideways twist of spacetime. The rotation of the Earth drags the local geometry of spacetime around with it, pulling the gyroscope spin axis sideways, perpendicular to the geodetic tilt, by a far smaller amount. General relativity predicts about 39 milliarcseconds per year; Gravity Probe B measured a frame-dragging drift of about 37 milliarcseconds per year (−37.2 ± 7.2), consistent with the prediction.

## Dedication

v1.217 Gravity Probe B is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's orbital test of Einstein's general relativity, the experiment that held four perfect gyroscopes steady in space and measured the faint tip the Earth's mass and rotation give to local spacetime: the Gravity Probe B team at NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center, Stanford University, and Lockheed Martin, who designed, built, and operated the mission; principal investigator Francis Everitt, who carried the experiment across decades to its confirmed result; the founders Leonard Schiff, George Pugh, and William Fairbank, who proposed the gyroscope test around 1960; the gyroscope and SQUID-readout engineers, who made the most perfectly spherical rotors ever built and read their spin axes from the London moment; the cryogenics team, who held the instrument near absolute zero in superfluid helium; the drag-free-control team, who flew the spacecraft around a free-floating proof mass so only gravity acted on it; and the guide-star astronomy team, who locked the telescope on IM Pegasi. Gravity Probe B turned a half-century quest into a measured confirmation of the geodetic effect and frame-dragging.
