The release is the first Research mega-wave but not the last — forty-three additional projects and six extensions are queued in the Mega-Batch backlog behind it.

**Pike Place Market (PPM) is the anchor of the wave and documents 119 years of a single building continuously doing the same social-infrastructure job.** The PPM project ships five Research modules totaling 1,599 research lines — Module 01 (Founding Era, 293 lines) walks the 1907 origin story as a municipal response to commission-merchant wholesale fraud that was doubling the price of produce for Seattle households; Module 02 (Japanese-American Legacy, 312 lines) documents the 80% of pre-war farm stalls run by Japanese-American families, the 1942 Executive Order 9066 internment that emptied the Market overnight, and the post-war partial return; Module 03 (Preservation Movement, 319 lines) walks Victor Steinbrueck's 1971 ballot initiative that defeated urban-renewal demolition plans and established the Pike Place Market Historical District; Module 04 (Shops and Merchants, 319 lines) catalogs the contemporary vendor ecology — DayStall craftspeople, highstall farmers, fish-throwers, cheesemongers, florists, and the Low-Income Housing Authority sites layered above the shopping levels; Module 05 (Architecture and Social Mission, 356 lines) walks the specific architectural features (the seven-block arcade, the hillclimb, the Main and North arcades) and the PDA (Pike Place Market Preservation and Development Authority) social-mission charter that binds low-income housing, food-access, and vendor incubation into the building's operating model. Eighty-two-plus sources verify the claims. The project is the first place-based Research entry to explicitly foreground a building as an active institution rather than as setting, and every subsequent PNW-places Research project inherits that framing.

**Central District (CDS) is the musical-lineage anchor of the wave and merges two complementary source packs into the wave's richest single project.** The CDS project ships six Research modules totaling 2,151 research lines — Module 01 (Neighborhood Foundation, 351 lines) walks the pre-1900 farmland origin, the 1890s streetcar suburb phase, the post-1900 African-American in-migration that concentrated in the CD after redlining locked most of Seattle off-limits, and the Jackson Street jazz corridor that by the 1940s ran from 12th and Jackson to 23rd and Madison; Module 02 (Musical Lineage, 341 lines) walks the jazz-to-R&B-to-hip-hop lineage through Quincy Jones (Garfield High), Ernestine Anderson, Ray Charles (arriving from Florida at age 16), Sir Mix-A-Lot, and the Nastymix Records independent-label arc; Module 03 (Cassette Economy, 368 lines) documents the DIY 1980s and 1990s tape-duplication economy that let CD hip-hop reach regional and then national distribution before digital platforms existed; Module 04 (Radio Ecosystem, 352 lines) walks the KRIZ / KZIZ / KZOK-to-KUBE 93.3 progression that carried CD music through commercial radio with varying degrees of local fidelity; Module 05 (Community Spaces and Artists, 360 lines) catalogs the churches, the Langston Hughes Performing Arts Institute, the Northwest African American Museum, and the small-venue circuit that gave touring artists a 2026-active reason to stop in Seattle; Module 06 (Displacement and Preservation, 379 lines) walks the gentrification arc from a 70%+ African-American neighborhood in 1970 to under 20% in 2020, the Africatown Community Land Trust response, and the policy tools (Community Preference, TOPA, Landmark designation) deployed to slow displacement. CDS merges the `cd_mission.tex` (1,023 lines) and `central-district-mission.tex` (1,084 lines) source packs into one project — the wave's first two-source-merge and a pattern the Research series adopts going forward when a subject has two complementary source perspectives worth preserving together.

**Seattle Street Music (SSM) is the wave's street-culture anchor and documents the First Amendment scaffolding that keeps busking legal.** The SSM project ships five Research modules totaling 1,844 research lines — Module 01 (Historical Roots, 354 lines) walks the pre-Prohibition saloon-district music scene and the mid-twentieth-century transit-station informal performance tradition; Module 02 (Legendary Figures, 367 lines) documents Artis the Spoonman (whose 1980s-2000s Pike Place performances made him a subject of Soundgarden's 1994 "Spoonman" and a practical case study in what long-term busking looks like at peak), the Pike Place fish-throwers, and the Seattle Symphony Orchestra musicians who sometimes busk between seasons; Module 03 (Legal Governance, 372 lines) walks the 1974 Seattle busking-legalization ordinance, the 1997 Berger v. City of Seattle Ninth Circuit ruling that struck down overbroad time/place/manner restrictions, and the subsequent permit regime at the Pike Place Market Preservation and Development Authority; Module 04 (Pavement to Stage, 372 lines) walks the busking-to-venue-to-recording-artist career arc through specific Seattle-area musicians; Module 05 (Present-Day Ecosystem, 379 lines) documents the 2020-2024 COVID impact on street performance, the venue-circuit collapse and partial recovery, and the current busking ecosystem at Pike Place, the Seattle Center Monorail station, Pioneer Square First Thursday, and the Ballard Sunday Market. SSM is the first PNW Research project to foreground legal scaffolding as a Research subject — the project's premise is that Berger v. City of Seattle is load-bearing infrastructure for the cultural scene and that the scene would be very different in 2026 without it.

**Bellingham Market (BHM) anchors the northern end of the corridor and documents cross-border regional economics that Seattle-centric reporting usually misses.** The BHM project ships five Research modules totaling 2,022 research lines — Module 01 (Housing and Real Estate, 401 lines) walks Whatcom County's 2026 housing market, the WWU (Western Washington University) student-population effect on rental supply, and the cross-border-buyer dynamic that Canadian parity shifts have driven through Bellingham residential real estate since the 1990s; Module 02 (Economic Base and Employers, 402 lines) catalogs PeaceHealth St. Joseph Medical Center, WWU, Bellingham Cold Storage, Haggen/Albertsons, and the Nooksack River agricultural base as the five-pillar regional economy; Module 03 (Tribal Economic Sovereignty, 405 lines) documents the Lummi Nation and Nooksack Indian Tribe economic enterprises, the 1974 Boldt Decision that secured treaty fishing rights for the region's tribes, and the post-Boldt reconstruction of tribal fisheries, gaming, and retail enterprises; Module 04 (Higher Education and Workforce, 403 lines) walks the WWU-Bellingham Technical College pipeline that keeps the regional workforce tied to the municipal economy rather than leaking to Seattle or Vancouver; Module 05 (Regional Position and Cross-Border, 411 lines) walks the I-5 corridor dynamics, the Peace Arch border crossing economics, and the US-Canada regulatory arbitrage that shapes everything from gas prices to Costco membership patterns. BHM is the wave's most economics-heavy project and the first Research entry to treat a mid-sized PNW city as a regional economic node rather than as a Seattle satellite.

**Silicon Forest Corridor (SFC) is the wave's longest Research project and maps the Everett-Bellevue-Redmond-Portland tech corridor from its timber-industry substrate.** The SFC project ships six Research modules totaling 2,483 research lines — Module 01 (Sno-Isle IOOF Civic Layer, 400 lines) documents the Independent Order of Odd Fellows civic halls that seeded Snohomish and Island County social infrastructure and now host co-op creative spaces; Module 02 (Hwy 99 Creative Commons, 395 lines) walks the post-industrial light-industrial corridor along old Highway 99 that has partially transitioned into artist studios, maker spaces, and micro-manufacturing; Module 03 (Paine Field JIT Zone, 406 lines) documents the Boeing Everett factory's just-in-time supplier ecosystem across Mukilteo, Everett, and the greater Paine Field area, the 777X production line, and the Pacific Assembly Circuit's regional-supplier dependency structure; Module 04 (Ferry Mesh Regional Coordination, 411 lines) walks the Washington State Ferries system as economic infrastructure — specifically the Mukilteo-Clinton, Edmonds-Kingston, and Seattle-Bainbridge runs that function as commute corridors for the tech workforce; Module 05 (Global Co-op Knowledge Network, 388 lines) documents the Sno-Isle TECH Skills Center, the WSU North Puget Sound program, and the Edmonds College STEM pipeline that seeds the regional labor pool; Module 06 (Pacific Assembly Circuit, 483 lines) walks the full supply-chain architecture from timber to composite materials to electronics assembly that underlies the corridor's apparent "tech" identity. SFC's framing — Silicon Forest as timber-industry-turned-tech-corridor — is the project's single most load-bearing editorial decision and the piece that gives SFC durable reach into later Research work.

**Millwork Co-op (MWC) is the wave's deepest project at 4,322 research lines and ships as a full GSD-ready mission package rather than as a standalone paper.** The MWC project ships seven Research modules plus a complete eight-component mission-pack (totaling 25 files and 5,071 lines) — Module 01 (Cooperative Economics, 460 lines) walks RCW 23.86 (Washington cooperative law) and the specific statutory mechanisms that make worker-ownership cooperatives viable; Module 02 (Timber Heritage, 381 lines) documents the Weyerhaeuser-and-Synsor millwork lineage that anchors the project's subject matter; Module 03 (Millwork Craftsmanship, 445 lines) walks AWI (Architectural Woodwork Institute) Premium and Custom grade specifications, hand-crafted-versus-CNC production tradeoffs, and joinery-quality metrics; Module 04 (Supply Chain Network, 395 lines) maps the FSC-certified timber supply chain from PNW forests through regional mills to finish-millwork shops; Module 05 (Workforce Development, 430 lines) walks the apprenticeship-into-journeyman-into-partner pipeline that a cooperative workforce requires; Module 06 (Client Pipeline, 350 lines) documents the commercial-residential-institutional client mix that cooperative millwork shops balance; Module 07 (The Joinery Report, 404 lines) is a case-study deep dive into a single PDX cooperative millwork shop as an applied example of the preceding six modules. The mission-pack component adds a vision doc, a research reference, a milestone spec, a wave execution plan, a test plan, a README, and seven component specs — shipped in markdown format rather than LaTeX and processing cleanly alongside the TeX-format packs in the rest of the wave. MWC is the first Research project that ships as an executable mission package rather than as standalone Research; the pattern seeds future Research-to-mission conversion work.

**Place is the hidden unit of analysis and foregrounding it reorders the entire PNW research landscape.** The six projects share a single editorial frame: place is not setting and not background but the active unit of analysis. PPM is about a building; CDS is about a neighborhood; SSM is about sidewalks, transit stations, and plazas; BHM is about a city-as-regional-node; SFC is about a 150-mile corridor; MWC is about a distributed network of cooperative shops tied to a timber-heritage landscape. The decision to treat place as the unit of analysis (rather than industry, demographic group, time period, or cultural movement) produces cross-references that were not visible before — PPM connects to BHM through shared market-institution structure; SFC connects to MWC through shared timber-heritage-to-contemporary-craft lineage; CDS connects to SSM through shared Seattle-music-ecosystem substrate; SSM connects to PPM through shared legal-and-vendor-permit scaffolding at the Pike Place PDA. Every project in the wave connects to seven or more existing Research projects because places ARE intersections — one of the release's three headline lessons.

**Markets are Rosetta Stones and the wave has two of them.** Pike Place and the Bellingham Farmers Market both function as translation layers where supply chain meets culture meets community meets policy meets labor. A farmer's stall at Pike Place is simultaneously an agricultural product, a nutrition-access channel, a vendor's income, a tourist's experience, a municipal social-mission asset, a Japanese-American-diaspora cultural artifact, and a real-estate-commodity node. The same stall read through only one of those lenses loses the other six. The market-as-Rosetta-Stone framing is the wave's second headline lesson and organizes the editorial approach across both PPM and BHM — neither project reads the market as a single-dimension artifact, and both deliberately hold multiple translations open at once. Future Research projects on market-like institutions (food halls, artisan markets, swap meets, flea markets) should default to the same multi-translation framing.

**Two-source merges produce richer projects than single-source packs and CDS proves the pattern.** CDS ingested `cd_mission.tex` (1,023 lines) and `central-district-mission.tex` (1,084 lines) as two complementary source packs covering overlapping-but-distinct perspectives on the Central District — the first pack emphasizing musical lineage and the cassette economy, the second pack emphasizing community spaces and displacement. The merged CDS project is 2,151 research lines across six modules, the deepest non-MWC project in the wave, and the first Research project to intentionally process two sources into one canonical output. The mechanism: module assignment by subject-coverage rather than source-origin, conflict resolution by which source has deeper verified sourcing on the contested claim, and preservation of both sources' bibliographies as reference layers. Future Research projects with overlapping source material should adopt the same two-source merge pattern; the wave's third headline lesson is that the merge produces a richer project than either source alone.

The pattern is an operational extension of v1.49.43's three-commit atomic-content discipline — the atom is now a project, and the convoy linearizes the project-atoms into a bisectable sequence. Mega-waves 2 through N+1 will run the same convoy pattern.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **The Gastown convoy pattern delivered six parallel projects in a 77-minute wall-clock window with zero merge conflicts.** Four Polecat builder-agents executed PPM, CDS, SSM, BHM, SFC, and MWC in parallel worktrees while the Mayor coordinator serialized the commit stream. The only shared file across the six projects (`www/tibsfox/com/Research/series.js`) was edited exactly once, in PPM's commit, with six entries added; the subsequent five commits left it alone. The convoy pattern is the operational answer to Research-series throughput and it held through the mega-wave without editorial-quality drift.
- **CDS proved that two-source merges produce richer projects than either source alone.** The `cd_mission.tex` (1,023 lines) and `central-district-mission.tex` (1,084 lines) source packs merged into a 2,151-line six-module project — the richest non-MWC project in the wave. The merge pattern (assign modules by subject-coverage, resolve conflicts by deeper sourcing, preserve both bibliographies) is reusable across any future project with complementary source material.
- **MWC shipped as a GSD-ready mission package rather than as standalone Research and the pattern held.** MWC's 25-file commit includes a vision doc, a research reference, a milestone spec, a wave execution plan, a test plan, a README, and seven component specs alongside seven research modules. The markdown-format mission-pack processed cleanly alongside the five TeX-format packs in the rest of the wave and opens the path for future Research-to-mission conversion work.
- **Place as the unit of analysis produced cross-references that were not visible before.** PPM-to-BHM through market-institution structure; SFC-to-MWC through timber-heritage-to-contemporary-craft lineage; CDS-to-SSM through Seattle-music-ecosystem substrate; SSM-to-PPM through legal-and-vendor-permit scaffolding. Every project in the wave cross-references to seven or more existing Research projects because places ARE intersections, and the cross-reference density is the structural proof that the editorial frame is right.
- **The multi-domain docroot `www/tibsfox/com/Research/` that v1.49.38 reserved absorbed six new project subdirectories without schema drift.** Every new project slots into `Research/<CODE>/` with the standard five-file shell and the standard research/mission-pack subdirectories. No navigation drift, no stylesheet drift, no build-system changes. The discipline continues to pay off at mega-wave scale.
- **Each project shipped its own subject-specific three-color palette and the register carried.** PPM's market-warm palette, CDS's jazz-red-and-gold palette, SSM's street-neutral palette, BHM's Pacific-Northwest-borderland palette, SFC's timber-green-into-tech-steel palette, MWC's craft-wood-and-brass palette — the three-color discipline from COI/ATC/K8S/OTM/PLT extends naturally to PNW-places subject matter.

### What Could Be Better

- **SFC took a local Everett-and-Sno-Isle angle rather than the broader Portland-to-Seattle Silicon Forest corridor.** The project's 2,483 lines cover the northern half of the corridor in depth (Paine Field, Sno-Isle TECH, ferry-mesh commute dynamics) but leave the Portland tech scene (Intel Hillsboro, Nike's Beaverton campus, the post-Tektronix Oregon electronics manufacturing ecosystem) for a future extension. The project's name promises more than the project delivers and a follow-on SFC-South extension should close the gap.
- **BHM is economics-heavy and could use a cultural or food-scene module.** Five modules on housing, employers, tribal sovereignty, higher education, and cross-border economics leave the Bellingham cultural scene (Mount Baker Theatre, the Pickford Film Center, the Bellingham Farmers Market as a cultural institution, the Fairhaven arts district) under-covered. A Module 06 on Bellingham cultural and food-scene institutions would balance the economics-heavy framing.
- **The SSM legal-governance module cites Berger v. City of Seattle but does not walk the subsequent time/place/manner jurisprudence.** Module 03 names the 1997 Ninth Circuit ruling but leaves the post-Berger Seattle municipal code evolution, the 2013 sound-ordinance amendments, and the 2020-2024 pandemic-era busking restrictions to implicit reference. A revision pass should add a post-Berger timeline to close the legal-governance gap.
- **Every project except MWC ships with a LaTeX mission-pack; MWC ships with a markdown mission-pack; the pattern is not documented.** The TeX-versus-markdown mission-pack decision for MWC was correct (GSD tooling consumes markdown natively) but the rationale is not captured anywhere reachable by a future Research builder. A `.planning/` note explaining when to use TeX versus markdown would prevent future mega-waves from rediscovering the decision.

### What Needs Improvement

- **The wave's cross-reference binding is asserted in README metadata but not mechanically verified across the six projects.** Every project's README block names seven-to-eight cross-referenced projects, but no tooling verifies that the cross-references actually resolve to existing project directories in `www/tibsfox/com/Research/`. A follow-on verification pipeline should walk the cross-reference graph and flag broken links at commit time.
- **The Mega-Batch queue (43 projects + 6 extensions) exists as a plan document but has no throughput projection.** The wave established a 6-projects-per-convoy-pass throughput at ~77 minutes wall-clock; extrapolating that to 49 remaining items suggests roughly 8 more mega-waves. Those 8 mega-waves are not scheduled. The Mega-Batch should have an explicit release schedule mapping mega-waves to calendar weeks.
- **The convoy pattern produced six clean commits but no convoy-level metadata was captured.** There is no record of which Polecat agent built which project, how long each project took in builder-time (as distinct from wall-clock), what the source-pack-to-output-line ratio was per project, or where the builder agents encountered friction. A future convoy-run should capture that metadata into a structured log that subsequent convoys can learn from.
- **The mission-pack LaTeX sources vary in line count (pikeplace 1,092 / central-district 1,084 / seattle-street-music 1,060 / bellingham-market 996 / silicon-forest 1,025) without a line-count rationale.** The roughly-1,000-lines target emerged from JNS (v1.49.53) as a mission-pack convention but is not codified. A `.planning/` note should explicitly state the convention and the rationale (roughly one hour of reader time, roughly one semester-module-unit of coverage).
- **The parser-generated chapter files land at confidence 0.35 for v1.49.82 versus 0.95 for most Research releases.** The lower confidence reflects the parser's difficulty with the mega-wave's aggregated structure (six projects in one release). A parser revision to handle mega-wave structure explicitly should lift the confidence back to 0.95 and should recognize the MW1/MW2/… Mega-Batch naming scheme.

## Lessons Learned

- **Places ARE intersections and place-based research naturally cross-references.** Every project in the wave cross-references to seven or more existing Research projects because place is not setting but active substrate. PPM intersects agriculture, fish supply, Japanese-American history, preservation policy, food access, and tourism; SFC intersects timber, aerospace assembly, community college workforce pipelines, ferry commute dynamics, and the IOOF civic-hall network. The cross-reference density is structural evidence that the editorial frame (place as unit of analysis) is correct. Future Research projects on place-like subjects — neighborhoods, corridors, watersheds, ecoregions, trade zones — should default to treating place as the active substrate and should expect seven-or-more cross-references as a result.
- **Markets are Rosetta Stones — they translate supply chains into culture, labor into experience, and policy into material life.** Pike Place and the Bellingham Farmers Market both function as translation layers where multiple readings of the same stall are simultaneously valid: agricultural product, nutrition access, vendor income, tourist experience, municipal social-mission asset, diaspora cultural artifact, real-estate-commodity node. Single-lens readings miss the translation work the market is doing. Future Research projects on market-like institutions (food halls, artisan markets, swap meets, ports, trading posts) should hold multiple translations open at once and should refuse to collapse the market to any single dimension.
- **Two-source merges produce richer projects than either source alone.** CDS merged two complementary TeX source packs into a single six-module project with 2,151 research lines; the merged project is richer than either source would have produced individually. The merge mechanism — assign modules by subject-coverage rather than source-origin, resolve conflicts by deeper verified sourcing, preserve both bibliographies as reference layers — is reusable across any future project with overlapping source material. The pattern is a deliberate editorial discipline, not an expedient.
- **The Gastown convoy pattern is the operational answer to Research-series throughput.** Four Polecat builder-agents under a Mayor coordinator produced six atomic content commits in a 77-minute wall-clock window with zero merge conflicts and no editorial-quality drift. The convoy extends the three-commit atomic-content discipline from single-project to multi-project scope while preserving bisect-clean history. Future Research mega-waves should run the same convoy pattern and should expect similar throughput.
- **Ship mission packages rather than standalone papers when the subject has actionable build implications.** MWC shipped as a 25-file GSD-ready mission package (vision doc, research reference, milestone spec, wave execution plan, test plan, seven component specs, seven research modules) rather than as a standalone paper. The mission-package framing makes the subject actionable — the Research output can be directly consumed by a downstream GSD project that wants to build the described cooperative-millwork-network. Future Research projects on subjects with clear actionable implications should default to the mission-package shape rather than the standalone-paper shape.
- **Name the convoy composition so the pattern is reusable.** Mayor + four Polecats is a specific operational template: the Mayor handles dispatch, conflict serialization, and commit orchestration; the Polecats handle independent project builds in parallel worktrees. Naming the roles (rather than describing them inline) makes the pattern reusable across future mega-waves and lets the convoy pattern evolve as a named discipline. Future parallel-build patterns in the project should follow the same role-naming discipline.
- **The three-color-palette discipline scales across subject matter without breaking.** Six subject-specific palettes shipped in one release (PPM market-warm, CDS jazz-red-and-gold, SSM street-neutral, BHM Pacific-Northwest-borderland, SFC timber-green-into-tech-steel, MWC craft-wood-and-brass) and the three-color constraint held in every one. The discipline is load-bearing: readers recognize subject register from palette before they read text. Every Research project should ship a subject-specific three-color palette and should treat the palette as a structural decision, not a decorative one.
- **Cross-references are scaffolding and naming compositional neighbors strengthens the whole series.** Each project in the wave explicitly names its cluster neighbors (PPM names AGR/SAL/AWF/ECO/GDN/WSB/ACC; CDS names MIX/GRV/PJM/KUB/TIBS/BRC/FFA; and so on). The named-neighbor discipline is the scaffolding that lets the Research series accumulate into a coherent architecture study rather than a collection of isolated papers. Future Research projects should name their compositional neighbors explicitly in README metadata so the cross-reference graph stays readable at scale.
- **Legal scaffolding is load-bearing infrastructure and deserves Research-project treatment.** SSM's Module 03 treats Berger v. City of Seattle (Ninth Circuit, 1997) as load-bearing cultural infrastructure — without the ruling, Seattle busking would be governed by a very different regulatory regime and the scene would look very different in 2026. BHM's Module 03 treats the Boldt Decision (US v. Washington, 1974) as load-bearing tribal-economic infrastructure. Future Research projects on culturally-embedded subjects should default to foregrounding the legal scaffolding rather than treating it as background or assumption.
- **Mega-waves preserve bisect discipline if and only if the convoy serializes commits.** Parallel builds can produce interleaved commits that break bisect; the Gastown convoy's serialization step (Mayor merges Polecat outputs in deterministic order) is what keeps v1.49.81..v1.49.82 bisectable. Any future parallel-build operation that skips the serialization step will break bisect and will break forensic recoverability. The serialization step is non-optional; it is the price of convoy-scale throughput.
- **Parser confidence reflects structural fit and lower confidence is a parser revision signal, not a content signal.** The parser generated chapter files for v1.49.82 at confidence 0.35 versus the usual 0.95 — not because the release is low-quality but because the parser does not yet model mega-wave structure (six projects aggregated into one release). Low parser confidence on a high-quality release is a signal to extend the parser, not to degrade the release. Future mega-waves should trigger a parser revision that explicitly recognizes the MW1/MW2/... Mega-Batch scheme.
