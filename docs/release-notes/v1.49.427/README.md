**Dead Kennedys (influence)** arrive at E=9 -- the San Francisco hardcore band whose shadow reaches deep into the Pacific Northwest hardcore scene, documented here as an influence entry that acknowledges how the Dead Kennedys' abrasive politics and extreme velocity reshaped what Pacific Northwest bands believed was possible. The Dead Kennedys formed in San Francisco in 1978 around Jello Biafra (vocals), East Bay Ray (guitar), Klaus Flouride (bass), and Ted (drums). Their catalog -- *Fresh Fruit for Rotting Vegetables* (1980), *Plastic Surgery Disasters* (1982), *Frankenchrist* (1985), *Bedtime for Democracy* (1986) -- established the template for American hardcore: fast, political, confrontational, laced with dark satire that made the confrontation sustainable over multiple albums. The Dead Kennedys were not the first hardcore band, but they were the most fully formed: the combination of East Bay Ray's surf guitar dissonance, Klaus Flouride's bass runs, and Jello Biafra's half-spoken, half-screaming performance style produced a sound that was harder than punk and smarter than most of what claimed to be harder than punk.

The Dead Kennedys' Pacific Northwest influence runs through the Seattle and Portland hardcore scenes of the early 1980s and continues into the post-hardcore evolution that produced the bands appearing at degrees 287-289. The Jello Biafra connection to the Northwest is structural: his Alternative Tentacles label was a model for the kind of politically committed independent label infrastructure that Pacific Northwest bands built and used; his later spoken-word work brought him back to Northwest audiences repeatedly; and the Dead Kennedys' specific combination of hardcore velocity with satirical intelligence is the template that Northwest bands took as permission to be both extreme and smart.

**Storm Petrel Colony Midnight Chatter** arrives at E=6 on the SPS track -- the collective vocalizations of storm petrels (*Hydrobates* spp.) at breeding colonies during nighttime activity, when the birds return to their burrow nest sites under cover of darkness to evade aerial predators. Storm petrels are the smallest seabirds in the Pacific Northwest -- sparrow-sized, weighing less than 50 grams, flying thousands of miles across open ocean -- and their colonial nesting vocalizations are unusual: a purring, chattering midnight chorus produced by hundreds of birds arriving and departing burrows simultaneously, identifiable as individual calls only by close inspection and identifiable as a colony event by the ambient acoustic density it creates. The midnight chatter is a sound of effort and navigation: birds finding burrows in darkness, calling to mates inside, being answered.

**FIRST HYDROBATIDAE.** The storm petrel is the first member of family Hydrobatidae to enter the SPS track. Hydrobatidae are the storm petrels: small, tube-nosed seabirds that spend the majority of their lives at sea and return to land only to breed. The entry of Hydrobatidae opens the seabird arc that will define the SPS track through degrees 286-292: Storm Petrel, Cormorant (Brandt's and Double-crested), and Gull and Tern (Laridae). The seabird arc follows the marine mammal survey's close and represents a shift in the SPS track's focus from mammals to birds -- colonial nesting species, coastal and nearshore, acoustically complex.
