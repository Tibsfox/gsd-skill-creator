1. **First US Moon orbit achieved.**
   The Moon now has a surveyor on its surface (Surveyor 1, degree 45) and an orbiter in its sky (Lunar Orbiter 1, degree 47). Apollo's reconnaissance infrastructure is in place. Both lunar-mechanical and lunar-photographic precursors are now green-lit. The path from here to Apollo 11 runs through more Surveyors, more Lunar Orbiters, and the crewed Apollo test flights -- but the unmanned reconnaissance has proven the Moon is landable and mappable.

2. **Film developed in space.**
   The Bimat process was Kodak's solution to the impossible problem of darkroom chemistry in vacuum and microgravity. Rollers, a processing web, diffusion, peel. No liquids. No baths. The elegant engineering that television cameras could not match in resolution. This was the highest-resolution lunar photography possible with 1966 technology, and it required inventing an entirely new class of photographic process to achieve it.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #2040_

3. **The first Earthrise.**
   August 23, 1966. The camera turned the other way. Earth as a half-illuminated crescent above the dead lunar limb. The image that anticipated Apollo 8 (1968) and Apollo 17's Blue Marble (1972) and became an early icon of the environmental movement. A moment of perspective in a mission devoted to surface detail. The photograph was an almost-afterthought by a crew member at Langley or Boeing who had the imagination to ask: what would it look like pointed the other way.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #2041_

4. **Boeing builds the Moon orbiter in Seattle.**
   The spacecraft that first orbited the Moon was built in Kent, Washington, by Boeing's Aerospace Division under contract to NASA Langley Research Center. The same metropolitan area where Pedro the Lion makes music. The Pacific Northwest connection is concrete, not metaphorical: local aerospace heritage and local songwriter heritage occupy the same geography and the same cultural period.

5. **5-for-5 program success.**
   All five Lunar Orbiters (1966-1967) worked. The program cost approximately $200 million in 1967 dollars and produced the definitive lunar photographic atlas that remained the highest-resolution lunar map for 43 years, until the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter began returning data in 2009. Clifford Nelson's team at Langley ran one of the cleanest programs in NASA history.

14. **The junco's altitudinal migration is compressed.**
   Dark-eyed Juncos in the Pacific Northwest perform short-distance altitudinal migration rather than continent-scale latitudinal migration: they move from higher-elevation breeding grounds down into lowland valleys for winter. This is ecologically distinct from classic long-distance migration and deserves more explicit treatment.
