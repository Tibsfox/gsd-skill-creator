# Retrospective — v1.49.548

## Lessons Learned

1. **First US Moon orbit achieved.** The Moon now has a surveyor on its surface (Surveyor 1, degree 45) and an orbiter in its sky (Lunar Orbiter 1, degree 47). Apollo's reconnaissance infrastructure is in place.
2. **Film developed in space.** The Bimat process -- Kodak's solution to the impossible problem of darkroom chemistry in vacuum and microgravity. Rollers, a processing web, diffusion, peel. No liquids. No baths. The elegant engineering that television cameras could not match in resolution.
3. **The first Earthrise.** August 23, 1966. The camera turned the other way. Earth as a half-illuminated crescent above the dead lunar limb. The image that anticipated Apollo 8 and Apollo 17 and the environmental movement. A moment of perspective in a mission devoted to surface detail.
4. **Boeing builds the Moon orbiter in Seattle.** The spacecraft that first orbited the Moon was built in the same metropolitan area where Pedro the Lion makes music. The Pacific Northwest connection is concrete, not metaphorical.
5. **5-for-5 program success.** All five Lunar Orbiters worked. The program cost approximately $200 million in 1967 dollars and produced the definitive lunar photographic atlas for forty-three years.
6. **The folk triangle closes.** Three consecutive folk-adjacent degrees (45-46-47) with mutual connections: Jurado-Bazan (schoolmates, collaborators), Foubert-Votolato (producer-artist), all orbiting Pedro the Lion. The longest folk-family genre run in the engine.
7. **Taxonomic recursion established.** Degree 11 (Oregon Junco) is contained within degree 47 (Dark-eyed Junco). The species at degree 47 is a set that includes the subspecies at degree 11. First time the engine has encountered a species that encloses a previous entry.
8. **Three-degree Passerellidae streak.** Song Sparrow (45), Spotted Towhee (46), Dark-eyed Junco (47). Unprecedented. Exceeds the five-warbler streak (degrees 38-42) in consecutive density (three with no interruptions vs. five after a gap).
