## Mia Zapata


- Mia Zapata's murder in 1993 was one of the most significant losses in Seattle music history; the Seattle music community's response -- Home Alive, benefit performances, a decade of keeping the case active -- demonstrates the organizing capacity of a music community when it decides to respond to violence against its members; Home Alive, which Zapata's bandmates and community co-founded, has provided self-defense training and safety resources to thousands of Seattle musicians and community members since 1993; it is among the most durable and practically impactful legacies of the early 1990s Seattle underground; this engine entry documents The Gits through their music and acknowledges Mia Zapata's place in the Seattle record without reducing the band to the circumstances of her death

## FIRST PHYSETERIDAE


- the sperm whale is among the most unusual cetaceans: not a rorqual, not a dolphin, but a member of a distinct family with a distinct acoustic anatomy; the spermaceti organ -- the large, waxy structure that fills the sperm whale's enormous head -- is the acoustic apparatus that generates the world's loudest animal sound; the sperm whale's head is approximately one-third of its body length, and most of that space is spermaceti; the anatomy is the instrument

## Three cetacean families in eleven degrees


- the engine has documented Delphinidae (270-276, seven entries), Balaenopteridae (278-279, two entries), and Physeteridae (280, first entry) across eleven consecutive SPS degrees; no other eleven-degree stretch in the engine has introduced three new biological families; the concentration is intentional and reflects the biological richness of the Pacific Northwest marine environment

## Loudest biological sound at audible frequency


- the sperm whale click at 230dB is the loudest biological sound at audible frequencies; the blue whale's 188dB pulse is louder only in certain measurement conventions; the distinction is that the blue whale is louder at infrasonic frequencies while the sperm whale is louder in the audible range; E=7 vs E=5 reflects this: the click is heard as a sharp, audible percussion event; the pulse is felt as subsonic pressure
