## Summary

**IN MEMORY OF MIA ZAPATA. FIRST PHYSETERIDAE. LOUDEST BIOLOGICAL SOUND.**

**The Gits** arrive at E=8 -- the Seattle punk band formed in 1986 in Yellow Springs, Ohio, relocated to Seattle in 1989, and composed of Mia Zapata (vocals), Joe Spleen (guitar), Matt Dresdner (bass), and Steve Moriarty (drums). The Gits released one studio album -- *Frenching the Bully* (1992) on C/Z Records -- and a posthumous compilation, *Enter: The Conquering Chicken* (1994), before their dissolution following the death of Mia Zapata. They were one of the central bands in the early 1990s Seattle underground, operating alongside Nirvana, Soundgarden, and the riot grrrl community, and are among the most important bands in the history of Seattle punk.

Mia Zapata was murdered on July 24, 1993, at age 27. She was attacked while walking home after performing at a Capitol Hill bar. Her death was unsolved for a decade; DNA evidence led to the conviction of Jesus Mezquia in 2004. In the years following Mia Zapata's death, the Seattle music community organized around two responses: El Hombre Secreto (a project by Gits guitarist Joe Spleen) and a benefit album and series of tribute performances, and Home Alive, a nonprofit that still operates today providing self-defense classes and safety resources to musicians and community members across Seattle. Mia Zapata's death did not end The Gits' legacy. It deepened it.

The music of The Gits is defined by Mia Zapata's voice: a blues-inflected, raw, powerful instrument that operated at a volume and emotional intensity unusual in the punk context. *Frenching the Bully* is a document of that voice across a set of songs that move from fast punk fury to slow, heavy blues structures without losing the intensity that drives both. E=8 is correct. The Gits at full power are among the loudest and most emotionally direct bands in the Seattle punk record, and Mia Zapata's voice is the reason.

This entry is offered with respect for Mia Zapata, for her music, and for what the Seattle music community did in her memory. Home Alive is still here. The music is still here. The engine documents both.

**Sperm Whale Click Train** arrives at E=7 on the SPS track -- the biosonar click train of *Physeter macrocephalus*, the sperm whale, the loudest sound produced by any animal on Earth. Sperm whale clicks are generated in the spermaceti organ, a structure of waxy fluid unique to the species, and are focused into a directional beam by the melon and the skull's reflective surfaces. The clicks produced reach 230 decibels at the source -- louder than the blue whale's infrasonic pulses when measured at equivalent distances. The sperm whale click is not infrasoic but mid-frequency, in the range of 100-30,000 Hz, and is used both for biosonar prey detection at extreme depth and for social communication between individuals.

E=7 for the sperm whale click train is the correct assignment. Unlike the blue whale's infrasonic pulse -- which scores E=5 because the human auditory experience is one of subsonic pressure rather than conventional sound -- the sperm whale click is fully in the audible range, sharp, percussive, and rhythmically structured. The click train sounds like what it is: an active biological sonar system, pulsing into the deep water column, scanning for giant squid at 1,000 meters below the surface. E=7 reflects the audible intensity without overstating it.

Degree 280 marks the engine's first entry for Family Physeteridae -- the sperm whales, a family distinct from the Balaenopteridae and with a distinct acoustic signature. The engine has now documented three cetacean families: Delphinidae (orcas, degrees 270-276), Balaenopteridae (humpback and blue whale, degrees 278-279), and now Physeteridae.
