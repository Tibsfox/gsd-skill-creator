## Summary

v1.49.1011 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.201 to 1.202, adding the Spitzer Space Telescope — NASA's fourth and final Great Observatory, the infrared one — as the obs#2 INTRA-AXIS continuation that sustains the INFRARED-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis (axis-rotation #27 sustained at obs#2). The infrared-astronomy axis opened at v1010 IRAS with the first survey of the entire sky in infrared; the forward continuation sustains the axis at a second observation that is substrate-form-distinct, carrying the infrared way of seeing the sky from the all-sky survey IRAS made into deep, sustained study of individual targets. Where IRAS scanned the whole sky to compile the first census, Spitzer was a flagship that pointed at chosen targets and studied them at length, the deep pointed observatory that complements the survey.

Spitzer was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory with science operations at the Spitzer Science Center at Caltech. The observatory launched on 25 August 2003 aboard a Delta II 7920H from Cape Canaveral as SIRTF, the Space Infrared Telescope Facility, was renamed Spitzer in December 2003 for the astrophysicist Lyman Spitzer, who first proposed space telescopes, and observed in infrared until its decommissioning in January 2020. Spitzer carried an 85-centimeter beryllium telescope cooled by liquid helium to about five and a half kelvin, so the instrument's own thermal glow would not swamp the faint infrared signal, with the Infrared Array Camera, the Infrared Spectrograph, and the Multiband Imaging Photometer observing chosen targets from an Earth-trailing heliocentric orbit.

The signature of the mission is the first infrared Great Observatory. As the infrared member of NASA's Great Observatories, Spitzer joined the optical, gamma-ray, and X-ray flagships, giving infrared astronomy a deep pointed flagship where IRAS was a survey satellite.

The mission's deepest reach was the light of a world around another star. Spitzer measured the infrared glow of a hot Jupiter as it passed behind its star, the secondary eclipse, the first direct detection of light from a planet beyond the solar system, opening the study of exoplanet atmospheres and temperatures and producing the first exoplanet temperature maps.

The deep pointed observation let the observatory study faint sources in depth. Rather than scanning the whole sky, Spitzer pointed at chosen targets and stared, building deep infrared images and spectra of exoplanets, forming stars, dust-shrouded galaxies, and some of the most distant galaxies.

The Earth-trailing heliocentric orbit kept the telescope cold. By flying in an orbit around the Sun that trailed behind Earth and drifted slowly away from it, the observatory stayed away from Earth's warmth so a modest coolant supply lasted years, and when the helium ran out in 2009 it continued for another decade as the warm mission.

The axis-relevant substrate of Spitzer at this degree is its continuation of the INFRARED-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#2, the deep pointed observatory that follows the survey. Spitzer opens the INFRARED-ASTRONOMY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#2 NEW LOCKED, sustains the INFRARED-SPACE-TELESCOPE-LINEAGE thread at obs#2 cumulative, and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#74 cumulative (axis-rotation #27 sustained at obs#2). The forward continuation favors later infrared observatories that build on both the survey form IRAS opened and the pointed-observatory form Spitzer added.

Spitzer carries the infrared way of seeing the sky into the deep pointed observatory on the catalog, and the mission records the Spitzer team at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, the Spitzer Science Center at Caltech, and the instrument and science teams. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.201 to 1.202, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#85 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A deep pointed observatory complements an all-sky survey.** By pointing at chosen targets and staring at length, Spitzer studied faint sources IRAS could only catalog, showing that the survey and the pointed observatory together span the infrared way of seeing the sky.
2. **A cooled telescope sensitive enough can read the light of another world.** By measuring the infrared glow of a hot Jupiter at secondary eclipse, Spitzer made the first detection of light from a planet beyond the solar system, showing that deep infrared sensitivity can reach an exoplanet's own emission.
3. **A drifting orbit is a thermal strategy.** By flying an Earth-trailing heliocentric orbit, Spitzer drifted away from Earth's warmth so a modest coolant supply lasted years, showing that the orbit itself can keep a telescope cold.
4. **A mission can outlive its coolant.** By operating two near-infrared channels for another decade as the warm mission, Spitzer stayed productive long past its cryogenic phase, showing that a careful instrument design can extend the observing span.
5. **An axis sustains at a substrate-form-distinct second observation.** By following the IRAS all-sky survey with the deep pointed Great Observatory, Spitzer sustained the infrared-astronomy axis at obs#2 without repeating the survey form, showing that an intra-axis continuation deepens an axis by adding a distinct form.
6. **One observatory can reach from a nearby planet to the early universe.** By studying exoplanets, rings, forming stars, and the most distant galaxies, Spitzer showed that a deep infrared flagship can read sources across an enormous range of distance and temperature.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across a sibling mission.** Because Spitzer reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.201 IRAS template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the deep pointed observatory while keeping the infrared-warm palette, making the sibling-infrared mission a clean build.

## Dedication

v1.202 Spitzer is dedicated to the teams that built and flew NASA's infrared Great Observatory: the Spitzer team at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory who built the observatory and led the mission; the Spitzer Science Center at Caltech who ran the science operations and the data archive; the instrument teams who built the Infrared Array Camera, the Infrared Spectrograph, and the Multiband Imaging Photometer; and the science teams who turned the deep pointed observations into the first detection of light from a planet beyond the solar system, the seven-planet TRAPPIST-1 system, the largest ring of Saturn, and the reach to the most distant galaxies. It is dedicated as well to the engineers who cooled the 85-centimeter telescope with liquid helium and flew it on an Earth-trailing heliocentric orbit so it could run cold, and to the namesake Lyman Spitzer, who first proposed space telescopes. Spitzer studied the infrared sky in depth, read the light of a world around another star, and carried infrared astronomy from the survey into the deep pointed observatory.
