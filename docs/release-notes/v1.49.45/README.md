## Summary

The 43rd Research project and the direct continuation of GRV (Green River). PJM traces the "mainstream path" from Green River's 1987 split — through Mother Love Bone, Andrew Wood's death, a demo tape mailed to San Diego, and the band that fought Ticketmaster, survived Roskilde, and never made Ten again because they didn't have to. Nine research modules covering formation, the Ten album, full discography, the Ticketmaster fight, live performance and Roskilde, activism, musical evolution, and legacy.

Named "Ten" — the 1991 debut that went 13x Platinum (Diamond), defined a decade alongside Nevermind, and started with a surfer writing lyrics to instrumentals he got from a friend of a friend. The album that proved the mainstream path from Green River was right.

The **Music Research sub-cluster** now has four entries: WAL (parody) → DDA (wordplay) → GRV (geographic sound, the split) → **PJM** (the mainstream path). The Green River family tree is documented on both branches.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

1. **The GRV→PJM thread is the strongest lineage in the series.** Module 01 begins exactly where GRV Module 05 ends — the split. Module 08 resolves it: "both paths were right." Two projects that read as one story across two perspectives. This is the Research series working as an interconnected network, not isolated studies.

2. **The Ticketmaster module (04) is the most systems-relevant.** A band fighting a monopoly in 1994, losing commercially, being vindicated by the DOJ 30 years later — this maps directly to infrastructure (SYS), trust systems (BRC), and corporate ethics (WYR). Music research that produces transferable systems insights.

3. **Roskilde handled correctly.** Nine people named. The aftermath documented as institutional change, not dramatic narrative. The through-line ("every safety protocol since is a memorial") carries the weight without exploitation.

### Lessons Learned

1. **The Green River family tree proves that creative tension produces more than consensus.** Green River couldn't hold punk and rock together. The split produced Mudhoney (30+ years of indie credibility) AND Pearl Jam (85 million records, 12 albums, the Ticketmaster fight, Roskilde survival). Both paths were right. The tension was the creative engine — it just needed more than one band to contain it.

2. **"Never Ten Again" is a research principle.** Pearl Jam deliberately made each album different from the last. The Research series does the same — each project has its own palette, its own through-lines, its own voice. Repeating the formula is the easy path. Evolving is the work.
