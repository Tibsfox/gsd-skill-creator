## Marine-to-terrestrial nutrient transfer


- Pacific salmon spend 1-5 years at sea, accumulating marine-derived nutrients in their bodies; when they return to spawn and die, those nutrients enter freshwater and riparian systems; bears and eagles accelerate the transfer by distributing salmon remains into the forest; the spawning stream at peak run generates measurable nitrogen isotope signatures in streamside vegetation up to 100 meters from the water; the acoustic synthesis event is the sound of this nutrient transfer in process; the engine documents not just the sound but the ecological function it represents

## Bald eagle acoustic signature


- the bald eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*) is the Pacific Northwest's apex raptor and the United States' national symbol; at a salmon spawning stream, the eagle's acoustic signature is primarily mechanical: the deep wingbeat of a large raptor (7-foot wingspan) landing in streamside trees, the sharp crack of talons on bark, the splash of a strike attempt, the territorial call used to establish priority at high-value fishing locations; the eagle's vocalization is well-known but its mechanical acoustic signature (wings, talons, water impact) is less documented; the convergence event captures both

## Bear in riparian zone


- black bears at salmon streams produce a highly specific acoustic signature: the splash of a large mammal entering cold water, the shorter impact sounds of a successful strike, low huffing and jaw-popping vocalizations used to establish dominance hierarchy at the stream, the wet sound of fish being dragged to shore or into the forest; a productive spawning stream with multiple bears present generates continuous low-frequency acoustic activity from bear-sized bodies moving through shallow water; the engine documents this as the mammalian component of the convergence event's acoustic mix
