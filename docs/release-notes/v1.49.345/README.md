**Mountain Goat Kid and the Closing of the Mountain Goat Pair.** The **Mountain Goat** (*Oreamnos americanus*) is the North American caprid of the high-alpine zones, the only extant member of its genus, distributed across the **Olympic Mountains** (introduced population, partially translocated 2018-2020), the **Cascade Range** (native populations), the **British Columbia Coast Mountains**, the **Northern Rockies**, and **Southeast Alaska** high-alpine zones typically above 5,000 feet and frequently above 7,000 feet. Despite the common-name convergence, the species is not a true goat in the *Capra* lineage; it is a caprid closely related to the serows and gorals of Asian high-country and occupies an evolutionary position consistent with its distinctive high-alpine adaptation. The engine's documentation across degrees 203-204 covers two vocalizations — the adult **bleat** at degree 203 (E=4) and the juvenile **kid contact bleat** at degree 204 (E=3) — forming an adult-then-young pair that closes on vulnerability rather than on dominance. The **kid contact bleat** specifically is produced by kid goats to maintain dam-kid contact on exposed alpine ridges, where the acoustic environment includes persistent wind at speeds frequently exceeding 40 mph and where the vocal bond is not merely social but a survival mechanism.

**The Kid Contact Bleat as Wind-Competing Fragility.** The **kid contact bleat** is acoustically distinct from the adult bleat (degree 203) in ways that the E=3 rating captures but does not fully explain. The adult bleat at degree 203 (E=4) is produced by mature goats in a slightly lower-frequency register with greater projection across alpine acoustic space. The kid contact bleat at degree 204 (E=3) is a higher-pitched, thinner version of the same fundamental vocalization, produced by smaller lungs and smaller vocal tracts in kid goats whose body mass at birth is only 6-8 pounds and who reach only 20-25 pounds by the end of their first summer. The sound must compete with alpine wind on exposed ridges above 7,000 feet where wind speeds regularly exceed 40 mph and where atmospheric conditions attenuate high-frequency content disproportionately. The E=3 rating reflects the sound's **fragility** against this environment — the kid's contact bleat is barely audible above the wind except within a few tens of meters of the kid itself, and the dam must remain within close proximity to maintain the vocal contact necessary for kid survival. The acoustic fragility mirrors the physical fragility of kid Mountain Goats whose survival through the first summer depends on dam-kid proximity and vocal coordination across terrain where a single misstep is fatal.

**Alpine Parenting as Acoustic Survival Mechanism.** The dam-kid vocal bond in Mountain Goats is not a social nicety; it is a survival mechanism. Kid Mountain Goats must follow their dams across near-vertical rock faces within days of birth, traversing terrain where human climbers typically deploy ropes and specialized equipment. The kids are born in late May through early June after a six-month gestation, and their first summer is spent developing the musculoskeletal and behavioral capacity to navigate 60-70-degree slopes, narrow ledges, and exposed ridge traverses. The dam-kid vocal bond operates continuously through this period, with the kid's contact bleat signaling proximity and the dam's answering vocalizations providing directional cues across visually obstructed terrain. When the vocal bond fails — through kid separation, dam distraction, or acoustic masking by wind and weather — kids are acutely vulnerable to falls, predation (by Golden Eagles, Cougars, or Coyotes where habitat overlaps), and exhaustion. The **alpine-parenting-as-acoustic-survival** register that enters the engine's physiology-to-behavior vocabulary at degree 204 thus documents a specific case where a vocalization's primary function is survival-critical coordination rather than generalized social signaling, a register that differs meaningfully from the adult-to-adult social vocalizations documented at prior SPS degrees.

**The Olympic Mountain Goat Translocation Program and Ecological Integrity.** The Olympic Peninsula Mountain Goat population is not native; it was introduced in the 1920s from Alaskan stock for hunting purposes and subsequently expanded through protection within Olympic National Park after the park's 1938 establishment. The introduced population grew to ecologically disruptive levels, causing significant damage to Olympic alpine meadow vegetation and becoming a direct threat to visitor safety (documented including a 2010 fatal goring incident). Between **2018 and 2020**, Olympic National Park in coordination with Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife, the USDA Forest Service, and tribal partners (including the Muckleshoot Indian Tribe and Stillaguamish Tribe) conducted a multi-year capture-and-translocation program that relocated hundreds of Olympic goats to their native Cascade range, simultaneously restoring Olympic Peninsula ecological integrity and reinforcing depleted Cascade Mountain Goat populations. ## Part B: Mountain Goat Kid — The Juvenile Contact Bleat and the Alpine-Parenting Acoustic Survival Register

- **Species Baseline and Taxonomic Identity:** **Mountain Goat** (*Oreamnos americanus*) is a member of **Order Artiodactyla / Family Bovidae / Subfamily Caprinae / Genus Oreamnos / species americanus**, the only extant member of its genus. Despite the common-name convergence, the species is not a true goat in the *Capra* lineage; it is a caprid closely related to the serows (*Capricornis*) and gorals (*Naemorhedus*) of Asian high-country, occupying an evolutionary position consistent with its distinctive high-alpine adaptation across North American cordilleran mountain systems. Kid body mass at birth is 6-8 pounds, reaching 20-25 pounds by the end of first summer, with adults reaching 100-300 pounds.
- **Pacific Northwest and Cordilleran Distribution:** Mountain Goats are distributed across the **Olympic Mountains** (introduced 1920s, partially translocated 2018-2020), the **Cascade Range** (native Cascade populations, reinforced by 2018-2020 Olympic translocations), the **British Columbia Coast Mountains**, the **Northern Rockies**, and **Southeast Alaska** high-alpine zones typically above 5,000 feet and frequently above 7,000 feet. The species is a high-alpine specialist whose ecological niche concentrates in terrain that excludes most competing mammalian herbivores, producing the characteristic pattern of Mountain Goat occupation of cliff-and-ledge zones adjacent to sub-alpine meadows.
- **The Kid Contact Bleat as Low-Amplitude Vocalization:** The **kid contact bleat** at degree 204 (E=3) is a thin, high-pitched vocalization produced by kid Mountain Goats to maintain dam-kid contact on exposed alpine ridges. Acoustic characteristics include short-duration ascending pitch contour, high fundamental frequency reflecting the kid's small vocal tract and lungs, and low absolute amplitude that must compete with persistent alpine wind at speeds frequently exceeding 40 mph. The E=3 rating reflects both the low absolute amplitude and the sound's fragility against the high-wind alpine environment; the sound is barely audible above the wind except within a few tens of meters of the kid itself.
- **Wind-Competing Fragility on Exposed Alpine Ridges:** The acoustic environment in which the kid contact bleat operates is the most challenging in the engine's mammal documentation so far. Alpine ridges above 7,000 feet experience persistent wind at speeds regularly exceeding 40 mph, with atmospheric conditions that disproportionately attenuate high-frequency content and create acoustic masking from continuous wind-noise across the hearing range where kid vocalizations concentrate. The E=3 rating specifically reflects this **fragility-against-environment** rather than general indifference; the kid's contact bleat is not quiet because the kid is incapable of louder vocalizations but because the adaptive amplitude trade-off balances audibility to the dam against predator detection and energy expenditure.
- **Alpine Parenting as Acoustic Survival Mechanism:** The dam-kid vocal bond in Mountain Goats is a survival mechanism rather than a social nicety. Kid Mountain Goats must follow their dams across near-vertical rock faces within days of birth, traversing terrain where human climbers typically deploy ropes and specialized equipment. The kids are born in late May through early June after approximately six-month gestation, and their first summer is spent developing the musculoskeletal and behavioral capacity to navigate 60-70-degree slopes, narrow ledges, and exposed ridge traverses. The dam-kid vocal bond operates continuously through this period as the primary mechanism by which the kid maintains location-and-direction awareness relative to the dam across visually obstructed alpine terrain.
- **Mother-Kid Vocalization and Vocal-Bond Formation:** Mountain Goat dams and kids form vocal-recognition bonds within the first 24-48 hours after birth, with kids imprinting on the specific acoustic signature of their dam's vocalizations and dams imprinting on their kid's specific contact-bleat signature. The imprinting is essential for survival because Mountain Goat groups (nursery bands of dams and kids) frequently include multiple dam-kid pairs whose vocal similarity could produce confusion without individual-recognition imprinting. The vocal-bond formation is among the most time-critical behavioral processes in the species' life history.
- **Olympic Mountain Goat Translocation 2018-2020:** Between **2018 and 2020**, Olympic National Park in coordination with Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife, the USDA Forest Service, and tribal partners (including the Muckleshoot Indian Tribe and Stillaguamish Tribe) conducted a multi-year capture-and-translocation program that relocated hundreds of Olympic Mountain Goats to their native Cascade range. The translocation simultaneously restored Olympic Peninsula ecological integrity (the introduced population had grown to ecologically disruptive levels, damaging alpine meadow vegetation and creating visitor safety concerns documented including a 2010 fatal goring incident) and reinforced depleted Cascade Mountain Goat populations through integrated ungulate management. The dam-kid vocal bond was a specific management concern during capture-and-release operations, with translocation protocols designed to minimize vocal-bond disruption by moving dam-kid pairs together rather than separately.
- **Mountain Goat Pair Closes the Two-Degree Life-Cycle Arc:** Degree 204's kid contact bleat closes the Mountain Goat pair across degrees 203 (adult bleat, E=4) and 204 (juvenile contact bleat, E=3), forming a complete adult-plus-juvenile acoustic arc. The pair's amplitude pattern (4-3) inverts the typical adult-louder-than-juvenile expectation by emphasizing the juvenile's environment-competing fragility rather than its absolute amplitude, and the closure on the juvenile vocalization establishes a vulnerability-as-closure pattern distinct from the combat-as-closure pattern documented at the Roosevelt Elk rut roar (199) and the adult-aggression-as-closure pattern at the Black Bear jaw-pop (201).
- **Life-Cycle Pattern Now Established for Third Species Pair in Q3:** The Mountain Goat pair joins the **Black-tailed Deer pair** (195 doe + 196 buck or fawn) and the **Black Bear trio** (200 huff, 201 jaw-pop, 202 cub distress) as the third species in Q3 whose documentation spans adult-then-juvenile vocalizations. The pattern is now firmly established as a Q3 documentation strategy rather than an ad-hoc occurrence, and future SPS documentation is likely to extend the pattern further by selecting species whose juvenile vocalizations differ meaningfully from adult vocalizations and whose life-cycle acoustic arcs reward paired-degree treatment.
- **Foundational Research and Literature:** Scientific documentation of Mountain Goat behavior and ecology has developed across the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. **Côté and Festa-Bianchet** (2003) *Mountain Goats: Ecology, Behavior, and Conservation* is the foundational monograph establishing the scientific baseline for Mountain Goat research across North America. **Douglas Chadwick** (2002) *A Beast the Color of Winter* brought Mountain Goat biology to broader readership through decades of high-alpine observational fieldwork. **Festa-Bianchet, Urquhart, and Smith** dam-kid vocalization research contributes specifically to the acoustic-and-behavioral documentation that supports the engine's degree-204 entry. State wildlife agency management programs (Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife, Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife, British Columbia Ministry of Forests) sustain continuing population assessments and management interventions across the species' Pacific Northwest and cordilleran range.
