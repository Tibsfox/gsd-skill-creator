## Mission Overview

The Mars Exploration Rovers are the obs#4 sustain of the MARS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA twin-rover surface mission whose defining achievement is the first kilometers-scale field geology on another planet and the first in-situ mineralogical evidence of an ancient liquid-water environment. It was a wholly NASA / United States mission, developed and operated by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory under NASA's Mars Exploration Program (strategic theme "Follow the Water"), with Steven Squyres of Cornell as principal investigator. Two identical six-wheeled, solar-powered, rocker-bogie rovers — each about 185 kg (about 408 lb), roughly 1.5 m tall with the camera mast, drawing about 140 W initially from their solar arrays plus rechargeable lithium-ion batteries, with a top speed of about 5 cm/s and semi-autonomous onboard hazard avoidance and visual odometry, far larger than Pathfinder's ~10.6 kg Sojourner — were sent to opposite sides of Mars. Spirit (MER-A; NSSDCA/COSPAR 2003-027A) launched on a Delta II 7925 from Cape Canaveral SLC-17A on 10 June 2003 and landed in Gusev Crater (~14.57°S, 175.48°E) on 4 January 2004 (UTC). Opportunity (MER-B; NSSDCA/COSPAR 2003-032A) launched on a Delta II 7925H ("heavy") from SLC-17B on 7 July 2003 and landed in Meridiani Planum (~1.95°S, 354.47°E), inside the small Eagle Crater (a "hole in one"), on 25 January 2004 (UTC). Each reached the surface by the airbag-cushioned bouncing landing inherited and scaled up (about seventeen times Sojourner's mass) from Mars Pathfinder — through a cruise stage, a Viking/Pathfinder-derived aeroshell, a parachute, RAD retro-rockets, airbags, a bounce to rest, opening petals, and rover egress — NOT a first. The science payload was the Athena suite: Pancam (a mast color stereo camera), Mini-TES (the Miniature Thermal Emission Spectrometer), the Mössbauer spectrometer MIMOS II (iron mineralogy), the Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer (APXS, elemental chemistry — a redesigned successor instrument to Pathfinder's "Alpha Proton" unit, with the proton-scattering channel de-emphasized), the Microscopic Imager, and the Rock Abrasion Tool (RAT, built by Honeybee Robotics), a diamond grinding wheel that cut a ~45 mm circle ~5 mm deep to expose a fresh rock interior (first used on the rock "Adirondack," Spirit Sol 34, 6 February 2004). The mast remote-sensing instruments (Pancam and Mini-TES) chose targets, and a five-degree-of-freedom Instrument Deployment Device placed the contact instruments (Microscopic Imager, Mössbauer, APXS) and the RAT. At Meridiani, Opportunity found jarosite, hematite "blueberry" concretions, sulfate-rich outcrops (El Capitan, up to ~40 wt% SO₃) and cross-bedding / festoon lamination (Last Chance) — the March 2004 "shoreline of a salty, acidic sea" result, the first in-situ mineralogical evidence of an ancient aqueous environment; it then drove to ever-larger craters — Endurance, Erebus, Victoria (left August 2008), and the ~22 km Endeavour Crater (rim reached 9 August 2011 at Cape York / "Spirit Point"), where it examined the "Homestake" calcium-sulfate (gypsum) vein (November 2011) and ground-truthed in situ the clay minerals (phyllosilicates / smectite at Matijevic Hill) first detected from orbit by MRO's CRISM, recording a milder, neutral-pH water. At Gusev, the floor was olivine-bearing volcanic basalt rather than the hoped-for lakebed, so Spirit drove ~2.5 km to the Columbia Hills (seven hills named in honor of the Space Shuttle Columbia STS-107 crew); there the Mössbauer found goethite in the rock "Clovis," "Home Plate" proved a layered explosive-hydrovolcanic deposit with a bomb sag, and the dragging dead wheel (after a right-front wheel motor failed in March 2006) exposed bright soil of nearly pure (~90 wt%) opaline silica in 2007 ("Gertrude Weise" / "Kenosha Comets"), interpreted as a hydrothermal / hot-spring (once-habitable) deposit. On 24 March 2015 (Sol 3968) a 46.5 m drive took Opportunity's odometer to 42.198 km, crossing the marathon distance (42.195 km) — the first marathon off Earth — and its final lifetime odometry was about 45.16 km (28.06 mi), the farthest any vehicle has driven on another world (surpassing the lunar Lunokhod 2's ~39 km on 28 July 2014; the record still stands). Spirit became mired in soft soil at "Troy" (1 May 2009) and worked on as a stationary platform until its last contact on 22 March 2010 (declared complete 25 May 2011, ~6 years, ~7.73 km); Opportunity's solar panels were starved by the June 2018 planet-encircling dust storm in Perseverance Valley (last contact 10 June 2018, declared complete 13 February 2019, 5111 sols, ~14 years 136 days). **FIRST-IN-SITU-MINERALOGICAL-EVIDENCE-OF-AN-ANCIENT-AQUEOUS-ENVIRONMENT-ON-MARS:** At Meridiani Planum, Opportunity's Mössbauer spectrometer and Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer found jarosite (an iron sulfate that forms only in acidic liquid water; up to ~28% of the iron in the outcrop bedrock), small spherical gray hematite concretions nicknamed "blueberries," sulfate-rich rocks, and cross-bedding / festoon lamination — the first in-situ mineralogical evidence that liquid water once persisted at and chemically altered the Martian surface (an ancient aqueous environment, "the shoreline of a salty, acidic sea"). Corroborated by Spirit at Gusev / Columbia Hills, where the Mössbauer found goethite (an iron oxyhydroxide that forms only with water) and the dragging dead wheel exposed nearly pure (~90 wt%) opaline silica interpreted as a hydrothermal / hot-spring (potentially habitable) deposit. The qualifier is load-bearing: this is the first IN-SITU MINERALOGICAL identification of minerals (jarosite, sulfates, hematite concretions) that form only in liquid water — a qualitatively different observable from Mariner 9's ORBITAL channels-imply-water (obs#2, morphology from orbit) and Mars Pathfinder's GROUND-LEVEL flood MORPHOLOGY at Ares Vallis (obs#3, rock shapes read as flooding, not mineral identification), and NOT "discovered water" (water vapor and polar / near-surface ice were already known). The orbital hematite signal that sent Opportunity to Meridiani was mapped by the Thermal Emission Spectrometer on Mars Global Surveyor, not Odyssey. ## Surprises

- **Two small 90-sol rovers drove for years far beyond their design lives.** Each Mars Exploration Rover was built for a 90-sol primary mission of roughly 600 m to 1 km of driving; Spirit operated about six years and ~7.73 km, and Opportunity operated about fourteen years and ~45.16 km — redefining surface exploration as long-range field science rather than a single landing site.
- **The first off-Earth marathon and a distance record that still stands.** On 24 March 2015 (Sol 3968) a 46.5 m drive took Opportunity's odometer to 42.198 km, crossing the marathon distance (42.195 km) — the first marathon off Earth — and its final lifetime odometry of ~45.16 km is the farthest any vehicle has driven on another world, a record that still stands (surpassing the lunar Lunokhod 2's ~39 km on 28 July 2014).
- **The first in-situ mineral proof of an ancient salty, acidic sea was read directly from the rock.** At Meridiani, Opportunity's Mössbauer and Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer found jarosite (which forms only in acidic liquid water), hematite "blueberry" concretions, sulfate-rich rocks, and cross-bedding — the first in-situ mineralogical evidence that liquid water once persisted at the Martian surface, distinct from the orbital channels and the ground-level flood morphology of the predecessors.
- **A failed wheel turned into a discovery.** After Spirit's right-front wheel motor failed in March 2006, driving backward dragged the dead wheel through the soil and in 2007 exposed bright soil of nearly pure (~90 wt%) opaline silica — interpreted as a hydrothermal / hot-spring (once-habitable) deposit, a result the working rover could not have reached otherwise.
- **A small mustelid ranges enormous distances across the harshest high country.** The Wolverine (*Gulo gulo*), the largest terrestrial member of the weasel family, is documented ranging hundreds of square kilometers and moving tens of kilometers a day across the harshest snowbound, rocky, broken North Cascades terrain — enduring far out of proportion to its small body size (behavioral / ecological natural history only), a mirror of two small rovers that out-ranged and outlasted what their frames promised.
- **A dwarf circumboreal juniper persists for centuries on bare exposed rock.** The Common Juniper (*Juniperus communis*), the most widely distributed conifer in the world and among the longest-lived, is a low, slow-growing, often prostrate krummholz shrub that holds on for centuries on bare, exposed, rocky alpine ground where little else endures — distinct from the upright *Juniperus scopulorum* — a mirror of the rovers outlasting their 90-sol design by years.
