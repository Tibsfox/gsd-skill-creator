## What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived mission imagery.** The Mars Exploration Rover shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual Pancam panorama frames or a rover-on-the-surface image. A future revision could render a real Pancam mosaic (a Meridiani outcrop or the Endeavour rim, publicly available from the Planetary Data System) for a higher-fidelity view.
- **The Mars Exploration Rover diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of a six-wheel rocker-bogie rover with its instrument arm extended and the Rock Abrasion Tool against an outcrop.

## Surprises

- **Two small 90-sol rovers drove for years far beyond their design lives.** Each Mars Exploration Rover was built for a 90-sol primary mission of roughly 600 m to 1 km of driving; Spirit operated about six years and ~7.73 km, and Opportunity operated about fourteen years and ~45.16 km — redefining surface exploration as long-range field science rather than a single landing site.
- **The first off-Earth marathon and a distance record that still stands.** On 24 March 2015 (Sol 3968) a 46.5 m drive took Opportunity's odometer to 42.198 km, crossing the marathon distance (42.195 km) — the first marathon off Earth — and its final lifetime odometry of ~45.16 km is the farthest any vehicle has driven on another world, a record that still stands (surpassing the lunar Lunokhod 2's ~39 km on 28 July 2014).
- **The first in-situ mineral proof of an ancient salty, acidic sea was read directly from the rock.** At Meridiani, Opportunity's Mössbauer and Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer found jarosite (which forms only in acidic liquid water), hematite "blueberry" concretions, sulfate-rich rocks, and cross-bedding — the first in-situ mineralogical evidence that liquid water once persisted at the Martian surface, distinct from the orbital channels and the ground-level flood morphology of the predecessors.
- **A failed wheel turned into a discovery.** After Spirit's right-front wheel motor failed in March 2006, driving backward dragged the dead wheel through the soil and in 2007 exposed bright soil of nearly pure (~90 wt%) opaline silica — interpreted as a hydrothermal / hot-spring (once-habitable) deposit, a result the working rover could not have reached otherwise.
- **A small mustelid ranges enormous distances across the harshest high country.** The Wolverine (*Gulo gulo*), the largest terrestrial member of the weasel family, is documented ranging hundreds of square kilometers and moving tens of kilometers a day across the harshest snowbound, rocky, broken North Cascades terrain — enduring far out of proportion to its small body size (behavioral / ecological natural history only), a mirror of two small rovers that out-ranged and outlasted what their frames promised.
- **A dwarf circumboreal juniper persists for centuries on bare exposed rock.** The Common Juniper (*Juniperus communis*), the most widely distributed conifer in the world and among the longest-lived, is a low, slow-growing, often prostrate krummholz shrub that holds on for centuries on bare, exposed, rocky alpine ground where little else endures — distinct from the upright *Juniperus scopulorum* — a mirror of the rovers outlasting their 90-sol design by years.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A sustain keeps the predecessor's axis vocabulary and replaces only the predecessor-specific content.** The Mars Exploration Rovers share the Mars-exploration vocabulary with the Mars Pathfinder predecessor (Mars, the red planet, the surface, regolith, the atmosphere, dust, in situ, planetary exploration, past water, outflow channel) — that vocabulary is shared and correct. The rewrite removed only the Mars-Pathfinder-specific content (Mars Pathfinder, Sojourner, the Discovery Program / "faster, better, cheaper" / MESUR Pathfinder framing, the ~US$265M cost, the single small ~10.6 kg rover, Ares Vallis, the Carl Sagan Memorial Station, the first-airbag-Mars-landing and first-rover-to-operate and first-mobile-rover-rock-geochemistry anchors, the "Alpha Proton" APXS, Barnacle Bill / Yogi, Valerie Ambroise and the Sojourner-Truth naming, the 4 December 1996 launch / 4 July 1997 landing / LC-17B, the ~83-sol mission, the record-1997-web-traffic note, and the Oregon Tiger Beetle + Prairie Lupine pairing) and replaced it with Mars Exploration Rover content.
2. **A sustain has no axis rotation to frame — and 1.240 was itself a sustain, so there is no rotation framing to remove.** The Mars axis opened at 1.238 (Viking, substrate-axis rotation #36); 1.239 (Mariner 9), 1.240 (Mars Pathfinder), and 1.241 (the MERs) are all SUSTAINS, so the clone source carries no rotation narrative, and at most a single distant note records that the Mars axis itself opened at 1.238 by rotating from the X-ray-astronomy axis (rotation #35, held complete at obs#15). The X-ray-axis vocabulary does not appear.
3. **The airbag landing and the wheeled rover are heritage REUSED / scaled up from Pathfinder, claimed as neither first.** The MERs used Pathfinder's airbag-cushioned bouncing landing scaled up ~17×, and the rover descends from Sojourner; the catalog stamps neither "first airbag-cushioned Mars landing" nor "first rover to operate on another planet" (both Pathfinder, obs#3). The MER firsts are in-situ water mineralogy, long-distance field geology, and record endurance / distance.
4. **The water evidence is in-situ MINERALOGY, not "discovered water," and is a different observable from the predecessors'.** Opportunity's Mössbauer and APXS identified jarosite, sulfates, and hematite concretions — minerals that form only in liquid water — the first in-situ mineralogical evidence of an ancient aqueous environment; this is distinct from Mariner 9's ORBITAL channels (obs#2) and Pathfinder's GROUND-LEVEL flood MORPHOLOGY (obs#3), and the orbital hematite signal that selected Meridiani was from Mars Global Surveyor, not Odyssey.
5. **The APXS on the MERs is "Alpha PARTICLE," a redesigned successor to Pathfinder's "Alpha Proton."** Same abbreviation (APXS), flipped expansion; the proton-scattering channel was de-emphasized (Rieder et al. 2003). The catalog does not conflate the two instruments and does not re-claim Pathfinder's mobile-rover rock-geochemistry first.
6. **The records are precise, separate numbers with careful scoping.** The marathon distance (42.195 km, 24 March 2015) and the final lifetime odometry (~45.16 km, 2018) are different numbers, never paired; the longest-operating record is "as of its end (2019)" (Curiosity surpassed the duration in 2023), while the farthest-driven (~45.16 km) off-world record still stands; the mark Opportunity surpassed was lunar (Lunokhod 2, ~39 km), not a Mars predecessor.
7. **The Mars Exploration Rovers are a long-traverse field-geology mission, not an orbiter, not the first Mars landing, and not the first rover.** They drove kilometers across the surface from rock to rock; they did NOT orbit, did NOT map the planet, and they are NOT the first Mars landing (Viking), the first orbiter (Mariner 9), or the first rover (Sojourner, Mars Pathfinder); they are distinct from Mariner 4 (1.36, the first Mars flyby).
8. **NASA / U.S.-mission framing is an accuracy requirement here.** The Mars Exploration Rover mission was a wholly NASA / United States mission, developed and operated by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory under NASA's Mars Exploration Program, with no foreign-partner caveat; the PI was Steven Squyres (Cornell) and the theme was "Follow the Water."
9. **A sustain references its predecessor exactly once, deliberately.** The only correct predecessor reference is a single nav-prev lineage note to v1.240 Mars Pathfinder (Mars obs#3), plus lineage references to Mariner 9 (1.239, obs#2), Viking (1.238, obs#1), and Mariner 4 (1.36, the first Mars flyby). The predecessors' anchors stay with them — 0 re-stamps.
10. **Clone source is Mars Pathfinder (a NASA Discovery surface-mobility mission: a lander plus the small Sojourner rover, Delta II 7925, 1996–1997); the Mars Exploration Rovers are a NASA twin-rover field-geology mission (Spirit and Opportunity, Delta II 7925 / 7925H, 2003–2019).** The most structural replacement in the rewrite is removing ALL Mars-Pathfinder-specific content — "Mars Pathfinder," "Sojourner," "the Discovery Program / faster-better-cheaper / MESUR Pathfinder," "~US$265M," "the single ~10.6 kg rover," "Ares Vallis," "the Carl Sagan Memorial Station," "the first-airbag-Mars-landing / first-rover-to-operate / first-mobile-rover-rock-geochemistry anchors," "the Alpha Proton APXS," "Barnacle Bill / Yogi," "Valerie Ambroise / the Sojourner-Truth naming," "the 4 December 1996 launch / 4 July 1997 landing / LC-17B," "the ~83-sol mission," "the record-1997-web-traffic note," and "the Oregon Tiger Beetle + Prairie Lupine pairing" — and replacing with the Mars Exploration Program origin and the "Follow the Water" theme, the twin identical ~185 kg rovers Spirit (MER-A, Gusev) and Opportunity (MER-B, Meridiani), the 2003 Delta II launches and January 2004 landings, the inherited airbag EDL, the Athena payload (Pancam, Mini-TES, Mössbauer, Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer, Microscopic Imager, Rock Abrasion Tool), Steven Squyres, jarosite + hematite blueberries + sulfates + cross-bedding (Opportunity / Meridiani), goethite + opaline silica + Home Plate + Columbia Hills (Spirit / Gusev), the Endeavour / Victoria craters and the Homestake gypsum vein, the first off-Earth marathon and the ~45.16 km record, the ~14-year / ~6-year durations, NSSDCA/COSPAR 2003-027A / 2003-032A, and the Wolverine + Common Juniper pairing.
