2. **First all-up flight test of Saturn V launch vehicle.** Apollo 4 (AS-501) on November 9, 1967 was the first all-up flight test of the Saturn V — Mueller's 1963 decision to commit every Saturn V subsystem to first-flight validation rather than progressive incremental stage-by-stage testing. The all-up gambit was contested between the Office of Manned Space Flight (Mueller's pro-all-up position) and the Marshall Space Flight Center (von Braun's preferred incremental approach). Apollo 4 vindicated the all-up decision: every Saturn V subsystem was validated on first flight; the schedule-compression delivered by all-up testing made the 1969 lunar landing technically achievable. CHAIN-CONVENTIONS v1.5 candidate amendment: formalize ALL-UP COMMITMENT as §6.6 process-variant complement to LIFT-AND-RESET (first PROCESS-VARIANT pair under §6.6).

3. **First launch from KSC Launch Complex 39A.** Apollo 4 was the inaugural launch from LC-39A, the lunar-program-purpose-built complex at Kennedy Space Center constructed for Saturn V launches. LC-39A would subsequently host every Saturn V launch through Skylab 1 (SA-513), every Space Shuttle mission through STS-135 (with shared use by LC-39B), and from 2017 onward all SpaceX Falcon 9 / Falcon Heavy launches under the SpaceX-NASA lease. The historical-continuity thread is direct: the operational-launch-control architecture established at LC-39A for Apollo 4 — including the Mobile Launcher Platform + Crawler-Transporter + Vehicle Assembly Building + Launch Control Center integration — is the architectural primitive that subsequent generations of US human spaceflight launches inherit.

4. **CSM heat-shield validation at lunar-return reentry velocity.** The S-IVB second-burn restart placed the CSM stack on a high-apogee elliptical orbit; the SPS engine then drove the CSM to a reentry trajectory at 36,545 ft/s — within 200 ft/s of an actual Apollo lunar-return reentry. The Block I CM-017 heat-shield validated successfully across the lunar-return-velocity reentry envelope. This was the only test opportunity for the Block I heat-shield architecture (subsequent flights used the Block II redesign incorporating Apollo 1 fire-investigation findings); the validation was sufficient to clear the heat-shield design space for Apollo 8 (December 1968) actual lunar-return reentry.
