## Part B: Long-billed Curlew — The Fourth Scolopacidae and the Longest North American Shorebird Bill

- **Fourth Scolopacidae in the Engine.** The sandpiper family reaches its fourth consecutive engine degree (168-171) with the Long-billed Curlew, following the **Wilson's Snipe** at 168 (mechanical winnowing), the **Dunlin flock** at 169 (collective murmuration peep), and the **Whimbrel** at 170 (rapid seven-note whistled call); four distinct acoustic strategies from one family across four consecutive engine degrees, now the longest single-family run of the engine's first 171 degrees.
- **Second Consecutive Numenius Degree.** The Long-billed Curlew at degree 171 completes the two-degree Numenius run initiated by the Whimbrel at 170, registering the curlew pair as a first-class genus-level structural event; the within-genus bill-length variation from 8-9 cm (Whimbrel) to 20+ cm (Long-billed Curlew) documents the full decurved-bill spectrum within one lineage.
- **Longest Bill of Any North American Shorebird.** The Long-billed Curlew's bill exceeds **20 centimeters (8 inches)** in adult females — the longest bill of any North American shorebird and one of the longest shorebird bills in the world; the bill is dramatically downward-curving, sharply pointed, and adapted for probing deep into sand and mud burrows to extract fiddler crabs, marine worms, mollusks, and other invertebrates.
- **Emphatic Cur-LEE Whistle at E=5.** The defining acoustic signature is a loud, two-syllable, rising **cur-LEE** call delivered during display flights over short-grass prairie breeding grounds and during coastal wintering; louder and more emphatic than the Whimbrel's rapid seven-note whistled call at E=4, reflecting the Long-billed Curlew's larger body size (50-65 cm) and grassland breeding-habitat acoustic demands.
- **Grassland Breeding in the Great Basin and Interior West.** Breeds on short-grass prairie and sagebrush steppe across **Oregon**, **Idaho**, **Montana**, **Wyoming**, **Nevada**, **Utah**, and parts of Canadian prairies; interior-continental grassland breeding habitat contrasts dramatically with the Whimbrel's Arctic-tundra breeding at high latitudes and demonstrates that the Numenius genus encompasses multiple distinct breeding-habitat strategies.
- **Coastal and Agricultural Wintering.** Winters on Pacific coast mudflats and estuaries including **Washington coast** (Willapa Bay, Grays Harbor), **Puget Sound** tidal flats (Padilla Bay, Samish Bay, Skagit River estuary), **Oregon coast**, **California coast**, and **Mexican Pacific** shorelines; also winters on agricultural fields in California's Central Valley and parts of Texas and Arizona.
- **Largest North American Shorebird.** At 50-65 centimeters length with a wingspan approaching 100 centimeters, the Long-billed Curlew is the largest North American shorebird by both body and bill measurements; the size contrasts with the Whimbrel's 40-46 centimeter length and establishes the Long-billed Curlew as the physical peak of the Numenius lineage in North America.
- **Cryptic Buff-and-Cinnamon Plumage.** The subtle buff, cinnamon, and mottled-brown plumage provides crypsis on short-grass prairie breeding grounds and on mudflat wintering grounds; the plumage contrasts with the Whimbrel's darker crown-striped pattern and demonstrates within-genus plumage variation paralleling the within-genus bill-length and acoustic variation.
