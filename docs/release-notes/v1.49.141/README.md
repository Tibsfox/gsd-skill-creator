## Summary

The erased and the difficult. Patti Bown was one of the first Black women to lead a hard bop piano trio, recorded for Columbia, worked with Quincy Jones and Oliver Nelson — then disappeared from the canon. The Pacific-slope Flycatcher is literally named "difficilis" because you cannot identify it by sight — only by voice. Both teach the same lesson: identity defined by what you DO (play, sing), not what you look like.

Degree 6 introduces three structural firsts for the species engine: the first neotropical migrant (seasonal presence/absence), the first riparian habitat species, and the principle that voice IS identity (the 1989 taxonomic split was driven entirely by vocal differences). For the artist engine: the gender dimension deepens (Bown as instrumentalist faced higher barriers than Anderson as vocalist), and "The Erasure" becomes the 7th artist-city pattern.

### Key Findings
- **The Erasure pattern:** Bown left Seattle, succeeded in NYC, then was erased from the canon. The dark twin of The Export. The engine must document who the industry forgot.
- **Piano lineage inverts completely:** Berry (stride/stayed/recognized/male) → Bown (hard bop/left/erased/female). Every dimension reverses.
- **Voice as primary identity:** The flycatcher's 1989 split was entirely voice-driven. In jazz: you know Coltrane by his sound, not his face. Identity lives in the signal, not the image.
- **First migrant:** Seasonal presence introduces temporal envelope — the flycatcher is NOT always there. Absence is data.
- **Progression:** silence → cascade → drone → language → motif → metronome → **upslurred question**
- **6 of 7 degrees are Central District jazz.** Geographic rotation begins at degree 7.
