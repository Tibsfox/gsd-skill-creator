## Summary

The dream haze and the forest coo. Degree 56 extends the folk-adjacent streak to **twelve consecutive degrees** -- a record that now stretches from degree 45 through degree 56 without interruption, the dodecad -- and introduces **Columbidae** into the species engine as the **23rd family** and the first representative of **Columbiformes**, the engine's **6th order**. **Lemolo** is the dreampop/indie folk duo from Capitol Hill whose three albums across seven years wrap folk songwriting in reverb-drenched atmospheric production, creating music that sounds like it was recorded inside a waterfall -- which is appropriate, given that they named themselves after Lemolo Falls in Oregon's Umpqua National Forest. **Band-tailed Pigeon** is the largest native pigeon in western North America, the forest pigeon whose deep, soft, owl-like cooing resonates from inside the canopy of mature mixed forest at 400-600 Hz -- the lowest-frequency vocalization in the engine by a wide margin, an order of magnitude below the passerine chorus, opening the bass register for the first time in 57 degrees. The dream haze (Part A's 57th acoustic element) is the atmospheric quality produced when reverb-drenched layered vocals shimmer over folk-rooted instrumentation, dissolving the boundaries between voice, room, and electronic texture into a single luminous field. The forest coo (Part B's 57th acoustic element) is the deep, soft, owl-like cooing that resonates from inside the canopy of mature mixed forest, so low and quiet it might be mistaken for a distant owl or the wind through a hollow trunk. Both sides of degree 56 operate on the same principle: what is quiet can be immense -- two people in a room producing a sound larger than two bodies should create, one pigeon in the canopy producing the lowest voice in the forest.

Lemolo is a duo consisting of **Meagan Grandall** (vocals, guitar, keyboards) and **Kendra Cox** (drums, percussion, vocals), based in Seattle's **Capitol Hill** neighborhood. The duo formed in 2011, naming themselves after **Lemolo Falls**, a 100-foot cascade on the North Umpqua River in Oregon's Umpqua National Forest, surrounded by old-growth Douglas fir and western red cedar. The word "Lemolo" derives from **Chinook Jargon**, the pidgin trade language of the Pacific Northwest, where it meant "wild" or "untamed" -- a name carrying indigenous linguistic heritage through the Chinook Jargon layer, three layers of borrowing from a dead trade language to an Oregon waterfall to two musicians in Seattle. Lemolo's music fuses folk songwriting foundations -- acoustic guitar, lyrical narrative, melodic structure, intimate vocal delivery -- with dreampop production aesthetics: heavy reverb, layered vocals, shimmering electronic textures, and a sense of vast atmospheric space. The production philosophy is **additive rather than substitutive**: the folk bones remain visible beneath the dream skin. The guitar is still there, the voice is still there, the song structure is still there, but the reverb transforms the room into a cathedral, the layered vocals transform two voices into twenty, and the electronic shimmer transforms the acoustic into the ethereal. This additive approach is what keeps Lemolo within the Indie Folk classification despite the dreampop production: the folk is not replaced, it is enhanced.

The three albums trace the duo's arc across seven years. ***The Kaleidoscope*** (2012, self-released) established the core sonic identity: folk melodies sung through a dreampop filter, the kaleidoscope as production metaphor -- a single image refracted into multiple overlapping patterns that shift with each rotation. ***Swirl*** (2014, Tender Loving Empire) moved the duo from self-release to **Tender Loving Empire**, a Portland-based independent label and retail collective whose identity -- handmade goods alongside curated music, a physical retail presence doubling as gallery and gift shop, an emphasis on the PNW as creative identity -- aligns with Lemolo's aesthetic. The Tender Loving Empire connection activates a **Portland-Seattle corridor** through the I-5 corridor as a single creative market, introducing a Portland node into the Seattle 360 engine for the first time. ***Today, I Am Well*** (2018, self-released) returned to complete independence -- a reverse of the typical trajectory where artists seek progressively larger institutional support. The title is a present-tense affirmation: not "I was well" or "I will be well" but "today, I am well." The comma isolates the temporal marker -- *today* specifically, this moment, the daily practice of affirming wellness. The seven-year arc from debut through label partnership to confident self-release mirrors Shelby Earl's three-album arc at degree 55: three albums, seven years, widening intervals (two years between first and second, four years between second and third). Meanwhile, in the canopy of a mature mixed forest in western Washington -- Douglas fir, western hemlock, bigleaf maple, western red cedar -- a large, handsome, blue-gray pigeon with a white crescent on its nape and an iridescent bronze-green hindneck coos softly from a concealed perch among the upper branches. It does not sound like a city pigeon. It sounds like something ancient and arboreal, a voice from inside the forest canopy. The Band-tailed Pigeon (*Patagioenas fasciata*) is the **largest native pigeon in western North America** at 240-390 grams -- roughly 25-35 times heavier than the wrens at degrees 1 and 55 (8-12 grams). The genus name *Patagioenas* means "clattering pigeon" (Ancient Greek *patageo* "to clatter" + *oinas* "pigeon"), referencing the loud wingclaps on takeoff. The species epithet *fasciata* means "banded" (Latin *fascia* "band, stripe"), referencing the pale gray terminal band on the tail -- the field mark that gives the species its common name. Full translation: "banded clattering-pigeon." The pigeon carries the conservation legacy of the most famous bird extinction in North American history: the **Passenger Pigeon** (*Ectopistes migratorius*), driven from billions to zero between the 1870s and 1914. The Band-tailed Pigeon suffered its own severe population decline from market hunting but was saved by the conservation laws that the Passenger Pigeon's death helped create -- the Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918. The ghost of *Ectopistes* haunts every *Patagioenas*. The species recovers slowly because of its **single-egg clutch** -- one egg per nesting attempt, one to two broods per year, the lowest reproductive rate among the engine's recent entries. Each bird is an investment. Each lost adult takes years to replace.

The coo is a fundamentally different vocal mechanism: produced by the syrinx, shaped by the inflatable crop and large body mass into a woodwind tone rather than a reed instrument.

- **TAXONOMY: PATAGIOENAS FASCIATA (Say, 1823):** *Patagioenas* = "clattering pigeon" (Ancient Greek *patageo* "to clatter" + *oinas* "pigeon"). *fasciata* = "banded" (Latin *fascia* "band, stripe"). Full translation: "banded clattering-pigeon" -- tail mark and flight noise. Genus was split from *Columba* (Old World pigeons) based on molecular phylogenetics. Contains approximately 17 New World species. Largest native pigeon in western North America: 33-40 cm, 240-390 g. Large blue-gray body, white nape crescent, iridescent bronze-green hindneck, yellow bill with dark tip, gray terminal tail band. Pale yellow to gold eyes with pinkish orbital ring.

- **THE SOUND: THE FOREST COO -- 57th Acoustic Element (Species-Side):** Deep, soft, owl-like cooing at 300-800 Hz (fundamental 400-600 Hz). The lowest-frequency vocalization in the engine by a wide margin. Previous lowest: Northern Spotted Owl (degree 4) at ~500-800 Hz. No passerine below ~1,500 Hz fundamental. Four-octave span from pigeon (400 Hz) to Golden-crowned Kinglet (10,000+ Hz). Each coo ~0.5-1.0 seconds, delivered in bouts of 2-6 repetitions from concealed canopy perch. Quality: deep, soft, hollow, resonant, breathy, woodwind-like. Distinct from all nearby elements: torrent-song (52, loud), corvid alarm (53, harsh), sap-well rhythm (54, percussive), yard song (55, bright melodious learned). The forest coo differs from everything.

- **PASSERIFORMES INTERRUPTED AGAIN:** Second break in three degrees. Pattern: 16-degree streak (38-53), Piciformes (54), Passeriformes (55), Columbiformes (56). Two non-passerine orders in three degrees. Passeriformes still 49 of 57 (86.0%) but diversifying.

- **E=1: THE QUIET VOICE OF THE CANOPY:** E=1 rises to 5 entries (8.8%). Previous E=1: Golden-crowned Kinglet (8), Bushtit (10), Pine Siskin (12), Brown Creeper (51) -- all tiny birds, high-thin sounds. The pigeon is E=1 because cooing is inherently soft, not because the bird is small. A large bird, quietly cooing. New path to E=1. Quiet-side majority: E1+E2+E3 = 32 of 57 (56.1%).

- **HABITAT: THE CANOPY PIGEON:** Mature mixed conifer and hardwood forests. Douglas fir, western hemlock, western red cedar, bigleaf maple, red alder, Garry oak. Feeds on acorns, berries, seeds. Strong affinity for mineral springs and salt licks. Year-round resident with altitudinal migration in western Washington. The only native pigeon in most PNW forests -- not the urban Rock Pigeon.

- **DIET: THE FIRST LARGE FRUGIVORE:** Primarily frugivore and granivore. Acorns (especially Garry oak), cascara, elderberry, huckleberry, madrone berries, salal, dogwood. Important seed disperser for PNW plants. Mineral springs for detoxification of secondary plant compounds. A fundamentally different trophic role than every other species in the engine.

- **CONSERVATION: THE GHOST OF THE PASSENGER PIGEON:** Passenger Pigeon driven from billions to zero (1870s-1914). Band-tailed Pigeon suffered parallel decline from market hunting. Saved by Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918. Single-egg clutch, 1-2 broods per year -- slowest reproductive rate in the engine's recent entries. Populations declining in parts of range. IUCN Least Concern but designated game bird of conservation concern by USFWS. Threats: logging of mature forest, hunting, climate change, invasive Eurasian Collared-Dove.

- **NESTING: THE SINGLE-EGG STRATEGY:** Flimsy twig platform in canopy. One egg per clutch. 18-20 days incubation, shared. 25-30 days nestling period. Both parents produce crop milk. 1-2 broods per season. Low reproductive rate is the species' Achilles' heel: the same strategy that doomed the Passenger Pigeon.

- **PLUMAGE: THE WHITE CRESCENT AND IRIDESCENT NAPE:** Soft blue-gray to purple-gray overall. Diagnostic white nape crescent. Iridescent bronze-green to bronze-purple hindneck. Wine-pink breast wash. Gray terminal tail band (namesake). Yellow bill with dark tip. Structural coloration in feather barbules.

- **CSV SUBSTITUTION: ENSATINA REPLACED -- Twelfth Consecutive:** Ensatina salamander (*Ensatina eschscholtzii*) replaced by Band-tailed Pigeon. Both year-round residents of moist Pacific Northwest forests. Both E=1. Both quiet. Salamander on the moist forest floor; pigeon in the canopy above. Twelfth consecutive amphibian-class replacement (45-56). Engine continues through lungless salamander zone into plethodontid heartland.

### Parallel: Lemolo x Band-tailed Pigeon

Four resonance axes:

**1. Quiet immensity -- two people fill a room, one pigeon anchors the bass.** Lemolo produces a sound vastly larger than two bodies should create through reverb, layering, and electronic atmosphere. The Band-tailed Pigeon, the largest body in the recent engine, produces the softest sound. Both demonstrate that scale and volume are independent. The duo sounds enormous at E=2. The pigeon sounds ancient at E=1. Neither overwhelms. Both immerse.

**2. Atmospheric overlap -- dream haze and forest coo both create sonic weather.** The dream haze is the dissolution of boundaries between voice, room, and texture -- sound that the listener moves through rather than listens to from outside. The forest coo is a deep resonance that fills the canopy of mature forest -- a sound you notice when the forest is quiet, a sound that seems to come from everywhere and nowhere. Both are atmospheric: neither discrete events nor sharp signals but ambient textures that fill space. The dream haze fills the studio. The forest coo fills the canopy.

**3. Shared landscape -- Lemolo Falls and the Band-tailed Pigeon's forest.** Lemolo Falls is in Oregon's Umpqua National Forest, a 100-foot cascade surrounded by old-growth Douglas fir and western red cedar. The Band-tailed Pigeon occupies the canopy of mature mixed forest including Douglas fir and western red cedar throughout the Pacific Northwest -- including the Umpqua drainage. The duo's name and the pigeon's habitat overlap in the same old-growth landscape 280 miles south of Seattle. The name carries the sound of falling water; the pigeon carries the sound of the canopy above the falls.

**4. Twelve-degree convergence -- dual rails from degree 45 through 56.** The twelfth consecutive folk-adjacent degree and the twelfth consecutive amphibian CSV substitution reach the same count at the same degree. Dual structural rails running in parallel for twelve degrees without interruption. The folk zone defines the artist-side texture. The amphibian belt defines the species-side transformation. Both streaks enter their second dozen together.
