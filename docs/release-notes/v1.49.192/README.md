**Lemolo** consists of **Meagan Grandall** (vocals, guitar, keyboards) and **Kendra Cox** (drums, percussion, vocals), based in Seattle's Capitol Hill neighborhood. The duo formed in 2011, naming themselves after Lemolo Falls in Oregon's **Umpqua National Forest**, surrounded by old-growth **Douglas fir** and **western red cedar**. The word "Lemolo" derives from **Chinook Jargon**, the pidgin trade language of the Pacific Northwest, where it meant "wild" or "untamed" -- a name carrying indigenous linguistic heritage through three layers of borrowing: a word from a dead trade language to an Oregon waterfall to two musicians in Seattle. Lemolo's music fuses folk songwriting foundations -- acoustic guitar, lyrical narrative, melodic structure, intimate vocal delivery -- with dreampop production aesthetics: heavy reverb, layered vocals, shimmering electronic textures, and a sense of vast atmospheric space. The production philosophy is **additive rather than substitutive**: the folk bones remain visible beneath the dream skin. The guitar is still there, the voice is still there, the song structure is still there, but the reverb transforms the room into a cathedral, the layered vocals transform two voices into twenty, and the electronic shimmer transforms the acoustic into the ethereal. This additive approach is what keeps Lemolo within the **Indie Folk** classification despite the dreampop production: the folk is not replaced, it is enhanced.

The Band-tailed Pigeon (*Patagioenas fasciata*) is the **largest native pigeon in western North America** at 240-390 grams -- roughly 25-35 times heavier than the wrens at degrees 1 and 55 (8-12 grams). The genus name *Patagioenas* means "clattering pigeon" (Ancient Greek *patageo* "to clatter" + *oinas* "pigeon"), referencing the loud wingclaps on takeoff. The species epithet *fasciata* means "banded" (Latin *fascia* "band, stripe"), referencing the pale gray terminal band on the tail -- the field mark that gives the species its common name. Full translation: "banded clattering-pigeon." The genus *Patagioenas* was split from *Columba* (Old World pigeons) based on molecular phylogenetics; *Patagioenas* contains approximately 17 New World species, with *fasciata* the most widespread and most northerly-distributed. The taxonomy carries naming logic distinct from earlier engine entries: the **Pacific Wren**'s *Troglodytes pacificus* foregrounds habitat (cave diver), the Bewick's Wren's *Thryomanes bewickii* foregrounds temperament (reed madness), and now *Patagioenas fasciata* foregrounds **mechanism** (the wingclap, the field mark). Three naming philosophies. Three genera. Three approaches to scientific identity.

The Band-tailed Pigeon carries the conservation legacy of the most famous bird extinction in North American history: the **Passenger Pigeon** (*Ectopistes migratorius*), driven from billions to zero between the 1870s and 1914. The Band-tailed Pigeon suffered its own severe population decline from market hunting in the late 19th and early 20th centuries but was saved by the conservation laws that the Passenger Pigeon's death helped create -- the **Migratory Bird Treaty Act** of 1918. The ghost of *Ectopistes* haunts every *Patagioenas*. The species recovers slowly because of its **single-egg clutch** -- one egg per nesting attempt, one to two broods per year, the lowest reproductive rate among the engine's recent entries. Each bird is an investment. Each lost adult takes years to replace. The conservation arc adds a dimension absent from earlier species entries: the engine now documents not only what a species sounds like and where it lives, but what laws were written to keep it alive. Without the Migratory Bird Treaty Act, the forest coo would already be a memory. The Passenger Pigeon's silence funded the Band-tailed Pigeon's survival.

## Part B: Band-tailed Pigeon -- The Forest Coo

Full deep research covering the Band-tailed Pigeon as the first Columbidae, the first Columbiformes, the lowest-frequency vocalization, and the Passenger Pigeon's conservation shadow:

- **Columbidae Enters Engine -- 23rd Family, 6th Order, First Pigeon.** Triple structural event: new family, new order, new acoustic character. Columbidae is a cosmopolitan family of approximately 350 species. Columbiformes is the 6th order -- first new order since Apodiformes at degree 35, breaking a 21-degree gap. No pigeon, no dove, no cooing in 56 degrees. The engine has heard cascades, trills, buzzes, whistles, drums, dives, screams, alarms, spirals, and torrents. It has never heard a coo. The coo is a fundamentally different vocal mechanism: produced by the syrinx, shaped by the inflatable crop and large body mass into a woodwind tone rather than a reed instrument. Six orders in 57 entries: Pelecaniformes, Passeriformes, Strigiformes, Piciformes, Apodiformes, Columbiformes.
- **Taxonomy: Patagioenas fasciata (Say, 1823) -- The Banded Clattering-Pigeon.** *Patagioenas* = "clattering pigeon" (Ancient Greek *patageo* "to clatter" + *oinas* "pigeon"). *fasciata* = "banded" (Latin *fascia* "band, stripe"). Full translation: "banded clattering-pigeon" -- tail mark and flight noise. Genus was split from *Columba* (Old World pigeons) based on molecular phylogenetics. Contains approximately 17 New World species. Largest native pigeon in western North America: 33-40 cm, 240-390 g. Large blue-gray body, white nape crescent, iridescent bronze-green hindneck, yellow bill with dark tip, gray terminal tail band. Pale yellow to gold eyes with pinkish orbital ring.
- **The Sound: The Forest Coo -- Fifty-Seventh Acoustic Element (Species-Side).** Deep, soft, owl-like cooing at 300-800 Hz (fundamental 400-600 Hz). The lowest-frequency vocalization in the engine by a wide margin. Previous lowest: Northern Spotted Owl (degree 4) at ~500-800 Hz. No passerine below ~1,500 Hz fundamental. Four-octave span from pigeon (400 Hz) to Golden-crowned Kinglet (10,000+ Hz). Each coo ~0.5-1.0 seconds, delivered in bouts of 2-6 repetitions from concealed canopy perch. Quality: deep, soft, hollow, resonant, breathy, woodwind-like. Distinct from all nearby elements: torrent-song (52, loud), corvid alarm (53, harsh), sap-well rhythm (54, percussive), yard song (55, bright melodious learned). The forest coo differs from everything.
- **Passeriformes Interrupted Again -- Second Break in Three Degrees.** Pattern: 16-degree streak (38-53), Piciformes (54), Passeriformes (55), Columbiformes (56). Two non-passerine orders in three degrees. Passeriformes still 49 of 57 (86.0%) but diversifying. The order lock loosens. The engine admits non-passerine voices with increasing frequency, signaling the second-pass diversification phase rather than first-pass passerine saturation.
- **E=1: The Quiet Voice of the Canopy -- New Path to Quiet.** E=1 rises to 5 entries (8.8%). Previous E=1: Golden-crowned Kinglet (8), Bushtit (10), Pine Siskin (12), Brown Creeper (51) -- all tiny birds, high-thin sounds. The pigeon is E=1 because cooing is inherently soft, not because the bird is small. A large bird, quietly cooing. New path to E=1. Quiet-side majority: E1+E2+E3 = 32 of 57 (56.1%). The category itself is reconfigured: E=1 includes both "tiny and thin" and "large and resonant." Acoustic presence relative to the surrounding soundscape, not absolute volume.
- **Habitat: The Canopy Pigeon -- Mature Mixed Forest, Year-Round Resident.** Mature mixed conifer and hardwood forests. Douglas fir, western hemlock, western red cedar, bigleaf maple, red alder, Garry oak. Feeds on acorns, berries, seeds. Strong affinity for mineral springs and salt licks for detoxification of secondary plant compounds. Year-round resident with altitudinal migration in western Washington. The only native pigeon in most PNW forests -- not the urban Rock Pigeon (*Columba livia*, introduced). Distinct ecological niche: the canopy frugivore-granivore.
- **Diet: The First Large Frugivore in the Engine.** Primarily frugivore and granivore. Acorns (especially Garry oak), cascara, elderberry, huckleberry, madrone berries, salal, dogwood. Important seed disperser for PNW plants. Mineral springs visited for calcium and sodium supplementation -- the salt-lick behavior is documented across the species' range. A fundamentally different trophic role than every other species in the engine: not insectivore (most passerines), not nectar-feeder (Anna's Hummingbird), not piscivore (Great Blue Heron), not fungivore. The pigeon as forest fruit-and-seed specialist.
- **Conservation: The Ghost of the Passenger Pigeon -- Migratory Bird Treaty Act 1918.** Passenger Pigeon driven from billions to zero (1870s-1914). Band-tailed Pigeon suffered parallel decline from market hunting in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Saved by Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918. Single-egg clutch, 1-2 broods per year -- slowest reproductive rate in the engine's recent entries. Populations declining in parts of range. IUCN Least Concern but designated game bird of conservation concern by USFWS. Threats: logging of mature forest, hunting, climate change, invasive Eurasian Collared-Dove competition. The conservation arc adds documentation: what laws keep this species alive.
- **Nesting: The Single-Egg Strategy -- Slow Recovery, K-Selected.** Flimsy twig platform in canopy. One egg per clutch. 18-20 days incubation, shared by both parents. 25-30 days nestling period. Both parents produce crop milk (a nutrient-rich secretion from the crop lining; one of the few vertebrates outside mammals that produces milk-equivalent for offspring). 1-2 broods per season. Low reproductive rate is the species' Achilles' heel: the same strategy that doomed the Passenger Pigeon.
- **Plumage: The White Crescent and Iridescent Nape.** Soft blue-gray to purple-gray overall. Diagnostic white nape crescent. Iridescent bronze-green to bronze-purple hindneck. Wine-pink breast wash. Gray terminal tail band (namesake). Yellow bill with dark tip. Structural coloration in feather barbules creates the iridescent effect -- not pigment but interference patterns from microscopic feather architecture. The plumage is sober from a distance, spectacular up close.
- **CSV Substitution: Ensatina Replaced -- Twelfth Consecutive Amphibian-Class Replacement.** Ensatina salamander (*Ensatina eschscholtzii*) replaced by Band-tailed Pigeon. Both year-round residents of moist Pacific Northwest forests. Both E=1. Both quiet. Salamander on the moist forest floor; pigeon in the canopy above. Twelfth consecutive amphibian-class replacement (45-56). Engine continues through lungless salamander zone into plethodontid heartland. The substitution preserves habitat (Pacific Northwest mature forest), residency (year-round), and energy (E=1).
