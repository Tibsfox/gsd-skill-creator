## Surprises

- **A great observatory rode the Space Shuttle up and was lifted out of the payload bay by a crew.** CGRO launched 5 April 1991 aboard the Space Shuttle Atlantis (STS-37) and was deployed from the orbiter on 7 April 1991; a spacewalk was needed to free a stuck high-gain antenna before the ~16-tonne observatory could be released into low Earth orbit, an orbit chosen to sit below the Van Allen radiation belts.
- **A whole-sky watch found gamma-ray bursts scattered evenly across the sky.** BATSE's eight detectors on the corners of the spacecraft watched the whole sky and found the bursts distributed isotropically, with a deficit of faint bursts — the uniform scatter that placed them at cosmological distances, far beyond the Milky Way's disk (Meegan et al. 1992), rather than nearby within the Galaxy.
- **A telescope drew the first complete map of the whole sky in high-energy gamma rays.** EGRET produced the first complete all-sky map and source catalog in high-energy gamma rays (above ~100 MeV), cataloguing blazars, gamma-ray pulsars (such as the Geminga pulsar), and the diffuse Galactic glow — 271 sources in the Third EGRET Catalog (Hartman et al. 1999) — distinct from the earlier Galactic-plane map made by COS-B.
- **A working observatory was brought down on purpose to keep its re-entry safe.** After a gyroscope (No. 3) failed on 6 December 1999, NASA chose a controlled deorbit rather than risk an uncontrolled re-entry; CGRO's deorbit burns culminated in a re-entry over the Pacific (about 2,400 miles southeast of Hawaii) on 4 June 2000, after about nine years of operation.
- **A small lizard throws a bright twitching tail to a predator and runs.** The Western Skink (*Plestiodon skiltonianus*), a small smooth-scaled lizard of the dry, rocky, open country of the Pacific Northwest, has a juvenile with a vivid electric-blue tail; when seized, the skink can drop the tail (caudal autotomy), and the detached tail keeps twitching as a sudden bright decoy while the skink escapes (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a brief bright flash thrown to the dark, a mirror of the burst sky CGRO watched.
- **A dry-slope wildflower opens a sudden burst of fiery four-petalled flowers on open ground.** The Western Wallflower (*Erysimum capitatum*), a Pacific Northwest native of dry, open, rocky slopes and sagebrush country in the mustard family, opens a dense terminal cluster of bright orange-to-yellow four-petalled (cross-shaped) flowers atop an erect stalk — a flare of fiery color on the open dry ground (ecological description only) — a sudden bright flash from the dark, a mirror of the brief brilliant flashes CGRO caught from across the sky.
