**The Rooster-Tail Splash: The Defining Kinetic Acoustic Event.** The Dall's Porpoise's defining behavior is the **rooster-tail splash** — a V-shaped cone of spray thrown up at high speed as the animal swims just below the surface. The splash is created when the body travels near the surface but the dorsal area breaks through, generating a wake that sprays upward and backward into the characteristic rooster-tail pattern. At top speeds approaching **35 mph (55 km/h)** — the fastest porpoise on Earth — the rooster tail is visible from over a mile away and is reliable enough as a field-identification marker that experienced boaters identify the species by splash pattern alone, often before any part of the animal is visible. The rooster tail is the species' acoustic signature for the engine's purposes: an explosive, percussive, kinetic event whose splash sound and wake turbulence constitute the audible register even though the animal itself produces no sound humans can hear. This is a structural innovation in the engine's acoustic vocabulary — every prior species entry across 185 degrees has been calibrated against vocalization-based acoustic registers (song, call, roar, bark, bleat, cry, click, whistle), and Dall's Porpoise is the first entry whose defining acoustic event is purely kinetic. The engine calibrates this at **E=5**: not the low E=1-2 range that a silent-by-our-measure species might otherwise command, but the mid-range E=5 that reflects the rooster tail's high-energy physical presence. **Speed as sound** enters the acoustic vocabulary.

**NBHF Stealth Echolocation and Silence as Survival Strategy.** The Dall's Porpoise produces **no audible vocalization** — its echolocation clicks operate in the **Narrow-Band High-Frequency (NBHF)** range, typically 120-160 kHz, entirely above the approximately 20 kHz upper limit of human hearing. More significantly, these frequencies also operate **above the approximately 100 kHz hearing ceiling of orcas**, the Dall's Porpoise's primary predator. **Morisaka & Connor (2007)** proposed in the *Journal of Evolutionary Biology* that NBHF clicks in odontocetes co-evolved with whistle-loss as an anti-predator adaptation: porpoises echolocate and communicate at frequencies their most dangerous predator cannot detect, making their acoustic activity invisible to orca ears while still enabling navigation, prey detection, and social interaction. This stealth-echolocation hypothesis reframes the Dall's Porpoise's silence as a structural strategy rather than an acoustic deficit: **silence as survival**. The Phocoenidae family-level characteristic of NBHF clicks means that all porpoises share this trait, and the engine's documentation at degree 186 establishes the NBHF stealth-echolocation vocabulary as a first-class acoustic-strategy entry that will carry forward to subsequent Phocoenidae entries (beginning with the projected Harbor Porpoise at degree 187).

## Part B: Dall's Porpoise — The First Cetacean, the Rooster-Tail Splash, NBHF Stealth Echolocation, and Silence as Survival Strategy

- **First Phocoenidae and First Cetacean in the Engine.** The Dall's Porpoise (*Phocoenoides dalli*) enters the engine at degree 186 as the **FIRST PHOCOENIDAE** (porpoise family) and simultaneously the **FIRST CETACEAN** in the engine's first 186 degrees, opening the **order Cetacea** (whales, dolphins, porpoises), the **suborder Odontoceti** (toothed whales), the **family Phocoenidae** (porpoises), and the **genus *Phocoenoides*** simultaneously; the cetacean order enters as the third mammalian order in the engine after Carnivora (pinnipeds 180-184) and the Mustelidae family within Carnivora (Sea Otter 185).
- **Porpoise-vs-Dolphin Family-Level Distinctions.** Phocoenidae (porpoises) differ from Delphinidae (dolphins) at the family level: porpoises have **spade-shaped (flat) teeth** versus dolphins' conical teeth; porpoises are generally smaller and more robust-bodied than dolphins; porpoises are generally less social than dolphins with smaller typical group sizes; porpoises' acoustic registers are characteristically narrow-band high-frequency (NBHF) versus dolphins' broader-band whistle and click repertoires; the distinctions set up the projected Delphinidae entries (beginning with the **Pacific White-sided Dolphin** at degree 188) as structural counterparts to the Phocoenidae opening at degree 186.
- **The Rooster-Tail Splash at 35 mph.** The Dall's Porpoise's defining behavior is the **rooster-tail splash** — a V-shaped cone of spray thrown up as the animal swims just below the surface at high speed; the splash is generated when the body travels near the surface but the dorsal area breaks through, creating a wake that sprays upward and backward into the characteristic rooster-tail pattern visible from over a mile away; at top speeds approaching **35 mph (55 km/h)** — the **fastest porpoise on Earth** — the rooster tail serves as the primary field-identification marker, reliable enough that experienced boaters identify the species by splash pattern alone.
- **Speed as Sound: Kinetic Acoustic Event at E=5.** The rooster-tail splash is the first entry in the engine's acoustic vocabulary whose defining acoustic event is **purely kinetic** rather than vocal — the splash's audible signature comes from wake turbulence, water impact, and wave propagation rather than from a vocalization — and the engine calibrates this at **E=5** to reflect the high-energy physical presence of the kinetic event; **speed as sound** enters the engine's acoustic-theory vocabulary as a new first-class register distinct from every prior species' vocalization-based acoustic calibration.
- **NBHF Echolocation at 120-160 kHz.** The Dall's Porpoise's echolocation operates in the **Narrow-Band High-Frequency (NBHF)** range, typically 120-160 kHz, entirely above the approximately 20 kHz upper limit of human hearing; NBHF clicks are a family-level characteristic of Phocoenidae, distinguishing the porpoise acoustic repertoire from the broader-band clicks of Delphinidae; the ultrasonic frequencies enable navigation, prey detection, and social interaction without any audible signature that humans can perceive directly, making the species effectively silent to our ears.
- **Silence Above Orca Hearing: The Anti-Predator Stealth Hypothesis.** The NBHF frequencies operate above the approximately **100 kHz hearing ceiling of orcas**, the Dall's Porpoise's primary predator; **Morisaka & Connor (2007)** proposed in the *Journal of Evolutionary Biology* that NBHF clicks in odontocetes co-evolved with whistle-loss specifically as an anti-predator adaptation — porpoises echolocate and communicate at frequencies their most dangerous predator cannot detect, making acoustic activity invisible to orca ears while still enabling normal porpoise functioning; the silence-as-strategy framing reframes the Dall's Porpoise's effective silence as a deliberate structural adaptation rather than an acoustic deficit.
- **Black-and-White Tuxedo Coloring and Robust Body.** The Dall's Porpoise has distinctive **black-and-white coloring** — predominantly black body with a large white patch on the flank and belly, plus white frosting on the dorsal-fin tip — that leads to casual comparison with **orcas** (killer whales) even though the Dall's Porpoise is much smaller (adult length 1.8-2.2 m versus 6-8 m for orcas); the body is thick, muscular, and robust rather than streamlined-acrobatic, built for raw power and speed rather than the acrobatic displays characteristic of some dolphin species; the coloring may serve disruptive camouflage, species recognition, or thermoregulatory functions.
- **Puget Sound Population, Habitat, and Distribution.** Estimated 2,000-4,000 Dall's Porpoises inhabit Washington inland waters (Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, San Juan Islands), with tens of thousands more in the outer coast and broader North Pacific; habitat preference is deep-water channels and areas with strong tidal currents, found primarily in the deep channels of central and northern Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and around the San Juan Islands; year-round resident with higher sighting rates in fall and winter; typical group size 2-10 individuals, occasionally aggregating to 100+ during feeding events; frequent **bow-riding** interaction with vessels accelerating to match boat speeds and surfing the pressure wave.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Bridge-artist genre admissions preserve genre integrity while connecting to existing vocabulary.** Erica Campbell's admission as FIRST GOSPEL through her gospel/hip-hop/R&B/pop fusion aesthetic rather than through a traditional-gospel entry is a deliberate structural choice: the engine admits new genres through artists who bridge the new genre to already-established registers, preserving the integrity of the new genre while providing a genealogical connection the engine can recognize. Future compound-first entries should compound-open rather than artificially-sequence when the multiple openings arrive naturally through the same entry.
4. **The engine's acoustic vocabulary can be extended beyond vocalization-based registers through kinetic acoustic events.** The Dall's Porpoise's rooster-tail splash introduces the **kinetic acoustic event** as a first-class acoustic category where the defining acoustic signature comes from physical motion rather than vocal production. The **speed as sound** framing produces a defensible E-calibration (E=5 in this case) at a physical-presence level. Future silent-species or non-vocalizing-species documentation can reference this kinetic-acoustic-event vocabulary rather than requiring a vocalization-based retrofit.
5. **Silence can be documented as a deliberate structural strategy rather than as an acoustic deficit.** The NBHF stealth-echolocation hypothesis (Morisaka & Connor 2007) reframes the Dall's Porpoise's effective silence as an anti-predator evolutionary adaptation. **Silence as survival** enters the engine's acoustic-strategy vocabulary as a new first-class register, and future entries where an organism's silence is evolutionarily significant (mimicry, camouflage, stealth-hunting, anti-detection) can reference this silence-as-strategy framing rather than treating silence as mere absence of signal.
6. **Geographical specificity within multi-city regional documentation deepens the engine's coverage.** Tacoma's third appearance at degree 186 with Hilltop-neighborhood specificity extends the engine's non-Seattle PNW geography documentation beyond city-level naming to neighborhood-level specificity; future multi-city regional documentation should include the neighborhood-level cultural-context specificity when it provides structurally meaningful context (as Hilltop does for the Black church gospel tradition that anchors Erica Campbell's roots).
7. **Cross-sync principles are strongest when they articulate a shared structural dynamic rather than a shared surface feature.** The crossover-at-speed principle organizes the paired coordinate at degree 186 not through a shared surface feature (both halves are "about crossings" in some superficial sense) but through a shared structural dynamic (both defining signatures are generated by the velocity of a crossing rather than by either endpoint). Future cross-sync formulations should target shared structural dynamics over shared surface features to preserve the multiplicative-structural-statement quality of the paired-coordinate framing.
8. **Taxonomic order openings establish the framework for subsequent family and species openings within the order.** The Cetacea order opening at degree 186 establishes the cetacean-order framework within which subsequent Phocoenidae entries (Harbor Porpoise at projected 187) and the first Delphinidae entry (Pacific White-sided Dolphin at projected 188) will operate. The order-opening-as-framework pattern enables efficient documentation of subsequent family and species entries without re-deriving the order-level vocabulary at each subsequent entry.
9. **The E-calibration can be defensibly decoupled from vocalization intensity when the acoustic event is kinetic.** The Dall's Porpoise's E=5 calibration is based on the rooster-tail splash's kinetic energy rather than on any vocalization intensity (the species has no audible vocalization); the decoupling preserves the E-calibration's meaningfulness as a physical-presence measure rather than reducing it to a vocalization-intensity proxy. Future non-vocalizing-species documentation can reference this decoupling to calibrate E at the physical-presence level rather than forcing a vocalization-based reading that would understate the species' engine presence.
10. **The scientific-literature anchoring for cetacean acoustics should cite both the primary observations and the evolutionary-interpretation papers.** The Dall's Porpoise documentation cites Au et al. (1999) for harbor porpoise echolocation beam-pattern observations, Dahlheim & Awbrey (1986) for the early Dall's porpoise acoustic documentation, and Morisaka & Connor (2007) for the evolutionary interpretation of NBHF clicks as anti-predator adaptation. Future cetacean-acoustic documentation should preserve this layered citation pattern (observation-paper + evolutionary-interpretation-paper + ongoing-monitoring-program) to provide readers the full structural basis for the engine's acoustic-strategy claims.
