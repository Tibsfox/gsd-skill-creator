## What Could Be Better

- **Cosmic Zingaro's specific discography, platform presence, and collaborator network were not documented in detail.** The documentation preserved the genre-fluid wanderer register and the post-marine-mammal-block opening context but did not include specific track names, album titles, release dates, digital-platform stream counts, or specific collaborator identities for Cosmic Zingaro's work. Future genre-fluid wanderer documentation should include at least representative track or album examples and collaborator-network context to anchor the artist identity beyond the generic wanderer role; the current documentation functions at the register level rather than the individual-artist detail level.
- **The specific Sea Otter subspecies structure (*E. l. lutris*, *E. l. kenyoni*, *E. l. nereis*) was not fully elaborated.** The documentation treated the Sea Otter as a single species without fully elaborating the three recognized subspecies: **Asian sea otter** (*Enhydra lutris lutris*, Kuril Islands / Kamchatka / Commander Islands), **Northern sea otter** (*Enhydra lutris kenyoni*, Aleutian Islands / Alaska / British Columbia / Washington), and **Southern sea otter** (*Enhydra lutris nereis*, California). Each subspecies has distinct population history, conservation status, and regional ecology. Future Sea Otter documentation should include subspecies-level detail to preserve the regional variation that single-species framing collapses.
- **The Alaska Native and Coast Salish cultural relationships with Sea Otters were not documented.** The documentation focused on Western scientific and regulatory frameworks without documenting the cultural relationships that **Alaska Native** peoples (particularly Aleut/Unangan, Alutiiq, and Yup'ik communities) and **Coast Salish** peoples of the Pacific Northwest have with Sea Otters, including harvest traditions, cultural significance, and contemporary co-management arrangements. Future Sea Otter documentation should include Indigenous cultural context as first-class alongside the scientific and regulatory frameworks.
- **The commercial fur-trade economic history (companies, labor practices, port economies) was noted but not detailed.** The documentation preserved the 1741-1911 commercial sealing era and the 1911 treaty but did not detail the specific companies (Russian-American Company, Hudson's Bay Company, Alaska Commercial Company), the port economies (Kodiak, Sitka, Astoria, Vancouver Island), or the labor practices (including indentured Indigenous labor) that drove the commercial hunt. Future near-extinction historical documentation could benefit from economic-history layer detail alongside biological and treaty layers.
- **The relationship between Sea Otter range-edge populations and climate change was not detailed.** The documentation noted climate-change-related prey shifts as a current threat but did not detail the specific dynamics at the range edges: the southern edge in California where white shark predation has increased as warming waters shift shark ranges, and the northern/western edges in the Aleutians where orca predation has increased as declining steller sea lion and harbor seal populations have shifted orca diets. Future climate-change-threat documentation could benefit from range-edge dynamic detail.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Consecutive-degree superlative-passing establishes comparative magnitude vocabulary.** The densest-fur superlative passed from the Northern Fur Seal (300K hairs/sq in at 184) to the Sea Otter (1M hairs/sq in at 185) in a 3-3.3× single-degree magnitude increase. The consecutive-degree format establishes a comparative magnitude that neither single-degree citation would produce in isolation, and the engine's densest-fur vocabulary is now anchored on two data points rather than a single reference. Future consecutive-degree superlative-passing should be documented with explicit magnitude framing to preserve the comparative vocabulary.
2. **Family-returns should be framed structurally through the most-specialized-member lens.** Mustelidae returned to the engine at degree 185 through its most aquatic member (the only fully marine mustelid). The most-specialized-member framing ties the family's re-entry to the broader family ecology — the Sea Otter's marine specialization is what makes it structurally distinctive within Mustelidae — and future family returns should be framed similarly to place the returning family within its evolutionary and ecological context.
3. **Keystone-species and trophic-cascade concepts should be introduced with historical lineage.** The Sea Otter's keystone role was introduced with its full historical lineage (Paine 1966 Mukkaw Bay starfish experiment → Estes & Palmisano 1974 Sea Otter trophic cascade) rather than as received ecological wisdom. The historical-lineage framing places the engine's ecology vocabulary within the actual history of ecological theory and makes the concept earnable rather than assumed. Future ecological-concept entries should include similar lineage framing.
4. **Tool-use documentation should include technique specificity.** The Sea Otter's tool use was documented with specific technique detail (supine posture, chest anvil, forearm-pouch rock transport, generational transmission, individual preference variation) rather than as generic tool-use citation. The technique-specific pattern makes the behavioral claim verifiable and reader-useful, and should be the baseline for future tool-use or specialized-behavior documentation.
5. **Thermoregulation-mechanism landscapes are most useful when documented as multi-strategy contrasts.** The Sea Otter's no-blubber thermoregulation was documented in direct contrast to blubber-based (Phocidae), fur-plus-blubber (Otariidae), and blubber-dominant (cetaceans) strategies. The multi-strategy contrast makes the engine's thermoregulation vocabulary explicit as a landscape of structurally distinct approaches rather than a single default. Future physiological-strategy documentation should use multi-strategy contrast framing when sufficient comparative data exist.
6. **Metabolic demand is best explained causally through its adaptation-driven necessity.** The 25%-of-body-weight daily feeding requirement was explained as a causal consequence of the no-blubber thermoregulation strategy — no blubber means metabolic heat production, which requires extreme food intake — rather than presented as an isolated factoid. Causal framing ties life-history facts to their adaptation-driven necessity and makes the engine's life-history documentation coherent as a structural system rather than a factoid collection.
7. **Block closures and openings are themselves first-class engine events, not neutral markers.** The post-marine-mammal-block opening at degree 185 was documented as a structural transition event rather than a sequential marker between two blocks. Block closures (184) and openings (185) are first-class engine events that deserve explicit documentation; future multi-degree sequence transitions should receive similar treatment rather than being collapsed into the degree-level events they bookend.
8. **Cross-sync parallels are most useful when they operate at multiple structural levels simultaneously.** The five cross-sync parallels at degree 185 (architecture-beneath-surface, superlative-shift-from-prior-degree, post-near-collapse-recovery, boundary-crossing, small-agent-holds-large-system) each operated at a different structural level (compositional, magnitudinal, temporal, categorical, scalar) and converted the paired-coordinate observation into a multiplicative structural statement at its own level.
