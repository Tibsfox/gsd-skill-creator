- ***Larus glaucescens* Taxonomy and the "Pale-Winged Gull" Etymology.** The Glaucous-winged Gull is scientifically named *Larus glaucescens* — with *Larus* as the Latin-derived genus name for gulls and *glaucescens* as the species name meaning "becoming glaucous" or "pale-grey-winged" (from the Greek *glaukos*, meaning "bluish-grey" or "pale blue-green"). The species name refers to the gull's distinctive wing coloration: the wingtip primaries are grey rather than black (as in the Herring Gull) or dark grey (as in the Western Gull), giving the wing a paler overall appearance in flight. The species was described by **Johann Friedrich Naumann** in the early 19th century, and the type locality is the Pacific coast of North America. The Glaucous-winged Gull is the most common large gull in the Pacific Northwest and the defining gull of the region in the same sense that the **Herring Gull** (*Larus argentatus*) is the Atlantic coast's defining gull and the **Western Gull** (*Larus occidentalis*) is the California coast's defining gull.
- **Geographic Range: Alaska to Northern California, PNW as Range Core.** The Glaucous-winged Gull's breeding range extends from the **Aleutian Islands** and coastal Alaska south through British Columbia, Washington, Oregon, and into northern California, with the Pacific Northwest as the range core and Puget Sound, the Washington coast, and the Salish Sea as the defining habitat. The species is a resident (non-migratory) in most of its range, though some populations move southward in winter. The range overlaps with the **Herring Gull** in the northern portions and with the **Western Gull** in the southern portions, and hybridization with both species is documented (the Glaucous-winged × Western Gull hybrid is sufficiently common along the northern California / Oregon coast that identification of individual gulls in the hybrid zone is a recognized ornithological challenge). The species' Pacific Northwest presence is year-round and ubiquitous: the gull is present at every interface of land and sea across the range core, from industrial waterfronts and urban rooftops to remote offshore islets and coastal cliffs.
- **Colonial Nesting: Protection Island, Smith Island, Destruction Island.** Glaucous-winged Gulls nest colonially on offshore rocks, coastal cliffs, and flat-topped structures throughout the range. Documented Puget Sound and Salish Sea colonial nesting sites include **Protection Island** (the large seabird colony site in the **Strait of Juan de Fuca** that also supports Rhinoceros Auklet and other seabirds), **Smith Island**, and **Destruction Island** (the Washington coast lighthouse island that also supports Steller Sea Lion haul-out, documented at degree 283). Colonies range from small (fewer than 100 nests) to large (several thousand nests), with the **Protection Island** colony being one of the largest in the Pacific Northwest. The colonial context is structurally important for the degree 289 documentation because the mew and alarm calls are most commonly produced at breeding colonies — the contact call (mew) is used among adults in the colony, and the alarm call is used in colony defense against avian and mammalian predators including **Bald Eagles**, **Peregrine Falcons**, raccoons, and mink.
- **The Mew Call at 1,000-3,000 Hz: The Signature Gull Contact Call.** The mew call of the Glaucous-winged Gull is a high-pitched, drawn-out "kee-ow" or "meee-ow" vocalization produced at a frequency range of approximately 1,000-3,000 Hz with peak energy around 1,500-2,000 Hz. The call is used in contact and low-intensity display contexts — pair bonding, parent-chick communication, and general colony acoustic signaling — and is the most commonly heard Glaucous-winged Gull vocalization away from the colony's most intense activity periods. The mew call is structurally similar to the mew calls of related large gulls (**Herring Gull**, **Western Gull**, **California Gull**) with species-specific pitch and duration variations that allow experienced listeners and acoustic analysis software to distinguish species at the recording level. The engine's documentation preserves the mew call as the first of the two Glaucous-winged Gull vocalizations registered at degree 289.
- **The Alarm Call at 500-5,000 Hz: Colony Defense Context.** The alarm call of the Glaucous-winged Gull is a rapid, short "kek-kek-kek" or "ha-ha-ha" vocalization produced at a frequency range of approximately 500-5,000 Hz with broadband energy distribution. The call is used in colony defense contexts — when avian predators (**Bald Eagles**, **Peregrine Falcons**), mammalian predators (raccoons, mink), or human approach trigger defensive aggregation — and is produced at high volume and high repetition rate to mobilize the colony's collective defense response. The alarm call is structurally distinct from the mew call in pitch, duration, and repetition rate, and the engine's documentation preserves the two-call register as the species' two most commonly produced colony-context vocalizations. The broader Glaucous-winged Gull vocal repertoire includes the long call (the sustained head-thrown-back "klee-aw-klee-aw-klee-aw" used in territorial display), the choke call (used in courtship), and the begging call (used by chicks), but the mew and alarm calls are the two the engine registers at first-class resolution for the degree 289 entry.
- **The Gull as Ambient Shoreline Acoustic Register.** The Glaucous-winged Gull's vocalizations constitute part of the ambient acoustic environment of the Pacific Northwest shoreline — always present, always recognizable, and producing the "gull sound" that is one of the most immediately recognizable acoustic registers of the coastal zone. - **Conservation Status: IUCN Least Concern, Stable Population.** The Glaucous-winged Gull is listed as **IUCN Least Concern** with a stable global population estimated at approximately 400,000 individuals across the breeding range. The species is protected under the **Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA) of 1918** in the United States and under equivalent legislation in Canada. No specific conservation concerns have been identified at the species level, though local colony-level impacts from human disturbance, oil spills, and marine debris ingestion are documented and contribute to ongoing colony-monitoring programs by the **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW)**, the **US Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS)**, and regional ornithological organizations including the **Washington Ornithological Society (WOS)**.
