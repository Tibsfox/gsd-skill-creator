3. **First in-situ lunar chemistry.** Anthony L. Turkevich's alpha-scattering spectrometer (University of Chicago Enrico Fermi Institute; built under JPL contract with flight-hardware engineering by Ernest J. Franzgrote; detector electronics and spectrum-analysis by James H. Patterson) operated for 83 cumulative hours on Mare Tranquillitatis via a curium-242 alpha-particle source irradiating a 100 cm² regolith sample with two solid-state semiconductor detectors recording the backscattered alpha-particle spectrum plus the proton emission from alpha-proton reactions on light nuclei. The result, reported in Turkevich, Franzgrote, and Patterson in *Science* 158:635-637 (November 3, 1967), established Mare Tranquillitatis regolith as compositionally **basaltic** — silicon dioxide approximately 58-63%, aluminum oxide approximately 14-15%, iron-plus-magnesium oxides 12-14% (strong mafic component), calcium oxide 6-7%. The result refuted the chondritic-Moon hypothesis and the highlands-anorthosite-maria hypothesis; it established the basaltic-maria consensus that Apollo 11 sample returns (July 1969) confirmed and refined into the specific high-titanium-basalt lunar composition variant. Turkevich's first-in-situ measurement required no retraction when higher-precision Apollo laboratory data became available.
