## Great Horned Owl -- The Streak Breaks

The Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*) enters the engine and **breaks the Anatidae streak** at 17 degrees. The longest single-family run in the engine's history is over. Strigidae -- the owl family -- enters for the first time.

This is the first new family since **Cinclidae** at degree 116 (American Dipper). That's 19 degrees since the last family change. The engine has been in Anatidae territory for so long that the shift feels tectonic.

The sound: a **deep, resonant *hoo-h'HOO-hoo-hoo*** -- the classic owl call that everyone recognizes but few can place. Five notes. The pattern is distinctive: two soft introductory hoots, one louder emphasized hoot, then two trailing hoots. The Great Horned Owl's call carries through forest at night with remarkable clarity. It is the sound of darkness, of old-growth, of the woods after midnight.

E=4. The call is deep and resonant but not loud by waterfowl standards. A Trumpeter Swan (E=6) or Canada Goose (E=5) produces more raw volume. The Great Horned Owl's power is in its carrying quality and its low frequency -- the call penetrates forest in a way that higher-pitched sounds cannot.

**Ecosystem shift: water to forest.** The Anatidae sequence (118-134) lived in water: rivers, lakes, bays, coastal zones, deltas, agricultural fields near water. The Great Horned Owl lives in **forest**. Old-growth, second-growth, suburban woodlots, parks -- anywhere with trees large enough for nesting. The habitat palette changes completely. The engine's acoustic environment shifts from open water and wind to enclosed canopy and darkness.

**300 psi talons.** The Great Horned Owl's grip strength is extraordinary -- approximately 300 pounds per square inch, comparable to much larger raptors. It takes prey that seems improbable for a bird its size: skunks (it has almost no sense of smell), porcupines, rabbits, and even other raptors including Barred Owls and Red-tailed Hawks. The apex nocturnal predator of North America.
