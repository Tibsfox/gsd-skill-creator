## Summary

**Valley of the Giants** arrives at E=6 -- the Seattle stoner rock project built around guitarist and vocalist Kris Norris and the dense, fuzz-saturated drone of Pacific Northwest heavy rock. Valley of the Giants operates in the stoner rock register: slow to mid-tempo, tuned down, riff-oriented, structurally patient in the way that all serious heavy music of the post-Melvins Pacific Northwest lineage tends to be. E=6 reflects a genre that is energetic within its own conventions -- stoner rock's energy is expressed through sustain and density rather than speed, and a mid-range assignment captures the band's place in the heavy rock spectrum accurately.

Stoner rock enters the engine at degree 255, two degrees after sludge entered at degree 253 through Helms Alee. The proximity is not coincidental -- stoner and sludge share a lineage, a tuning philosophy, and a set of influences, with sludge generally operating at slower tempos and heavier tones and stoner maintaining slightly more groove and rhythmic momentum. The Pacific Northwest has produced practitioners of both, and the engine is now moving through a concentrated passage of heavy genres that collectively document one of the region's most distinctive contributions to the American underground music scene.

On the soundscape side, **Log Truck Vibration** arrives at E=3 -- and it is the **FIRST ANTHROPOGENIC SOUND** in the SPS track's history. From degree 245 through degree 254, every SPS entry has been a natural or navigational acoustic event: rivers, waterfalls, rain, wind, tidal systems, fog horns. At degree 255, the SPS track documents for the first time a purely human-made industrial sound that is not a navigation signal but a byproduct of the Pacific Northwest's primary historical industry: logging. A log truck -- a loaded semi hauling timber from a clear-cut to a mill -- produces a characteristic vibration signature as it moves over highway and forest road surfaces. The vibration propagates through the road substrate, into the ground, and through the structures of anything adjacent to the road. At close range it is felt as much as heard. E=3 reflects the moderate intensity of this specific sound event: it is not loud in the way that a waterfall is loud, but it is omnidirectional and penetrating.

The entry of an anthropogenic industrial sound into the SPS track marks a genuine categorical expansion. The engine has documented the Pacific Northwest's acoustic landscape through its natural systems -- hydrology, weather, tidal dynamics. At degree 255, the record of human activity enters: the logging industry that cleared the old-growth forests, built the roads, and shaped the economic history of the region is now acoustically present in the engine. The log truck vibration is not a natural sound. It is the sound of industry meeting landscape, and in the Pacific Northwest, no industry has shaped the landscape more completely than logging.
