## Mission Overview

ICESat-2 is the obs#5 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA Goddard's Earth-observing mission that circles Earth in a near-polar orbit and measures the elevation of the high cold surfaces of the planet — the ice sheets, the sea ice, and the land — by timing the individual photons that return from a six-beam green laser. ★ It is a NASA mission, managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center; the spacecraft was built by Northrop Grumman and the ATLAS instrument by Goddard. The observer launched ~15 September 2018 on a Delta II from Vandenberg (SLC-2W), California — the last Delta II launch — and settled into a near-polar orbit at roughly 496 km with a ~92° inclination and a 91-day repeat: an advanced laser-altimetry Earth observer reaching the high latitudes where the ice lies. The story is the launch from Vandenberg, the climb into the ~496 km, ~92° orbit that reaches the poles, ATLAS firing its three beam-pairs of green photons down to the surface and timing the single photons that return, the ice sheets and the sea ice profiled in elevation pass after pass, the land and the forest canopy measured in height, and the long ongoing watch over the height of the frozen world that continues today. The science is central: ATLAS provides the first spaceborne photon-counting laser altimeter — a micropulse, multi-beam, 532 nm lidar firing at ~10 kHz, splitting into six beams (three pairs, each a strong and a weak beam), measuring surface elevation by timing individual returned photons with a fine along-track sampling (~0.7 m); the ice result is the precise measurement of ice-sheet elevation change (Greenland and Antarctica) and sea-ice freeboard → thickness; and the land result is the measurement of land elevation and vegetation / forest-canopy height from space. ★ The technique result is load-bearing: it is the first spaceborne PHOTON-COUNTING laser altimeter — NOT "the first laser altimeter in space," since the ORIGINAL ICESat (2003–2009) carried GLAS, a single-beam full-waveform laser altimeter; ICESat-2/ATLAS is the first photon-counting / multi-beam one (low per-photon energy, high repetition rate, six beams, dense sampling, finer precision). ★ The ice result is equally load-bearing: it is the precise measurement of ice-sheet elevation change and sea-ice thickness — the precision / photon-counting advance, not the first ice-sheet measurement (GLAS and radar altimeters preceded). ★ The land result is load-bearing: ICESat-2 measures land elevation and vegetation / forest-canopy height from space by the same photon-counting lidar. ★★ ICESat-2 is STILL OPERATING (since 2018), so the catalog frames its launch and completed milestones in the PAST (ICESat-2 launched ~15 September 2018, reached its orbit, deployed and checked out ATLAS, completed its commissioning phase, and began its elevation record) while its ongoing role is framed in the PRESENT (ICESat-2 orbits Earth, continues to measure the elevation of the ice sheets, the sea ice, and the land, has returned a long record, and remains operational). ## Surprises

- **A near-polar orbit lets ICESat-2 reach the high latitudes where the ice sheets lie.** Unlike a lower-inclination orbit, ICESat-2 flies a near-polar orbit at roughly 496 km with a ~92° inclination, so its ground track crosses Greenland and Antarctica and the high-latitude sea ice, and its 91-day repeat brings the same lines back across the ice again and again — letting it resolve the rise and fall of the ice surface over time, not just its shape once.
- **A laser that counts single photons rather than reading one strong pulse.** ICESat-2's ATLAS does not fire one high-energy pulse and read the full returned waveform the way the original ICESat's GLAS did; instead it fires low-energy micropulses at a high rate (~10 kHz) at 532 nm and times the individual photons that return — a photon-counting design that, across six beams, gives a denser, finer-resolution sampling of the surface, the first spaceborne photon-counting laser altimeter.
- **Six beams in three pairs read the surface and separate the canopy from the ground.** ATLAS splits its beam into six beams arranged as three pairs — each pair a strong and a weak beam ~90 m apart, the pairs ~3.3 km apart across track — so the surface is sampled along six dense lines at once; over forest, the strong and weak beams and the dense returned photons separate the top of the canopy from the ground beneath it, yielding the forest-canopy height as well as the bare land elevation.
- **A worm that lives its whole life inside the glacier ice.** The Ice Worm (*Mesenchytraeus solifugus*), a small dark annelid of Pacific Northwest and Cascades glaciers (Mount Rainier — the subspecies *rainierensis* — the North Cascades, the Olympics), lives within the surface ice itself, active only at near-freezing temperatures, rising to the glacier surface in the cool of evening and morning and dying if warmed even a few degrees: a mirror of ICESat-2 measuring the elevation of the high cold surfaces of the Earth from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only; a glacier oligochaete of the genus *Mesenchytraeus*, ranging from south-central Alaska down the Pacific-coast ranges).
- **A cushion plant clinging to the high cold rock beside the ice.** The Smooth Douglasia (*Douglasia laevigata*, now often *Androsace laevigata*), a cushion plant of high, cold, rocky alpine ridges, cliffs, talus, and fellfields in the PNW (the Olympics, the Cascades, the Columbia Gorge), forms tight low mats of bright deep-pink flowers clinging to the exposed rock near the summits (ecological description only; distinct from the moss campions *Silene acaulis* and the saxifrages *Saxifraga*) — bound with the throughline of the high cold surface, the same high cold ice and rock whose elevation ICESat-2 measures from orbit.

## Dedication

v1.285 ICESat-2 is dedicated to the people at NASA Goddard, at Northrop Grumman, and across the science teams who built and still fly the observer that measures the height of the frozen world. The teams who lifted it from Vandenberg on the last Delta II and turned it into a near-polar orbit that crosses Greenland and Antarctica and comes back along the same lines every ninety-one days, so the rise and fall of the ice can be read across the seasons. The engineers behind ATLAS — a green laser split into six beams, firing ten thousand soft pulses a second and timing the single photons that return. And the scientists who turn those photons into the thinning of an ice sheet, the freeboard of the sea ice, the height of a forest above its own ground. Together they measure a thing hard to reach and easy to lose — the cold high surface of the world, the ice at the poles and the rock at the summits — and make of it a record that grows season by season. They watch the height of the frozen world, and the watch continues.
