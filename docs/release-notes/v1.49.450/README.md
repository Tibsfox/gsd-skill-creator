## Summary

**GEORGETOWN ENTERS. THE SUMMIT OPENS. E=17 COMBINED. 85.8%.**

**TAD** arrive at E=8 -- the Georgetown band formed in 1988 by Tad Doyle (vocals, guitar), Kurt Danielson (bass), Gary Thorstensen (guitar), and Steve Wied (drums), signed to Sub Pop and operating at the absolute heaviest end of the Seattle sound before the commercial breakthrough of 1991 flattened the genre's range into something more radio-legible. TAD were never radio-legible. They were Sub Pop catalog numbers 19 and 27 and 44 and they were the band that shared the Bleach tour with Nirvana in 1989, playing European venues with a sound that was blunt-force where Nirvana was melodic, grinding where Nirvana was kinetic. Tad Doyle's Idaho background -- born in Salem, Oregon, raised in Boise -- gave the band a physical register distinct from the Seattle underground's art-school wing: TAD was meat and weight and volume, a band whose recordings captured the physical sensation of standing in a small room with very loud amplifiers pointed at your chest.

E=8 for TAD is the grunge summit's opening assignment, and it is correct: E=8 is the outer edge of the pre-summit register, the highest energy entry since the Melvins at degree 308, and the signal that what follows is not a continuation of the apex predator arc's documentation mode but an escalation into the summit's concentrated sequence. TAD is not E=9 because TAD's power is horizontal -- spread across a sustained low-frequency wall -- rather than vertical. The energy is enormous but it does not spike; it accumulates. E=8 is the right reading for a band whose weight is expressed through duration and density rather than through the sudden release that produces E=9.

**Great Horned Owl pair hooting simultaneously** arrives at E=9 on the SPS track -- *Bubo virginianus*, the Pacific Northwest's apex avian predator, producing the simultaneous duet that is the species' primary territorial and pair-bonding vocalization. The Great Horned Owl duet is one of the most acoustically complex vocalizations in the Pacific Northwest's nocturnal soundscape: the male's deeper, slower hoot and the female's higher, faster response produced simultaneously create an overlapping harmonic that functions as a single integrated territorial broadcast. The pair produces a sound larger than the sum of its parts -- two voices that are individually distinctive but collectively form a wall of acoustic presence that carries two kilometers through old-growth and second-growth forest on still winter nights.

E=9 for the simultaneous Great Horned Owl pair hooting is the SPS track's opening statement for the Grunge Summit arc. The Great Horned Owl pair did not appear as a duet in the apex predator arc -- degree 299 contained the *single* Great Horned Owl hooting battle. The simultaneous pair at degree 309 is a different event: not competition but coordination, the mated pair broadcasting territorial unity across the same airspace at the same moment. The owl duet opens the summit's SPS arc at E=9, matching the summit's arrival register.

**GEORGETOWN ENTERS THE ENGINE.** Georgetown is Seattle's southernmost industrial neighborhood, a working-class enclave of manufacturing plants, auto repair shops, and former Boeing-adjacent industry that produced a music scene physically and aesthetically distant from Capitol Hill and the U-District. TAD came out of the Georgetown and south Seattle orbit: the band's sound encoded the industrial weight of that geography. Georgetown was not Belltown, not the Eastlake strip, not the Sub Pop Capitol Hill network -- it was the part of Seattle that processed and built things, and TAD's music sounded like the part of Seattle that processed and built things. Georgetown enters the engine at degree 309. The Summit opens.

**E=8 + E=9 = E=17 COMBINED.** The degree 309 combined energy reading matches degree 308's apex predator arc maximum. The Grunge Summit opens at the same combined energy level at which the apex predator arc closed. The floor of the summit equals the ceiling of the arc. What follows will be higher.
