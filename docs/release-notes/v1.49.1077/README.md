## Mission Overview

The Spitzer Space Telescope is the obs#3 sustain of the SPACE-TELESCOPE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's flagship infrared observatory whose defining achievements are being the infrared member and the fourth and last by launch of NASA's four Great Observatories, making the first detection of light from a planet orbiting another star, and producing the first weather map of a planet beyond the Solar System. It is a NASA mission managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL, Caltech) with science operations run by the Spitzer Science Center (SSC) at IPAC on the Caltech campus — NOT Goddard/STScI and NOT Marshall/CXC. A cold infrared observatory carrying a 0.85-metre beryllium telescope cooled by liquid helium, it launched 25 August 2003 (05:35 UTC) on a Delta II (7920H "Heavy") expendable rocket from Cape Canaveral (SLC-17B) — NOT the Space Shuttle — and was placed in a distinctive Earth-trailing heliocentric orbit, drifting slowly behind Earth around the Sun to stay cold; it observed the infrared, the heat-light invisible to the eye. Spitzer's science payload comprises three instruments — IRAC (the Infrared Array Camera, imaging at 3.6, 4.5, 5.8, and 8.0 microns), IRS (the Infrared Spectrograph), and MIPS (the Multiband Imaging Photometer, at 24, 70, and 160 microns) — that together provide imaging, spectroscopy, and far-infrared photometry across roughly 3.6 to 160 microns, all fed by the helium-cooled beryllium optics. The story is the launch above the air on an expendable rocket, the slow drift behind Earth around the Sun, the cold cryogenic eye, and the warm hidden universe it revealed. The cooling is central: a liquid-helium cryostat chilled the telescope and detectors to a few kelvin so the telescope's own heat could not blind it, and when the helium was depleted on 15 May 2009 Spitzer ran a "warm mission" using IRAC's two shortest-wavelength channels (3.6 and 4.5 microns) at about 28 K. Spitzer used its infrared sensitivity to make the first direct detection of light from an exoplanet (the secondary eclipse of TrES-1 and HD 209458b, 2005), to produce the first weather/temperature map of a planet beyond the Solar System (HD 189733b, 2007), to help detect and characterize the seven temperate planets of TRAPPIST-1 (2017), and to peer through dust into star-forming nebulae, brown dwarfs, and distant dusty galaxies. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: Spitzer is NASA's flagship infrared observatory (NOT "the first infrared space telescope" — earlier infrared observatories existed, IRAS in 1983 and ESA's ISO in 1995); it is the FOURTH and LAST of NASA's four Great Observatories by launch (NOT "the second" or "the third" — Hubble, the Compton Gamma Ray Observatory, and Chandra preceded it); its first detection of an exoplanet's LIGHT is distinct from Hubble's first detection of an exoplanet's ATMOSPHERE; and it launched on an expendable Delta II into an Earth-trailing heliocentric orbit (NOT the Shuttle, NOT low Earth orbit, NOT high-Earth orbit) and was never serviced by astronauts. ## Surprises

- **A great telescope was launched on an ordinary expendable rocket and then sent into orbit around the Sun.** Spitzer launched 25 August 2003 on a Delta II (7920H "Heavy") from Cape Canaveral — not the Space Shuttle — and then drifted slowly into an Earth-trailing heliocentric orbit, falling gradually behind Earth around the Sun (about 0.1 AU per year) so the telescope would stay cold and away from Earth's heat.
- **A telescope had to be made colder than its targets to see them.** Because warm objects glow in the infrared, Spitzer's own heat would have swamped the faint infrared sky; a liquid-helium cryostat chilled the 0.85-metre beryllium mirror and the detectors to a few kelvin so the telescope could see the heat-light of cold dust, distant galaxies, and exoplanets — and when the helium ran out on 15 May 2009 it carried on a "warm mission" near 28 K.
- **An infrared telescope caught the actual light of a planet around another star.** In 2005 Spitzer made the first direct detection of light — infrared thermal emission — from an exoplanet, by measuring the small drop in brightness when the hot Jupiters TrES-1 and HD 209458b passed behind their stars (the secondary eclipse), reading the planet's own glow rather than its host star's.
- **A telescope drew the first weather map of a world beyond the Solar System.** In 2007 Spitzer produced the first weather/temperature map of a planet beyond the Solar System, mapping the day-to-night temperature contrast of the hot Jupiter HD 189733b from the way its infrared brightness varied through a full orbit (Knutson et al. 2007).
- **A large, fast hawkmoth hovers like a hummingbird to drink nectar in the failing light.** The White-lined Sphinx (*Hyles lineata*) flies at dusk, dawn, and into the night and hovers hummingbird-like in front of flowers to drink nectar through a long proboscis (its larvae are conspicuous "hornworms") — a dusk and night flier of the interior Pacific Northwest (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Spitzer, finding the glow when the day's light is gone.
- **A dry-slope wildflower opens its lemon-yellow star flowers at dusk and holds them through the night.** The Giant Blazing Star (*Mentzelia laevicaulis*), a tall wildflower of dry, open, rocky Pacific Northwest slopes and sagebrush-steppe, opens its large lemon-yellow, five-petalled star flowers — surrounding a bright burst of many central stamens — in the late afternoon and at dusk and keeps them open overnight when most flowers have closed, pollinated by night-flying hawkmoths and bees (ecological description only) — a glow held when the light is gone, a mirror of the warm hidden universe Spitzer watched.
