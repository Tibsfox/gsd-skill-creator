On August 23, 1961, an Atlas-Agena A launch vehicle lifted off from Launch Complex 12 at Cape Canaveral. On top of the Agena sat a 306-kilogram spacecraft -- Ranger 1, the first mission of NASA's dedicated lunar exploration program, built by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Pasadena. This was a new program and a new launch vehicle configuration. The Atlas performed nominally -- the same steel-balloon booster that would later carry Glenn and Schirra, its stage-and-a-half design dropping the outboard engines after 130 seconds while the sustainer burned to completion. The Agena A upper stage ignited its hypergolic Bell 8081 engine (UDMH and inhibited red fuming nitric acid, propellants that ignite on contact) and delivered the spacecraft into a parking orbit at 160 kilometers. This was the first time the United States had used a parking orbit for a deep space mission -- an elegant technique that decoupled the launch window from trajectory geometry. The spacecraft and Agena coasted in low Earth orbit, waiting for the precise moment when a second burn would boost them onto a highly elliptical trajectory extending to 1,100,000 kilometers from Earth, far beyond the Moon's orbit. The restart command was sent. The propellant valves opened. The hypergolic propellants should have ignited on contact. They did not. The Agena's engine produced no thrust. The problem was propellant settling in zero gravity: during the weightless coast phase, the nitrogen pressurant gas that was supposed to push the liquid propellants into the feed lines found paths around the liquid rather than through it. The propellants formed spheres, clung to tank walls, oscillated in slow-motion slosh modes that the tank baffles could not damp. The engine received a mixture of gas and liquid rather than pure propellant. Ranger 1 remained in its 160-kilometer parking orbit, carrying 306 kilograms of unfired intentions on a path that led nowhere but back down. It reentered the atmosphere on August 30 and burned up.

But this failure is qualitatively different from Pioneer 0's catastrophic explosion at T+77 seconds (degree 1). Ranger 1 reached orbit. It achieved stable parking orbit. The Atlas worked. The Agena's first burn worked. The spacecraft was healthy. The failure occurred at the highest point yet -- not on the pad, not in the atmosphere, but in orbit, at the transition from Earth orbit to deep space. The failure altitude increases with each new program. Pioneer (degree 1): explosion from the surface. Mercury-Atlas 1 (degree 16): structural breakup in the atmosphere. Ranger 1 (degree 25): stranded in orbit. The pattern predicts that the next new program will fail at an even higher level. The fix for Ranger was not exotic -- better plumbing, better baffles, better understanding of fluid dynamics in microgravity. The Agena B, starting with Ranger 3, would incorporate improved propellant management. The engineering iterates. The altitude rises.
