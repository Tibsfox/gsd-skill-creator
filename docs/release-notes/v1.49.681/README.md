**Commander Col Vladimir Georgievich Titov** (1947-01-01 Sretensk Chita Oblast → still living 2026) — Soviet Air Force test pilot. Soyuz TM-4 was his third career launch but second qualifying spaceflight: he had survived two previous catastrophic launch aborts. **Soyuz T-8 1983-04-20**: antenna deployment failure prevented rendezvous with Salyut 7; capsule returned same day after 2d 0h 17m. **Soyuz T-10-1 1983-09-26**: an RD-107 first-stage engine ignited a fire 90 seconds before liftoff at Baikonur LC-1; the Launch Escape System (the explosive bolts on Soyuz's nose-cone tower) fired and pulled the descent capsule clear at 14-17 g for 5 seconds. The booster exploded 2 seconds after capsule clearance. Titov + Strekalov landed safely 4 km from the pad. **FIRST COSMONAUT TO TWICE SURVIVE A LAUNCH ABORT** substrate-form NEW LOCKED. Subsequent career: STS-63 Discovery 1995 (first Russian on Shuttle, part of Shuttle-Mir program) + STS-86 Atlantis 1997. Twice Hero of the Soviet Union (1988 + 1997).

**Flight Engineer Col Musa Khiramanovich Manarov** (1951-03-22 Baku Azerbaijan SSR → still living 2026) — Soviet Air Force; rookie first flight at age 36. **First Lak (Caucasus ethnic group from Republic of Dagestan) cosmonaut** — substrate-distinct from Slavic-Soviet majority cohort. Subsequent: Soyuz TM-11/TM-12 EO-7 1990-1991 (175d additional). Cumulative 541.6 days in space at career close. Hero of the Soviet Union 1988.

**Cosmonaut-Researcher Col Anatoli Semyonovich Levchenko** (1941-05-21 Krasnokutsk Kharkov Oblast Ukrainian SSR → 1988-08-06 Moscow Russian SFSR) — Soviet Air Force test pilot at Gromov Flight Research Institute. **Buran-cosmonaut-detachment 1981-1988.** The Soviet Buran shuttle program (Energia-Buran reusable launch system) maintained a parallel cosmonaut-test-pilot training pipeline from 1978 onward; selected Buran-test-pilots were assigned to Soyuz/Mir cosmonaut-researcher visiting flights as orbital qualification prior to potential Buran orbital missions. Levchenko's Soyuz TM-4 visiting flight 1987-12-21 to 1987-12-29 (7d 21h 58m) was his orbital qualification. **He returned 1987-12-29 aboard Soyuz TM-3 with Romanenko + Aleksandrov (EO-1 closure).** Eight months one day later, **Levchenko died 1988-08-06 of brain tumor** — substrate-form-meaningful in-program memorial (substrate-form-distinct from preceding substrate-coincident-distinct calendar coincidences). Hero of the Soviet Union (posthumous 1990). Substrate-anchor for **FIRST-COSMONAUT-DEATH-WITHIN-9-MONTHS-OF-RETURNED-FLIGHT** + **BURAN-TEST-PILOT-COSMONAUT-RESEARCHER-COHORT-OPENS**.

**FIRST YEAR-IN-SPACE CREW.** Titov + Manarov inherited the Mir EO-3 long-duration assignment from the departing EO-1 crew (Romanenko + Aleksandrov + Levchenko via Soyuz TM-3) + ultimately became the **FIRST CREW TO COMPLETE A FULL YEAR IN SPACE**: launched 1987-12-21 11:18 UTC + landed Soyuz TM-6 1988-12-21 09:57 UTC = **365 days 22 hours 39 minutes 47 seconds** — exactly one calendar year plus one hour. The mission established YEAR-IN-SPACE substrate-anchor for the cumulative cohort that would extend forward through Polyakov's 437.7d 1994-1995 (longest-ever single flight) + Kelly/Kornienko 340d 2015-2016 (first American year) + Vande Hei 355d 2021-2022 + Rubio 371d 2022-2023 (first Hispanic-American year).

**Buran-shadow substrate-cohort closes substantially by 1993.** Buran flew unmanned once 1988-11-15 (Buran 1.01; orbital flight + autonomous landing at Yubileyniy runway Baikonur). The program was cancelled 1993 due to Soviet collapse + funding loss. The Buran-cosmonaut-detachment closed with three additional cosmonaut deaths within the period: Aleksandr Shchukin (Yak-18 crash 1988-08-18 = 12 days after Levchenko death) + Rimantas Stankevicius (Italian airshow 1990-09-09) + others medically grounded. Only Igor Volk (Soyuz T-12 qualification 1984) survived the program intact to subsequent civilian flying career.
