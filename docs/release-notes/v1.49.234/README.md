## Part B: Stonefly Hatch (Perlidae) — Wing Rustle on Stream Surface

- **NEW FAMILY Perlidae — 44th family.** The golden stonefly family, the largest Pacific Northwest stoneflies, sits as the 44th family in the engine after **Vespidae** (43rd, the wasp/hornet family at degree 93). The family-level entry covers the collective emergence behavior across PNW Perlidae species rather than profiling individual species. Perlidae includes some of the largest aquatic insects in PNW streams, with predatory nymphs that live under rocks in fast-flowing riffles and dramatic emergence events that produce visible synchronous hatches.
- **NEW ORDER Plecoptera — sixth insect order, first aquatic.** **Plecoptera** — the stoneflies — is the **sixth insect order** in the engine and the **first insect order with an obligate aquatic life stage**. The order joins **Orthoptera** (cricket 87, katydid 88), **Hemiptera** (cicada 89), **Diptera** (mosquito 91), **Hymenoptera** (bumblebee 92, hornet 93). The aquatic life stage is the structurally distinguishing feature — stoneflies are not insects that happen to live near water; they are insects whose entire larval/nymphal existence occurs IN water, with only the brief adult stage above the surface. Plecoptera opens the aquatic-insect category in the engine.
- **First aquatic insect — the Insect Era enters the water.** Every prior insect entry was **terrestrial or aerial**: cricket on the ground, katydid in vegetation, cicada on tree trunks, mosquito in air, bumblebee in flight, hornet around paper nests in trees and structures. The stonefly is the **first species in the engine whose larval/nymphal life stage occurs entirely in water**. The acoustic event is the brief emergence transition — the moment the aquatic-bound nymph becomes the airborne adult. Eight insect entries in eight degrees (87–94) now span five orders, three sound mechanisms, four prior postures plus the new emergence-rustle category.
- **The wing rustle on stream surface — 95th species-side acoustic element.** The barely audible sound of **membranous adult stonefly wings drying and flexing against rock and water** during the emergence event. Broad-spectrum, low amplitude (500–8000 Hz), often masked by the stream itself. **Not a buzz, not a chirp, not a call.** A **physical sound** of metamorphic emergence, audible only to a close listener paying attention. Distinct from the **aggressive papery hum** at degree 93 (collective Vespidae defense, E=5, sustained, threatening). Distinct from the **deep resonant buzz** at degree 92 (gentle Apidae bumblebee, low-pitched, continuous, aerial). Distinct from the **ultra-thin hum cloud** at degree 91 (individual mosquito wing whine in air). Distinct from the **electric buzzing throb** at degree 89 (cicada tymbal advertisement). The wing rustle is its own acoustic category — passive emergence rather than active signaling.
- **E=2 — quietest insect sound, ties mosquito.** The wing rustle is barely audible; the energy ties the **Mosquito Swarm** at degree 91 as the quietest insect energy in the engine. Both individual events. Both broad-spectrum low-amplitude. But the mosquito whines in **air**; the stonefly rustles on **water**. Aerial vs aquatic surface. Different mediums, same energy floor. E=2 reflects the emergence event's sonic reality — barely audible, masked by stream babble, audible only when you are close and paying attention.
- **MAXIMUM ENERGY DROP — E=5 to E=2 in one degree.** Degree 93's **Hornet Nest Buzz** sat at **E=5** (collective aggressive defense). Degree 94's **Stonefly Hatch** sits at **E=2** (individual passive emergence). **Three full energy levels** separate them — the largest single-degree drop in the species-side energy ledger to date. The drop **mirrors** the **Hymenoptera pair**'s rise from degree 92 (Bumblebee at E=2) to degree 93 (Hornet at E=5). Three levels up, then three levels down. The species energy ledger has now executed an **E=2 → E=5 → E=2** oscillation across three consecutive degrees. Minimum-to-maximum-to-minimum.
- **Stream indicator species — diagnostic species enters the engine.** Stoneflies are one of the most reliable **stream health indicators** in freshwater ecology. Their nymphs require **clean** (low pollution, low sediment, low organic waste), **cold** (below 20°C / 68°F), and **well-oxygenated** water with rocky substrate in fast-flowing riffles. **If stoneflies are present, the stream is healthy. If stoneflies are absent, something is wrong.** This opens a fundamentally new contribution category for the species engine: **the diagnostic species** whose presence IS the data. The American Dipper at degree 52 was also a stream indicator (it cannot tolerate polluted water either), though not framed that way at the time. The stonefly makes the diagnostic-species concept explicit.
- **Return to streams — 37-degree gap closed.** The engine has not visited streams since the **Belted Kingfisher** at degree 57. **Thirty-seven degrees** without a stream species. Three stream species across 94 degrees: **American Dipper** at degree 52 (mountain streams, foraging from above), **Belted Kingfisher** at degree 57 (cold streams, fishing from above), **Stonefly Hatch** at degree 94 (cold streams, living IN the water). The stonefly is the **first species in the engine that actually lives in the stream** rather than working above it. Bird-above-stream and insect-in-stream are different relationships to the same habitat.
- **PNW Perlidae ecological context.** The **Cascade Range** and **Olympic Mountains** provide extensive cold-stream habitat. Forested banks shade the water (maintaining cold temperature), contribute leaf litter (food for shredders that stoneflies eat), and supply woody debris (emergence substrate). PNW stoneflies are among the most diverse in **North America**. The Perlidae entry implicitly references this regional diversity through family-level coverage rather than single-species profiling.
- **Life cycle and emergence behavior.** Nymphal stage **1 to 4 years** in the stream, living under rocks, feeding on other aquatic invertebrates as predators. Emergence: nymphs crawl from the stream onto bankside rocks or vegetation; the adult splits the nymphal exoskeleton and emerges; the wings dry and flex against rock and water surfaces, producing the wing rustle. Adult stage: days to weeks. Males drum abdomens on substrate to attract females — a vibrational signal through the surface, not airborne sound. Females deposit eggs on the stream surface; eggs sink and hatch into nymphs; the cycle restarts.
- **Eight insect entries in eight degrees — deepest taxon-class run.** Cricket Chorus (87, Gryllidae), Katydid (88, Tettigoniidae), Cicada (89, Cicadidae), [Spring Peeper-Like Chorus 90, Hylidae — Amphibia interlude], Mosquito Swarm (91, Culicidae), Bumblebee Drone (92, Apidae), Hornet Nest Buzz (93, Vespidae), Stonefly Hatch (94, Perlidae). Eight insect entries in eight degrees with one Amphibia interlude. Six insect orders covered. Three sound mechanisms (stridulation, tymbal, wing vibration) plus the new emergence-rustle category. Four prior acoustic postures (mating, advertising, incidental flight, defensive) plus the new **passive emergence** posture. The Insect Era has become a sub-engine within the engine.
- **Thirty-fourth consecutive original CSV.** The stonefly entry is the thirty-fourth consecutive species drawn from the original source catalog without substitution (degrees 61–94). The original CSV continues to deliver. The longest unbroken run of original-catalog selections in the engine to date.

- **The producer-as-artist lineage is a tracked engine pattern.** With **Chris Walla** at degree 67 (indie rock production) and **Erik Blood** at degree 94 (experimental electronic and hip-hop production), two instances 27 degrees apart confirm the producer-as-artist as an engine lineage rather than an isolated case. - **The stonefly introduces the diagnostic species as a category.** No prior species in the engine functioned explicitly as an indicator of ecosystem health. The stonefly's presence IS the data. This opens a new contribution category for the species engine: species as diagnostic tool. Long gaps can create productive tension that makes the return more meaningful. Future habitat returns should consider how they extend the engine's view of the habitat rather than merely revisiting it.
- **The species energy ledger oscillates around extremes as the Insect Era deepens.** The E=5-to-E=2 drop (hornet 93 to stonefly 94) mirrors the E=2-to-E=5 rise (bumblebee 92 to hornet 93). An E=2 → E=5 → E=2 oscillation across three consecutive degrees creates dramatic structural texture. Future degrees in deep taxon-class runs should expect energy oscillation around extremes rather than smooth interpolation. The pattern is now a tested engine behavior.
- **Eight insect entries in eight degrees is the new ceiling for taxon-class depth.** The Insect Era spans six orders, seven families, three sound mechanisms plus the emergence-rustle category, four prior postures plus the new passive-emergence posture. Plan for taxon-class runs of eight or more degrees rather than treating four-deep runs as the maximum. The depth produces secondary structures (the diagnostic-species category, the aquatic-insect distinction, the passive-emergence posture) that shorter runs would not generate.
- **The studio-as-instrument philosophy is engine-trackable.** Erik Blood treats the studio as the instrument the music is played through. Production decisions are compositional decisions; mixing decisions are arrangement decisions; the signal chain is the song's structure. This philosophy distinguishes producer-artists from artists who work with producers and gives the producer's palette acoustic element its specific identity. Future producer-artist entries should be classified against the studio-as-instrument distinction.

The engine's social-graph vocabulary now distinguishes connection-by-collaboration-across-scenes as its own pattern.
- **Thirty-four consecutive original CSV species confirms source-catalog reliability.** Degrees 61–94 have not needed substitution or correction. The CSV is sturdy enough to support sustained engine operation without alternative-selection logic. Future engine planning can rely on the original catalog for the foreseeable future without contingency CSV preparation.

These gaps have crossed from notable to extreme and require explicit planning to address. Future degree planning should treat raptor, passerine, and marine inclusions as priority slots rather than continuing to defer them.
