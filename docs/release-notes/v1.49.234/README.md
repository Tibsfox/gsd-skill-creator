### STREAM INDICATOR SPECIES

The stonefly is one of the most reliable **stream health indicators** in freshwater ecology:

- **Clean water:** Stonefly nymphs require water with low levels of pollution, sediment, and organic waste.
- **Cold water:** Most Perlidae nymphs require cold water (below 20C / 68F). They are among the first species to disappear when streams warm.
- **Well-oxygenated:** Stonefly nymphs breathe through gills and require high dissolved oxygen levels.
- **Rocky substrate:** Nymphs live under rocks in fast-flowing riffles.

**If stoneflies are present, the stream is healthy. If stoneflies are absent, something is wrong.** The stonefly hatch is not just a sound event but a diagnostic event -- the presence of the sound confirms the ecological health of the stream.

### THE SOUND: WING RUSTLE ON STREAM SURFACE -- 95th Species-Side Acoustic Element

The stonefly hatch sound is the **wing rustle** of adult stoneflies emerging on stream surfaces. Stonefly nymphs crawl from the stream onto bankside rocks, the adult splits the nymphal exoskeleton and emerges, drying its wings. The wings flutter, producing a **faint rustling** on the stream surface.

**Acoustic character:** Barely audible rustle -- the sound of membranous wings drying and flexing against rock and water surfaces. Not a buzz, not a chirp, not a call. A physical sound. Broad-spectrum, low amplitude (500-8,000 Hz), often masked by the stream itself. You hear it when you are close. You hear it when you are paying attention.

**Why E=2:**
1. Barely audible -- the quietest insect sound in the engine.
2. Individual event -- one stonefly emerging, not a chorus.
3. Masked by stream sound -- exists within the stream's acoustic environment, not above it.
4. Short-lived -- adults live days to weeks.
5. E=2 matches mosquito (91) as the quietest insect energy.
6. After E=5 (hornet, 93), E=2 is the maximum energy drop -- 3 levels in one degree.

Contrasts:

- **Deep resonant buzz** (degree 92, Apidae) -- gentle but continuous, aerial. The stonefly rustle is barely audible, on-surface, aquatic context.

### ECOLOGY: THE STREAM INDICATOR

**Cold stream habitat.** Mountain streams, cold rivers, tributaries with rocky substrate and fast-flowing riffles. Forested banks provide shade (maintaining cold water temperature), leaf litter input, and woody debris (emergence substrate). PNW stoneflies are among the most diverse in North America.

**Life cycle:** Nymphal stage 1-4 years in stream, living under rocks, feeding on other aquatic invertebrates (Perlidae are predators). Emergence: nymphs crawl from stream onto rocks or vegetation, adults emerge from nymphal exoskeleton. Adult stage: days to weeks. Males drum abdomens on substrate to attract females -- vibrational signal through surface, not airborne. Females deposit eggs on stream surface.

**Ecological significance:** Stonefly diversity is a signature of the PNW's clean, cold, forested stream systems. The Cascade Range and Olympic Mountains provide extensive cold-stream habitat.
