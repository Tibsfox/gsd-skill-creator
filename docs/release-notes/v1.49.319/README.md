**The Two-Day Chick Departure and the Most Precocial Seabird.** The Ancient Murrelet's defining behavioral signature — and one of the most remarkable events in North American bird biology — is the **precocial chick departure at 1-3 days old** (typically two days). Chicks leave the nest burrow when they are only a day or two old, weighing approximately **25-30 grams**, having **never eaten in the burrow**, having **never seen the ocean**, and being **completely incapable of flight**. The departure happens at **night**. The parents call from the sea below the colony island, and the chicks respond by **running, tumbling, and falling through the dark vegetation** toward the sound of their parents' voices. The chicks may travel hundreds of meters through forest and undergrowth, and predation during this journey is a major mortality source. Once the chicks reach the water, the family group **swims away from the island** — potentially traveling tens or hundreds of kilometers at sea — and the chicks are fed at sea by both parents for several weeks. The precocial strategy minimizes time at the vulnerable colony: burrow-nesting seabirds on islands are especially vulnerable to introduced predators (rats, cats, raccoons), and by leaving at two days the chick spends the minimum possible time at the most dangerous location. The two-day departure is dramatically earlier than other seabirds' nestling periods: the **Common Murre**'s chick leaves at 15-25 days (and then jumps off the cliff ledge before it can fly, in the famous "jump event" documented at degree 177); most seabirds spend weeks or months in the nest. The Ancient Murrelet's 1-3-day departure is one of the most precocial departure times of any bird species, and the engine's documentation at degree 179 preserves the precocial-departure register as a structural first in the species catalog's life-history vocabulary.

**The Shrill Nocturnal Twitter and the Second Darkness Call.** The Ancient Murrelet's **shrill nocturnal twitter** enters the engine's acoustic vocabulary as the **second nocturnal-colony acoustic register** after the Rhinoceros Auklet's **moaning uurrr** at degree 175. The colony aggregate of hundreds or thousands of Ancient Murrelets arriving and departing at night produces a shimmering shrill chorus distinct from the deeper moan-chorus of the Rhinoceros Auklet colonies. Gaston**'s 1992 T&AD Poyser monograph *The Ancient Murrelet: A Natural History in the Queen Charlotte Islands* — the continental reference for the species; the Haida Gwaii colonies anchor the species in Pacific Northwest geography despite the bird's primary breeding range being slightly north of Washington's typical seabird-colony locations.
