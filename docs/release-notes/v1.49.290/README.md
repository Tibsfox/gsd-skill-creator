**The Mourning Dove — The Most Recognizable Dove Call in North America.** The **Mourning Dove** (*Zenaida macroura*) is one of the most familiar birds in North America, with a population of **300-400 million** and a presence in every U.S. state and southern Canada. The species' iconic call — a soft, five-note **"oo-WOO-woo-woo-woo"** with the second note highest and the remaining three descending in pitch — has soundtracked American mornings for centuries. The call is repeated from **high exposed perches** (power lines, dead branches, rooftops) during dawn choruses and extends throughout the day in the breeding season (March-September) and continues at reduced frequency through fall and winter. The fundamental frequency is **350-550 Hz** with harmonics reaching **2000 Hz**, and the complete five-note phrase lasts **2.5-3.5 seconds** with **3-8 second** inter-phrase intervals. The call is often mistaken for an owl due to the low pitch, the repetitive "whoo" quality, and the dawn/dusk calling pattern — a misidentification that has contributed to the cultural ambiguity surrounding the species. The **common name "Mourning"** comes directly from this call: early European settlers heard the cooing as mournful (a bird in grief), and the name has stuck for over 200 years, permanently associating the species with melancholy, nostalgia, and peaceful solitude. The cultural resonance is deep — poets, songwriters, and novelists have used the Mourning Dove's call as shorthand for summer mornings, rural landscapes, and suburban dawn in American cultural memory.

**The Wing Whistle and the Second Mechanical Sound.** Beyond the vocal cooing, the Mourning Dove produces a distinctive **wing whistle** on takeoff — a sharp, ascending high-frequency sound generated not by vocal apparatus but by **air passing through the modified ninth primary flight feather**. The ninth primary is narrowed and tapered, and during the rapid wingbeats of takeoff, air vibrates through this modified feather at frequencies of **1000-3000 Hz** for durations of **0.5-1.5 seconds**. The wing whistle serves as an **alarm signal**: studies by **Coleman (2008)** and **Hingee & Magrath (2009)** have demonstrated that when a Mourning Dove flushes from the ground, the whistle alerts nearby doves to potential danger, and the whistle alone (without any vocal call) is sufficient to trigger flight in other doves. The mechanical sound stands as the engine's **second mechanical (non-vocal) acoustic event** following the **Common Goldeneye flight whistle** at degree 124 and preceding the **Rock Pigeon wing clap** that will arrive at degree 152. The three mechanical sounds cluster in waterfowl and Columbidae territory: flight-whistle in Goldeneye (modified inner primaries), wing-whistle in Mourning Dove (modified ninth primary), wing-clap in Rock Pigeon (wings slapped above body). The progression from feather-generated sounds to wing-body contact documents two distinct mechanisms for non-vocal sound production within the engine's acoustic vocabulary. The contrast within the Mourning Dove's own acoustic signature — the gentle, low-pitched vocal coo versus the sharp, high-pitched mechanical whistle — gives the species a **dual acoustic identity** that the engine documents at first principles.

**America's Most Hunted Game Bird — The Cultural Paradox.** Behind the gentle cooing is one of the most intensely hunted bird species in America. The **United States Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS)** documents that approximately **20 million Mourning Doves** are killed by hunters each year in the United States — more than all duck species combined — making it by far the most harvested migratory game bird in the country. **40 of 50 U.S. states** allow Mourning Dove hunting, with seasons typically running September-January and bag limits of **10-15 per day**. Ten states (including Washington until 2019) have historically banned Mourning Dove hunting on cultural grounds. The **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW)** opened the state's first Mourning Dove hunting season in **2019** after decades of debate, representing the cultural tension between the species' status as a beloved backyard bird and its status as a sustainable game bird. Despite the 20-million-annual harvest, the Mourning Dove population remains large and stable. The species' **extraordinary reproductive capacity** — **2-6 broods per year** depending on latitude, with incubation at **14 days** and nestling period at **12-14 days** producing a complete breeding cycle of about one month — compensates for hunting mortality. The cultural paradox is structurally significant: the Mourning Dove is simultaneously one of America's most beloved backyard birds (whose mournful call is embedded in poetic and musical tradition) and one of its most hunted game birds (whose 20-million-annual harvest makes it a major economic and management species). ## Part B: Mourning Dove — The Most Recognizable Dove Call in North America, Second Columbidae, Second Mechanical Sound, America's Most Hunted Game Bird

- **The Mourning Dove is the second Columbidae at E=3 establishing the native pigeon contrast pair.** The **Mourning Dove** (*Zenaida macroura*) enters the engine at degree 150 as the **second Columbidae** following the **Band-tailed Pigeon** at degree 149. The two native pigeons share the E=3 energy calibration but occupy radically different ecological niches. The **Band-tailed Pigeon** is a **forest canopy specialist** with a Pacific coast range, hidden in the upper canopy of old-growth forests, with a **declining** population trend of 2-3% loss per year. The **Mourning Dove** is an **open-habitat generalist** with continental range, ubiquitous on every power line and rooftop, with a **stable to growing** population at **300-400 million**. The native pigeon contrast pair (149-150) documents the family's ecological range within a single energy band: the forest ghost and the suburban familiar at the same E=3, same family, same native-North-American status, but opposite habitat types and opposite population trajectories. The contrast pair at consecutive degrees is structurally elegant — two entries, same energy, opposite ecology.
- **The most recognizable dove call in North America is documented at structural depth.** The Mourning Dove's five-note **"oo-WOO-woo-woo-woo"** coo is one of the most recognized bird sounds in North America. Structurally, the call consists of **five notes** with the second note highest and most emphatic and the remaining three notes descending in pitch — a pattern that is invariable and diagnostic across the species' continental range. The **fundamental frequency** is **350-550 Hz** with harmonics reaching **2000 Hz**, and the complete five-note phrase lasts **2.5-3.5 seconds** with **3-8 second** inter-phrase intervals. The call is delivered from **high exposed perches** (power lines, dead branches, rooftops) during dawn choruses and extends throughout the day in breeding season (March-September) and continues at reduced frequency through fall and winter. The call's cultural resonance is embedded in American poetry, song, and literature as shorthand for melancholy, nostalgia, and peaceful solitude — a cultural weight that few other bird calls carry.
- **The wing whistle enters the engine as the second mechanical (non-vocal) acoustic event.** Beyond the vocal cooing, the Mourning Dove produces a distinctive **wing whistle** on takeoff — a sharp ascending high-frequency sound generated not by vocal apparatus but by **air passing through the modified ninth primary flight feather**. The ninth primary is narrowed and tapered, and during the rapid wingbeats of takeoff, air vibrates through this modified feather at frequencies of **1000-3000 Hz** for durations of **0.5-1.5 seconds**. The wing whistle is the engine's **second mechanical (non-vocal) acoustic event** following the **Common Goldeneye flight whistle** at degree 124 (modified inner primaries mechanism) and preceding the **Rock Pigeon wing clap** that will arrive at degree 152 (wings slapped above body mechanism). The three mechanical sounds document two distinct mechanisms for non-vocal sound production within the engine's acoustic vocabulary: feather-generated sounds (Goldeneye, Mourning Dove) and wing-body contact (Rock Pigeon).
- **The dual acoustic identity spans vocal coo and mechanical whistle.** The Mourning Dove has a **dual acoustic identity** — the gentle low-pitched vocal coo and the sharp high-pitched mechanical whistle. The contrast between the two sounds is extreme: the cooing at 350-550 Hz fundamental is soft and mournful and delivered from high perches during territorial and pair advertisement; the wing whistle at 1000-3000 Hz is sharp and startling and delivered during takeoff as an alarm signal. The two sounds serve different biological functions (territorial advertisement and pair communication versus alarm signaling) and occupy different acoustic registers. The dual-acoustic-identity documentation at degree 150 establishes a first-of-its-kind register in the engine's species axis and opens the possibility that future species with multiple acoustic modes (vocal plus mechanical, contact plus flight, song plus alarm) may be documented with similar structural depth.

The dual-function alarm concept is significant for animal communication research because it establishes that non-vocal mechanical sounds can serve the same anti-predator function as vocal alarm calls. The engine's documentation at degree 150 incorporates the primary research anchors and preserves the functional interpretation at first principles.

The cultural paradox is structurally significant: the Mourning Dove is simultaneously one of America's most beloved backyard birds (whose mournful call is embedded in poetic and musical tradition) and one of its most hunted game birds (whose 20-million-annual harvest makes it a major economic and management species). The same species occupies radically different positions in American culture — bird-watcher beloved versus hunter's pursuit — and the engine's documentation at degree 150 holds both positions simultaneously without resolving the tension.

Washington had historically been one of ten states banning Mourning Dove hunting on cultural grounds. The 2019 opening was a contested policy change that involved public hearings, scientific population assessments, and consultation with hunting and birding communities. The Washington-specific opening is documentary-important because the Seattle 360 engine is grounded in Washington state and Pacific Northwest cultural context, and the WDFW 2019 decision is a recent event within the engine's contemporary scope.

The species' **extraordinary reproductive capacity** — **2-6 broods per year** depending on latitude, with incubation at **14 days** and nestling period at **12-14 days** producing a complete breeding cycle of about one month — compensates for hunting mortality. In southern states, Mourning Doves can breed in every month of the year. Even in the Pacific Northwest, breeding extends from March through September. The combination of high reproductive rate, broad habitat tolerance, and adaptability to human landscapes makes the Mourning Dove one of the most resilient bird species in North America, and the engine's documentation at degree 150 preserves the population-ecology analysis at first principles.

The **ubiquity paradox** is structurally significant because it reveals the limits of ambient-sound perception: the most frequently heard bird call in North America is also one of the least consciously perceived, because conscious attention tends to filter for novelty and unfamiliar sounds while discounting familiar ambient presence. In the breeding season (March-September), males call extensively from high exposed perches to advertise territory and attract mates and to participate in dawn choruses. In fall and winter, calling is reduced but not absent — occasional cooing on warm winter days continues to mark the species' year-round presence. The year-round vocalization is structurally different from the seasonal-only patterns of most songbirds and contributes to the Mourning Dove's status as the most reliable ambient call in North American soundscapes.
