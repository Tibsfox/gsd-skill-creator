## Taxonomic State

- **Family:** Columbidae (2nd entry -- Band-tailed Pigeon 149, Mourning Dove 150)
- **Order:** Columbiformes (continuing)
- **Class:** Aves
- **Species note:** The Mourning Dove (*Zenaida macroura*) is the most abundant and widespread native dove in North America, with an estimated population of 350 million birds. Its **mournful "oo-woo-woo-woo-woo"** -- a soft, descending five-note coo often mistaken for an owl -- is one of the most universally recognized bird sounds on the continent, heard from suburban backyards to desert scrub. The name itself comes from this melancholy call. Beyond the voice, the Mourning Dove produces a sharp **wing whistle** on takeoff and landing, generated by air rushing through modified outer primary feathers. This is an honest mechanical signal -- it intensifies with urgency, serving as an alarm that alerts nearby doves to danger. It is the engine's **2nd mechanical sound**, following the Common Nighthawk's wing boom (146). Despite being the **most harvested game bird in North America** -- approximately 20 million shot annually across 40 states that allow hunting -- the species' prolific breeding (up to 6 broods per year, one of the longest breeding seasons of any North American bird) keeps populations stable. A bird that sounds like grief, flies like a whistle, and thrives under pressure.
