## Surprises

- **A 1973 probe reached Saturn first of any craft.** Pioneer 11 was the first spacecraft to fly by / reach Saturn on 1 September 1979, closest approach about 20,900 km above the cloud tops — more than a year ahead of Voyager 1 and about two years ahead of Voyager 2 — returning the first close-up data on the ringed planet, its rings, and its moons.
- **A probe discovered a new ring of Saturn.** Pioneer 11 discovered Saturn's narrow outer F ring during the 1979 flyby — a thin ring just beyond the main A ring that Voyager 1 would later image in detail — the first new Saturn ring found by a spacecraft.
- **A probe found Saturn's magnetic field for the first time.** Pioneer 11 crossed Saturn's bow shock on 31 August 1979 and made the first detection of Saturn's intrinsic magnetic field and magnetosphere, the first in-situ characterization of its magnetic environment and radiation belts.
- **A much closer Jupiter pass gave the first look at the polar regions.** Pioneer 11 flew by Jupiter on 3 December 1974 (UTC) about three times closer than Pioneer 10 — roughly 43,000 km from the cloud tops — arcing high over the polar regions and using Jupiter's gravity to redirect itself across the solar system to Saturn.
- **A secretive snake of the Pacific Northwest wears a bright ring at its neck.** The Ring-necked Snake (*Diadophis punctatus*), a small, slender snake of Pacific Northwest forests and woodland edges, is dark above with a distinctive bright orange-to-yellow ring around its neck and a vivid orange-red underside it reveals when alarmed (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Pioneer 11 reaching the ringed world and finding its narrow F ring.
- **A moist-forest perennial carries its leaves in rings around the stem.** Sweetscented Bedstraw (*Galium triflorum*), a slender, trailing perennial of moist Pacific Northwest forest understories, bears its small leaves in whorls — a ring of leaves encircling the square stem at each node — and clings by tiny hooked bristles, sweetly vanilla-scented when dried (ecological description only) — a mirror of the encircling rings of Saturn and the band that wraps around.

1. **A sustain KEEPS the predecessor's shared domain vocabulary — it does not remove it.** Unlike a rotation, this is a SUSTAIN (mode=continuation): Pioneer 11 and its sister Pioneer 10 share almost all of their domain vocabulary, so the rewrite KEPT the shared outer-planets content (the outer solar system, deep space, the giant planets, Jupiter, Saturn, the asteroid belt, RTG power, the Pioneer plaque, the escape trajectory, the gravity assist, the flyby, NASA Ames, TRW) and replaced only the predecessor-specific facts — Pioneer 10 as the subject, the 2 March 1972 launch, the first asteroid-belt traverse, the first Jupiter flyby of December 1973, the first escape trajectory, the first message-to-ETI plaque, the 23 January 2003 last signal, and the Marbled Godwit + American Dunegrass pairing — with Pioneer 11's: the 6 April 1973 launch, the closer Jupiter flyby of December 1974 and the gravity assist on to Saturn, the first flyby of Saturn, the F-ring discovery, the first detection of Saturn's magnetic field, the study of Titan, the help validating the Voyager route, the ~1995 end, and the Ring-necked Snake + Sweetscented Bedstraw pairing.

3. **The Saturn result is the first FLYBY of Saturn — first to reach Saturn, not first to orbit.** Pioneer 11 made the first flyby of Saturn (1 September 1979); the catalog scopes this as the first to fly by / reach Saturn and notes that Voyager 1 (1980) and Voyager 2 (1981) came later, that Cassini was the first orbiter (2004), and that the Voyagers launched first (1977) but Pioneer 11 reached Saturn first.
