## Mission Overview

Pioneer 11 is the obs#2 sustain of the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA deep-space probe and the sister of Pioneer 10 whose defining achievements are all at Saturn: the first spacecraft to fly by Saturn, the discovery of Saturn's narrow outer F ring, and the first detection of Saturn's intrinsic magnetic field and magnetosphere. It is managed by NASA's Ames Research Center and built by TRW (NOT JPL). A spin-stabilized flyby probe with a large dish high-gain antenna — not an orbiter or a lander — it launched on an Atlas-Centaur (AC-30) from Cape Canaveral LC-36B on 6 April 1973 (02:11 UTC; 5 April local), powered by two SNAP-19 radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) fueled with plutonium-238 rather than solar panels, because sunlight is far too weak in the outer solar system. Its payload, the sister suite to Pioneer 10's, included a magnetometer, plasma and charged-particle detectors, cosmic-ray and trapped-radiation instruments, the Imaging Photopolarimeter (IPP) that built spin-scan images of Jupiter and Saturn, an infrared radiometer, and meteoroid and asteroid-dust detectors. The story is the long outbound voyage on to Saturn: across the inner solar system on RTG power, through the main asteroid belt (the second craft to cross it, after Pioneer 10), and on to a much closer Jupiter flyby than Pioneer 10 — closest approach 3 December 1974 (UTC), about 43,000 km from the cloud tops, roughly three times closer, with the first look at Jupiter's polar regions and a dive through the intense radiation belts. That Jupiter flyby was the second of the giant planet (Pioneer 10 was first), and the gravity assist there redirected Pioneer 11 across the solar system to Saturn — the assist described, not anchored, because Mariner 10 used a Venus assist earlier (February 1974). On 1 September 1979 it became the first spacecraft to fly by / reach Saturn (closest approach about 20,900 km above the cloud tops), threading past the ring plane and returning the first close-up data on the ringed planet, its rings, and its moons; it discovered the narrow outer F ring, crossed Saturn's bow shock on 31 August 1979 and made the first detection of Saturn's magnetic field and magnetosphere, returned about 440 images, and studied Titan (too cold for life), while its carefully chosen path helped validate a safe route for the later Voyager flybys. The scoping is load-bearing: the Saturn flyby is the first spacecraft to fly by / reach Saturn (Voyager 1 came in November 1980 and Voyager 2 in August 1981; Cassini was the first orbiter, 2004; the Voyagers launched in 1977 but Pioneer 11 reached Saturn first); the F ring was DISCOVERED by Pioneer 11 (Voyager imaged it in detail later); the Jupiter→Saturn gravity assist is not a "first gravity assist" (Mariner 10, February 1974); and Pioneer 11's Jupiter flyby is the SECOND (Pioneer 10 was first). Routine contact ended in 1995 (the last routine contact about 30 September 1995, the final usable engineering data about 24 November 1995), and the inert probe continues outward (past tense). Pioneer 11 SUSTAINS the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION substrate-axis at obs#2 (the first consecutive sustain on the new axis opened by Pioneer 10). NSSDCA/COSPAR 1973-019A.

## Surprises

## Dedication

v1.251 Pioneer 11 is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's first probe to Saturn and carried the road to the giant planets on past Jupiter. The Ames Research Center team who managed the mission; the TRW engineers who raised the spin-stabilized probe, sister to Pioneer 10, with its great dish antenna and its nuclear power supply; the launch team who lit the Atlas-Centaur off Cape Canaveral in April 1973; the navigators who threaded the spacecraft a third again closer past Jupiter and bent its path clear across the solar system to Saturn; and the scientists behind the magnetometer, the particle and radiation detectors, the imaging photopolarimeter, and the dust counters. They sent a small craft where none had gone — high over Jupiter's poles, then on to the ringed planet itself, first of any spacecraft to reach it, threading the ring plane to find the narrow F ring and the field that wraps around the world. From there the probe climbed away from the Sun, answering fainter and fainter until the last signals came home in 1995, far out in the open country its sister had opened, the rings of Saturn behind it.
