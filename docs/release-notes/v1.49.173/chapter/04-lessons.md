1. **Succession in consecutive degrees is a rare structural event.**
   The Hendrix-Jones succession pair at degrees 36 and 37 is the engine's first consecutive-degrees placement where two artists share neighborhood, school, instrument, genre, family connection, and explicit claimed lineage. This density will not recur at every degree, and when it does recur it should be tracked as a distinct engine-level event. Future consecutive-degree pairs should be checked against the succession-pair criteria to identify whether the placement is structural or coincidental.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #5404_

2. **Family trios are unprecedented and deserve their own ledger.**
   Before degree 37, no avian family had held three consecutive positions from a standing start at zero. Swallows (Hirundinidae) held five degrees but started from prior coverage of the family. Turdidae and Parulidae have five members each but spread across many degrees. Trochilidae jumped from absent to three consecutive members. Future family-trio candidates should be flagged in advance and the cumulative-statistics ledger should add a "Family Trios" line.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #5405_

3. **Genre repetition in consecutive degrees signals a living tradition.**
   Blues Rock at 36 (Hendrix) and Blues Rock at 37 (Jones) is the engine's first consecutive-degrees genre repetition. The repetition persists because Jones is the direct inheritor of the tradition Hendrix represented. Future genre repetitions should be read as structural evidence of continuity rather than as scheduling artifacts.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #5406_

4. **The ABA genus pattern matches the acoustic diminuendo.**
   Selasphorus*-*Calypte*-*Selasphorus* on the taxonomic axis matched thrum-sonic boom-filament on the acoustic axis and matched loud-loudest-quietest on the energy axis. When three independent axes align in the same order across the same three degrees, the engine should track the alignment as a meta-structural event worth naming.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #5407_

5. **Sound scales with mass in hummingbirds.**
   The largest hummingbird in the trio (Anna's at 4.3 g) produced the loudest sound (sonic boom at 4 kHz, louder than its own vocal song). The smallest (Calliope at 3.1 g) produced the quietest (filament barely audible against ambient noise). The middle (Rufous at 3.4 g) produced the middle signal (continuous thrum at 52 Hz). Future small-bird degrees should predict signal magnitude from mass and measure the prediction against the actual data.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #5408_

6. **The Inheritor pattern fills the gap between the Supernova and the Deep Root.**
   The Deep Root (35, Cray) stayed in the PNW throughout a fifty-year career. The Supernova (36, Hendrix) left for London, burned for four years at maximum, and died. The Inheritor (37, Jones) grew up in the specific crater the Supernova left and built something new from the same materials. Three modes of PNW-to-global trajectory: stay, leave and burn out, arrive after the burn. Future PNW-lifer or PNW-returnee candidates should be checked against the three-mode taxonomy.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #5409_

7. **Altitude partitions the hummingbird niche cleanly.**
   Anna's holds the lowlands (0-500 m, urban and coastal). Rufous ranges broadly (0-3,000+ m, generalist). Calliope specializes in the mountaintops (1,200-3,400 m, subalpine). From Seattle backyard feeders to Mount Rainier summit meadows, the trio partitions the full elevational gradient. Future multi-species family arcs should check for elevational niche partitioning as a structural mechanism.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #5410_

8. **Taxonomy correctness can cost naming meaning.**
   Stellula calliope* ("little star of Calliope") became *Selasphorus calliope* ("Calliope's flame-bearer") when AOS merged the genera. The molecular evidence is unambiguous. But the old name fit the bird. Future taxonomic merges should be documented with both the scientific justification and the nomenclatural cost, and the engine should preserve old genus names where they carry poetic information.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #5411_

9. **Three-generation blues-guitar chains are documentable through repertoire.**
   Elmore James → Jimi Hendrix → Ayron Jones. Hendrix covered James's "Bleeding Heart" with Band of Gypsys. Jones covered James's "The Sky Is Crying" on *Chronicles of the Kid* (2023). The lineage is not just biographical but repertorial. Future blues-lineage degrees should track which artists have covered which standards and how those covers map generational transmission.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #5412_

10. **The Central District is a structural engine-level phenomenon, not a demographic coincidence.**
   Ten of thirty-eight degrees (26.3%) originate in the same few square miles of Seattle. This density is too high to be accidental. It reflects documented historical migration patterns (Black American migration from the South during and after World War II, Jackson Street corridor development, Garfield High School as cultural anchor), documented redlining and urban renewal policies, and documented current gentrification pressures. Future CD degrees should be treated as part of a neighborhood-as-character portrait, not as isolated artist entries.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #5413_

11. **Fender corporate endorsement maps artistic lineage in this engine.**

Corporate lineage mirrors artistic lineage: Fender → Hendrix → Cray → Jones. Future Stratocaster degrees (and future single-instrument runs) should track corporate endorsements as part of the instrument's documentary trail.

Future degrees encountering AOS genus reshuffles should explicitly weigh the taxonomic gain against the naming cost.
