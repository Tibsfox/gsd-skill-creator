## Mission Overview

Deep Impact is the obs#4 sustainer of the COMET-AND-ASTEROID-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's Discovery-program mission to comet 9P/Tempel 1, a flyby spacecraft plus a self-guided probe whose defining firsts are the first look beneath a comet's surface, the first controlled cratering experiment on a comet nucleus, and the first self-guided probe to reach a comet's nucleus. The spacecraft was built by Ball Aerospace & Technologies (Boulder, CO) and the mission was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), with principal investigator Michael A'Hearn of the University of Maryland. Deep Impact launched 12 January 2005 on a Delta II from Cape Canaveral and cruised six months to the Jupiter-family comet 9P/Tempel 1. The spacecraft comprised a flyby spacecraft and a 372 kg self-guided probe with a copper core — about half copper by mass (~49%, with a dedicated ~113 kg all-copper cratering mass), chosen because copper is absent from comet spectra and so would not contaminate the measured composition. The encounter was the mission: on 4 July 2005 the probe separated and, steering itself with its own targeting camera, reached the sunlit face of Tempel 1's nucleus at ~10.3 km/s relative speed, while the flyby spacecraft watched from nearby and Earth/space telescopes observed. The event produced a brilliant flash and a rising plume of fine, previously-hidden material — the first look beneath a comet's surface. The story is the launch from Cape Canaveral, the cruise to Tempel 1, the release of the self-guided probe, the approach and the brilliant flash, and the reading of the plume — the first study of fresh subsurface cometary material and the first deliberately-created, instrumented crater on a comet nucleus. The science is central: the ejected material was far finer (powder-like) and more abundant than expected, and the nucleus proved highly porous and weakly bound — a primitive, fluffy, low-density body; water ice, carbon dioxide, and organic compounds were detected in the plume. The newly-formed crater was obscured by the bright dust at the time and was not clearly imaged then; Stardust-NExT (1.272) later imaged that crater during its February 2011 Tempel 1 flyby — a direct lineage tie between two missions on this axis. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: Deep Impact looked BENEATH a comet's surface and studied its interior — it returned NO sample (distinct from Stardust's comet-sample return and OSIRIS-REx's asteroid-sample return); it was a Discovery mission (the eighth — NEAR=1, Mars Pathfinder=2, Lunar Prospector=3, Stardust=4, Genesis=5, CONTOUR=6, MESSENGER=7, Deep Impact=8), NOT the first Discovery mission (NEAR holds that); and earlier comet missions — the 1986 Halley armada, Giotto, ICE — observed only the surface and coma from flyby and never reached or exposed the subsurface. ★★ The mission concluded in 2013 (launched 2005, encountered Tempel 1 on 4 July 2005, flew the EPOXI extension to comet 103P/Hartley 2 on 4 November 2010, contact lost and concluded September 2013), so the catalog frames the story in the PAST tense throughout (Deep Impact flew, released, observed, revealed, studied). ★ The dates are kept distinct: launch 12 January 2005, Tempel 1 encounter 4 July 2005, Hartley 2 (EPOXI) 4 November 2010, concluded 2013. ## Surprises

- **A spacecraft looked beneath a comet's surface for the first time.** Deep Impact's self-guided probe reached the sunlit nucleus of comet 9P/Tempel 1 on 4 July 2005, and the brilliant flash and rising plume of fine, previously-hidden material gave the first look beneath a comet's surface — exposing fresh subsurface material that had never been studied before.
- **The ejecta was far finer and more abundant than expected.** The plume was made of powder-like material far finer and more abundant than predicted, and the nucleus proved highly porous and weakly bound — a primitive, fluffy, low-density body rather than the harder surface some had expected.
- **Water ice, carbon dioxide, and organics in the interior.** The spectra of the plume detected water ice, carbon dioxide, and organic compounds — the chemistry of a primitive relic of the early solar system, read directly from below a comet's surface.
- **A copper-core probe chosen so it would not contaminate the spectra, and a crater later imaged by a sibling mission.** The 372 kg probe had a copper core (about half copper) precisely because copper is absent from comet spectra, so it would not contaminate the measured composition; and because the newly-formed crater was obscured by the bright dust at the time, Stardust-NExT (1.272) later imaged that crater during its February 2011 Tempel 1 flyby — a direct lineage tie between two missions on this axis.
- **The surviving flyby spacecraft flew on to a second comet.** After the Tempel 1 encounter, the still-healthy flyby spacecraft was retargeted as EPOXI and flew past comet 103P/Hartley 2 on 4 November 2010, returning detailed images of a small, active, dumbbell-shaped nucleus, before contact was lost in 2013 and the mission was declared concluded.
- **A small lizard that flashes its hidden blue underside into view.** The Western Fence Lizard (*Sceloporus occidentalis*), a small sun-basking lizard of the rocky, open and woodland-edge country of the interior Pacific Northwest — widespread in interior Oregon and reaching its northern limit in Washington along the Columbia River Gorge — does push-up displays on exposed rocks and logs that flash its normally-hidden bright-blue throat and belly patches into view, a mirror of the mission that brought a comet's hidden interior into view.
- **A shaded-forest flower whose petals sweep back so the bloom looks turned inside-out.** The Inside-out Flower (*Vancouveria hexandra*), a delicate perennial of moist, shaded Pacific Northwest forests west of the Cascades, holds its petals and sepals sharply swept backward so each small white bloom looks turned inside-out, its inner parts brought forward into view (ecological description only) — bound with Deep Impact's bringing of the hidden interior to light.

## Dedication

v1.274 Deep Impact is dedicated to the people across NASA, the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, the University of Maryland, and Ball Aerospace who built and flew the first mission to look beneath the surface of a comet. The engineers in Boulder who built a flyby spacecraft and a small, self-guided probe that steered itself with its own camera to the sunlit face of 9P/Tempel 1, and the flight team who held the cruise across six months and the encounter to the second. The teams behind the copper-core probe, chosen so it would leave the comet's own chemistry untouched in the spectra, and the navigators who guided that probe to the nucleus while the flyby spacecraft watched and read the brilliant flash and the rising plume of fine, previously-hidden material. The scientists who studied the interior they had brought to light — the water ice, the carbon dioxide, the organics, and the fine, porous, primitive body beneath the crust. The mission carries the work of Michael A'Hearn. They looked beneath the surface and read the interior — the first study of fresh material from below a comet's surface, and a window onto the early solar system.
