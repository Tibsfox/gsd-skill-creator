## Summary

v1.49.1009 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.199 to 1.200, a round-number capstone, adding Parker Solar Probe — the first spacecraft to fly through the Sun's corona — as the obs#5 INTRA-AXIS continuation that sustains the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY substrate-axis (axis-rotation #26 sustained at obs#5). The axis opened at v1005 OSO with the continuous solar-cycle monitoring program, sustained at v1006 TRACE with the high-resolution extreme-ultraviolet imaging of the corona, sustained at v1007 RHESSI with the hard-X-ray and gamma-ray flare imaging spectroscopy, and sustained at v1008 IRIS with the interface-region UV imaging spectroscopy. Parker Solar Probe carries that solar watch all the way to the Sun: where the prior observatories observed the Sun from afar, Parker Solar Probe flew through the corona and sampled the plasma and fields where the solar wind is born, the first spacecraft to reach and measure that region in place. The axis now holds five observations on the Sun, the continuous-monitoring program at OSO, the high-resolution EUV imaging at TRACE, the high-energy flare imaging spectroscopy at RHESSI, the interface-region UV imaging spectroscopy at IRIS, and the first in-situ flight through the corona at Parker Solar Probe, sustaining at obs#5 cumulative and now spanning remote sensing and in-situ measurement at the source.

Parker Solar Probe is a NASA Heliophysics / Living With a Star flagship built and operated by the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory, managed through NASA Goddard Space Flight Center, and named for Eugene Parker, who predicted the solar wind in 1958 — the first NASA mission named for a living person at launch. The mission launched on 12 August 2018 aboard a Delta IV Heavy and continues to operate. Using seven Venus gravity assists to lower its perihelion across successive orbits, Parker Solar Probe drew progressively closer to the Sun, flying through the corona behind a Sun-facing carbon-composite thermal protection shield that radiates the heat away and holds the spacecraft body and instruments near room temperature. Behind the shield the FIELDS, SWEAP, IS☉IS, and WISPR investigations measure the plasma and fields in place and image the corona in white light.

The signature of the mission is the first spacecraft to fly through the solar corona. Drawing nearer the Sun than any spacecraft before it, Parker Solar Probe flew inside the corona and turned it from a remotely observed glow into a medium measured from within, the first in-situ measurements from inside the corona.

The mission opened the in-situ study of the solar-wind source region. Inside the corona Parker Solar Probe sampled the plasma and fields where the wind is accelerated, the first in-place measurements of the wind's source, and discovered the magnetic switchbacks, the sharp S-shaped reversals of the field in the near-Sun solar wind, a new clue to how the corona launches the wind. It also crossed the Alfvén critical surface, the outer boundary of the corona where the wind becomes supersonic and decouples from the Sun.

The carbon-composite thermal protection shield gave the mission its foundation. By absorbing the sunlight and radiating it away, the radiating, non-ablative shield keeps the instruments near room temperature while the shield faces the Sun, so the Sun's heat is the operating condition the mission was engineered to fly within.

The axis-relevant substrate of Parker Solar Probe at this degree is its continuation of the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY substrate-axis at obs#5 on the Sun, carrying the solar watch from remote sensing all the way to the source. Parker Solar Probe sustains the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#5 NEW LOCKED, opens the SOLAR-WIND-SOURCE-IN-SITU-LINEAGE thread at obs#1 cumulative, and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#72 cumulative (axis-rotation #26 sustained at obs#5). The forward continuation favors either later solar and heliospheric observatories or a substrate-axis rotation #27 to an under-served axis.

Parker Solar Probe opens the in-situ-at-the-source end of the solar watch on the solar axis, and the mission records the Parker Solar Probe team at the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory, NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, and the namesake Eugene Parker. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.199 to 1.200, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#83 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Flying to the source measures the wind where remote sensing only inferred it.** Rather than observing the corona from afar, Parker Solar Probe flew into it and sampled the plasma and fields in place, showing that an in-situ measurement at the source reads the wind where it is born.
2. **A radiating shield turns the heat into the operating condition.** By absorbing the sunlight and radiating it away, the carbon-composite shield keeps the instruments near room temperature, showing that the Sun's heat can be the condition a mission is engineered to work within rather than a thing to avoid.
3. **Borrowed planetary momentum can bring a spacecraft to the Sun.** By using seven Venus gravity assists to lower the perihelion across successive orbits, Parker Solar Probe reached the corona without the fuel a direct dive would require, showing that a sequence of flybys can carry a spacecraft to the source.
4. **An in-place measurement finds structure a distant view smooths away.** By reading the near-Sun solar wind directly, Parker Solar Probe discovered the magnetic switchbacks, showing that the young wind holds structure only an in-situ measurement at the source can reveal.
5. **A continuation can carry an axis from remote sensing to in-situ measurement.** By flying to the source the prior observatories watched from afar, Parker Solar Probe sustained the solar-observatory axis at obs#5 on the same target, showing that an axis can deepen from remote sensing to direct measurement without rotating away.
6. **A round-number capstone can complete an axis.** By carrying the solar watch all the way to the Sun at degree 1.200, Parker Solar Probe completed the axis from monitoring and imaging down to in-situ measurement at the source, showing that a capstone can close a thread cleanly.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across a distinct-palette mission.** Because Parker Solar Probe reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.199 template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the near-Sun flight with a distinct corona-gold / switchback-orange / shield-bright palette, making the distinct-palette mission a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The hottest place in the catalog is the one the mission was built to work within.** Parker Solar Probe flies through the corona, yet the radiating shield holds the instruments near room temperature, so the catalog's near-Sun entry is the mission for which the Sun's heat is the designed operating condition.
- **A fire-following woodpecker mirrors the spacecraft that flies toward the Sun.** The Black-backed Woodpecker, which moves toward the heat-touched forest other birds avoid, enters as the moving-toward-the-heat mirror to the first spacecraft through the corona, so the catalog pairs a fire-following woodpecker with a Sun-bound mission.
- **A pine whose cones open in the heat mirrors the mission enabled by the heat.** The Lodgepole Pine's serotinous cones open only in intense heat, so the catalog's near-Sun mission is paired with a mountain pine whose potential, like the mission, is released by the very heat others endure.

## Dedication

v1.200 Parker Solar Probe is dedicated to the team that conceived and flew the first spacecraft through the Sun's corona: the Parker Solar Probe team at the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory who built and operate the spacecraft, its carbon-composite thermal protection shield, and the FIELDS, SWEAP, IS☉IS, and WISPR investigations; NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, which manages the mission within the Heliophysics and Living With a Star program; the instrument teams across the partner institutions who read the plasma and fields of the near-Sun solar wind; and the operations teams who hold the shield toward the Sun through every close pass. It is dedicated as well to Eugene Parker, who predicted the solar wind in 1958 and saw the mission named for him fly to the Sun to measure that wind at the source. Parker Solar Probe flies through the corona, samples the solar-wind source region in place, and carries the solar watch all the way to the Sun.
