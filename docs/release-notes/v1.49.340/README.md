**Roosevelt Elk and the Closing of the Elk Trio.** The **Roosevelt Elk** (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*) is the Pacific Northwest coastal subspecies of North American elk, the largest elk subspecies by body mass, named for **Theodore Roosevelt** who campaigned for early-twentieth-century Olympic Peninsula conservation protections leading to **Mount Olympus National Monument** (1909) and ultimately **Olympic National Park** (1938). The engine's documentation across degrees 197-199 covers three vocalizations — the **bugle** at degree 197 (E=7, long-distance challenge), the **cow call** at degree 198 (E=5, intra-herd social contact), and the **rut roar** at degree 199 (E=7, close-combat bellowing) — forming a complete acoustic arc of the species' social-communication repertoire. The rut roar specifically is produced during physical confrontation between rival bulls at peak rut (typically September-October in the Pacific Northwest), accompanying antler-locked shoving matches, structural collision, and occasional serious injury or death. Where the bugle carries across valleys as a challenge and the cow call coordinates the herd, the rut roar is the vocalization of maximum exertion at contact range, an expression that 1,000-pound animals are producing at their physiological limits.

**The Rut Roar as Close-Combat Vocalization.** The **rut roar** is acoustically distinct from both the bugle and the cow call in ways that the E=7 rating captures but does not fully explain. The bugle at degree 197 (also E=7) is a sustained ascending-then-descending vocalization produced at chest-expanded amplitude with the bull's head elevated and the sound projected across open terrain at distances up to two kilometers under optimal conditions; it is the advertisement-and-challenge vocalization, produced at distance, designed to carry. The rut roar at degree 199 (E=7) is a deep, guttural, sustained bellowing produced during physical combat itself, with the bull's head often lowered in antler-engagement posture, the respiratory effort diverted to combat-exertion rather than sound-projection, and the acoustic signature characterized by a rougher, lower-frequency texture than the bugle's clear tonal projection. Both vocalizations register at E=7 but occupy different functional registers: the bugle is strategic, the rut roar is tactical. Combat encounters can last from seconds to several minutes, with the rut roar produced continuously throughout as the bulls engage in shoving matches, attempts at unbalancing the opponent, and occasional disengagement-and-reengagement sequences. Injuries are common — antler damage, eye trauma, structural antler breakage — and deaths from stress cardiomyopathy or combat-induced exhaustion are documented though not rare-but-not-routine. Hearing ranges during October rut are common in **Hoh Rainforest**, **Quinault Rainforest**, **Queets Valley**, and lowland Olympic National Park valleys, where acoustic monitoring programs and visitor-observation opportunities converge.

## Part B: Roosevelt Elk Rut Roar — The Close-Combat Vocalization and the Closing of the Elk Trio

- **Species Baseline and Taxonomic Identity:** **Roosevelt Elk** (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*) is a member of **Order Artiodactyla / Family Cervidae / Genus Cervus / species canadensis / subspecies roosevelti**, the Pacific Northwest coastal subspecies of North American elk and the largest elk subspecies by body mass. The subspecies is distinguished from Rocky Mountain Elk (*C. c. nelsoni*) by darker pelage, larger body, and smaller antlers relative to body size, representing the coastal-temperate-rainforest adaptation within the broader North American elk lineage. Adult bulls reach 700-1,100 pounds with exceptional individuals exceeding 1,200 pounds, and antler spreads of 3-5 feet with main-beam lengths up to 45 inches are typical for prime-age bulls.
- **Pacific Northwest Distribution and Habitat:** Roosevelt Elk populations concentrate across the **Olympic Peninsula** (the species' type locality and the densest extant population), the **Cascade Range western slopes**, the **Oregon Coast Range**, and the **northern California redwood zone** (including Redwood National Park and surrounding state parks). The distribution corresponds closely to the Pacific temperate rainforest biome, with elk occupying old-growth and second-growth forest interiors, river-valley lowland meadows, coastal prairie transitions, and sub-alpine summer range. The species is largely absent from the Cascade eastern slopes and from the broader inland Pacific Northwest dry-forest zone, which is instead occupied by Rocky Mountain Elk in the continuous elk geographic overlap.
- **Theodore Roosevelt and the Subspecies Naming:** The Roosevelt Elk subspecies is named for **Theodore Roosevelt**, the twenty-sixth President of the United States, whose early-twentieth-century campaign for Olympic Peninsula conservation led to the **Mount Olympus National Monument** designation in 1909 and ultimately to **Olympic National Park** establishment in 1938. Roosevelt's conservation advocacy — including the broader Antiquities Act (1906), Grand Canyon protection, and the establishment of multiple national parks and monuments across the western United States — is among the most consequential federal conservation interventions in American history. The Olympic Peninsula protections specifically preserved the Roosevelt Elk's core habitat at a time when market-hunting pressures, logging, and land development threatened the species' persistence. The subspecies naming honors this specific Roosevelt intervention.
- **Rut Timing and Seasonal Dynamics:** The Roosevelt Elk **rut** (breeding season) typically runs September through October in the Pacific Northwest, with peak intensity in the last two weeks of September and the first two weeks of October. Bulls arrive at rut condition through the summer-months antler-hardening and physiological-conditioning cycle, shedding velvet in late August and entering rut-competitive condition by early September. Cows come into estrus synchronously across the herd, with receptive windows of approximately 12-24 hours per cow, creating intense competitive pressure among bulls for reproductive access during short peak-rut windows. The compressed temporal structure of rut — high-intensity competition across a four-to-six-week window after which bulls disengage and return to low-energy winter recovery — produces the physiological and behavioral extremes that the rut roar vocalization accompanies.
- **The Rut Roar as Close-Combat Vocalization:** The **rut roar** is acoustically distinct from both the bugle (long-distance challenge) and the cow call (intra-herd contact). The vocalization is a deep, guttural, sustained bellowing produced during physical combat between rival bulls at peak rut, characterized by a rougher, lower-frequency texture than the bugle's clear tonal projection and by continuous rather than ascending-then-descending amplitude contour. The sound is produced at maximum respiratory effort with the bull's head often lowered in antler-engagement posture, so that the combat-exertion diverts respiratory capacity from sound-projection and produces the distinctive combat-amplitude texture. The E=7 rating matches the bugle's energy but captures a different register: the bugle is strategic (advertisement at distance), the rut roar is tactical (combat at contact).
- **Antler-Lock Mechanics and Combat Injuries:** Roosevelt Elk bull-bull combat encounters begin with an approach phase where bulls assess each other visually, escalate through bugling and posturing, and if not resolved by one bull's retreat, proceed to antler engagement. The bulls lower their heads and lock antlers, initiating a shoving match that can last from seconds to several minutes, with the bulls attempting to unbalance each other, force a directional movement, or induce disengagement through sustained pressure. Injuries are common: antler damage (breakage of main-beam tines, occasional fracture at the base), eye trauma from secondary-tine contact, structural skull fractures in severe encounters, and deaths from stress cardiomyopathy or combat-induced exhaustion (though fatal outcomes are documented but not routine). The combat is audible from hundreds of yards away through the combination of the rut roar vocalization and the secondary acoustic signatures of antler impact, vegetation disturbance, and ground-thud from foot-planting during shoving.
- **Olympic Peninsula Rut Observation Geography:** The Roosevelt Elk rut is most readily observed in **Olympic National Park** lowland valleys, particularly the **Hoh Rainforest**, **Quinault Rainforest**, and **Queets Valley** zones where dense elk populations concentrate during rut and where visitor infrastructure (roads, trails, observation platforms) enables direct acoustic and visual documentation. Park-service interpretive programs during October rut provide structured observation opportunities, and acoustic-monitoring research programs deploy hydrophone-equivalent terrestrial microphone arrays across rut habitat for long-term vocalization documentation. The rainforest canopy's acoustic properties — high humidity, dense foliage, consistent ambient noise from creek-and-river flow — create specific acoustic conditions that shape both the bull vocalizations themselves and the species' long-evolutionary adaptation to these rainforest communication constraints.
- **The 7-5-7 Elk Trio Energy Arc:** The three-degree arc across degrees 197-199 traces the acoustic amplitude signature of Roosevelt Elk social dynamics: E=7 bugle (degree 197, bulls at distance, challenge amplitude), E=5 cow call (degree 198, cows in herd, contact amplitude), E=7 rut roar (degree 199, bulls at contact, combat amplitude). The arc is not arbitrary. The cows occupy the social and amplitude center of the herd, with their vocalizations coordinating movement, maintaining calf-cow contact, and establishing the amplitude floor around which bull vocalizations oscillate. The bulls occupy the volatile extremes — advertisement at distance and combat at contact both push amplitude to E=7 through different mechanisms. The 7-5-7 pattern is the acoustic signature of a species where reproductive success concentrates in the bull cohort through competitive display and combat while social-group stability maintains in the cow cohort through lower-amplitude coordination.
- **Conservation Status and Population Recovery:** Roosevelt Elk populations across the Pacific Northwest have recovered substantially from late-nineteenth-century and early-twentieth-century lows driven by market hunting, land conversion, and habitat fragmentation. The 1909 **Mount Olympus National Monument** designation and 1938 **Olympic National Park** establishment preserved the core Olympic Peninsula habitat, and subsequent state management (**Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife**, **Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife**, **California Department of Fish and Wildlife**) and federal management (**Olympic National Park**, **Redwood National Park**, **Olympic National Forest**, **Siuslaw National Forest**) sustain the species across its Pacific Northwest range. Current population estimates place the Olympic Peninsula Roosevelt Elk population at approximately 5,000-7,000 individuals, with smaller populations across Oregon and California. The species is not federally listed but is monitored under state wildlife management programs for population trend, rut-season harvest management, and habitat-connectivity across its fragmented Cascade-Coast-Range-Redwood distribution.
- **Foundational Research and Literature:** The scientific documentation of Roosevelt Elk behavior and ecology has developed across the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. **Murie** (1951) *The Elk of North America* established the foundational cervid monograph covering all North American elk subspecies with detailed Roosevelt Elk treatment. **Peek** (2003) extended the behavioral-and-ecological treatment. **Geist** (1998) *Deer of the World* placed Roosevelt Elk within the broader global deer taxonomy. State wildlife agency population assessments and rut-behavior research programs contribute continuing documentation, and acoustic-monitoring research programs across Olympic National Park and Redwood National Park maintain long-term vocalization documentation that supports the species' entry into the engine's SPS documentation across the elk trio at degrees 197-199.
