TIROS-1 launched on April 1, 1960, from Cape Canaveral's Launch Complex 17A on a Thor-Able rocket. A 120-kilogram drum-shaped satellite, 107 centimeters in diameter and 48 centimeters tall, carrying two RCA vidicon cameras -- one wide-angle (104-degree field of view, 1,280-km ground footprint, 3-km resolution) and one narrow-angle (12-degree, 130-km footprint, 300-meter resolution). Spin-stabilized at twelve revolutions per minute, orbiting at 693-756 km altitude, circling once every 99.2 minutes. The cameras pointed at the Earth only during favorable geometry -- roughly 25 minutes per 99-minute orbit. In those windows, it saw everything. In 78 days of operation, TIROS-1 took 22,952 photographs of Earth's cloud cover, approximately 13,770 of sufficient quality for meteorological analysis. Before TIROS-1, the total number of satellite weather photographs in existence was zero.

On the first day, TIROS-1 photographed a previously unknown vortex system near New Zealand -- a cyclonic cloud pattern over the Southern Ocean that no ground station had reported because there are no weather stations there. A weather system discovered by satellite on day one. Within the first week, images were being used in operational weather forecasting. Within the first month, imagery of tropical disturbances, frontal systems, and cloud patterns that ground-based networks had never fully characterized was flowing into the National Weather Bureau's analysis centers. The quiet revolution: forecasters saw things they had not been able to see before. Frontal positions corrected by 200 kilometers. Tropical disturbances identified twelve hours earlier. The improvement was incremental. In aggregate, it was transformative. Before TIROS-1, the Great Galveston Hurricane of 1900 killed 6,000-12,000 people because nobody knew it was coming. After TIROS-1's descendants matured, Hurricane Andrew (Category 5, 1992) killed 65 -- because satellite tracking provided days of warning. The difference is measured in hundreds of thousands of lives saved per decade.
