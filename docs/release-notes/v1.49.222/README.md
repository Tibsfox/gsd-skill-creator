### THE NORTHERN FLYING SQUIRREL: THE NOCTURNAL GLIDER

The Northern Flying Squirrel is a small, strictly nocturnal, old-growth-dependent gliding mammal of the Pacific Northwest's ancient coniferous forests. At 110-230 grams (4-8 oz), it is the smallest member of the Sciuridae octet. It is almost never seen -- strictly nocturnal, inhabiting the darkest canopy of old-growth forest, making its barely audible "tseet" contact calls at frequencies most humans cannot hear.

This is the engine's **EIGHTH consecutive Sciuridae** (Yellow-bellied Marmot 75, Hoary Marmot 76, Olympic Marmot 77, Townsend's Ground Squirrel 78, Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel 79, Douglas Squirrel 80, Eastern Fox Squirrel 81, Northern Flying Squirrel 82) -- the **Sciuridae OCTET**. The longest family run in engine history extends to **EIGHT**. Three marmots + five squirrels. Six genera. Eight habitats. Alpine to old-growth.

This is also the engine's **first gliding mammal** -- the first species that can "fly" (glide) using a patagium, a membrane of skin stretching from wrist to ankle.

And this is the **twenty-second consecutive original CSV species**.

### TAXONOMY: *Glaucomys sabrinus*

- **Glaucomys** -- from Greek *glaukos* ("silver" or "gray") + *mys* ("mouse"). The silver mouse -- a reference to the squirrel's soft, silvery-gray fur.
- **sabrinus** -- from Latin *Sabrinus*, relating to the Severn River. Described by Shaw (1801).
- **Common names:** Northern Flying Squirrel (standard). The "flying" is a misnomer -- the animal glides, it does not fly.

- **Class:** Mammalia (21st mammalian entry; 19th species)

### THE SOUND: HIGH THIN "TSEET" -- 83rd Species-Side Acoustic Element

The Northern Flying Squirrel's vocalization is a **high, thin, barely audible "tseet" contact call** -- a nearly ultrasonic note used for social coordination in the nocturnal canopy:

- **Frequency range:** approximately 8,000-20,000+ Hz. At the upper edge of human hearing.
- **Duration:** 0.05-0.15 seconds per note. Very brief.
- **Structure:** Single thin notes. "Tseet." Brief and soft.
