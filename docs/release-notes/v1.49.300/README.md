## Taxonomic State

- **Family:** Odontophoridae (2nd and FINAL entry -- California Quail 159, Mountain Quail 160)
- **Order:** Galliformes (closing after 8 consecutive degrees, 153-160)
- **Class:** Aves
- **Species note:** The Mountain Quail (*Oreortyx pictus*) is the **largest quail in North America** -- roughly 11 inches long and 8-9 ounces, noticeably larger than the California Quail (159). In the Pacific Northwest, Mountain Quail inhabit dense brush on steep mountain slopes, from low-elevation chaparral to mid-elevation forest edges in the Cascades, Olympics, and Siskiyous. The **quee-ark call** is a loud, far-carrying single note -- a clear, descending whistle followed by a harsh terminal syllable. E=4, matching the California Quail's energy level. Where the California Quail's chi-CA-go is a three-note rhythmic assembly call, the Mountain Quail's quee-ark is a single piercing note that cuts through dense vegetation on steep slopes. The Mountain Quail wears a **long, straight head plume** -- two thin feathers that project straight backward from the crown, in stark visual contrast to the California Quail's forward-curving comma-shaped topknot. The plume comparison encapsulates the pair: California Quail is social, abundant, suburban-tolerant, forward-curving plume; Mountain Quail is secretive, range-restricted, slope-dwelling, backward-projecting plume. Both Odontophoridae, different ecological strategies. Mountain Quail are among the most elusive game birds in the Pacific Northwest -- they prefer to run upslope through dense brush rather than fly, and their cryptic coloring (rich chestnut, gray, and bold white flank bars) makes them nearly invisible in their preferred habitat. They undertake one of the few **altitudinal migrations** among quail -- walking (not flying) upslope in spring and downslope in autumn, sometimes covering several miles on foot. As the **FINAL Odontophoridae**, the Mountain Quail closes a two-degree New World quail bracket. The contrast with Phasianidae is instructive: where Phasianidae ran for six degrees (153-158) with species ranging from 4 pounds (Wild Turkey) to 12 ounces (White-tailed Ptarmigan), Odontophoridae ran for two degrees (159-160) with species ranging from 6 ounces (California Quail) to 9 ounces (Mountain Quail). The scale shift from grouse to quail marks a thinning of the Galliformes presence as the engine prepares to transition to whatever family comes next.
- **Galliformes complete run:** 8 consecutive degrees (153-160) is the longest single-order run in the engine. Two families: Phasianidae (153-158, 6 degrees) and Odontophoridae (159-160, 2 degrees). The order demonstrated a full acoustic range from the Wild Turkey's 1-mile gobble (E=6) to the White-tailed Ptarmigan's soft alpine cackling (E=3), and a full ecological range from lowland agricultural edges to alpine tundra above treeline. The energy curve across the order: 6, 5, 5, 5, 4, 3, 4, 4 -- a descent through the grouse followed by a stabilization through the quail.
- **Spokane enters:** Ryan Lewis grew up in **Spokane, Washington** -- eastern Washington, across the Cascades, 280 miles from Seattle. His entry marks only the 2nd non-Seattle city origin in the music track. Spokane's music scene is distinct from Seattle's: smaller, more DIY out of necessity, with fewer venues and labels but a scrappy independence. Lewis moved to Seattle and connected with Macklemore, and together they built one of the most commercially successful independent hip-hop releases ever. *The Heist* (2012) debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200, went platinum, won four Grammys -- all without a major label. Lewis's production (lush, sample-driven, cinematic) was essential to the project's success.
- **Quail pair summary:** California Quail (159) -- social, covey-forming, suburban-tolerant, chi-CA-go assembly call, forward-curving topknot plume, 6 oz, most recognizable quail in the West. Mountain Quail (160) -- secretive, slope-dwelling, dense brush specialist, quee-ark piercing call, straight backward plume, 9 oz, largest North American quail. Both Odontophoridae, both E=4, both ground-dwelling, but different ecological strategies and visual signatures. The pair brackets demonstrate the engine's comparative method.
