## Summary

The engine encounters its first name collision. For thirty-three degrees, every search has returned either the right data or no data. At degree 34, the search returns the wrong data. "The Tempos Seattle" produces a documented result -- a big band/swing ensemble founded in 1958 by Boeing engineer Henry Fichtner, sixteen pieces strong, led alongside Dr. Vernon J. Harkness, formally named in the fall of 1963 when bassist Wayne Watling proposed "The Tempos" for the nine-piece group, performing community dances across Washington State for sixty years. This is not the R&B/Soul group the CSV describes. The CSV places The Tempos in the Central District, genre R&B/Soul, era 1960s, energy E=5, label local (or small labels). None of these attributes match the documented big band. The big band Tempos were swing, Boeing-based, community-dance-oriented, function-hall-focused. The R&B/Soul Tempos of the Central District are hidden behind their better-documented namesake. The name collision is structurally distinct from degree 33's pure documentation gap: Larry English returned zero results (absence); The Tempos return results for the wrong ensemble (misdirection). Misdirection is more dangerous than absence because a researcher who finds the PNW Bands page might conclude the search is complete. The data exists -- it is simply data about a different band. The Shadow Name -- the thirty-fifth artist-city pattern -- is the documentation gap's more insidious cousin: instead of absence, a decoy. Two unrelated bands in the same city adopted the same name during the same era; band names in the 1960s were not registered or centrally catalogued; the big band's continuous sixty-year run created a durable archival footprint that the R&B group's unknown lifespan did not.

Meanwhile, in a Douglas-fir canopy somewhere in the Pacific Northwest -- could be the lowlands, could be the mountains, could be the coast, wherever the cones are heavy and the scales are tight -- a sound like pebbles clicking. Kip-kip-kip. Sharp. Percussive. A small flock hanging upside down from cones, working the scales with bills that cross at the tips like misaligned scissors -- except these scissors were designed this way, designed by a million years of answering the cone's armor with the bill's lever. The Red Crossbill. *Loxia curvirostra*. "Cross-billed curved-beak" -- both genus and species name the same defining feature, the crossed mandibles. Evolution's lockpick. The upper mandible curves one way, the lower curves the other, creating an X at the tip that pries open closed cones and extracts seeds that no straight-billed bird can reach. The bill is the key to a taxonomic puzzle: at least ten distinct call types in North America, each with a diagnostic flight call, a bill morphology optimized for a particular conifer, and largely assortative mating. Type 3 haunts Douglas-fir and western hemlock, the core PNW types; Type 10 follows Sitka spruce along the coast; Type 2 traces ponderosa pine through the eastern Cascades; Type 5 tracks lodgepole pine; Type 9 was elevated to full species status in 2017 as the Cassia Crossbill, *Loxia sinesciuris* -- "without squirrels" -- confined to roughly seventy square kilometers of southern Idaho where the absence of Red Squirrels freed the seeds for specialized evolution. This is speciation in progress -- an adaptive radiation observable through flight calls and bill measurements, happening now. The dialect -- the thirty-fifth acoustic element -- is the call that maintains these boundaries.

**THE CROSSED MANDIBLES -- EVOLUTION'S LOCKPICK.** One of the most extraordinary morphological adaptations in any bird. The upper mandible curves to one side while the lower curves the opposite way, creating an X-shaped crossing at the tip. Insert closed bill between overlapping scales of a conifer cone, bite, the lateral crossing acts as a lever pushing the scale sideways, the tongue extracts the seed. A few seconds per scale. This gives crossbills access to CLOSED cones -- cones that have not yet opened to release seeds naturally. The Pine Siskin (degree 12) waits for cones to open. The Cassin's Finch (degree 33) takes seeds from cones already released. The crossbill goes in while the cone is still locked shut. Individual crossbills are either "left-billed" or "right-billed" in approximately 1:1 ratio. Craig Benkman's research quantified the coevolution: bill depth correlates with cone hardness; husking groove width correlates with seed size; foraging efficiency is type-specific. The bill is the key, the cone is the lock. Each type has a different key. The flight call is the label on the key.

**THE TYPE SYSTEM -- INCIPIENT SPECIATION OBSERVABLE IN REAL TIME.** What appears to be a single species is actually a complex of at least ten distinct call types, each associated with different conifer species, different bill morphologies, and different flight calls. Jeff Groth in the late 1980s and 1990s demonstrated that Red Crossbills sort into groups based on flight calls -- each individual gives only calls of a single type, consistent through life. Craig Benkman built on this with decades of research showing an ongoing adaptive radiation -- each type has evolved a bill optimized for a particular conifer. Types most relevant to the PNW: Type 3 (western hemlock / Douglas-fir, small bill, core PNW range), Type 10 (Sitka spruce, coast), Type 2 (ponderosa pine, eastern Cascades), Type 5 (lodgepole pine). The most dramatic outcome: Type 9 was elevated to full species in 2017 as the Cassia Crossbill (*Loxia sinesciuris*, "without squirrels"), confined to roughly seventy square kilometers of lodgepole pine in southern Idaho where the absence of Red Squirrels allowed specialized evolution. Type 8 (Newfoundland) is listed as Endangered in Canada. This is the engine's first species with a vocal type system representing active speciation.

**THE DIALECT -- THIRTY-FIFTH ACOUSTIC ELEMENT.** A population-level vocal marker that identifies the speaker's group, maintains social cohesion within the group, and enforces reproductive boundaries between groups. After the cadenza's individual virtuosity (degree 33), the dialect introduces collective identity. The progression: whisper (31) to buzz (32) to cadenza (33) to dialect (34). From the quietest collective signal through the loudest collective signal through the most complex individual signal to the most socially significant group signal. The dialect is the first vocalization in the engine that SEPARATES populations -- that functions as a barrier rather than a bridge. The chickadee's language (degree 3) encodes information universally. The robin's familiarity (degree 25) is universally recognizable. The crossbill's flight call declares tribal identity and other types hear it as "not mine." Sound as fence, not bridge. The Red Crossbill's vocal repertoire has a dual structure: the flight call (kip-kip-kip, 3-5 kHz, 50-100 ms per note, type-diagnostic) and the warble song (2-7 kHz, 2-10 seconds, individually variable, territorial/courtship).

**THE FRINGILLIDAE PAIR CLOSES -- MAXIMUM FUNCTIONAL CONTRAST ACROSS GENERA.** The engine's second consecutive same-family pair (after Bombycillidae 31-32). Different genera (*Haemorhous* vs. *Loxia*). Standard bill vs. crossed mandibles. Mimicry cadenza vs. type-diagnostic flight call. Fixed summer territory vs. nomadic irruptive breeding. Mountain conifer (place-based) vs. cone crop (resource-based). The Cassin's Finch mimics the crossbill; the crossbill does not mimic back. The pair inverts: the Cassin's Finch is the individual (one bird, many voices, quoting everyone), the Red Crossbill is the tribe (one voice per type, shared by all members, declaring allegiance). The waxwing pair (31-32) was a variation within a genus. The Fringillidae pair is a contrast between genera -- more structurally informative.

**THE QUOTATION CIRCLE -- DEGREE 33 QUOTED DEGREE 34 BEFORE DEGREE 34 ARRIVED.** The Cassin's Finch at degree 33 mimicked the Red Crossbill's kip-kip-kip flight call. The Red Crossbill arrives at degree 34. The species that was quoted shows up in person. A quotation circle: the mimic came first, then the source. Degree 33 quoted degree 34 before degree 34 existed. The relationship is asymmetric: the Cassin's Finch borrows from the crossbill, but the crossbill does not borrow from the Cassin's Finch. Like a jazz musician quoting a folk song -- the folk song does not quote back. Both Part A and Part B live inside this asymmetry: The Tempos are quoted (misdirected-to) by a Boeing engineer's big band; the Red Crossbill is quoted by the Cassin's Finch. One direction of reference in each case. Both groups are sources, not borrowers.

**CONE CROP -- FIRST RESOURCE-DEFINED ECOSYSTEM.** Previous ecosystems have been place-defined: old-growth, riparian, mountain conifer, open sky, open farmland. "Cone crop" is a condition: the state of having mature cones with accessible seeds, wherever that occurs. The crossbill's home is the food, not the forest. The bird goes where cones are, from coastal Sitka spruce to montane lodgepole to suburban Douglas-fir. This adds a new category to the engine's ecosystem vocabulary: resource-defined rather than vegetation-defined. Masting behavior -- synchronized heavy cone production in some years, near-failure in others -- drives the crossbill's nomadism. Good cone years produce irruptions; bad years produce local absence.

**THE FOUR-DEGREE ARC (31-34) -- THE ENGINE'S DENSEST STRUCTURAL SEQUENCE.** Degrees 31-34 form the engine's most coherent multi-degree sequence: Cedar Waxwing (whisper -- passive collective texture), Bohemian Waxwing (buzz -- amplified collective identity), Cassin's Finch (cadenza -- individual virtuosity with borrowing), Red Crossbill (dialect -- group marker enforcing boundaries). Two families (Bombycillidae, Fringillidae), two pairs, four acoustic elements, four fundamentally different relationships between sound and social organization. The arc was not planned -- it emerged from the species order -- but it coheres as if designed.

The Cassin's Finch (degree 33) takes seeds from cones already released. The crossbill goes in while the cone is still locked shut. Individual crossbills are either "left-billed" or "right-billed" in approximately 1:1 ratio.

- **THE TYPE SYSTEM -- 10+ CALL TYPES, POTENTIALLY CRYPTIC SPECIES COMPLEX, SPECIATION IN PROGRESS.** What appears to be a single species is actually a complex of at least ten distinct call types, each associated with different conifer species, different bill morphologies, and different flight calls. Jeff Groth in the late 1980s-1990s demonstrated Red Crossbills sort into groups based on flight calls -- each individual gives only calls of a single type, consistent through life. Craig Benkman built on this with decades of research showing an ongoing adaptive radiation -- each type has evolved a bill optimized for a particular conifer. Types most relevant to the PNW: Type 3 (western hemlock / Douglas-fir, small bill, core PNW range), Type 10 (Sitka spruce, coast), Type 2 (ponderosa pine, eastern Cascades), Type 5 (lodgepole pine). Type 9 was elevated to full species in 2017 as the Cassia Crossbill (*Loxia sinesciuris*), confined to ~70 km^2 of lodgepole pine in southern Idaho where absence of Red Squirrels allowed specialized evolution.

- **THE COEVOLUTIONARY ARMS RACE -- CROSSBILLS VS. CONIFERS FOR ONE MILLION YEARS.** Conifers evolve thicker scales, harder cones, deeper seed wells. Crossbills evolve larger bills, stronger jaw muscles, more precisely crossed tips. Benkman's research quantified: bill depth correlates with cone hardness, husking groove width correlates with seed size, foraging efficiency is type-specific. The bill is the key, the cone is the lock. Each type has a different key. The flight call is the label on the key.

- **THE IRRUPTIVE LIFESTYLE -- NOMADIC, BREEDS ANY MONTH WHEN CONE CROPS PERMIT.** Not migratory (no fixed breeding/wintering grounds), not strictly resident (does not remain if cones fail), not seasonal (breeds in virtually any month -- January through September documented). The timing of reproduction is driven by food availability, not photoperiod. The Pine Siskin (degree 12) was also irruptive, but the siskin's irruptions are primarily winter dispersal. The crossbill's irruptiveness extends to breeding itself. In the PNW: Type 3 most common in lowland/mid-elevation forests, Type 10 along the coast.

- **CONE CROP -- FIRST RESOURCE-DEFINED ECOSYSTEM, A FOOD TYPE NOT A HABITAT TYPE.** Previous ecosystems have been place-defined: old-growth, riparian, mountain conifer, open sky. "Cone crop" is a condition: the state of having mature cones with accessible seeds, wherever that occurs. The crossbill's home is the food, not the forest. The bird goes where cones are, from coastal Sitka spruce to montane lodgepole to suburban Douglas-fir. Resource-defined rather than vegetation-defined.

- **THE DUAL VOICE -- FLIGHT CALL + SONG, TWO EVOLUTIONARY FUNCTIONS.** The Red Crossbill's vocal repertoire has a dual structure. (1) The flight call (kip-kip-kip / jip-jip-jip): short, sharp, percussive, 3-5 kHz, ~50-100 ms per note, given constantly in flight and foraging -- the type-diagnostic signal, the tribal identifier that maintains reproductive isolation. (2) The warble song: loose, sweet trill, 2-7 kHz, 2-10 seconds, less studied, individually variable, territorial/courtship. The flight call is identity (which type, which tribe). The song is advertisement (I am here, I am male). The Red Crossbill does not mimic other species -- the Cassin's Finch mimics the crossbill, but the crossbill does not mimic back. The quotation runs one direction.

- **THE DIALECT -- THIRTY-FIFTH ACOUSTIC ELEMENT (SPECIES-SIDE), SOUND AS SPECIES BOUNDARY.** A population-level vocal marker that identifies the speaker's group, maintains social cohesion within the group, and enforces reproductive boundaries between groups. The progression: whisper (31) to buzz (32) to cadenza (33) to dialect (34). The dialect is the first vocalization in the engine that SEPARATES populations. The chickadee's language (degree 3) encodes information universally. The robin's familiarity (degree 25) is universally recognizable. The crossbill's flight call declares tribal identity and other types hear it as "not mine." Sound as fence, not bridge.

- **THE FRINGILLIDAE PAIR CLOSES -- MAXIMUM FUNCTIONAL CONTRAST ACROSS GENERA.** Second consecutive same-family pair (after Bombycillidae 31-32). Different genera (*Haemorhous* vs. *Loxia*). Standard bill vs. crossed mandibles. Mimicry cadenza vs. type-diagnostic flight call. Fixed summer territory vs. nomadic irruptive breeding. Mountain conifer (place-based) vs. cone crop (resource-based). The Cassin's Finch mimics the crossbill; the crossbill does not mimic back. The pair inverts: Cassin's Finch is the individual (one bird, many voices), Red Crossbill is the tribe (one voice per type).

- **THE QUOTATION CIRCLE -- DEGREE 33 QUOTED DEGREE 34 BEFORE DEGREE 34 ARRIVED.** The Cassin's Finch at degree 33 mimicked the Red Crossbill's kip-kip-kip flight call. The Red Crossbill arrives at degree 34. The species that was quoted shows up in person. A quotation circle: the mimic came first, then the source. Degree 33 quoted degree 34 before degree 34 existed.

- **THE FOUR-DEGREE ARC (31-34) -- WHISPER, BUZZ, CADENZA, DIALECT.** Degrees 31-34 form the engine's densest structural sequence: Cedar Waxwing (whisper -- passive collective texture), Bohemian Waxwing (buzz -- amplified collective identity), Cassin's Finch (cadenza -- individual virtuosity with borrowing), Red Crossbill (dialect -- group marker enforcing boundaries). Two families (Bombycillidae, Fringillidae), two pairs, four acoustic elements, four fundamentally different relationships between sound and social organization. Emerged from species order, coheres as if designed.

- **CONSERVATION -- LEAST CONCERN AT SPECIES LEVEL, BUT TYPE-SPECIFIC VULNERABILITY MAY BE MASKED.** IUCN Least Concern. Global population 90-180 million mature individuals. North American population ~26 million (Partners in Flight). Continental Concern Score 8/20. The conservation paradox: the species as a whole is abundant, but individual types could be vulnerable without being recognized as separate conservation units. The Cassia Crossbill (~5,000-6,000 individuals in 70 km^2) illustrates the stakes. Type 8 (Newfoundland) listed as Endangered in Canada. Old-growth logging reduces cone crops. Climate change disrupts cone timing. The "Least Concern" label may mask genuine threats to component populations.

## Parallel: The Tempos x Red Crossbill

The Part A / Part B resonance at degree 34 operates on three axes:

**1. Hidden collective identity and declared collective identity.** The Tempos are a group whose identity is hidden -- the name returns the wrong band, the membership is unknown, the music is unrecorded. The R&B/Soul Tempos existed as a collective but their collective identity is buried under the shadow of a big band namesake. The Red Crossbill's identity is declared -- the flight call broadcasts tribal identity to every bird within range, Type 3 or Type 10, heard and recognized immediately. Both are about collective identity: the band and the flock, the ensemble and the type. One is hidden by a name collision. The other is declared by a diagnostic call. The Shadow Name conceals what the dialect reveals.

**2. Misdirection and tribal marking.** The name collision is acoustic misdirection -- the search finds the wrong signal, the wrong band, the wrong genre. The crossbill's flight call is the opposite: the most precise acoustic signal in the engine, a call that sorts birds into their correct types with no ambiguity. In human music, the name "Tempos" sends the researcher to the wrong ensemble. In crossbill biology, the call kip-kip-kip sends the listener to the right flock. The human archive mislabels. The bird call identifies correctly.

**3. The unnamed ensemble and the typed flock.** The Tempos are a group whose internal structure is unknown -- how many members, who played what, who sang lead. The Red Crossbill flock is a group whose internal structure is precisely sorted -- every bird calls one type, mates within that type, forages on that type's preferred conifer. The Tempos are undifferentiated (we cannot distinguish individual members). The crossbill flock is maximally differentiated (we can distinguish types by flight call, bill measurement, and preferred conifer). Both are about groups. One group's structure has been lost to time. The other group's structure is the subject of one of the most detailed taxonomic investigations in modern ornithology.

The deeper resonance: both are about how groups identify themselves. The Tempos identified themselves through their collective sound -- the ensemble blend, the tight R&B arrangements, the group energy that earned them E=5. That identification has been lost. The crossbills identify themselves through their collective call -- the dialect, the kip-kip-kip that declares "Type 3" or "Type 10." That identification is the key to an ongoing adaptive radiation. One group's identity was erased by the archive. The other group's identity is being actively studied as a model for speciation. The engine holds both: the collective that lost its voice and the collective whose voice is building new species.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **The name collision is the engine's most important documentation finding since The Unrecorded.** At degree 33, the engine discovered that the absence of documentation is data. At degree 34, the engine discovers that the presence of wrong documentation is also data -- and more treacherous, because misdirection masquerades as success. The three documentation states (correct, absent, misdirected) form a complete taxonomy of research outcomes. The engine's methodology has expanded from cataloging musicians to cataloging the documentation infrastructure and its failure modes.

- **The dialect is the right name for the thirty-fifth acoustic element.** After the cadenza (individual borrowing everyone's voice), the dialect (group marker declaring tribal identity) is the natural complement. The two form a conceptual pair: the mimic and the identifier, the absorber and the declarer. The dialect captures the crossbill's essential innovation -- the flight call as species-boundary marker -- with a single word carrying the right connotations: regional identity, group membership, the way you speak as an indicator of where you belong.

- **The Fringillidae pair is more structurally informative than the Bombycillidae pair.** The waxwing pair (31-32) was a variation within a genus. The Fringillidae pair (33-34) contrasts across genera, bills, vocal strategies, seasonal patterns, and ecosystems. It demonstrates the internal diversity of the world's largest songbird family through three genera, three bill morphologies, three conifer-exploitation strategies.

- **The type system is the engine's most important evolutionary story.** No previous species has come with a built-in demonstration of speciation in progress. The crossbill type system -- 10+ call types, diagnostic flight calls, matched bill morphologies, preferred conifers, assortative mating, one type already split as species -- enriches the engine with genuine evolutionary process, not just species description.

- **The four-degree arc (31-34) is the engine's best structural sequence.** Whisper to buzz to cadenza to dialect. Four consecutive degrees, three families, four acoustic elements, each introducing a new relationship between sound and social organization. Emerged from the species order rather than being planned, but coheres as if designed.

- **The quotation circle is satisfying.** The Cassin's Finch at degree 33 mimicked the Red Crossbill's flight call. The Red Crossbill arrives at degree 34. The quoted species shows up in person. The circle closes.

- **E=5 three-mode taxonomy is analytically rich.** Personal tidal (32), scene-cumulative tidal (33), collective tidal (34). Three degrees, three interpretations of maximum energy, all Central District. The category has structure rather than being a simple label.

- **The Part A / Part B parallel works through structural opposition.** Hidden collective identity vs. declared collective identity. Misdirection vs. tribal marking. The unnamed ensemble vs. the typed flock. The parallels are precise and illuminating.

### What Could Be Better

- **BEYOND CRITICAL: No marine species in 34 degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound."** Thirty-four degrees. The water still has not spoken. Every retrospective since degree 25 has flagged this. The project is named for the largest body of salt water in the Pacific Northwest, and not one marine bird -- not a gull, loon, grebe, cormorant, alcid, or shorebird -- has appeared. A marine species at degree 35 is non-negotiable.

- **EXTREME: Twelve consecutive Passeriformes (degrees 23-34).** The longest order-level streak in the engine. A marine species at degree 35 would almost certainly be non-Passeriformes, solving both gaps simultaneously.

- **URGENT: No corvid in 34 degrees.** No Steller's Jay, American Crow, or Common Raven. The most intelligent, acoustically complex PNW birds remain absent.

- **URGENT: No raptor in 34 degrees.** No Bald Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, or Osprey. Top predators absent.

- **OVERDUE: No nocturnal species since degree 4.** Thirty degrees since the Spotted Owl. Great Horned Owl, Barred Owl, or Common Nighthawk needed.

- **E=3 absent for 14 degrees.** The lower-energy range has not been used since the Orange-crowned Warbler at degree 20. E=4 dominance (13 species-side degrees, 37.1%) risks dynamic monotony.

- **Second consecutive documentation-challenged artist degree (33-34).** While the name collision finding is valuable, two consecutive degrees of undocumented/misdirected artists may create a pattern that limits the engine's ability to produce rich biographical material. A well-documented artist at degree 35 would restore balance.

- **Physical archives remain unsearched.** Peter Blecha's *Stomp and Shout* (2023), Seattle Public Library's Northwest Collection, MoPop archives, UW Special Collections, and personal collections have not been consulted. The R&B/Soul Tempos may have left traces in these physical resources that no online search can reach.

## Lessons Learned

- **Name collisions are a structural feature of music history, not an accident.** In the 1960s, there was no internet, no centralized band-name registry, no way for a Central District R&B group to know that a Boeing engineer's swing band across town had the same name. The collision survived into the archival era because one band's documentation outlasted the other's. The engine must now verify that found data matches the search target -- not just that data was found.

- **Three documentation states form a complete taxonomy.** Correct documentation (the found data matches the target), absent documentation (no data found), and misdirected documentation (found data matches a different entity). The engine encountered all three within eight degrees (27, 33, 34). Every future search should classify its results into one of these three states.

- **The dialect is the engine's first acoustic element that separates rather than connects.** Every previous element -- language, familiarity, the hook, the congregation -- functioned to communicate, attract, or display. The dialect functions to sort: same type joins, different type passes. This is a new acoustic category. The engine's vocabulary now includes signals that build walls as well as signals that build bridges.

- **Cone crop as resource-defined ecosystem is genuinely new.** Previous ecosystems were places (old-growth, riparian, mountain conifer). Cone crop is a condition (having mature cones, wherever that occurs). The crossbill's home is the food, not the forest. This adds a necessary dimension for nomadic species.

- **The type system demonstrates speciation observable in real time.** The Red Crossbill is not one species or ten -- it is a species complex caught in the act of diverging. The call types are not just identification tags but markers of an ongoing adaptive radiation driven by bill-cone coevolution. This is the richest evolutionary narrative in the engine so far.

- **The four-degree arc should be recognized and named.** Degrees 31-34 (whisper-buzz-cadenza-dialect) form the engine's most coherent multi-degree sequence. Each introduces a fundamentally new relationship between sound and social organization. The arc was not planned but emerged from the species order. Future sequences of this quality should be identified and documented.

- **E=5 has three modes and may have more.** Personal tidal, scene-cumulative tidal, collective tidal. The category is richer than a single definition. Future E=5 degrees should specify which mode applies. A fourth mode may emerge.

- **Physical archives remain the primary gap in research methodology.** For the second consecutive degree, the research identifies Seattle Public Library's Northwest Collection, MoPop archives, UW Special Collections, and personal collections as unsearched. These physical resources may contain the documentation that online searches cannot find. Peter Blecha's *Stomp and Shout* (2023) is the highest-priority supplementary source for the Central District R&B scene.
