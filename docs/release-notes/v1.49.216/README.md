### TAXONOMY: *Marmota caligata*

- **Marmota** -- from French *marmotte*, from Romansch *murmont* (mountain mouse), ultimately from Latin *murem montis* (mouse of the mountain). Fourteen species worldwide across mountains of North America, Europe, and Asia.
- **caligata** -- Latin "wearing boots" or "booted," referring to the dark brown to black fur on lower legs and feet contrasting with the silvery-gray body. Named by Johann Friedrich Eschscholtz in 1829 from Alaska specimens. *Marmota caligata*: "the booted mountain mouse."
- **Common names:** Hoary Marmot ("hoary" = white/gray-haired), whistler (the name that gave Whistler Mountain its name), rock chuck, gray marmot.

### ECOLOGY: THE ALPINE MEADOW ARCHITECT

**Habitat:** Alpine and subalpine meadows with adjacent talus or boulder fields. 1,500-3,000+ meters. Requires BOTH open meadow (foraging) AND rocky talus (denning, predator escape). **Hibernation -- The Longest in the Engine:** 7-8 months (October-April), approximately 200-240 days. Body temperature 37 C to 3-5 C. Heart rate 100+ bpm to 3-5 bpm. Metabolic rate drops to 1-2%. Same alpine habitat, opposite winter strategies.

**Social:** Colonial, 5-15 individuals. Sentinel system. Different whistle patterns for different predator types. Notably playful -- juveniles wrestle, chase, tumble; adults sun-bathe on boulders.

### WHISTLER MOUNTAIN: THE MARMOT THAT NAMED A PLACE

Whistler, British Columbia -- one of the world's most famous ski resorts and host of the 2010 Winter Olympics -- is named for the Hoary Marmot. No other species in the engine has named a major geographic feature through its vocalization. The sound became a place name. The marmot's voice is permanently embedded in the geography of the Pacific Northwest. The Whistler connection extends the engine's geography beyond the US border -- Whistler is 120 km north of Vancouver.
