**Wellwater Conspiracy as Creative Surplus Side Project.** Wellwater Conspiracy is the band Matt Cameron made when he had ideas that did not fit either of his primary bands. The formation was unhurried — Cameron, McMillan, and McBain began working together in the mid-1990s with no commercial pressure, no label deadline, and no audience expectation — and the result was a psychedelic space rock project that occupies a parallel-universe position from the commercial rock world Cameron was otherwise navigating. The music is characterized by heavy, slow, distortion-saturated arrangements; drone pedals held for extended passages; instrumental sections that extend beyond conventional song form; and lyrical content that reaches toward mythological, cosmic, and ritualistic registers rather than toward the confessional or political registers typical of 1990s commercial rock. The catalog — *Declaration of Conformity* (1997), *Brotherhood of Electric* (1999), and *The Scene at the End of the Movie* (2001) — was released on successively more established independent labels (**Third Gear**, **Time Bomb Recordings**, **TVT Records**) but never on a major, and the band's genre misfit status was part of its defining structural position. At E=6, Wellwater Conspiracy sits in the committed middle register — present and weighted, not explosive, not quiet, but occupying the space with conviction, a calibration consistent with the band's willingness to take its time and sustain drone textures rather than drive toward anthem payoff. **The same** E=6 calibration at degree 224 (Mad Season) and degree 225 (Temple of the Dog) sets up an amplitude floor for the supergroup cluster, and Wellwater Conspiracy's E=6 holds that floor at the cluster's closing position.

**Cooper's Hawk and the Accipiter Trio Middle Position.** The **Cooper's Hawk** (*Accipiter cooperii*) is the middle entry of the three-consecutive accipiter sub-arc that began at degree 225 with the **Northern Goshawk** (*Accipiter gentilis*, E=6) and will continue at degree 227 with the **Sharp-shinned Hawk** (*Accipiter striatus*). The genus *Accipiter* contains three North American representatives, all three of which the engine now documents in a single three-degree window — one of the most taxonomically concentrated sub-arc documentations in the Seattle 360 catalog. The three species are differentiated primarily by body size: Northern Goshawk at 720-1,200 g (female), Cooper's Hawk at 220-680 g (female, 40% smaller), and Sharp-shinned Hawk at 150-220 g (female, another 40% smaller). The body-size spectrum is encoded in the vocalizations: all three share the accipiter genus-level call template — a rapid series of notes with identical rhythmic pattern — but the frequency shifts inversely with body size, giving the Northern Goshawk the lowest frequency, the Sharp-shinned Hawk the highest, and the Cooper's Hawk the middle. **In parallel** with the GRUNGE SUPERGROUP CLUSTER's musician-scale middle position at degree 226 (Wellwater Conspiracy between Temple of the Dog and the cluster's subsequent absence), the Cooper's Hawk's accipiter-scale middle position at the same degree establishes the middle-position structural motif as the degree 226 organizing principle on both axes.

**The Cak-Cak-Cak Suburban Accipiter Call.** The Cooper's Hawk's primary vocalization is the rapid **cak-cak-cak** alarm/territorial call — a series of 5-15 sharp notes at approximately 1,500-3,500 Hz, often delivered from a perch near the nest or in flight above territory. The rhythmic structure (4-6 notes per second) is identical to the Northern Goshawk's *ka-ka-ka-ka* and to the Sharp-shinned Hawk's even-higher-pitched *kik-kik-kik-kik*, reflecting the genus-level vocal template. **The same** template with frequency encoding allows experienced birders to identify Cooper's Hawk calls without visual confirmation, and the frequency shift is one of the more reliable vocal-identification markers in the accipiter genus. The call is used primarily during breeding season (March through July in Seattle) and is frequently heard from residential neighborhoods near nest sites. The call's acoustic register is adapted to carry through dense vegetation rather than across open water — where the Osprey's chewk-chewk-chewk at degree 224 needs to carry across lakes and coastal waters, the Cooper's Hawk's cak-cak-cak needs to penetrate the urban-suburban canopy of Douglas firs, maples, and ornamental plantings that defines the species' habitat in the Pacific Northwest.

**The Suburban-Accipiter Urban-Adaptor Story.** The Cooper's Hawk's success in cities and suburbs is one of the more dramatic ecological stories of the late 20th century Pacific Northwest. Populations that were suppressed or absent from urban areas in the 1970s (the late DDT era) have expanded dramatically in the 1990s and 2000s as the hawk learned to exploit the concentrated prey available at bird feeders and in ornamental plantings. ## Part B: Cooper's Hawk cak-cak-cak — The Accipiter Trio Middle Position, the Suburban Accipiter, and the Urban-Adaptor Ecology

- **Species Baseline and Accipiter Genus Context:** **Cooper's Hawk** (*Accipiter cooperii*) is a member of **Order Accipitriformes / Family Accipitridae / Genus Accipiter / species cooperii**, and the genus *Accipiter* contains three North American representatives — the Northern Goshawk (*Accipiter gentilis*, largest at 720-1,200 g), the Cooper's Hawk (220-680 g, 40% smaller), and the Sharp-shinned Hawk (*Accipiter striatus*, smallest at 150-220 g, another 40% smaller). - **Pacific Northwest Distribution and Year-Round Urban Residency:** Cooper's Hawk populations in the Pacific Northwest are year-round residents, with substantial breeding populations in Seattle and surrounding urban-suburban areas. Breeding occurs across residential neighborhoods from **Ballard** to **Rainier Valley** and within urban parks including **Discovery Park**, **Seward Park**, **Lincoln Park**, and **Schmitz Preserve Park**. Nest sites are typically in Douglas firs, bigleaf maples, or tall ornamental plantings 10-20 meters above ground, and the nests are constructed from sticks with a cup of bark and green sprigs. Clutch size is 2-5 eggs, incubation is 30-36 days, and fledging occurs 27-34 days after hatching. The arrival of Cooper's Hawk calls from a residential neighborhood in March or April signals the beginning of the breeding cycle, and the species is the quintessential urban raptor of Pacific Northwest residential environments.
- **The Accipiter Trio Middle Position and the 40% Body-Mass Step-Down:** The Cooper's Hawk's position as the middle accipiter is defined by its 40% body-mass step-down from the Northern Goshawk (225) and its 40% step-up from the Sharp-shinned Hawk (227). The rhythmic structure (4-6 notes per second) is identical to the Northern Goshawk's *ka-ka-ka-ka* and to the Sharp-shinned Hawk's even-higher-pitched *kik-kik-kik-kik*, reflecting the genus-level vocal template. **The same** template with frequency encoding allows experienced birders to identify Cooper's Hawk calls without visual confirmation, and the frequency shift is one of the more reliable vocal-identification markers in the accipiter genus. The call is used primarily during breeding season (March through July in Seattle) and is frequently heard from residential neighborhoods near nest sites. The call's acoustic register is adapted to carry through dense vegetation rather than across open water — where the Osprey's chewk-chewk-chewk at degree 224 needs to carry across lakes and coastal waters, the Cooper's Hawk's cak-cak-cak needs to penetrate the urban-suburban canopy.

Populations that were suppressed or absent from urban areas in the 1970s have expanded dramatically in the 1990s and 2000s as the hawk learned to exploit concentrated prey available at bird feeders and in ornamental plantings. The species' hunting strategy — rapid ambush pursuit through dense vegetation — is well-suited to the urban-suburban canopy, and the species is fast enough to pursue prey through dense shrubbery and agile enough to navigate between buildings at full hunting speed. The **European Starling**, **House Sparrow**, **Rock Pigeon**, and **American Robin** populations that concentrate at bird feeders and in ornamental plantings are the primary prey base sustaining Seattle's urban Cooper's Hawk population, and the urban-adaptor ecology represents a distinct specialization from the forest-interior pattern of the Northern Goshawk and the migratory-through-urban pattern of the Sharp-shinned Hawk.
- **Strong Sexual Dimorphism and Female-Larger Pattern:** Cooper's Hawks exhibit strong reversed sexual size dimorphism with females approximately 40% larger than males — a pattern common to most accipiter and falcon species but more pronounced in Cooper's Hawks than in many congeners. The male Cooper's Hawk (220-440 g) is roughly the size of a female Sharp-shinned Hawk, and field identification between small male Cooper's Hawks and large female Sharp-shinned Hawks is one of the most challenging accipiter-ID problems in North America. The female-larger pattern has evolutionary explanations in prey-niche partitioning (males and females within a breeding pair hunt different prey sizes), intraspecific competition (larger females can defend nests from predators), and incubation requirements (larger females provide better thermal mass for eggs). The pattern is consistent across the accipiter genus and is a key field identification marker for raptor observers.

The **pursuit** phase can reach speeds exceeding 60 km/h through dense shrubbery, and the hawk is capable of navigating between branches, around buildings, and through narrow gaps at full speed. The **strike** uses the accipiter's long legs and needle-sharp talons to capture prey in mid-flight or as it attempts to flush. Studies document a 10-20% catch success rate — lower than the Osprey's 25-70% but consistent with the accipiter genus's general pursuit-hunting catch rates — and the lower success rate is offset by the high prey-encounter frequency in urban environments.

After DDT's 1972 ban, populations began recovering across the Eastern US and by the 1980s had expanded into urban and suburban areas that had previously been population sinks. The urban-expansion timeline in Seattle specifically documents the 1990s and 2000s as the decades during which Cooper's Hawks became common residential-neighborhood residents — a shift documented by both **Seattle Audubon Society** long-term monitoring and **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife** raptor-population tracking. The current IUCN Red List status is **Least Concern**, with North American population estimated at 700,000-1,000,000 individuals and Pacific Northwest populations stable-to-increasing.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Three-degree clusters can synthesize three distinct motivations into a completed structural triad.** The GRUNGE SUPERGROUP CLUSTER (224-226) synthesizes three distinct collaborative-music-formation motivations (recovery, grief, creative surplus) into a completed three-entry triad whose structural weight exceeds any single-motivation cluster could achieve. Future multi-degree cluster documentation should be evaluated for triad-completion potential rather than only for cluster-member merit, because triad clusters carry distinct synthesis weight that isolated-entry clusters cannot.
2. **Drummer-as-side-project-originator is a distinct register from guitarist-as-side-project-originator.** Wellwater Conspiracy's Matt-Cameron-as-drummer origination differs structurally from Brad's Stone-Gossard-as-guitarist origination (degree 219) in ways that matter for genre orientation, arrangement priorities, and creative-surplus expression. Future side-project documentation should record the originating primary-member's instrument explicitly, because drummer-originated side projects tend toward different genre orientations (space rock, instrumental rock, jazz-adjacent rock) than guitarist-originated side projects (mellow groove rock, melodic rock, roots rock).
3. **Middle positions within three-degree windows carry distinct interpretive weight from opening or closing positions.** The Cooper's Hawk's middle position within the accipiter trio (225-227) and Wellwater Conspiracy's closing position within the GRUNGE SUPERGROUP CLUSTER (224-226) demonstrate that structured three-entry windows generate different documentation requirements for each of their three positions. Future three-entry-window documentation should treat middle positions as distinct from opening-register-establishment positions (first entries) and closing-synthesis positions (terminal entries), because middle positions require both continuation-from-opening and transition-toward-closing structural work that neither terminal position requires.
4. **Genus-level vocal templates can encode species-specific information through frequency modulation.** The accipiter trio's shared rhythmic pattern with frequency-encoded body-size information demonstrates that vocal similarity within a genus does not negate species-specific information — the information is encoded in the frequency dimension while the rhythm dimension preserves the template. Future multi-species-from-same-genus documentation should analyze rhythmic-template-shared versus frequency-encoded-difference patterns explicitly, because the pattern provides a robust species-discrimination mechanism that pure rhythm-based documentation misses.
5. **Urban-adaptor ecological registers fill documentation gaps that forest-interior and open-country registers cannot cover.** The Cooper's Hawk's urban-adaptor pattern is structurally distinct from forest-interior and open-country specialization modes, and its first-instance documentation at degree 226 fills a gap in the engine's raptor ecological vocabulary. Future raptor and generalist-species documentation should evaluate urban-adaptor potential as a distinct ecological dimension from habitat-specialist dimensions, because urban-adaptor patterns reflect late-20th-century landscape-change dynamics that traditional habitat-specialization frameworks do not capture.
6. **Cross-scene collaboration via external-scene members is a structurally distinct property from internal-scene supergroup collaboration.** Wellwater Conspiracy's inclusion of John McBain (Monster Magnet, New Jersey space-rock scene) differs structurally from Mad Season's all-Seattle lineup and Temple of the Dog's all-Seattle lineup, and the difference matters for genre orientation, cultural-touchstone connections, and collaborative-network structure. Future supergroup and side-project documentation should record cross-scene-vs-internal-scene collaboration explicitly, because cross-scene projects tend toward genre-boundary-crossing orientations that internal-scene projects rarely achieve.
7. **Primary-member side projects can run continuously through multiple primary-band contexts.** Matt Cameron's Wellwater Conspiracy ran continuously through his Soundgarden tenure (1986-1997), the Soundgarden dissolution period, his Pearl Jam tenure (1998-present), and the Soundgarden reformation period (2010-2019). The continuity through multiple primary-band contexts is structurally distinct from side projects that operate within a single primary-band window, and the long-running continuity supports genre-orientation stability across decades that shorter-window side projects cannot sustain. Future side-project documentation should record primary-band context continuity explicitly, because long-running side projects carry different interpretive weight from short-window projects.
8. **Partially-posthumous entries maintain elegiac register across multi-degree clusters.** Ben McMillan's 2008 death makes Wellwater Conspiracy a partially-posthumous entry at degree 226, maintaining the GRUNGE SUPERGROUP CLUSTER's elegiac register that was established through Staley's and Saunders's posthumous presence at degree 224 and Wood's and Cobain's peripheral-touchstone posthumous presence across 224 and 225. Future multi-degree cluster documentation should track cumulative posthumous-member counts as a first-class structural property, because clusters with multiple posthumous members across their constituent entries carry distinct elegiac weight that single-posthumous-member clusters cannot match.
9. **Space-rock-as-parallel-universe establishes a distinct genre slot from psychedelic rock, post-grunge, and rock-supergroup.** Wellwater Conspiracy's space rock orientation is clearly distinct from Love Battery's psychedelic rock at 221, Candlebox's post-grunge at 223, Mad Season's rock-supergroup at 224, and Temple of the Dog's rock-supergroup-tribute at 225 — each occupies a different genre slot within the broader rock-adjacent landscape. Future genre-positioning documentation should analyze the full available genre-slot taxonomy rather than defaulting to the closest matching primary genre, because genre-slot distinctions carry real structural information that default-to-primary-genre documentation loses.
10. **Ninth-cross-axis-register accumulation sustains Q3 as the engine's most cross-axis-dense quadrant.** The surplus-capacity-colonizing-adjacent-niches cross-axis at degree 226 is the ninth documented Q3 cross-axis register after sustained-commitment-at-amplitude (189), perception-versus-physics (194), sustained-extreme-output (199), convergence-as-closure (204), formation-stage-as-distinct-register (209), sustained-complexity-as-intensity (214), tenacious-digging-as-depth-strategy (219), threshold-zone-habitation (224), and the current surplus-capacity register. The accumulation of nine cross-axis registers in forty-six degrees of Q3 progression sustains Q3 as the engine's most cross-axis-dense quadrant to date, and future quadrant-level cross-axis tracking should reference the Q3 accumulation density as the benchmark for subsequent quadrant cross-axis tracking.
