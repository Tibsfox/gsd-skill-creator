## Mission Overview

Juno is the obs#7 sustain of the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA solar-powered orbiter of Jupiter whose defining achievement is being the first spacecraft to orbit Jupiter in a polar orbit, the first to map the planet's deep interior and global deep atmosphere from orbit, and the farthest solar-powered spacecraft yet flown. It is a New Frontiers-program mission managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL — like Galileo and the Cassini orbiter), with principal investigator Scott Bolton of the Southwest Research Institute. An orbiter built around a spinning solar-powered platform, it launched aboard an Atlas V 551 from Cape Canaveral (Space Launch Complex 41) on 5 August 2011. Because a direct high-energy Jupiter trajectory was beyond the launch energy available, the mission used an Earth gravity assist on 9 October 2013 (about 500 km altitude, adding roughly 7.3 km/s) to build up the speed to reach Jupiter over a roughly five-year cruise. Juno carried the six-frequency Microwave Radiometer (MWR), the Gravity Science experiment (X-/Ka-band Doppler tracking), a fluxgate magnetometer (MAG) with its scalar advanced magnetometer, the Jupiter Energetic-particle Detector Instrument (JEDI), the Jovian Auroral Distributions Experiment (JADE), the Waves radio-and-plasma-wave instrument, the Jovian Infrared Auroral Mapper (JIRAM), the Ultraviolet Spectrograph (UVS), and the public-engagement visible-light camera JunoCam. The story is the long road to Jupiter and the work in the Jovian system: out from Earth on the gravity-assist road, then years in a long looping polar orbit. On 4–5 July 2016 Juno fired its main engine for about 35 minutes to capture into a highly elliptical near-polar orbit (inclination near 90 degrees), becoming the first spacecraft to orbit Jupiter pole-to-pole; at each perijove it skims close over the cloud tops (about 4,200 km above them) and reads the planet from the inside out. The scoping is load-bearing: the orbit is the first POLAR orbit of Jupiter (Galileo, obs#5, was the first spacecraft to orbit Jupiter at all, in 1995, in an equatorial / low-inclination orbit) — NOT the first Jupiter orbit; the deep-interior mapping is the first GLOBAL remote sounding plus interior mapping from orbit (its Microwave Radiometer sounds from about 1 bar to about 1,000 bars and its Gravity Science reveals the mass distribution, the dilute "fuzzy" core, and the depth of the winds), distinct from Galileo's single-point atmospheric ENTRY PROBE; and the solar power makes Juno the most distant solar-powered spacecraft yet flown and the first to operate at Jupiter, where the Pioneers, the Voyagers, Galileo, and Cassini all used nuclear RTGs. Across its mission Juno studied Jupiter's interior, gravity field, magnetic field, auroras, and polar cyclones, and in its extended mission made close flybys of Ganymede (2021), Europa (2022), and Io (2023–2024); the final extension was scheduled through 30 September 2025. - **One spacecraft flew a giant planet pole-to-pole and read it from the inside out.** On 4–5 July 2016 Juno fired its engine for about 35 minutes and dropped into a highly elliptical near-polar orbit around Jupiter — the first craft ever to orbit the planet pole-to-pole — and from each perijove pass low over the cloud tops it sounded the deep atmosphere and mapped the interior.
- **A microwave radiometer saw far beneath the visible clouds.** Juno's six-frequency Microwave Radiometer sounded Jupiter's atmosphere from about 1 bar down to about 1,000 bars, mapping water, ammonia, and temperature far below the cloud tops — the first global look beneath the visible weather of a giant planet from orbit.
- **Gravity tracking revealed a dilute core and winds thousands of kilometres deep.** Juno's Gravity Science experiment, tracking the spacecraft's motion with precise Doppler measurements, mapped Jupiter's interior mass distribution and revealed a dilute "fuzzy" core and belts, zones, and winds that run thousands of kilometres deep — a reading of the hidden interior from orbit.
- **A spacecraft ran on sunlight five times farther from the Sun than Earth.** Juno operates on three large solar arrays at Jupiter (~5 AU from the Sun), making it the most distant solar-powered spacecraft yet flown and the first solar-powered spacecraft at Jupiter, where every earlier outer-planet mission used nuclear RTGs; it took the solar-distance record from ESA's Rosetta in January 2016.
- **A sea duck dives deeper than almost any other duck, far beneath the cold water.** The Long-tailed Duck (*Clangula hyemalis*), a small sea duck that breeds across the high Arctic and winters on the cold coastal waters of the Pacific Northwest, is among the deepest-diving of all ducks, plunging far beneath the surface (documented well over 200 feet / 60 metres) to feed near the sea floor (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Juno peering deepest beneath Jupiter's clouds.
- **A golden mountain wildflower turns the thin high-elevation light to gold.** The Broadleaf Arnica (*Arnica latifolia*), a bright golden-yellow sunflower-family wildflower of cool, high Pacific Northwest mountain forests and meadows, opens its sun-gold flower heads in the short cool mountain summer to gather the thin high-elevation light (ecological description only) — a mirror of Juno's solar arrays drinking the faint distant sunlight far from the Sun.
