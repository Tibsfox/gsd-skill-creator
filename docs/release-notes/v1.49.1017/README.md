## Summary

v1.49.1017 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.207 to 1.208, adding the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) — the all-sky transit survey of the nearest bright stars — as the obs#2 INTRA-AXIS continuation that SUSTAINS the EXOPLANET-DETECTION substrate-axis (axis-rotation #29 sustained at obs#2). The exoplanet axis opened at v1016 Kepler with the transit-photometry survey of a single fixed deep field of about 150,000 faint distant stars, the first statistical census, the abundance of small worlds, and the first Earth-size worlds in habitable zones; TESS extends it, taking the transit method Kepler established for one deep field and remaking it as a survey of almost the whole sky's nearest, brightest stars. Where Kepler counted how common worlds are, TESS finds the worlds best suited to detailed follow-up.

TESS was built as a NASA Astrophysics Explorer mission, led by principal investigator George Ricker at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, with the spacecraft built by Orbital ATK (now Northrop Grumman). It reached a highly elliptical high-Earth orbit on 18 April 2018, carried aloft as a SpaceX Falcon 9 payload from Cape Canaveral, and it continues to operate. The observatory carries four identical wide-field cameras, each built from four charge-coupled devices, together imaging a tall strip of sky about 24 by 96 degrees.

The signature of the mission is the all-sky transit survey. TESS watches almost the whole sky sector by sector with its tall four-camera strip, watching one sky sector for about 27 days and then stepping to the next, thirteen sectors to a hemisphere, twenty-six over the two-year primary mission, covering about 85 percent of the sky.

The bright-nearby-star focus makes the worlds amenable to follow-up. TESS concentrates on the nearest and brightest stars, so the worlds it finds are close enough and their host stars bright enough to have their masses weighed from the ground and their atmospheres read by later telescopes.

The follow-up target catalog hands the community its yield. TESS produces a large catalog of TESS Objects of Interest — bright-star planet candidates, hundreds confirmed and growing — the source list for much of the detailed study of nearby worlds.

The lunar-resonant orbit makes the survey practical. TESS flies a highly elliptical high-Earth orbit in a two-to-one resonance with the Moon, kept stable for decades by the Moon's gravity, giving long, uninterrupted views of each sky sector with little fuel.

The nearby habitable-zone worlds joined the census with studied targets. Among its bright-star worlds, TESS found a world about Earth's size in the habitable zone of a nearby cool dwarf star, close enough for the kind of follow-up study a distant field does not permit.

The axis-relevant substrate of TESS at this degree is its continuation of the EXOPLANET-DETECTION substrate-axis at obs#2, the all-sky transit survey of the nearest bright stars. TESS sustains the EXOPLANET-DETECTION-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#2 NEW LOCKED and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#80 cumulative (axis-rotation #29 sustained at obs#2). The forward continuation may favor a later exoplanet mission that sustains the axis at obs#3 — a transit-spectroscopy mission of planetary atmospheres or a direct-imaging mission — or a substrate-axis rotation #30 to an under-served axis.

TESS extends the exoplanet way of seeing the sky on the catalog, and the mission records the TESS team at the MIT Kavli Institute, principal investigator George Ricker, NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, and the Orbital ATK (now Northrop Grumman) builders. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.207 to 1.208, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#91 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A wide survey of the nearest, brightest targets finds the most studiable worlds.** By surveying almost the whole sky's nearest bright stars rather than one deep field of faint distant ones, TESS found the worlds whose masses can be weighed and whose atmospheres can be read, showing that breadth and proximity together pick out the targets best suited to detailed study.
2. **A survey that hands its yield as a catalog multiplies its reach.** By producing a catalog of TESS Objects of Interest for the whole community, TESS turned its own discoveries into the source list for ground-based and space-based follow-up, showing that a survey's value grows when its yield is handed onward as a working target list.
3. **A bright host star is what makes detailed study possible.** By concentrating on bright nearby stars, TESS made its worlds amenable to the radial-velocity mass measurement and the transmission-spectroscopy atmosphere reading, showing that the brightness and proximity of a host star, not just the detection of a world, is what opens the world to detailed study.
4. **A resonant orbit can stay stable for decades on its own.** By flying a highly elliptical high-Earth orbit in a two-to-one resonance with the Moon, TESS gained long, uninterrupted views of each sky sector with little fuel, showing that a well-chosen resonance turns the Moon's gravity into a free, lasting stabilizer.
5. **An axis sustains at a substrate-form-distinct second observation.** By remaking the transit method as an all-sky survey of the nearest bright stars, TESS sustained the exoplanet-detection axis at obs#2 distinct from Kepler's single-deep-field census, showing that an INTRA-AXIS continuation extends an axis by giving its established method a new form.
6. **A small host star deepens a small world's transit.** Because TESS favors small, cool dwarf stars, a world of a given size makes a deeper, easier-to-measure dip against them, showing that the choice of host stars can make small worlds easier to find, not harder.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across an INTRA-AXIS continuation.** Because TESS reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.207 Kepler template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the all-sky survey while keeping the warm palette, making the axis-continuation a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The whole-sky survey watches each patch only briefly, yet reaches the best worlds.** TESS watches each sky sector for only about 27 days, far shorter than Kepler's years on one field, yet by concentrating on the nearest bright stars it finds the worlds best suited to detailed study, so a short, broad watch of the right targets outperforms a long, narrow watch for follow-up.
- **A low-quartering hawk mirrors the wide, systematic sky survey.** The Northern Harrier, the raptor that sweeps a whole field patch by patch and turns to the nearest, clearest signal, enters as the sweep-the-whole-field-and-focus-on-the-nearest mirror to TESS surveying almost the whole sky sector by sector and concentrating on the nearest, brightest stars.
- **A bright-barked tree mirrors the focus on the brightest stars.** Pacific Madrone, the broad-ranging tree whose bright bark and conspicuous berries stand out across a whole landscape, enters as the broad-range-and-stand-out-by-brightness mirror, so the catalog's all-sky bright-star survey is paired with a tree that ranges broadly and is picked out by its brightness.

## Dedication

v1.208 TESS is dedicated to the team that built and flew NASA's all-sky exoplanet transit survey: principal investigator George Ricker, who led the mission, and the TESS team at the MIT Kavli Institute who designed the four wide-field cameras and turned the all-sky survey into a catalog of the nearest worlds best suited to detailed study; the team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center who managed the mission and the lunar-resonant orbit; and the Orbital ATK (now Northrop Grumman) team that built the spacecraft. TESS points its tall camera strip across almost the whole sky sector by sector, concentrating on the nearest and brightest stars, and it hands the wider community a catalog of follow-up targets — including a world about Earth's size in the habitable zone of a nearby cool dwarf star — whose masses can be weighed from the ground and whose atmospheres can be read by later telescopes. TESS continues to operate.
