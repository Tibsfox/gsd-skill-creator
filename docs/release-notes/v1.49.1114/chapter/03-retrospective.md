## Surprises

- **Six instruments read the water cycle from one afternoon-orbit platform.** Aqua carries AIRS, AMSU-A, HSB, AMSR-E, CERES, and MODIS together — sounding the atmosphere's temperature and water vapor at high spectral resolution; sounding temperature by microwave; reading (historically) precipitation, sea ice, sea-surface temperature, and soil moisture; weighing the radiation budget; and imaging the daylit globe. The suite is the point: the components of the water cycle read together from the same sun-synchronous afternoon orbit.
- **AIRS sounds the atmosphere with about a hundred times the spectral resolution of prior infrared sounders.** AIRS measures across exactly 2378 infrared channels from 3.7 to 15.4 µm, returning atmospheric temperature and water-vapor profiles at a spectral resolution about a hundred times finer than earlier infrared sounders — a measurable benefit to numerical weather prediction.
- **A flagship still reading the water cycle more than two decades past its design life.** Aqua was built for a 6-year mission and launched in 2002; more than two decades later it still returns data, with AIRS, AMSU-A, CERES, and MODIS active — a landmark, long-running flagship. The orbit has been allowed to drift (Aqua left the A-Train formation in about January 2022), and HSB and AMSR-E ended long ago (February 2003 and October 2011), yet the daily record of the water cycle continues, projected toward an end of science around September 2026.
- **A PNW freshwater mussel that lives its century in the cold clean water Aqua watches from above.** The Western Pearlshell (*Margaritifera falcata*), a long-lived Pacific Northwest freshwater mussel of cold, clean, fast streams, filters stream water for decades to over a century, its larvae (glochidia) riding the gills of salmonid hosts, and is a recognized indicator of clean cold freshwater — the living freshwater the water-cycle flagship watches from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **An emergent wetland herb that stands where water meets land.** The Water Parsley (*Oenanthe sarmentosa*, Pacific Water-parsley), a PNW-native emergent perennial of marshes, wet ditches, pond margins, and slow streams, carries sprawling hollow stems and lacy compound leaves rooted in shallow water and saturated mud where land meets freshwater — distinct from the other water-dropworts — bound with the throughline of the living water.
