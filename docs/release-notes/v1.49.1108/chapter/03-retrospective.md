## Surprises

- **One satellite carried a laser built to fire straight down.** Rather than a scanning imager, CALIPSO carried a two-wavelength polarization lidar — CALIOP — that fired its 532/1064 nm pulses straight down and timed the backscatter returning from each layer of the atmosphere directly beneath it. The instrument did not sweep a swath; it drew a vertical curtain of the thin veil along the ground track, and from that curtain came the height and content of the aerosols and the thin clouds.
- **Two wavelengths and the orthogonal polarization to read the layers.** CALIOP worked at 532 nm and 1064 nm, with its polarization sensitivity — two orthogonal components — at 532 nm and total backscatter at 1064 nm. The depolarization of the returned light is what let the lidar tell the ice from the water in a thin cloud and read the type of an aerosol layer, a different physics and purpose from CloudSat's 94 GHz radar.
- **The lidar and the radar flew in close formation.** CALIPSO did not fly alone: it flew in tight formation with CloudSat in the A-Train, the two spacecraft reading the same column of sky almost at once. The lidar caught the thin cloud and aerosol layers; the radar caught the cloud ice and water content; together they built a three-dimensional view of the clouds and the aerosols among them.
- **A nocturnal moth on wings set with translucent windows.** The Polyphemus Moth (*Antheraea polyphemus*), a large North American saturniid (giant silk) moth, flies the dark air by night on broad wings set with translucent crescent "window" eyespots through which the light passes; the adult lives only a short while and does not feed, ranging across North America including the Pacific Northwest, its larvae feeding on the leaves of many broadleaf trees — a creature of the night air and the translucent wing, the living version of the thin veil that CALIPSO read with its pulses of light (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **An orchid that makes no light-food yet stands pale and translucent.** The Phantom Orchid (*Cephalanthera austiniae*), a ghostly, pure-white, leafless orchid of the shaded Pacific Northwest conifer forests, makes no chlorophyll — a mycoheterotroph that feeds through soil fungi — and stands pale and translucent in the forest gloom and then vanishes; it is the only *Cephalanthera* native to the Americas (ecological description only; distinct from the other pale forest mycoheterotrophs *Monotropa*, *Pterospora*, and *Allotropa*) — bound with the throughline of the translucent veil, the thing the faint light passes through.
