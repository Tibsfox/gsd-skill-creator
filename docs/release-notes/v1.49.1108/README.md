## Mission Overview

CALIPSO is the obs#11 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — a joint NASA/CNES Earth-observing mission that read the thin veil of the sky, carrying a two-wavelength polarization lidar that fired pulses of light straight down and profiled the vertical distribution of the faint suspended layers of the atmosphere. ★ It was a joint NASA + CNES (France) mission: NASA's Langley Research Center (LaRC) led the mission and the science and provided the CALIOP lidar (built by Ball Aerospace), and CNES funded/provided the enhanced PROTEUS spacecraft bus (Jason-1 heritage, built by Thales Alenia Space) and contributed to operations. The CALIOP (Cloud-Aerosol Lidar with Orthogonal Polarization) was a two-wavelength (532 nm + 1064 nm) elastic-backscatter lidar — its polarization sensitivity (two orthogonal components) measured at 532 nm, with 1064 nm reading total backscatter — and it profiled the vertical distribution of aerosols and thin clouds: the backscatter timed from each layer read as the height and content of the suspended material, and the depolarization at 532 nm telling aerosol type and ice-from-water cloud. The payload also carried the IIR (Imaging Infrared Radiometer, three thermal-IR channels) and the WFC (Wide Field Camera, visible). The mission launched 28 April 2006 on a Delta II 7420-10C from Vandenberg, together with CloudSat on the same rocket, and joined the A-Train (the afternoon constellation) in a sun-synchronous ~705 km orbit, ~98.2° inclination: a single spacecraft flying in close formation with CloudSat, the lidar and the radar together reading the same column of sky. The story is the launch from Vandenberg, the pair settling into the A-Train, the 532/1064 nm pulses firing down and the light scattering back from the suspended layers, the vertical distribution of the aerosol and thin-cloud column read by day and by night, the global maps of the thin veil built scan by scan, and the long watch over the thin veil that ran for some seventeen years before it ended. The science was central: CALIOP fired its laser pulse straight down and timed the backscatter returning from each layer of the atmospheric column, recovering the vertical distribution of the suspended material — the dust and smoke and sea salt and pollution, the thin clouds, the ice told from the water by the depolarization — and, joined with the CloudSat radar, building a three-dimensional view of clouds and the aerosols among them. CALIPSO was the first long-duration spaceborne lidar mission; it returned the first global, multi-year, day-and-night profiling of the vertical distribution of aerosols and thin clouds by polarization lidar; and, with CloudSat, it flew the first formation pairing a lidar and a cloud radar for a combined 3-D cloud/aerosol view. ★ The mission-first result is load-bearing: CALIPSO was the first LONG-DURATION spaceborne lidar mission — NOT "the first lidar in space" (LITE on the Shuttle in 1994 and GLAS on ICESat in 2003 preceded; the advance is the multi-year atmospheric-profiling lidar). ★ The survey result is equally load-bearing: CALIPSO returned the first global, multi-year, day-and-night profiling of aerosol + thin-cloud vertical distribution by polarization lidar — the day-and-night capability a key lidar advantage over passive sensors. ★ The formation result is load-bearing: CALIPSO and CloudSat flew in tight formation so lidar and radar together saw the clouds and aerosols in three dimensions. ★★★ CALIPSO is RETIRED (2006–2023), so the catalog frames its whole story in the PAST (CALIPSO launched 28 April 2006, joined the A-Train, profiled the thin veil, followed CloudSat into the C-Train in 2018, and ended in 2023 — the science mission completed 1 August 2023, the spacecraft passivated 15 December 2023). CALIPSO SUSTAINS the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis at obs#11 (substrate-axis rotation #42 continued), the catalog staying on Earth and turning from profiling the layered sky with radar to reading the thin veil with light. ## Surprises

- **One satellite carried a laser built to fire straight down.** Rather than a scanning imager, CALIPSO carried a two-wavelength polarization lidar — CALIOP — that fired its 532/1064 nm pulses straight down and timed the backscatter returning from each layer of the atmosphere directly beneath it. The instrument did not sweep a swath; it drew a vertical curtain of the thin veil along the ground track, and from that curtain came the height and content of the aerosols and the thin clouds.
- **Two wavelengths and the orthogonal polarization to read the layers.** CALIOP worked at 532 nm and 1064 nm, with its polarization sensitivity — two orthogonal components — at 532 nm and total backscatter at 1064 nm. The depolarization of the returned light is what let the lidar tell the ice from the water in a thin cloud and read the type of an aerosol layer, a different physics and purpose from CloudSat's 94 GHz radar.
- **The lidar and the radar flew in close formation.** CALIPSO did not fly alone: it flew in tight formation with CloudSat in the A-Train, the two spacecraft reading the same column of sky almost at once. The lidar caught the thin cloud and aerosol layers; the radar caught the cloud ice and water content; together they built a three-dimensional view of the clouds and the aerosols among them.
- **A nocturnal moth on wings set with translucent windows.** The Polyphemus Moth (*Antheraea polyphemus*), a large North American saturniid (giant silk) moth, flies the dark air by night on broad wings set with translucent crescent "window" eyespots through which the light passes; the adult lives only a short while and does not feed, ranging across North America including the Pacific Northwest, its larvae feeding on the leaves of many broadleaf trees — a creature of the night air and the translucent wing, the living version of the thin veil that CALIPSO read with its pulses of light (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **An orchid that makes no light-food yet stands pale and translucent.** The Phantom Orchid (*Cephalanthera austiniae*), a ghostly, pure-white, leafless orchid of the shaded Pacific Northwest conifer forests, makes no chlorophyll — a mycoheterotroph that feeds through soil fungi — and stands pale and translucent in the forest gloom and then vanishes; it is the only *Cephalanthera* native to the Americas (ecological description only; distinct from the other pale forest mycoheterotrophs *Monotropa*, *Pterospora*, and *Allotropa*) — bound with the throughline of the translucent veil, the thing the faint light passes through.

## Dedication

v1.291 CALIPSO is dedicated to the people at NASA's Langley Research Center, at the French space agency CNES, at Thales Alenia Space, and at Ball Aerospace, and to the engineers and scientists of NASA and France who built and flew the lidar that read the thin veil of the sky. The teams who put a long-lived laser in orbit and pointed it straight down, tuned to two colours so it could read the faint, translucent layers of dust and smoke and sea salt and thin cloud that a passive eye would miss. The crews who launched it from Vandenberg with CloudSat and flew the two spacecraft together, so the lidar and the radar drew the sky in three dimensions. The people who kept the watch into the lower C-Train orbit, carrying the mission on for some seventeen years. And the scientists who, from the light scattering back layer by layer, read the vertical distribution of the aerosols and the thin clouds — the ice told from the water by the turning of the light — building the first long, global, day-and-night record of the thin veil. The laser is quiet now, and that long record belongs to them.
