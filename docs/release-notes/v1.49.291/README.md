**The Eurasian Collared-Dove — The First Non-Native Columbidae and the Fastest Avian Range Expansion.** The **Eurasian Collared-Dove** (*Streptopelia decaocto*) enters the engine as the **third Columbidae** after the **Band-tailed Pigeon** (149) and the **Mourning Dove** (150), but crosses the **native / non-native boundary** for the first time in the family. The species' range expansion is one of the most remarkable events in modern ornithology. Native to India, Sri Lanka, and Myanmar, the Collared-Dove was confined to southern Asia until the early twentieth century. Beginning in the 1930s it expanded northwest from Turkey through the Balkans into Europe, reaching Britain by the 1950s and nearly all of Europe by the 1970s at an average rate of roughly 75 kilometers per year. In the mid-1970s, Collared-Doves were imported to a pet shop in Nassau, Bahamas. A handful escaped or were released. By 1982 they had reached southern Florida. From Florida the expansion was explosive — Gulf Coast states by 1990, interior states by 1995, the Pacific Northwest by 2000, virtually all of the continental United States by 2005, British Columbia and southern Alberta by 2010. The North American expansion averaged **100 to 150 kilometers per year**, even faster than the European expansion and one of the fastest avian range expansions ever documented on any continent.

**Why So Fast — The Enablers of Invasive Success.** Several factors enabled the extraordinary expansion. **Human commensalism**: the Collared-Dove thrives near human settlement and feeds at grain elevators, cattle feedlots, bird feeders, and waste-grain sources, finding suitable habitat (human settlement) everywhere it spread. **Year-round breeding**: like the **Mourning Dove**, the Collared-Dove produces multiple broods per year, and high reproductive output fuels population growth at the invasion front. **Naive predator populations**: the expanding front encounters raptors, cats, and other predators that have not learned to target the new species, and the front moves faster than the predator learning curve. **Industrial grain economy**: the Midwest and West's grain infrastructure provides unlimited food, and grain elevators, feedlots, and agricultural waste are Collared-Dove buffets. **Climate tolerance**: despite Asian tropical origins, the Collared-Dove tolerates a wide range of climates from Florida heat to Pacific Northwest rain to Northern Plains cold, far more climate-flexible than its origin range would suggest. The combination of traits that enabled the European expansion operated even more powerfully in North America, and the engine's documentation at degree 151 preserves the multi-factor ecological analysis at first principles.

**The Three-Note "Hoo-HOO-Hoo" as a New Sound in the PNW Soundscape.** The Eurasian Collared-Dove's call is a **three-note "hoo-HOO-hoo"** with emphasis on the second (middle) note, delivered at a fundamental frequency of **400-700 Hz** from rooftops and power lines in persistent bouts of **ten to thirty minutes**. The call is structurally distinct from both native Columbidae — it is not the **Band-tailed Pigeon**'s deep canopy hoot (149) and not the **Mourning Dove**'s five-note mournful "oo-WOO-woo-woo-woo" (150). Most significantly, the three-note "hoo-HOO-hoo" **did not exist in the Pacific Northwest soundscape before the early 2000s**. Anyone over the age of twenty-five in the PNW grew up without this sound; anyone under the age of twenty cannot remember a time when it was absent. The engine's acoustic documentation discipline requires recording what is rather than what was, and the three-note "hoo-HOO-hoo" is now a pervasive feature of suburban and agricultural acoustic environments across the region. The engine documents the invasive sound without editorial judgment because the sound is a fact of the modern PNW soundscape even though its presence represents a recent ecological change.

**The Black Neck Collar as Diagnostic Field Mark.** The Eurasian Collared-Dove's defining field mark is the **thin black crescent on the back of the neck** — the "collar" that gives the species its name. The collar is diagnostic: no native North American dove has it. The **Mourning Dove** is smaller, slimmer, longer-tailed, and collar-less; the **Band-tailed Pigeon** is larger, heavier, darker, and collar-less; the **Rock Pigeon** is far more variable in plumage but never shows a clean, thin black collar on an otherwise pale nape. Field identification of the Eurasian Collared-Dove is therefore unusually unambiguous for a dove — the black collar separates it cleanly from every native species. 5. **The fastest avian range expansion in modern ornithology deserves timeline-specific documentation rather than anecdote.** The Eurasian Collared-Dove's expansion benefits from specific dates (1982, 1990, 1995, 2000, 2005, 2010) and specific rates (75 km/year European, 100-150 km/year North American) rather than general descriptions of "rapid spread." The lesson for future range-expansion documentation is that **timeline specificity** is the vocabulary that converts mystery into science, and the engine's documentation discipline should prefer dates and rates over adjectives.
6. **The pet-shop-burglary origin story preserves the small-cause-to-continental-outcome trajectory.** The Eurasian Collared-Dove's North American colonization traces to a handful of birds escaping or being released from a Bahamas pet shop in the mid-1970s. Preserving the small-origin narrative is structurally important because it demonstrates that **large ecological outcomes can have small human-acted origins**, and the engine's documentation should preserve origin narratives at first principles rather than presenting established populations as if they had always been present. 7. **The new-sound-in-the-soundscape framing is a vocabulary for documenting recent acoustic additions.** The three-note "hoo-HOO-hoo" **did not exist in the PNW soundscape before the early 2000s**, and the engine's acoustic documentation discipline requires naming the novelty explicitly. The lesson is that **soundscapes are not static** — they accumulate new content over time, and the engine's documentation must include what is even when what is represents recent change. The new-sound-in-the-soundscape framing provides vocabulary for future degrees that document recently-arrived species, whether invasive, range-expanding, or recovering from historical absence.
8. **The engine documents what is, not what was or what should be.** The editorial neutrality of documenting the Eurasian Collared-Dove as a fact of the modern PNW soundscape — without either celebrating the invasion or lamenting it — is a structural commitment of the engine. The lesson for future invasive-species entries or other ecologically-contested subjects is that **editorial neutrality preserves the documentary record** better than advocacy-driven framing, and the engine's record will be more valuable to future readers if it describes the present state of the acoustic environment rather than promoting a particular policy response to that state.
9. **The complement-or-competition question is honestly unresolved in most new-arrival events.** Sol's arrival in Seattle hip-hop and the Eurasian Collared-Dove's arrival in PNW habitat both raise the **complement-or-competition question**. In both cases the honest answer is probably both, and the engine's documentation at degree 151 preserves the tension rather than forcing resolution. The lesson for future new-arrival documentation is that **unresolved complementarity-competition is structurally valid content** and does not require a definitive ruling to be useful. The record of the question is itself the record.
