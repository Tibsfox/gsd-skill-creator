## Mission Overview

NEAR Shoemaker is the obs#1 opener of the COMET-AND-ASTEROID-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's first Discovery-program mission, whose defining firsts are the first orbit of an asteroid, the first landing on an asteroid, and its standing as the first launched Discovery mission. It was built and operated by the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory (JHU APL, Laurel MD), the same laboratory that would later build the heliophysics-axis missions, though here the firsts are asteroid firsts, not the builder. NEAR launched 17 February 1996 on a Delta II (7925-8) from Cape Canaveral, flew past the main-belt asteroid 253 Mathilde on 27 June 1997 (returning the first close images of a C-type asteroid), used an Earth gravity assist, and entered orbit around the near-Earth asteroid 433 Eros on 14 February 2000 — the first human-made object to orbit a minor planet. It carried the Multi-Spectral Imager (MSI), the Near-Infrared Spectrograph (NIS), the NEAR Laser Rangefinder (NLR), the X-ray/Gamma-ray Spectrometer (XGRS), the Magnetometer (MAG), and radio science. The story is the launch from Cape Canaveral, the cruise with the Mathilde flyby and the Earth gravity assist, the year in orbit around Eros mapping the elongated rocky body from orbits as low as a few km, and the slow, controlled descent that ended in the first touchdown on an asteroid (12 February 2001), with surface transmissions continuing for about two weeks afterward (last contact 28 February 2001). The science is central: NEAR mapped the shape, density (~2.67 g/cm³), regolith, craters, and elemental composition of Eros, showing it to be a primitive, undifferentiated, consolidated (fractured but coherent) S-type body — a ~34-km relic of the early solar system. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: NEAR is a SMALL-BODY / asteroid mission, NOT a solar / heliophysics mission and NOT a rover driving a surface, and re-stamps NO heliophysics-axis anchor; it was built and operated by JHU APL as NASA's first Discovery-program mission; it was the first spacecraft to ORBIT an asteroid (earlier missions only flew PAST asteroids); it was the first spacecraft to LAND on an asteroid, scoped to ASTEROID (the U.S. soft-landed on the Moon with Surveyor in 1966, so the landing first is NOT broadened to "first U.S. landing on a celestial body"); and it was the first launched Discovery mission. ★★ NEAR ended with the February 2001 landing (last contact 28 February 2001), so the catalog frames the mission in the PAST tense throughout (NEAR flew, orbited, landed, measured), in contrast with the present-tense Solar Orbiter page. ★ The three February dates are kept distinct: launch 17 February 1996, orbit entry 14 February 2000, landing 12 February 2001.
