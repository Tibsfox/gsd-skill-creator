## Surprises

- **A spacecraft that became the first to settle onto an asteroid.** NEAR was an ORBITER, never designed as a lander, yet at the end of its mission it made a slow, controlled descent and touched down gently (~4 mph) on 433 Eros on 12 February 2001 — the first spacecraft to land on an asteroid — and continued transmitting surface data for about two weeks afterward (last contact 28 February 2001).
- **The first object to orbit a minor planet.** Before NEAR, every spacecraft to visit an asteroid had only flown PAST it (Galileo's Gaspra in 1991 and Ida in 1993, and NEAR's own Mathilde flyby in 1997); on 14 February 2000 NEAR entered orbit around 433 Eros and circled it for about a year from orbits as low as a few km — the first human-made object to orbit a minor planet.
- **A primitive relic read from orbit and from the surface.** NEAR mapped the shape, density (~2.67 g/cm³), regolith, craters, and elemental composition of Eros — an elongated, ~34-km, undifferentiated S-type body — showing it to be a primitive, consolidated (fractured but coherent) relic of the early solar system, read by the MSI, NIS, NLR, XGRS, and MAG instruments.
- **NASA's first Discovery-program mission.** NEAR was the first mission of NASA's Discovery program, both selected first (December 1993) and launched first (17 February 1996, ahead of Mars Pathfinder's December 1996 launch), and was renamed "NEAR Shoemaker" on 14 March 2000 to honor planetary geologist Eugene "Gene" Shoemaker.
- **A surefooted mountain sheep at home on bare rock.** The Bighorn Sheep (*Ovis canadensis*), a wild mountain sheep of the rugged, rocky country of the interior Pacific Northwest (canyon cliffs, talus, dry rocky slopes; present-day populations partly the result of reintroduction), moves with surefooted precision across precarious, near-vertical bare rock and plants itself securely on the smallest ledges (behavioral / ecological description only) — a mirror of the mission that circled a small rocky world and settled gently onto its rock.
- **A "stone-breaker" rooted in bare rock crevices.** Spotted Saxifrage (*Saxifraga bronchialis*), a low, mat-forming perennial whose name means "stone-breaker," roots directly in bare rock crevices on cliffs, ledges, and talus across the rocky interior and alpine Pacific Northwest, holding fast to the bare rock and flowering from it (small white, dark-spotted petals; ecological description only) — bound with NEAR's settling onto Eros by being at home on the bare rock.
