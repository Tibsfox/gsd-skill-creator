## Surprises

- **A geostationary orbit gives TEMPO the continuous, hour-by-hour view a low-Earth orbit cannot.** Unlike a low-Earth-orbit instrument that passes over a place about once a day, TEMPO sits in a geostationary orbit at roughly 35,786 km parked at ~91°W, turning with the Earth so it holds greater North America in continuous view — which lets its ultraviolet-visible imaging grating spectrometer scan the same field of regard again and again through the daylight, resolving the diurnal rise and fall of the pollution over the cities and roads that a once-daily overpass would miss entirely.
- **A spectrometer reading the reflected sunlight in the ultraviolet and the visible.** TEMPO's single instrument is an ultraviolet-visible imaging grating spectrometer that reads two ranges of the sunlight reflected off the atmosphere — ~290–490 nm in the ultraviolet and visible and ~540–740 nm — at ~0.6 nm resolution, and the depth of the absorption features in those bands yields the tropospheric column amounts of nitrogen dioxide, ozone, formaldehyde, and aerosols, the trace gases that signal the quality of the air.
- **A hosted payload that rides a commercial satellite to geostationary orbit.** Rather than flying its own dedicated spacecraft, TEMPO rides as a hosted payload on the Intelsat 40e commercial geostationary communications satellite (built by Maxar) — launched together on a SpaceX Falcon 9 from Cape Canaveral on ~7 April 2023 — sharing the host's COSPAR ID (2023-052A, NORAD 56174) and benefiting from the host's planned ~15-year stay in orbit, a cost-effective path to a continuous geostationary watch.
- **A dragonfly that hunts the open air far from the water.** The Paddle-tailed Darner (*Aeshna palmata*), a large mosaic-darner dragonfly of the Pacific Northwest and western North America, as an adult patrols the open air over meadows, clearings, and forest edges — often far from the water where its aquatic larvae develop — hawking small flying insects through the late-summer air: a creature of the open air, crossing on the wing the same air whose quality TEMPO watches from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only; family Aeshnidae).
- **A lichen that reads the quality of the air on the bark.** The Ragbag Lichen (*Platismatia glauca*, also varied rag lichen), a pale grey-green foliose lichen that drapes the bark and branches of Pacific Northwest conifers, is a fungus-alga symbiosis (the green algal photobiont *Trebouxia*) — not a plant — that, like all lichens, draws its water and nutrients directly from the air, which makes it acutely sensitive to air quality: a living bioindicator that thrives in clean air and fades where the air is fouled (ecological description only; distinct from the lung lichens *Lobaria*, the beard lichens *Usnea*, and the horsehair lichens *Bryoria*) — bound with the throughline of the air over the living land, reading on the bark the same air whose quality TEMPO watches from orbit.
