1. **Genre transitions are not events but processes, and the engine has now documented a three-step mechanism: soften (compound genre), break (superposition/transcendence), shift (new primary genre with old as modifier).**
   This mechanism may be generalizable. If the engine encounters another genre boundary in the future (soul to hip-hop, for example), it should watch for the same three-step pattern: first a fusion or compound label appears, then an artist who transcends the boundary entirely, then the center of gravity shifts to the new genre with the old as technique. The mechanism operates over multiple degrees, not at a single point. Genre transitions are gradual even when they feel sudden. The engine should not expect to identify a single "genre change degree" -- it should expect a transition zone spanning multiple degrees where the boundary dissolves progressively.

4. **Harmonic thinking and melodic thinking are the two fundamental axes of pitch organization, and the species engine now documents both through species in the same genus.**
   This distinction -- vertical (simultaneous) vs. horizontal (sequential) -- is the deepest structural axis in Western music theory. Harmony and melody are not just different aspects of music; they are different ways of thinking about pitch. The Hermit Thrush thinks harmonically (overtone series, vertical intervals). The Swainson's Thrush thinks melodically (ascending sequence, horizontal trajectory). The species engine, by documenting both through *Catharus*, provides a biological case study for the most fundamental distinction in music theory. Future music-theory nodes should reference the Catharus triptych as the biological grounding for the harmonic/melodic axis.
