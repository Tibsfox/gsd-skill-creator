## Part A: Malfunkshun — Glam Punk at E=7, Bainbridge Island 1980-1990, Andrew Wood + Kevin Wood + Regan Hagar, L'Andrew the Love Child, *Deep Six* (1986) and *Return to Olympus* (1995), the Earlier Bainbridge Vessel for Andrew Wood's Glam Vision Before Mother Love Bone

- **Bainbridge Island 1980 Formation: Andrew Wood, Kevin Wood, Regan Hagar.** Malfunkshun formed in 1980 on **Bainbridge Island** around **Andrew Wood** (vocals, bass), his older brother **Kevin Wood** (guitar), and **Regan Hagar** (drums). The three-piece configuration is unusual for a glam-influenced band — glam's visual and sonic maximalism tends toward five-piece or six-piece lineups with multiple guitars and keyboards — but Malfunkshun's three-piece worked because Andrew Wood's bass-and-vocal combination supplied the low-end and the melodic center simultaneously while Kevin Wood's guitar provided the harmonic and textural layer. The compact instrumentation sits against the theatrical performance register to produce Malfunkshun's specific sonic identity: a small band making big theatrical gestures, the sonic equivalent of the stage presence Wood cultivated across the band's decade-long run.

- **L'Andrew the Love Child: Andrew Wood's Stage Persona.** **Andrew Wood** developed the stage persona "L'Andrew the Love Child" during Malfunkshun's 1980s run. The persona was not ironic and was not a pose; it was the actual expressive register through which Wood understood rock performance, and the name combines French affectation ("L'Andrew") with 1960s-counterculture self-identification ("Love Child") to produce a character that sat at the intersection of glam theatrics, British rock aesthetics, and personal expressive commitment. Wood wrote songs of theatrical extravagance. He wore costumes on stage. He performed with the kind of charismatic conviction that transformed small clubs into something larger than themselves, and the Seattle scene's initial uncertainty about what to do with him in the mid-1980s — before his **Mother Love Bone** emergence made the glam-grunge register legible to broader audiences — is itself part of the historical record. The L'Andrew persona carried forward into **Mother Love Bone** where it gained wider recognition, but the Malfunkshun work is the documentary record of the persona in its earlier fully-formed Bainbridge Island version.

- ***Deep Six* (1986) Compilation Appearance as Founding Seattle Grunge Document.** Malfunkshun appeared on *Deep Six* (1986, **C/Z Records**) alongside **Green River**, **Soundgarden**, **Skin Yard**, the **Melvins**, and the **U-Men** — the six-band compilation that is now recognized as the founding document of Seattle grunge and the first collected statement of the sound that would transform American popular music in 1991-1992. **Jack Endino** engineered the compilation at **Reciprocal Recording** in Seattle, and the compilation itself — run by **Daniel House** who also played in **Skin Yard** — received modest attention at release (several thousand copies sold, mostly through Seattle record stores and mail order) but has been retrospectively recognized as one of the most historically significant compilations in American rock history. Malfunkshun's presence on *Deep Six* establishes the band's position at the material origin of the Seattle sound alongside the five other founding participants, and the compilation provides the cross-reference that links Malfunkshun to the broader 1986 Seattle heavy-rock scene.

The ferry crossing is not an incidental biographical detail but the physical arc of the band's career: from the Bainbridge landing to the Seattle stage, the 35-minute crossing repeated until the island was the band's place of origin and Seattle was their arena. ## Part B: Rhinoceros Auklet Colony — Second Alcidae at E=6, *Cerorhinca monocerata*, Nocturnal Burrow Moans at 300-700 Hz, Protection Island in the Strait of Juan de Fuca as Largest North American Colony, Horn-Billed Breeding Morphology, the Two-Degree Alcidae Family Arc Closure

- **Second Alcidae Entry and the Two-Degree Family Arc Closure.** The **Rhinoceros Auklet Colony** at degree 295 is the second member of family **Alcidae** to enter the SPS track, closing the two-consecutive-degree Alcidae family arc that the preceding **Tufted Puffin** opened at degree 294. The two-degree span covers the full temporal range of the outer coast rocky island alcid colony — diurnal activity during the Tufted Puffin's daytime colony presence at degree 294, nocturnal activity during the Rhinoceros Auklet's after-dark return at degree 295 — and the acoustic registers of both species are concentrated in their respective temporal windows. The arc's closure at degree 295 allows the engine to move on to other seabird families and registers at subsequent degrees without leaving the Alcidae family under-documented, and the preparation for the degree-296 transition to the **Peregrine Falcon stoop** will end the nine-degree seabird run (286-295) and open the engine's raptor register.

- ***Cerorhinca monocerata* and the Bill Horn That Gives the Species Its Common Name.** The **Rhinoceros Auklet** is named for the small horn at the base of the breeding-season bill — a keratinous projection approximately 2 cm tall that appears in breeding adults and is shed at the end of the breeding season, producing a bird whose appearance differs substantially between its breeding and non-breeding states. The species is not closely related to the rhinoceros (mammalian order Perissodactyla) and the common name refers only to the visual resemblance of the horn. *Cerorhinca monocerata* (Greek: "horn-nosed, one-horned") is the species' scientific name and captures the same descriptive observation. The bill itself is dark gray-black with a yellow-orange cutting edge, and the horn emerges from the upper mandible base and extends forward approximately 2 cm before terminating. Two thin white plumes extend from above and below the eye during the breeding season, adding to the species' breeding-morphology distinctiveness, and the plumes are shed along with the horn at the end of the breeding season to produce the non-breeding adult's plain dark brown-black head.

- **Body Dimensions and Overall Morphology.** Rhinoceros Auklets are mid-sized alcids — body length 14-16 inches, wingspan 22-23 inches, weight 450-620 grams — smaller than the **Common Murre** (which will enter the engine at a future degree) but larger than the **Cassin's Auklet**. Their plumage is almost entirely dark brown-black with a slightly paler underside; the overall visual register is less dramatic than the preceding Tufted Puffin's high-contrast black-white-gold-orange-red breeding plumage. The species is often overlooked by casual birders precisely because its visual field identity is muted and because its acoustic register is nocturnal rather than diurnal, making the species less visible from boats and shore during daylight hours than the Tufted Puffin and the common gull species. The body shape is the Alcidae family's typical torpedo-like form adapted for underwater propulsion, and the wings are small relative to the body mass — a characteristic of the family that produces the rapid whirring wingbeat audible at colony launches.

- **Nocturnal Colony Return and the Moaning Chorus at 300-700 Hz.** Rhinoceros Auklets return to their colonies after dark to avoid aerial predators — primarily **Bald Eagles**, **Peregrine Falcons**, and **Glaucous-winged Gulls** that harass and predate alcids at colony edges during daylight hours — and the colony's acoustic life is concentrated in the night hours when thousands of birds navigate to specific burrow entrances through the darkness. The moaning chorus produced from within the soil-burrow network at night is audible above ground as a murmuring, subterranean acoustic event, typically in the 300-700 Hz frequency range, that is the species' primary colony acoustic. The quality of the moans is low-pitched, guttural, and sustained — less sharp than gull calls, less percussive than tern calls, distinct from the preceding Tufted Puffin's higher-range grunts — and the aggregate acoustic event at peak summer in a large colony like Protection Island produces a continuous low-frequency wash that is audible from boats in the Strait of Juan de Fuca passing near the island during night hours. The E=6 placement reflects this acoustic character: present and substantial with real presence in the moaning chorus and the colony's aggregate night acoustic, but not at the sharper piercing high-frequency intensity of the tern entries earlier in the seabird run.

- **Protection Island National Wildlife Refuge: Largest North American Colony.** **Protection Island National Wildlife Refuge** in the **Strait of Juan de Fuca** is the largest Rhinoceros Auklet breeding colony in North America, supporting approximately 70,000 breeding pairs each spring and summer — roughly 70% of the North American population. The island is a 364-acre glacial moraine managed by the **US Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS)** and closed to public access to protect the soil-burrow network that the species excavates on the island's flat tops and gently sloping terrain. Protection Island's status as the largest single-colony acoustic event in the North American Rhinoceros Auklet range makes the island's nocturnal moaning chorus one of the most substantial seabird acoustic events on the Pacific Northwest coast, and the colony's scale — tens of thousands of birds arriving at burrows over a two-to-three-hour window each night during the April-through-August breeding season — produces a densely layered acoustic texture that is qualitatively different from smaller-scale alcid colonies.

- **Secondary Pacific Northwest Colony Network.** Secondary Rhinoceros Auklet breeding colonies complete the species' Pacific Northwest colony network: **Destruction Island** offshore of Jefferson County (also documented at degree 294 as a Tufted Puffin colony site, establishing Destruction Island as a multi-species shared-colony register), **Smith Island** in the eastern Strait of Juan de Fuca, **Alexander Island** off the Washington Olympic coast, and **Tatoosh Island** at **Cape Flattery** (also documented at degree 294 as a Tufted Puffin colony site) host the smaller but still substantial secondary colonies. The **San Juan Islands** and **Gulf Islands** host smaller breeding groups. The Pacific Northwest colony network is distinct from the **British Columbia** and **Alaska** colonies to the north and the **California** and **Oregon** colonies to the south, and the regional colony network is structurally important because it concentrates approximately 70% of the North American population in a relatively small geographic area centered on the Strait of Juan de Fuca.

- **Offshore Foraging and the Herring / Sand Lance Diet.** During daylight hours when birds are away from the colony, Rhinoceros Auklets forage at sea 10-50 km from the colony, diving for small schooling fish — primarily **Pacific Herring** (*Clupea pallasii*), **Sand Lance** (*Ammodytes hexapterus*), and juvenile **Salmon** species — using the underwater wing-propulsion characteristic of the Alcidae family. The diet is energetically critical during the breeding season because both parents participate in chick provisioning and must deliver fish to the burrow through the nocturnal return window. A typical adult will deliver multiple small fish per return, held crosswise in the bill and carried to the chick inside the burrow. The offshore foraging behavior makes the species' daytime presence acoustically minimal: the birds are at sea during daylight hours, and the colony acoustic is entirely a nocturnal phenomenon. This temporal separation from the preceding **Tufted Puffin's** diurnal colony register establishes the two alcids as acoustic complements on the SPS track.

- **Conservation: IUCN Least Concern, Stable or Slightly Increasing Regional Population.** The Rhinoceros Auklet is listed as **IUCN Least Concern** with a stable global population, and Pacific Northwest populations have been stable or slightly increasing over recent decades — a conservation outcome distinct from the preceding Tufted Puffin's regional decline concern documented at degree 294. The species is protected under the **Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA) of 1918** in the United States, and **Protection Island NWR** is managed under USFWS authority to preserve the colony's soil-burrow network. The population difference between the two alcids at degrees 294 and 295 is itself structurally productive documentation: two closely related species with similar breeding behaviors and overlapping colony sites have produced divergent population trajectories over the past several decades, and the engine's preservation of both species at first-class resolution maintains the register needed for future species-comparison documentation. Colony monitoring programs by the **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW)**, the **US Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS)**, and the **Washington Ornithological Society (WOS)** track the regional population trends.
