**The Fartz** arrive at E=8 -- the Seattle hardcore punk band formed in 1980, one of the founding acts of the Pacific Northwest hardcore scene, and the correct band to stand at the three-quarters mark of the engine. The Fartz -- Loud Tom Niemeyer (guitar, later of Accused and Gruntruck), Steve Verwolf (vocals), Frank Brassard (drums), Paul Solger (bass) -- were active from 1980 to 1983, releasing *Because This Fuckin' World Still Stinks* (1982) and *World Full of Hate* (1982) on their own label before dissolving. Their music is fast, raw, politically angry, and produced with the minimum of studio intervention: it is hardcore punk in the form it takes when the community producing it has no infrastructure, no industry support, and no expectation of commercial success. The Fartz played because they had something to say and because hardcore punk was the correct form for saying it in 1980-1983 Seattle.

E=8 for The Fartz ties the engine maximum. It is the correct score. The Fartz are not a band of nuance or dynamic variation. They are a band of maximum energy, maximum speed, maximum aggression, and maximum political directness. Their records are short and loud and over fast. That is exactly what they are supposed to be. The **ORCA ARC OPENS** at degree 270.

**Orca S01 Pod Call** arrives at E=8 -- the vocalizations of a Southern Resident Killer Whale pod in the Salish Sea, the family-group acoustic communication that Southern Resident Killer Whales (*Orcinus orca*, SRKW population) use to maintain cohesion, coordinate movement, and affirm social bonds within the multi-generational matrilineal groups that constitute their society. The Southern Resident Killer Whales are a critically endangered population: as of 2026, fewer than 75 individuals remain. They inhabit the Salish Sea -- the inland marine waters of Puget Sound, the San Juan Islands, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the Georgia Strait -- and their acoustic culture is one of the most complex and socially embedded communication systems documented in the cetacean record.

The Orca Arc spans degrees 270-276 -- seven consecutive SPS entries documenting the acoustic behaviors of Southern Resident Killer Whales. The arc is the engine's most sustained engagement with a single species, and it is sustained because the species deserves it. SRKW pod calls, whistles and clicks, transient orca hunting tactics, orca breaching, orca spy-hopping, orca pec-slapping, and orca echolocation: seven acoustic behaviors, seven consecutive entries, a complete acoustic portrait of a whale population whose survival is contingent on the recovery of the Chinook salmon runs that are their primary food source.

S01 is the first Southern Resident pod call documented in the engine. The S01 pod call is the acoustic signature of the S pod, one of three pods in the Southern Resident community (J, K, and L pods are the others; the pod naming convention in the research literature uses J/K/L rather than S, but S01 designates a specific call type in the SRKW acoustic catalog). The pod call -- a discrete, stereotyped vocalization used by all members of a pod in collective acoustic display -- is the most socially fundamental of SRKW vocal behaviors. It is the sound of a family announcing itself to itself and to the world: this is who we are, we are together, we are here.

At the three-quarters mark, the engine turns to the orcas.
