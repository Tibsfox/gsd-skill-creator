## Surprises

- **A Sun-synchronous orbit gives OCO-2 the consistent sunlight its passive spectrometers need.** Unlike an active instrument that supplies its own light, OCO-2 reads reflected SUNLIGHT, so it flies a Sun-synchronous orbit at roughly 705 km with a ~98.2° inclination and a ~1:30 PM equator-crossing — the same local solar time on every pass — giving its three grating spectrometers the consistent illumination they need to retrieve the column carbon dioxide accurately, and at launch it flew at the front of the A-Train so its soundings could be matched to those of the constellation behind it.
- **Three spectrometers reading the same sunlight in three narrow bands.** OCO-2's single instrument carries three high-resolution grating spectrometers that look at the same scene at once — one in the O2 A-band (~0.765 µm) to fix the air mass and surface pressure, one in the weak CO2 band (~1.61 µm), and one in the strong CO2 band (~2.06 µm) — and the depth of the dark absorption lines in the two carbon bands, weighed against the oxygen band, yields the column-averaged CO2 dry-air mole fraction at about one part per million.
- **The carbon sources and sinks are inferred, not measured directly.** OCO-2 directly retrieves XCO2 — the column-averaged carbon dioxide — but the carbon sources and sinks (the fluxes) are not read off the spectrum; they are inferred by feeding the high-precision global XCO2 into atmospheric inversion / data-assimilation models, so the map of where the carbon rises and falls is a model-constrained reading of where the air carries more or less carbon, bias-corrected against the ground-based TCCON network to about two-tenths of a part per million.
- **A millipede that returns the carbon of the forest to the soil.** The Yellow-spotted Millipede (*Harpaphe haydeniana*, the almond-scented millipede), a black-bodied, bright-yellow-spotted millipede of Pacific Northwest and Pacific-coast conifer forests (the coast and the Cascades, Alaska to California), is a forest-floor detritivore that feeds on decaying conifer-needle and leaf litter and, in breaking it down, returns carbon and nutrients to the soil — a key decomposer that processes a large share of the fallen litter in its range; when disturbed it coils into a spiral and secretes an almond-scented defensive compound: a mirror of the "given back" half of the breath OCO-2 watches from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only; the genus *Harpaphe* has a southern sibling, *H. pottica*, in southern California).
- **A plant that grows new life on its own leaf and draws carbon in beneath the canopy.** The Piggyback Plant (*Tolmiea menziesii*, youth-on-age), a soft-leaved saxifrage-family perennial of moist, shaded Pacific Northwest forests and streambanks (Alaska to California, mostly west of the Cascades), photosynthesizes in the low light beneath the canopy — drawing carbon in — and is named for the way new plantlets form on the mature leaf, where the blade joins the stalk (ecological description only; distinct from the foamflowers *Tiarella* and the fringecups *Tellima*) — bound with the throughline of the breath of the living land, the "drawn down" half of the carbon whose column OCO-2 measures from orbit.
