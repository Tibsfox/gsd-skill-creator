- **The Douglas Fir Crown in Gale Is a Simultaneous Three-Mechanism Compound Acoustic Event.** The Douglas Fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) crown in gale conditions produces a sustained moan-roar that is not a single acoustic event but the simultaneous product of three distinct acoustic-generation mechanisms: aerodynamic needle turbulence producing the hiss at 1000-6000 Hz, structural trunk and primary-branch flex producing the groan at 20-200 Hz with a 5-15 second gust-cycle rhythmic pulse, and Aeolian vortex shedding off cylindrical branch elements producing the tonal moan at 75-500 Hz. The three-mechanism framework gives the degree 326 SPS entry its analytical structure and allows each mechanism to be documented at its appropriate acoustic-physics resolution rather than collapsing the compound event to a single "wind noise" attribution.
- **The Aerodynamic Mechanism Produces Broadband Hiss Through Needle-Layer Turbulent Wake.** At wind speeds above approximately 20-25 mph, the flow of air through the Douglas Fir's needle structure becomes acoustically significant. The needles (2-3 cm long, flat, arranged alternately along small branchlets) create turbulent wakes as air flows past them, and the turbulence is random and broadband rather than rhythmic. The needle layer converts laminar wind flow into turbulent wake, and the turbulence radiates as acoustic energy at 1000-6000 Hz across the full gale spectrum. At 40-50 mph gale wind, this aerodynamic mechanism produces a continuous broadband sound — the characteristic hiss component of the moan-roar — that does not pause between gust cycles because the needle-layer turbulence is present even at the between-gust wind speeds. The aerodynamic hiss is the highest-frequency component of the combined event and contributes the continuous breadth of the sustained sound.
- **The Structural Mechanism Produces Deep Creak and Groan Through Trunk and Branch Flex Under Sustained Load.** Under sustained gale loads, the Douglas Fir trunk flexes within its elastic limit. The flex is not visible as rotation or bending at the human eye scale but is large enough — millimeters to centimeters of displacement at the crown of a 75-meter tree — to generate internal stress waves in the wood. The trunk fundamental resonance at 0.1-0.5 Hz is below the audible range and is felt rather than heard, but audible harmonics at 20-200 Hz from the trunk and at 2-500 Hz from primary branches (diameter 0.3-0.8 meters, length 5-15 meters) produce the deep creak and groan of wood under sustained cyclic stress. The Pacific Northwest winter storm gust cycle at 5-15 seconds per gust peak creates a rhythmic pulse in this structural output: as each gust loads the tree the flex deepens and the creak intensifies; as each gust fades the flex reduces and the creak softens. The structural component contributes the periodic depth of the sustained event.
- **The Aeolian Mechanism Produces Pure Tonal Emergence Through Vortex Shedding Off Cylindrical Branches.** Under specific conditions — particular wind speeds creating specific Reynolds numbers for cylindrical elements of specific branch diameters — Aeolian tones emerge from the broadband noise background: pure tones created by the vortex shedding behind a cylinder in wind. The frequency of an Aeolian tone follows the formula f = (Strouhal number × wind speed) / cylinder diameter, with the Strouhal number approximately 0.21 for tree branches. For a 5 cm branch at 40 mph (18 m/s) wind: f ≈ (0.21 × 18) / 0.05 ≈ 75 Hz. For a 2 cm branchlet at the same speed: f ≈ (0.21 × 18) / 0.02 ≈ 190 Hz. These Aeolian tones are superimposed on the broadband aerodynamic and structural noise and emerge as quasi-pure tones from the noise background when the wind is sufficiently steady and the structural element is sufficiently cylindrical. The Aeolian component is the moan in the moan-roar — the tonal signature that makes the Douglas Fir gale specifically identifiable rather than generic wind-noise.

The structural resonance of a 150-ton tree trunk sits at 5-20 Hz for the low modes — sub-bass felt before it is heard. The crown at old-growth scale is a complex three-dimensional structure with primary branches extending 5-15 meters from the trunk, secondary and tertiary branching systems, and billions of needle fascicles providing the aerodynamic transducer surface. The deep taproot (3-5 meters) and extensive lateral root system anchor the tree structurally, allowing the trunk to bend and flex and transmit the flex as acoustic energy rather than tipping over. The wood properties (density approximately 530 kg/m³, high modulus of elasticity, sound speed 5000 m/s along the grain against 343 m/s in air) make the trunk a single acoustically coupled structure where a stress wave initiated at the root base propagates to the crown in approximately 15 milliseconds.

Ridge crests experience topographic acceleration effect — the wind forced over a ridge crest must accelerate to conserve mass — producing ridge-crest wind speeds 20-40% higher than the adjacent valley. A Pacific Northwest winter storm that delivers 35 mph winds in the valley may deliver 45-50 mph at the ridge crest, and the Douglas Fir at the edge of the subalpine zone is at its upper elevation limit: shorter and more exposed than the same species at lower elevations but still large enough to produce the characteristic gale acoustic event. The combination of maximum exposure and still-large stature makes the subalpine ridge Douglas Fir the most acoustically productive gale setting.
