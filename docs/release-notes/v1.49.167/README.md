## Summary

After the landmark. After Ray Charles invented soul music at degree 30 and the Barn Swallow closed the swallow quintet with the most complex liquid warble the engine had produced — after the biggest name since Quincy Jones and the densest family cluster in engine history — the engine does what engines do. It turns one more degree. Degree 31 belongs to Brenda Lee Eager: not a generational figure but a tradition-carrier, not the inventor of soul but one of the women who kept it alive through backup singing, duet partnership, songwriting, and choral direction. Born in Mobile, Alabama, raised in Lower Peach Tree on the banks of the Alabama River in the Black Belt, drawn from rural Wilcox County to Chicago's soul machine, she became the warm voice beneath Jerry Butler's Iceman cool on "Ain't Understanding Mellow" — a gold single, over a million copies sold, No. 3 R&B and No. 21 Hot 100 in early 1972. She sang backup for Ray Charles himself. She wrote songs recorded by Aretha Franklin, Gladys Knight, Mavis Staples, Bobby Womack, Shirley Brown, Cliff Richard, Peaches & Herb, and Prince. She directs the T.H.E. Choir at the City of Angels Church in Culver City, California, today. The tradition that Charles created, Eager carried. The relay.

In the mountain ash at the edge of the park, a tree full of sleek, crested shapes — dozens of them, warm brown melting to gray, black masks sharp against pale sky, yellow tail bands bright as punctuation, tiny drops of red wax catching the light on the secondary wing feathers. They are eating. They are passing berries from branch to beak with mechanical efficiency, sixteen minutes from mouth to seed, several hundred berries per bird per day. And from the tree comes a sound that is not a song but something else — a high, thin, shimmering see-see-see, see-see-see, dozens of voices overlapping, each one a whisper, all of them together a crystalline curtain that fills the air like heat shimmer. *Bombycilla cedrorum*. The silk-tail of the cedars. The Cedar Waxwing. The engine's 17th family. The first frugivore. The first genus named for tactile quality — not behavior, not anatomy, but what the feathers feel like: silk. After five swallows made a river of liquid song, the Cedar Waxwing whispers. And the whisper carries.

**Six structural events converge at degree 31.** Bombycillidae enters the engine as the 17th family, only three species worldwide, phylogenetically isolated; the engine will hold two of three (66.7% coverage, the highest proportional family representation). **The waxwing pair begins** — Cedar (31) and Bohemian (32), the third same-genus consecutive pair after *Catharus* (18-19) and *Tachycineta* (27-28). **The sibilant timbral category opens** — after thirty degrees of tonal, liquid, and rough, the see-see-see introduces a fourth color: thin, high, crystalline, glassy. From the river to the wire. **The first fruit-defined ecosystem** arrives: "fruiting trees" is the first habitat in the engine defined by food source rather than physical structure. Where the berries are, the waxwings are. **The Relay** becomes the 32nd artist-city pattern, the first non-residential connection documented through direct musical collaboration. Eager never lived in Seattle; she sang for the man who carried Seattle's Central District forward. **Eleventh consecutive E=4** extends the streak from decet to undecet, the longest energy plateau in engine history, now critically overdue to break. In parallel, both sides of the degree describe recovery and continuation — the mellow undertow and the high sibilant whisper — each the quiet counterpoint to degree 30's explosive landmark, and the same structural note struck in two different registers.

**Brenda Lee Eager — from Lower Peach Tree to the mellow undertow.** The Bumps Blackwell–Ray Charles–Quincy Jones Central District cluster from 1947 to 1950 produced the foundations of soul music. Eager, born in 1947 the year Charles arrived in Seattle, absorbed the same gospel tradition in rural Alabama churches that Charles had absorbed in Greenville, Florida. By the late 1960s she had relocated to Chicago, drawn by the recording industry and the concentration of gospel-trained Black vocalists. By 1971 she was the lead singer of Peaches, Jerry Butler's female backup vocal group. "Ain't Understanding Mellow," released late 1971 on Mercury Records and written by Homer Talbert and Herscholt Polk, arranged by James Mack, produced by Gerald Sims and Jerry Butler, reached No. 3 on the Billboard R&B chart and No. 21 on the Hot 100, went gold in April 1972, and sold over a million copies in a market dominated by Motown, Philadelphia International, and Stax. Chicago soul was the fourth pole. Butler and Eager are the exemplary case of the Chicago soul duet form — distinct from the Memphis soul duet (both voices hot and raw) and the Motown duet (both voices polished into unified sheen), the Chicago soul duet places two distinct vocal temperatures in conversation and lets the contrast carry the emotional weight. The Love We Have, The Love We Had, their full duet album on Mercury Records in 1973 recorded at Mark XVI Recording Studio in Memphis and Universal Recording Studio in Chicago and mixed at Paragon Recording Studio, structured one side around ongoing romance and the other around its aftermath, with compositions by Terry Callier and Billy Butler including "Lean On Me," "As the Seasons Change," "Were We Lovers, Were We Friends," and a soul-recast of Bacharach-David's "(They Long to Be) Close to You."

**Cedar Waxwing — the whisper carries.** *Bombycilla cedrorum* belongs to family Bombycillidae — the engine's 17th family and the first entirely new family since Hirundinidae entered at degree 26. *Bombycilla* derives from Latin *bombyx* ("silk") and Modern Latin *-cilla* ("tail"), yielding "silk-tail" — a reference to the smooth, sleek plumage. *Cedrorum* is Latin genitive plural meaning "of the cedars," referring to the eastern red cedar (*Juniperus virginiana*) whose blue-gray berries are a critical winter food source. The full binomial translates to "silk-tail of the cedars" — a name built from texture (silk), anatomy (tail), and diet (cedar berries). Where the swallow genera named themselves by myth (*Progne*), behavior (*Tachycineta*), anatomy (*Stelgidopteryx*), and identity (*Hirundo*), the waxwing genus names itself by texture. Bombycillidae contains only three species worldwide — Cedar Waxwing, Bohemian Waxwing (*B. garrulus*), and Japanese Waxwing (*B. japonica*) — and molecular phylogenetics places Bombycillidae in superfamily Bombycilloidea with silky-flycatchers (Ptiliogonatidae) and the Palmchat (Dulidae), none of which are PNW species. Bombycillidae stands phylogenetically isolated in the engine's taxonomic graph. The Cedar Waxwing's primary vocalization — high-pitched, thin, sibilant, typically transcribed as see-see-see or sreee — has a fundamental frequency of 6.0-10.0 kHz (higher than any swallow), peak energy at 7.0-9.0 kHz (nearly an octave above the Barn Swallow's 3.0-6.0 kHz peak), and call duration of 0.3-0.8 seconds (an order of magnitude shorter than the Barn Swallow's 3-8 seconds). It is a contact call, not a song. A single Cedar Waxwing sounds thin and lost; a flock calling together sounds like the tree itself is vibrating.

**The Relay — first non-residential artist-city pattern.** The Relay is the engine's first instance of a non-residential artist-city relationship documented through direct musical collaboration. Eager did not live in Seattle. No specific documentation confirms a Seattle residency, performance, or recording session. The connection is musical: she sang backup for Ray Charles — the artist whose three years in Seattle's Central District (1947-1950) produced the seeds of soul music — and her voice is literally on Ray Charles recordings. She carried his vocal tradition forward through harmony, counterpoint, and the rhythmic support that allowed his lead voice to work. She carried the tradition further through her songwriting for Franklin, Knight, Prince, Womack, and Staples — all artists in the soul lineage Charles initiated — and through her choral direction, returning the tradition to its gospel roots. The relay mechanism: city to artist to tradition to next artist. Distinct from Export (degree 0 — the artist leaves the city), The Incubation (degree 30 — the city transforms the artist), The Catalog (degree 29 — the relationship is mediated through recordings as a listener), and Draw (degree 1 — the artist is attracted to the city). The Relay artist is attracted to the tradition, not the geography. The same way the Cedar Waxwing is attracted to the fruit, not the forest, and mirrors the nomadic frugivore pattern on the species side, the Relay describes musical tradition-following as the primary engine of artist mobility.

**The mellow undertow — thirty-second acoustic element.** The mellow undertow is the warm, steady, slightly pulling vocal current that runs beneath the lead melody, exerting emotional gravity without ever breaking the surface. In the Butler-Eager duets, the undertow is literal: Eager's voice runs beneath Butler's Iceman lead, providing harmonic warmth, melodic counterpoint, and the emotional pull that gives the cool surface its depth. Without the undertow, Butler's coolness would be merely cold. With it, the coolness becomes emotional restraint — the listener feels the warmth underneath. The mellow undertow follows the rough brrit (degree 30) in the acoustic progression: after the eruption (Charles's gospel-drenched intensity), the pull (Eager's warm, steady, professional-grade vocal support). The brrit creates; the undertow carries. The three-element sequence liquid brook (29) to rough brrit (30) to mellow undertow (31) maps three sonic states in succession: flow, rupture, and recovery — the most dramatic three-element sequence since the liquid triad itself. Both the mellow undertow (Part A) and the whisper (Part B) share a single structural property: both are sufficiency rather than intensity. Neither is loud. Both carry. Each side of this degree documents enough-ness on its own axis.

**R&B/Soul — genre stage 13, Jazz fully absent for the first time.** R&B/Soul at degree 31 is the first compound genre in the engine that contains no Jazz component. Every prior compound genre included Jazz as either primary or secondary element. At degree 31, Jazz is fully absent. Both R&B and Soul are descendants of the gospel-secular fusion that Charles created. The sequence R&B/Jazz (30) to R&B/Soul (31) maps a handoff: Jazz passes the baton to Soul as the modifier. R&B remains primary. The genre structure mirrors the artist-city pattern: The Incubation (30) passed to The Relay (31); R&B/Jazz passed to R&B/Soul. Both are relay transitions. Mercury Records (founded 1945 in Chicago by Irving Green, Berle Adams, Arthur Talmadge, and Ray Greenberg) is the oldest of the three-label triad completed at this degree. The Prestige-Atlantic-Mercury sequence at degrees 29-30-31 represents three historically significant labels in three consecutive degrees — Prestige (1949, New York, Jazz), Atlantic (1947, New York, Soul/R&B/Rock), Mercury (1945, Chicago, Soul/R&B/Funk). Mercury is the only one headquartered outside New York, and its Chicago roots explain why Butler, Gene Chandler, the Dells, and the Ohio Players all made their most consequential records there.

**The sibilant timbral category.** The engine's timbral palette has expanded from three categories to four. Tonal (degrees 0-25): clear pitch, harmonic structure, melodic. Liquid (degrees 26-28, 30): flowing, water-like, continuous modulation. Rough (degree 29): broadband, buzzy, grating. Sibilant (degree 31): thin, high, crystalline, glassy — the Cedar Waxwing's see-see-see. The shift from degree 30 to degree 31 is one of the sharpest timbral transitions in the engine: from rich, complex, sustained melodic warbling (liquid) to thin, high, repetitive sibilance. From the river to the wire. From water to glass. In parallel, the Chicago soul duet form places Butler's polished, controlled, slightly metallic vocal timbre in conversation with Eager's warm, breathy, gospel-trained delivery — two voices that operate in adjacent timbral registers the same way the Cedar Waxwing and the Barn Swallow operate in adjacent acoustic registers. Both sides of degree 31 document timbral adjacency as a productive condition: polished cool meets warm gospel, and sibilant whisper meets liquid warble. Each is the other's necessary counterpart.

**Frugivory as ecosystem.** "Fruiting trees" is the first ecosystem in the engine defined by food source rather than habitat structure. Previous ecosystems described places (estuary, old-growth, farmland). This ecosystem describes a resource (fruit). The Cedar Waxwing's "place" changes with the seasons and with crop success. Its habitat is mobile, ephemeral, unpredictable. Key food plants in the PNW: Pacific madrone (*Arbutus menziesii*), mountain ash (*Sorbus*), serviceberry (*Amelanchier*), hawthorn (*Crataegus*), holly (*Ilex*), crabapple (*Malus*), and Oregon grape (*Mahonia*). Fruit comprises approximately 84% of the diet during non-breeding season. The digestive tract processes berries in as little as 16-45 minutes. Cedar Waxwings are among the most important seed dispersers in North American ecosystems. The "waxwing" in the common name refers to small, brilliantly red, waxy tips on the secondary flight feathers — not pigmented barbs but flattened extensions of the feather shaft coated with carotenoid pigment (astaxanthin). Orange wax tips have appeared since the late 1960s, coinciding with non-native honeysuckle (*Lonicera*) introduction — rhodoxanthin from honeysuckle berries replaces astaxanthin, literally recoloring a native bird. An introduced plant changes a native bird's appearance. The Cedar Waxwing wears its diet on its wings — the same way Brenda Lee Eager's Chicago soul aesthetic wore the Jackson Street gospel tradition on every phrase.

The relay-kinetic subtype at this degree describes energy at a different scale than prior subtypes: not performance force (ensemble, vocal, groove) or creative force (tectonic), but transmission force — the steady current that carries a tradition from one generation to the next. The eleven E=4 subtypes: ensemble-kinetic (21), collective-kinetic (22), vocal-kinetic (23), dynastic-kinetic (24), congregational-kinetic (25), metamorphic-kinetic (26), incubator-kinetic (27), ambient-kinetic (28), groove-kinetic (29), tectonic-kinetic (30), relay-kinetic (31). The same note struck again in two different instruments.

## Part A: Brenda Lee Eager — The Mellow Undertow

- **Brenda Lee Eager — from Lower Peach Tree to the mellow undertow.** Born August 8, 1947, in Mobile, Alabama, and raised in Lower Peach Tree in rural Wilcox County in the Black Belt region. The town had no music industry; it had churches. Eager began singing in those churches as a child, absorbing the same gospel tradition that Ray Charles (degree 30) had absorbed in the Black churches of Greenville, Florida, seventeen years earlier. By tenth grade she was leading her own vocal group. At seventeen she sang professionally at the Kings Club in Prichard, Alabama. By the late 1960s she had relocated to Chicago, drawn by the same gravitational pull that had attracted generations of Black Southern musicians — the promise of a recording industry, a larger stage, and a community of gospel-trained vocalists making records. The engine follows a generational figure with a working professional. This is not a problem. It is a feature. The 360-degree rotation cannot be all landmarks; it needs the relay runners as much as it needs the sprinters.
- **Jerry Butler and the Mercury Records years — "The Iceman" meets the warm voice.** By 1971 Eager had become the lead singer of Peaches, Jerry Butler's female backup vocal group. Jerry Butler — born December 8, 1939, in Sunflower, Mississippi, raised in Chicago — was a foundational figure of Chicago soul. He co-founded the Impressions with Curtis Mayfield, recorded "For Your Precious Love" (1958), and earned his nickname "The Iceman" from Philadelphia disc jockey Georgie Woods for his cool, controlled, emotionally precise vocal delivery. Butler died February 2, 2025, at age 85. The Butler-Eager vocal pairing worked because their two vocal temperatures created productive tension: ice and warmth, restraint and openness, the question and the answer. The Chicago soul duet form places two distinct temperatures in conversation and lets the contrast carry the emotional weight.

- **The waxwing pair — third same-genus consecutive pair, Cedar (31) + Bohemian (32).** Three same-genus pairs now in the engine, each exploring a different contrast axis: *Catharus* (18-19, spiral direction — ascending vs. descending), *Tachycineta* (27-28, ecological niche — cliff specialist vs. field generalist), *Bombycilla* (31-32, geographic range — temperate resident vs. boreal irruptive). The quintet-to-pair compression is structural: after the most expansive family exploration in engine history (five in five), the engine immediately delivers the most compact (two in two). A family can be explored through five species across four genera (Hirundinidae) or through two species in one genus (Bombycillidae). Both are valid.
- **The high thin see-see-see — 6-10 kHz narrow register, sibilant category, contact call not song.** The Cedar Waxwing's primary vocalization is a high-pitched, thin, sibilant trill — typically transcribed as see-see-see or sreee. Fundamental frequency 6.0-10.0 kHz (higher than any swallow). Peak energy 7.0-9.0 kHz (nearly an octave above the Barn Swallow's 3.0-6.0 kHz peak). Call duration 0.3-0.8 seconds (an order of magnitude shorter than the Barn Swallow's 3-8 seconds). Timbre: thin, glassy, metallic, sibilant. A single Cedar Waxwing sounds thin and lost. A flock calling together sounds like the tree itself is vibrating.
- **Sibilant — the fourth timbral category.** After tonal (degrees 0-25), liquid (degrees 26-28, 30), and rough (degree 29), the sibilant category opens at degree 31. Thin, high, crystalline, glassy — the Cedar Waxwing's see-see-see. The shift from degree 30 to degree 31 is one of the sharpest timbral transitions in the engine: from rich, complex, sustained melodic warbling (liquid) to thin, high, repetitive sibilance. From the river to the wire. From water to glass. The palette grows.
- **The whisper — 32nd acoustic element.** "Whisper" captures the Cedar Waxwing on every dimension. The sound is a whisper (thin, high, quiet individually, shimmering collectively). The plumage is whisper-smooth (silk-tailed, gradient-soft). The ecology is a whisper (nomadic, appearing and disappearing with the fruit). The vocalization is a whisper (no song, just a contact call — exactly enough, nothing more). After the Barn Swallow's synthesis — the loudest, longest, most complex swallow song — the Cedar Waxwing whispers. The engine does not follow a shout with another shout. It follows a shout with a whisper. And the whisper carries.
- **Fruiting trees — first food-defined ecosystem.** "Fruiting trees" is the first ecosystem in the engine defined by food source rather than habitat structure. The Cedar Waxwing's "place" changes with the seasons and with crop success. Key food plants in the PNW: Pacific madrone (*Arbutus menziesii*), mountain ash (*Sorbus*), serviceberry (*Amelanchier*), hawthorn (*Crataegus*), holly (*Ilex*), crabapple (*Malus*), Oregon grape (*Mahonia*). Fruit comprises approximately 84% of the diet during non-breeding season. Several hundred berries per bird per day. Cedar Waxwings are among the most important seed dispersers in North American ecosystems.
- **Ethanol intoxication — fermented berry mortality, a cost of frugivory.** When late-season berries ferment on the branch (crabapples, mountain ash, hawthorn), they can accumulate ethanol concentrations of 1-5%. Cedar Waxwings, consuming large quantities rapidly, can become visibly intoxicated. Necropsy studies have found liver damage consistent with acute ethanol toxicity. The Cedar Waxwing produces alcohol dehydrogenase more efficiently than many passerines, but the capacity has limits. When fermented berry availability coincides with cold weather, intoxication risk increases. A species that eats hundreds of berries per day bears a cost that generalist feeders avoid.
- **The wax tips — modified keratin, 200+ years of debate, diet-driven plumage change.** The "waxwing" in the common name refers to small, brilliantly red, waxy tips on the secondary flight feathers — not pigmented barbs but flattened extensions of the feather shaft coated with carotenoid pigment (astaxanthin). Tips increase with age, functioning as an age signal; studies show assortative mating by wax tip count. Orange wax tips have appeared since the late 1960s, coinciding with non-native honeysuckle (*Lonicera*) introduction — rhodoxanthin from honeysuckle berries replaces astaxanthin, literally recoloring a native bird.
- **Vocal sufficiency — a passerine without song, the see-see-see is exactly enough.** The Cedar Waxwing is among the few passerines that lack true territorial song. Its primary vocalization is innate, not learned. No geographic dialects. No age-related complexity increase. No breeding-season elaboration. The absence of song is tied to its nomadic, non-territorial ecology: a bird that does not defend a territory does not need a territorial song. After the engine explored vocal complexity (Barn Swallow's elaborate warble), vocal simplicity (Bushtit's thin seep), and vocal absence (Great Blue Heron's silence), the Cedar Waxwing adds a fourth category: vocal sufficiency.
- **Physical description — sleek, crested, silk-tailed, the most aesthetically unified bird in North America.** Medium-small passerine (15-18 cm, 30-36 g). Prominent pointed crest giving a swept-back aerodynamic profile. Black mask from bill base through eye, bordered above by thin white line. Warm brown body fading to gray on wings and tail — uniquely smooth and silky, individual feathers barely distinguishable. Pale yellow belly wash. Yellow terminal tail band (sometimes orange from diet). Red (or orange) waxy secondary feather tips.
- **Conservation — stable to slightly increasing, favored by non-native plantings and edge habitat.** Unlike the declining Barn Swallow, the Cedar Waxwing is stable or slightly increasing (0.2-0.5% per year since the 1960s). Favored by non-native fruit-bearing ornamental plantings, forest fragmentation creating edge habitat with fruiting shrubs, warming temperatures extending growing seasons, and broad geographic range buffering local declines. Common year-round resident in the Puget Sound region. Peak flock sizes in Seattle: 200-400 individuals. A conservation success story within a broader picture of avian decline.
- **The energy undecet — eleventh consecutive E=4 (degrees 21-31), the ground bass holds.** The E=4 plateau has now survived a family transition (Hirundinidae to Bombycillidae), a timbral category change (liquid to sibilant), an ecosystem change (farmland to fruiting trees), a seasonal change (summer to resident), and a social structure change (loosely colonial to nomadic flock). E=4 is the one thing that does not change. The only constants across eleven degrees are Energy=4 and Order=Passeriformes. Breaking it will be seismic.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **Post-landmark sequencing is a structural feature, not a problem.** The engine follows a generational figure (Ray Charles) with a working professional (Brenda Lee Eager). The contrast is productive: it demonstrates how traditions propagate through professionals, not just through geniuses. The 360-degree rotation needs both the sprinters and the relay runners.
- **The Relay pattern is genuinely new.** The first non-residential artist-city pattern expands the taxonomy's scope from geographic proximity to musical connection. This opens a category that may recur — other artists in the engine may have similar relay connections through direct musical collaboration with Seattle-connected artists.
- **The Jerry Butler partnership is well-documented.** "Ain't Understanding Mellow" has complete chart data, RIAA certification records, and discographic documentation. Butler's recent death (February 2025) generated extensive obituary coverage that contextualized the partnership.
- **The new family introduction is a genuine structural event.** Bombycillidae adds a 17th family and opens new taxonomic territory after five degrees inside Hirundinidae. New phylogenetic context, new ecological package, new timbral space.
- **The sibilant timbral category enriches the palette.** After thirty degrees of tonal, liquid, and rough, the engine needed a fourth color. The Cedar Waxwing provides it. Genuinely different from anything the engine has produced.
- **Frugivory as ecosystem is a conceptual advance.** Defining an ecosystem by food source rather than physical habitat introduces nomadism, phenological tracking, and resource-driven distribution. The engine's ecological vocabulary has expanded.
- **Vocal sufficiency is a valuable lesson.** After the Barn Swallow's elaborate warble, the waxwing's simple contact call is a corrective: sometimes less is more. The engine's acoustic theory now includes the concept of "enough."
- **The Prince connection is verifiable.** "Somebody's Somebody" documented through Prince-focused sites, SecondHandSongs, Wikipedia. Co-writing credit and double-platinum certification confirmed.
- **The hip-hop sampling trajectory is fully documented.** WhoSampled provides complete sample chains from "When I'm With You" to Ghostface Killah and Snoop Dogg.

### What Could Be Better

- **CRITICAL: No marine species in 31 degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound."** Not a single species from Puget Sound itself — no gull, no loon, no grebe, no cormorant, no alcid. Thirty-one degrees overdue. After two consecutive waxwings (31-32), the marine gap will reach 33 degrees. The project is named for the Sound. The water must speak.

- **CRITICAL: Eleven consecutive E=4 (degrees 21-31).** Over one-third of all degrees at one energy level. The dynamic range is approaching structural collapse. If degree 32 stays E=4, the plateau reaches twelve. The ground bass must break.
- **CRITICAL: No corvid in 31 degrees.** Steller's Jay, American Crow, Common Raven — the most intelligent, acoustically complex PNW birds are absent.
- **CRITICAL: No raptor in 31 degrees.** Bald Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, Osprey — top predators are absent.
- **Seattle connection is musical, not geographic.** Eager's placement is engine-derived, not biographically confirmed. No specific documentation of Seattle residency or performance. Documented honestly as ENGINE PLACEMENT with the Relay pattern addressing the non-residential connection directly.

## Lessons Learned

- **The engine needs relay runners as much as sprinters.** The 360-degree rotation documents the full ecosystem of a musical tradition — not just the peaks but the connective tissue between them. Eager's degree proves that the post-landmark position is structurally valuable. Every landmark is followed by a continuation; the continuation is not a lesser thing.
- **Non-residential artist-city connections are documentable and meaningful.** The Relay pattern demonstrates that a city's musical influence propagates beyond its borders through direct musical collaboration. The tradition travels through people, not geography. Future degrees will encounter similar relay links and the vocabulary now exists to name them.
- **The mellow undertow follows the rough brrit as recovery follows rupture.** The three-element sequence (liquid brook, rough brrit, mellow undertow) maps flow-rupture-recovery — a dramatic arc that mirrors the artist progression from steady professional (Boogaloo Joe Jones) to genre inventor (Ray Charles) to tradition-carrier (Brenda Lee Eager). The acoustic and biographical narratives move in parallel.
- **Family transitions are timbral resets.** Moving from Hirundinidae to Bombycillidae reset the entire acoustic palette — from liquid to sibilant, from complex warble to thin whisper, from river to wire. New families bring new sounds. Track timbral transitions at every family introduction going forward.
- **Frugivory changes how the engine thinks about habitat.** Resource-defined ecosystems (fruiting trees) require different documentation than structure-defined ecosystems (old-growth, farmland). The habitat is a phenological event, not a geological feature. This expands the engine's ecological vocabulary permanently, and subsequent frugivore and nectarivore entries will inherit the framework.
- **Vocal sufficiency is adaptive, not a limitation.** Nomadic frugivores that do not defend territory do not need territorial song. The Cedar Waxwing's simple contact call is not a limitation — it is an optimization. The engine's acoustic theory now includes the concept of "exactly enough," which reframes the absence of song from privation to precision.
- **The energy plateau is independent of everything else.** E=4 has survived family transitions, timbral shifts, ecosystem changes, seasonal changes, and social structure changes. It is the one parameter that does not respond to structural boundaries. Breaking it will require deliberate attention at the degree-selection level, not hoping the breakdown emerges as a byproduct.
- **Gender visibility tracks backup-singer and duet-partner credits.** Eager's most successful work is catalogued under Butler's and Prince's names. Making her a degree makes visible what the industry's crediting practices obscured. Apply the same standard to future degrees — look below the single-name attribution for the women whose vocal labor made the record possible.
- **Three same-genus pairs is now a pattern, not a coincidence.** *Catharus* (18-19), *Tachycineta* (27-28), and *Bombycilla* (31-32) each explore a different contrast axis within a single genus. Same-genus pairs are a reliable structural tool for exploring within-genus variation without consuming five degrees of single-family investment.
- **Ecosystem vocabulary has two orthogonal axes.** "Fruiting trees" introduces the resource axis; "old-growth forest" lives on the structure axis. Future ecosystems will fall on one, the other, or both, and the engine's habitat documentation should name which axis dominates.
