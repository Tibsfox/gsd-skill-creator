### Part A: Brenda Lee Eager -- The Mellow Undertow (THE RELAY, Chicago Soul Duet Form, Relay-Kinetic E=4 Subtype, Mercury Records Label Triad, R&B/Soul Genre Stage 13, Songwriter Thread, Hip-Hop Sampling Pipeline, Sixth Woman in the Engine)

Full deep research covering Brenda Lee Eager as the engine's first tradition-carrier after a generational figure, the Relay as the 32nd artist-city pattern, the mellow undertow as the 32nd acoustic element, the Chicago soul duet form as a new theory node, Mercury Records completing the Prestige-Atlantic-Mercury label triad, R&B/Soul as the first Jazz-free compound genre, the songwriter thread connecting Franklin-Knight-Prince-Womack-Staples, and the hip-hop sampling pipeline from 1970s soul to 1990s production:

- **BRENDA LEE EAGER -- FROM LOWER PEACH TREE TO THE MELLOW UNDERTOW, Born August 8, 1947, Mobile, Alabama, Raised in Rural Wilcox County, Church-Trained Gospel Voice, The Relay Runner After the Sprinter:** Brenda Lee Eager was born in Mobile, Alabama, and raised in Lower Peach Tree, Alabama -- a small rural community on the banks of the Alabama River in the Black Belt region. The town had no music industry. It had churches. Eager began singing in those churches as a child, absorbing the same gospel tradition that Ray Charles (degree 30) had absorbed in the Black churches of Greenville, Florida, seventeen years earlier. She was a direct inheritor of the tradition Charles formalized. By tenth grade she was leading her own vocal group. At seventeen she sang professionally at the Kings Club in Prichard, Alabama. By the late 1960s she had relocated to Chicago, drawn by the same gravitational pull that had attracted generations of Black Southern musicians: the promise of a recording industry, a larger stage, and a community of gospel-trained vocalists making records. The engine follows a generational figure with a working professional. This is not a problem. It is a feature. The 360-degree rotation cannot be all landmarks. It needs the relay runners as much as it needs the sprinters. Eager's degree documents how musical traditions propagate: not through geniuses alone but through the professionals who carry them forward, one recording session, one duet, one songwriting credit, one choir rehearsal at a time.

- **JERRY BUTLER AND THE MERCURY RECORDS YEARS -- "The Iceman" Meets the Warm Voice, Peaches Backup Vocal Group, The Temperature-Differential Dialogue:** By 1971, Eager had become the lead singer of Peaches, Jerry Butler's female backup vocal group. Jerry Butler -- born December 8, 1939, in Sunflower, Mississippi, raised in Chicago -- was a foundational figure of Chicago soul. He co-founded the Impressions with Curtis Mayfield, recorded "For Your Precious Love" (1958), and earned his nickname "The Iceman" from Philadelphia disc jockey Georgie Woods for his cool, controlled, emotionally precise vocal delivery. Butler died February 2, 2025, at age 85. Eager's voice -- warm, controlled, gospel-trained -- distinguished her from the other Peaches members and caught Butler's attention as a duet partner. The Butler-Eager vocal pairing worked because their two vocal temperatures created productive tension: ice and warmth, restraint and openness, the question and the answer. This is the defining mechanism of the Chicago soul duet form -- distinct from the Memphis soul duet (both voices hot and raw) and the Motown duet (both voices polished into unified sheen). The Chicago soul duet places two distinct temperatures in conversation and lets the contrast carry the emotional weight. Butler and Eager are the exemplary case.

- **"AIN'T UNDERSTANDING MELLOW" -- Gold Single, No. 3 R&B, No. 21 Hot 100, Over One Million Copies Sold, The Record That Places Eager in the Engine:** Released late 1971 on Mercury Records, "Ain't Understanding Mellow" reached No. 3 on the Billboard R&B chart and No. 21 on the Hot 100 in early 1972. RIAA Gold certification in April 1972. Over one million copies sold. Written by Homer Talbert and Herscholt Polk, arranged by James Mack, produced by Gerald Sims and Jerry Butler. The song's structure is a male-female dialogue -- a conversation between two lovers navigating the gap between emotional truth and mutual comprehension. Butler's Iceman cool is set against Eager's warmer, more emotionally open delivery, and the tension between the two vocal temperatures IS the song. The arrangement -- strings, horns, rhythm section -- is classic early-1970s Chicago soul: sophisticated enough to be lush, rhythmically propelled enough to be danceable, melodically clear enough to lodge in memory. This is not the operatic drama of Gaye and Terrell. It is not the raw Memphis heat of Redding and Thomas. It is Chicago soul duet at its most polished: two voices in dialogue, neither overwhelming the other. Eager is not a backing vocalist promoted to occasional lead lines -- she is a full partner with her own melodic space, her own emotional register, her own vocal identity. The gold certification is significant: in 1972, gold required one million copies in a market dominated by Motown (Detroit), Philadelphia International (Philadelphia), and Stax (Memphis). Chicago soul was the fourth pole. "Ain't Understanding Mellow" was one of its commercial peaks.

- **THE LOVE WE HAVE, THE LOVE WE HAD -- Full Duet Album (1973), Mercury Records, Memphis and Chicago Studios, Conceptual Structure:** Butler and Eager released a full duet album on Mercury Records in 1973. Recorded at Mark XVI Recording Studio in Memphis and Universal Recording Studio in Chicago, mixed at Paragon Recording Studio in Chicago. The concept was structural: one side ("The Love We Have") explored ongoing romance, the other ("The Love We Had") explored its aftermath. Compositions by Terry Callier (Chicago folk-soul artist) and Billy Butler (Jerry's brother). Tracks included "Lean On Me," "As the Seasons Change," "I Like Your Lovin'," "Can't Understand It," "How Long Will It Last," and "Were We Lovers, Were We Friends." Additional charting duets: "(They Long to Be) Close to You" (No. 6 R&B, No. 91 Hot 100 -- a Bacharach-David cover recast through a soul lens), "Can't Understand It" (R&B chart entry), "Power of Love" (R&B chart entry).

- **SOLO CAREER -- Songwriter, Session Singer, Theatrical Performer, Choral Director, The Multifaceted Professional:** After the Butler partnership wound down in the mid-1970s, Eager pursued a career that never matched the chart success of "Ain't Understanding Mellow" but demonstrated a breadth of musical engagement that the hit-single narrative cannot capture. Solo singles: "Good Old Fashioned Lovin'" (Playboy Records, 1975, No. 71 R&B), "When I'm With You" / "Let Me Be" (1974, produced by Larry Mizell -- did not chart significantly but became a sought-after Northern Soul and rare groove collector's item, later sampled by Ghostface Killah and Snoop Dogg), "Watch My Body Talk" (Private-I Records, 1984, minor R&B chart entry). Musical theatre: *The Message is in the Music* (alongside Della Reese), *Wild Woman Blues* (European tour with Maine Weldon), *Grace* (2010, one-woman show based on Eager's life, premiered in Los Angeles). Choral direction: since 2006, Eager has directed The Heaven on Earth (T.H.E.) Choir at the City of Angels Church in Culver City, California. The arc from Lower Peach Tree churches to Culver City choir passes through Chicago, Mercury Records, gold singles, Prince compositions, and European theatre tours -- and it ends where it began: in a church, leading voices.

- **THE SONGWRITER THREAD -- Franklin, Knight, Staples, Prince, Womack, Brown, Richard, Peaches & Herb:** Eager's most underappreciated contribution is her songwriting. The arc from writing songs recorded by Bobby Womack and Aretha Franklin to co-writing a double-platinum Prince single represents a range few songwriters achieve. Key credits: Prince -- "Somebody's Somebody" (1996, *Emancipation* album, co-written with Prince and Hilliard Wilson, double-platinum certification); Aretha Franklin -- "Just to Be Loved"; Mavis Staples -- "More Than Beautiful"; Gladys Knight -- "Friends"; Bobby Womack -- songs recorded (specific titles NEEDS VERIFICATION); Shirley Brown -- three songs; Cliff Richard and Peaches & Herb -- songs penned (specific titles NEEDS VERIFICATION). The Prince connection is particularly notable: "Somebody's Somebody" appeared on *Emancipation*, Prince's triple-disc artistic independence statement after his battle with Warner Bros. That Prince chose a song co-written by Eager suggests musical trust transcending the standard songwriter-artist relationship. Eager's songwriting connects her to the tradition of Black women songwriters whose work for other artists overshadows their own performing careers -- women whose names appear in small print on liner notes rather than on marquees.

- **BACKGROUND VOCAL WORK -- Ray Charles, Diana Ross, Stevie Wonder, Graham Nash, Mavis Staples, Carl Anderson:** Eager worked as a background singer for Ray Charles (degree 30), Mavis Staples, Diana Ross, Stevie Wonder, Graham Nash, and Carl Anderson (including his "Heavy Weather" in 1996). The Ray Charles connection is the most structurally significant: Eager sang backup for the artist who invented the gospel-secular fusion that made her entire career possible. Degree 31 sang for degree 30. The most direct consecutive-degree musical link in the engine since the founding cluster.

- **THE HIP-HOP SAMPLING PIPELINE -- "When I'm With You" (1974) to Ghostface Killah and Snoop Dogg:** "When I'm With You," produced by Larry Mizell, is the Eager recording with the longest cultural afterlife. Ghostface Killah featuring M.O.P. sampled it for "Sample 420." Snoop Dogg featuring Big Bub sampled it for "Love Around the World." This trajectory -- 1970s soul single to hip-hop sample source -- is one of the defining cultural pathways of the late 20th century. The warmth, texture, and melodic clarity of 1970s soul production became the raw material for hip-hop production in the 1990s and 2000s. Eager's voice, preserved on vinyl, entered a new genre decades after the original recording.

- **THE RELAY -- THIRTY-SECOND ARTIST-CITY PATTERN, First Non-Residential Connection, Musical Collaboration as Geographic Proxy:** The Relay is the engine's first instance of a non-residential artist-city relationship documented through direct musical collaboration. Eager did not live in Seattle. No specific documentation confirms a Seattle residency, performance, or recording session. The connection is musical: she sang backup for Ray Charles -- the artist whose three years in Seattle's Central District (1947-1950) produced the seeds of soul music. Eager's voice is literally on Ray Charles recordings. She carried his vocal tradition forward through harmony, counterpoint, and the rhythmic support that allowed his lead voice to work. She carried the tradition further through her songwriting for Franklin, Knight, Prince, Womack, and Staples -- all artists in the soul lineage Charles initiated -- and through her choral direction, returning the tradition to its gospel roots. The relay mechanism: city to artist to tradition to next artist. Distinct from Export (degree 0 -- the artist leaves the city), The Incubation (degree 30 -- the city transforms the artist), The Catalog (degree 29 -- the relationship is mediated through recordings as a listener), and Draw (degree 1 -- the artist is attracted to the city). The Relay artist is attracted to the tradition, not the geography.

- **THE MELLOW UNDERTOW -- THIRTY-SECOND ACOUSTIC ELEMENT, Named for "Ain't Understanding Mellow," Post-Brrit Recovery, Warm Vocal Current Beneath the Lead:** The mellow undertow is the warm, steady, slightly pulling vocal current that runs beneath the lead melody, exerting emotional gravity without ever breaking the surface. In the Butler-Eager duets, the undertow is literal: Eager's voice runs beneath Butler's Iceman lead, providing harmonic warmth, melodic counterpoint, and the emotional pull that gives the cool surface its depth. Without the undertow, Butler's coolness would be merely cold. With it, the coolness becomes emotional restraint -- the listener feels the warmth underneath. The mellow undertow follows the rough brrit (degree 30) in the acoustic progression: after the eruption (Charles's gospel-drenched intensity), the pull (Eager's warm, steady, professional-grade vocal support). The brrit creates; the undertow carries. The three-element sequence liquid brook (29) to rough brrit (30) to mellow undertow (31) maps three sonic states in succession: flow, rupture, and recovery -- the most dramatic three-element sequence since the liquid triad itself.

- **R&B/SOUL -- GENRE STAGE 13, Jazz Fully Absent for the First Time in a Compound Genre:** R&B/Soul at degree 31 is the first compound genre in the engine that contains no Jazz component. Every prior compound genre included Jazz as either primary or secondary element. At degree 31, Jazz is fully absent. Both R&B and Soul are descendants of the gospel-secular fusion that Charles created. The sequence R&B/Jazz (30) to R&B/Soul (31) maps a handoff: Jazz passes the baton to Soul as the modifier. R&B remains primary. The genre structure mirrors the artist-city pattern: The Incubation (30) passed to The Relay (31); R&B/Jazz passed to R&B/Soul. Both are relay transitions.

- **MERCURY RECORDS -- LABEL TRIAD COMPLETE (Prestige-Atlantic-Mercury, Degrees 29-30-31), Three Consecutive Major Labels, Chicago Institution:** Mercury Records (founded 1945 in Chicago by Irving Green, Berle Adams, Arthur Talmadge, Ray Greenberg) is the oldest of the three-label triad. Headquarters at One IBM Plaza, label design featuring Marina City, the Hancock Center. Roster: Butler, Chandler, the Dells, Ohio Players. The triad maps the institutional infrastructure of mid-century American Black music: jazz (Prestige), the genre-crossing soul tradition (Atlantic), and the Chicago soul specialization (Mercury). Mercury is the only one headquartered outside New York.

### Part B: Cedar Waxwing -- The Whisper (BOMBYCILLIDAE ENTERS THE ENGINE, Waxwing Pair Begins, Sibilant Timbral Category, First Fruit-Defined Ecosystem, Vocal Sufficiency, Energy Undecet)

Full deep research covering the Cedar Waxwing as the engine's first Bombycillidae, the 17th family, the opening voice of the waxwing pair (degrees 31-32), the introduction of the sibilant timbral category as the fourth color on the engine's acoustic palette, the first ecosystem defined by food source rather than habitat structure, vocal sufficiency as a new concept in the engine's acoustic theory, and the energy plateau extending to eleven consecutive E=4:

- **BOMBYCILLA CEDRORUM -- "SILK-TAIL OF THE CEDARS," Vieillot 1808, First Genus Named for Tactile Quality, 17th Family, Only 3 Species Worldwide:** The Cedar Waxwing (*Bombycilla cedrorum*) belongs to family Bombycillidae -- the engine's 17th family and the first entirely new family since Hirundinidae entered at degree 26. *Bombycilla* derives from Latin *bombyx* ("silk" or "silkworm") and Modern Latin *-cilla* ("tail"), yielding "silk-tail" -- a reference to the smooth, sleek plumage. *Cedrorum* is Latin genitive plural meaning "of the cedars," referring to the eastern red cedar (*Juniperus virginiana*) whose blue-gray berries are a critical winter food source. The full binomial translates to "silk-tail of the cedars" -- a name built from texture (silk), anatomy (tail), and diet (cedar berries). This is the first genus in the engine named for tactile quality. Where the swallow genera named themselves by myth (*Progne*), behavior (*Tachycineta*), anatomy (*Stelgidopteryx*), and identity (*Hirundo*), the waxwing genus names itself by texture -- what the feathers feel like, not what the bird does. The naming strategy is aesthetic, not functional. Bombycillidae contains only three species worldwide: Cedar Waxwing, Bohemian Waxwing (*B. garrulus*), and Japanese Waxwing (*B. japonica*). The engine will hold two of the three -- 66.7% proportional coverage, the highest of any family. Molecular phylogenetics places Bombycillidae in superfamily Bombycilloidea, related to silky-flycatchers (Ptiliogonatidae) and the Palmchat (Dulidae). None of these relatives are PNW species. Bombycillidae stands phylogenetically isolated in the engine's taxonomic graph.

- **THE WAXWING PAIR -- Third Same-Genus Consecutive Pair, Cedar (31) + Bohemian (32), Geographic Range Contrast Axis:** The Cedar Waxwing begins a waxwing pair that mirrors the Tachycineta pair at degrees 27-28. Three same-genus pairs now in the engine, each exploring a different contrast axis: Catharus (18-19, spiral direction -- ascending vs. descending), Tachycineta (27-28, ecological niche -- cliff specialist vs. field generalist), Bombycilla (31-32, geographic range -- temperate resident vs. boreal irruptive). The quintet-to-pair compression is structural: after the most expansive family exploration in engine history (5 in 5), the engine immediately delivers the most compact (2 in 2). A family can be explored through five species across four genera (Hirundinidae) or through two species in one genus (Bombycillidae). The quintet maps breadth; the pair maps within-genus variation. Both are valid, both are informative.

- **THE HIGH THIN SEE-SEE-SEE -- A New Timbral Territory, Contact Call Not Song, 6-10 kHz High-Narrow Register, Sibilant Category:** The Cedar Waxwing's primary vocalization is a high-pitched, thin, sibilant trill -- typically transcribed as *see-see-see* or *sreee*. It is not a song in the traditional oscine sense. The call's acoustic properties: fundamental frequency 6.0-10.0 kHz (higher than any swallow), peak energy 7.0-9.0 kHz (nearly an octave above the Barn Swallow's 3.0-6.0 kHz peak), call duration 0.3-0.8 seconds (an order of magnitude shorter than the Barn Swallow's 3-8 seconds), frequency modulation rate approximately 15-30 Hz producing a perceived trill, spectrally simple narrow-bandwidth tonal whisper with subtle internal modulation. Timbre: thin, glassy, metallic, sibilant -- the quality of a fine wire vibrating at the edge of audibility. Some observers describe it as "cedary" -- a dry, thin, slightly buzzy whistle evoking the texture of cedar wood itself. Amplitude: low to moderate; Energy 4 reflects the persistence and frequency of calling rather than volume. A feeding flock in a mountain ash tree produces a shimmering curtain of thin calls, each individual adding its see-see-see to the collective texture. A single Cedar Waxwing sounds thin and lost. A flock calling together sounds like the tree itself is vibrating.

- **SIBILANT -- THE FOURTH TIMBRAL CATEGORY, After Tonal (0-25), Liquid (26-28, 30), Rough (29):** The engine's timbral palette has expanded from three categories to four. Tonal (degrees 0-25): clear pitch, harmonic structure, melodic -- from the Great Blue Heron's silence through the American Robin's caroling. Liquid (degrees 26-28, 30): flowing, water-like, continuous modulation -- introduced by the Purple Martin. Rough (degree 29): broadband, buzzy, grating -- the Rough-winged Swallow's brrit. Sibilant (degree 31): thin, high, crystalline, glassy -- the Cedar Waxwing's see-see-see. The shift from degree 30 to degree 31 is one of the sharpest timbral transitions in the engine: from rich, complex, sustained melodic warbling (liquid) to thin, high, repetitive sibilance. From the river to the wire. From water to glass. The palette grows.

- **THE WHISPER -- 32ND ACOUSTIC ELEMENT, Named for the Cedar Waxwing's Essential Quality on Every Axis:** "Whisper" captures the Cedar Waxwing on every dimension. The sound is a whisper (thin, high, quiet individually, shimmering collectively). The plumage is whisper-smooth (silk-tailed, gradient-soft, barely distinguishable feathers). The ecology is a whisper (nomadic, appearing and disappearing with the fruit, here today, gone tomorrow). The vocalization is a whisper (no song, just a contact call -- exactly enough, nothing more). After the Barn Swallow's synthesis -- the loudest, longest, most complex swallow song, the archetype that absorbed everything -- the Cedar Waxwing whispers. The engine does not follow a shout with another shout. It follows a shout with a whisper. And the whisper carries.

- **FRUITING TREES -- FIRST FOOD-DEFINED ECOSYSTEM, Phenological Habitat, Nomadism, Seed Dispersal Mutualism:** "Fruiting trees" is the first ecosystem in the engine defined by food source rather than habitat structure. Previous ecosystems described places (estuary, old-growth, farmland). This ecosystem describes a resource (fruit). The Cedar Waxwing's "place" changes with the seasons and with crop success. Its habitat is mobile, ephemeral, unpredictable. Where the berries are, the waxwings are. Key food plants in the PNW: Pacific madrone (*Arbutus menziesii*), mountain ash (*Sorbus*), serviceberry (*Amelanchier*), hawthorn (*Crataegus*), holly (*Ilex*), crabapple (*Malus*), Oregon grape (*Mahonia*). Fruit comprises approximately 84% of the diet during non-breeding season. The digestive tract processes berries in as little as 16-45 minutes. Several hundred berries per bird per day. Cedar Waxwings are among the most important seed dispersers in North American ecosystems -- the bird-fruit mutualism is bidirectional: plant provides food, bird provides seed transport.

- **ETHANOL INTOXICATION -- Fermented Berry Mortality, A Cost of Frugivory:** When late-season berries ferment on the branch (crabapples, mountain ash, hawthorn), they can accumulate ethanol concentrations of 1-5%. Cedar Waxwings, consuming large quantities rapidly, can become visibly intoxicated -- disorientation, impaired flight, loss of coordination. Necropsy studies have found liver damage consistent with acute ethanol toxicity. The Cedar Waxwing has some physiological capacity to metabolize ethanol -- its liver produces alcohol dehydrogenase more efficiently than many passerines. But the capacity has limits. When fermented berry availability coincides with cold weather (which slows metabolism), intoxication risk increases. A species that eats hundreds of berries per day from a potentially fermented source bears a cost that generalist feeders avoid. Evolution mitigating the cost of specialization.

- **THE WAX TIPS -- Red Secondary Feather Tips, Modified Keratin, 200+ Years of Debate, Diet-Driven Plumage Change:** The "waxwing" in the common name refers to small, brilliantly red, waxy tips on the secondary flight feathers -- not pigmented barbs but flattened extensions of the feather shaft coated with carotenoid pigment (astaxanthin). Tips increase with age, functioning as an age signal; studies show assortative mating by wax tip count. Orange wax tips have appeared since the late 1960s, coinciding with non-native honeysuckle (*Lonicera*) introduction -- rhodoxanthin from honeysuckle berries replaces astaxanthin, literally recoloring a native bird. An introduced plant changes a native bird's appearance. The Cedar Waxwing wears its diet on its wings.

- **VOCAL SUFFICIENCY -- A Passerine Without Song, The See-See-See Is Exactly Enough:** The Cedar Waxwing is among the few passerines that lack true territorial song. Its primary vocalization is innate, not learned. No geographic dialects. No age-related complexity increase. No breeding-season elaboration. The absence of song is tied to its nomadic, non-territorial ecology: a bird that does not defend a territory does not need a territorial song. The see-see-see serves the functions that matter for a nomadic flocking species: flock cohesion, feeding coordination, predator alarm. After the engine explored vocal complexity (Barn Swallow's elaborate warble), vocal simplicity (Bushtit's thin seep), and vocal absence (Great Blue Heron's silence), the Cedar Waxwing adds a fourth category: vocal sufficiency. The sound does what it needs to and nothing else. In a landscape where warblers, thrushes, and swallows pour out complex songs, the Cedar Waxwing whispers. And the whisper is enough.

- **PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION -- Sleek, Crested, Silk-Tailed, The Most Aesthetically Unified Bird in North America:** Medium-small passerine (15-18 cm, 30-36 g). Prominent pointed crest giving a swept-back aerodynamic profile. Black mask from bill base through eye, bordered above by thin white line. Warm brown body fading to gray on wings and tail -- uniquely smooth and silky, individual feathers barely distinguishable. Pale yellow belly wash. Yellow terminal tail band (sometimes orange from diet). Red (or orange) waxy secondary feather tips. The overall impression: a bird designed by a graphic artist. Smooth gradients, clean lines, no harsh boundaries. Where the Barn Swallow had distinct color blocks, the Cedar Waxwing has continuous gradients with precise accent marks placed like typographic elements.

- **CONSERVATION -- Stable to Slightly Increasing, Favored by Non-Native Plantings and Edge Habitat:** Unlike the declining Barn Swallow, the Cedar Waxwing is stable or slightly increasing (0.2-0.5% per year since the 1960s). Favored by non-native fruit-bearing ornamental plantings, forest fragmentation creating edge habitat with fruiting shrubs, warming temperatures extending growing seasons, and broad geographic range buffering local declines. Common year-round resident in the Puget Sound region. Peak flock sizes in Seattle: 200-400 individuals. One of the latest-nesting PNW passerines, timing breeding to peak summer fruit availability. A conservation success story within a broader picture of avian decline.
