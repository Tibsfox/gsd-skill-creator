## Surprises

- **A non-sun-synchronous orbit lets GPM sample the precipitation across all local times.** Unlike the sun-synchronous orbits of its axis siblings (PACE, SWOT, SMAP), GPM flies a non-sun-synchronous orbit at roughly 407 km with a ~65° inclination, so it crosses the equator at a slowly shifting local time and samples the precipitation through the full diurnal cycle — the morning rain and the afternoon storm alike — rather than always at the same Sun angle, a design choice that lets it map the timing of rain as well as its amount.
- **Two radar frequencies see the rain and the snow differently — and together resolve the storm in three dimensions.** GPM's Dual-frequency Precipitation Radar reads the same column at Ku band (~13.6 GHz) and Ka band (~35.5 GHz); because the two frequencies attenuate and scatter differently in rain and snow, comparing them constrains the drop-size distribution and resolves the precipitation in three dimensions — the first spaceborne dual-frequency precipitation radar, where TRMM's single Ku-band radar (1997) saw only the tropics in one frequency.
- **The Core Observatory is the standard the whole constellation is measured against.** The GPM Core Observatory's well-calibrated dual-frequency radar and microwave imager serve as the reference standard against which the international GPM constellation of partner satellites' microwave radiometers are inter-calibrated; the morphing algorithm then merges their intermittent overpasses into IMERG, a half-hourly ~0.1° global precipitation map — so a single well-calibrated observer turns a scattered fleet into one continuous picture of the falling water.
- **A rain beetle whose brief flight is keyed entirely to the first heavy rains.** The Rain Beetle (*Pleocoma crinita*), a Pacific Northwest scarab of the family Pleocomidae, has males that fly only during the first heavy autumn and winter rains, often in cold rain near the snow line, while the flightless females wait in deep burrows; short-lived and non-feeding as adults, the beetle's whole above-ground life is keyed to the falling rain: a mirror of GPM measuring the precipitation falling across the whole Earth from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only; a Washington / north-of-the-Columbia rain beetle distinct from the east-of-the-Cascades arid-zone *Pleocoma oregonensis*).
- **An epiphytic moss that revives with the autumn rains and the fog it drinks from the air.** The Hanging Moss (*Antitrichia curtipendula*), a pendulous epiphytic moss draping the branches of PNW oaks (especially Garry / Oregon white oak) and conifers, is poikilohydric — drying in the summer and reviving with the autumn rains and fog, living on the water the sky gives (ecological description only; a pendulous epiphytic moss distinct from the beard lichens *Usnea* / *Alectoria* it drapes alongside, its family placement unsettled and now often placed in its own family Antitrichiaceae) — bound with the throughline of the falling water, the same rain and fog whose global measurement GPM makes from orbit.
