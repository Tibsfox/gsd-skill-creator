## Mission Overview

GPM is the obs#4 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — the joint NASA/JAXA Earth-observing Core Observatory that circles Earth in a non-sun-synchronous orbit and measures the precipitation falling across the whole planet, the rain and the snow, with a dual-frequency radar that cuts down through a storm and a microwave imager that reads the falling water across a wide swath. ★ It is a joint mission of NASA and JAXA (the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency); the Core Observatory anchors an international constellation of partner satellites. The observer launched ~28 February 2014 on an H-IIA from Tanegashima Space Center, Japan, and settled into a non-sun-synchronous orbit at roughly 407 km with a ~65° inclination: an advanced precipitation Earth observer. The story is the launch from Tanegashima, the climb into the ~407 km, ~65° orbit that samples the tropics to the mid-latitudes across all local times, the DPR's Ku and Ka beams resolving the rain and snow in three dimensions, the GMI reading the falling water across a wide swath, the precipitation of the tropics and the mid-latitudes mapped, light rain and falling snow included, and the long ongoing watch that continues today. The science is central: the DPR provides the first spaceborne dual-frequency (Ku+Ka) precipitation radar — the Ku band (KuPR, ~13.6 GHz, 245 km swath, an updated TRMM precipitation radar) reading moderate to heavy rain and the Ka band (KaPR, ~35.5 GHz, 120 km swath, the new Ka addition) reading light rain and snow, the two together constraining the drop-size distribution; the GMI (a 13-channel conical-scanning radiometer from ~10 to ~183 GHz, ~885 km swath) extends the measurement to light rain and falling snow and to the mid-latitudes; and the Core Observatory serves as the reference standard unifying the international GPM constellation into merged global products (IMERG, half-hourly at ~0.1°). ★ The radar result is load-bearing: it is the first spaceborne DUAL-frequency precipitation radar — NOT "the first precipitation radar in space," since TRMM (1997, also joint NASA/JAXA) carried the first spaceborne precipitation radar, a SINGLE-frequency Ku-band PR confined to the tropics (±35°). ★ The coverage result is equally load-bearing: it is the global measurement of light rain and falling snow, extending precipitation measurement beyond TRMM's tropics to the mid-latitudes (precipitation observed to ~68° N/S; the orbit inclination is ~65°, distinct from the latitude band of the precipitation coverage). ★ The constellation result is load-bearing: the Core Observatory is the reference standard that unifies the international GPM constellation of partner satellites into merged global precipitation products (IMERG). ★★ GPM is STILL OPERATING (since 2014), so the catalog frames its launch and completed milestones in the PAST (GPM launched ~28 February 2014, reached its orbit, deployed and checked out its instruments, completed its commissioning phase, and began its global precipitation record) while its ongoing role is framed in the PRESENT (GPM orbits Earth, continues to measure the precipitation falling across the planet, anchors the international constellation, has returned a long record more than ten years deep, and remains operational). ## Surprises

- **A non-sun-synchronous orbit lets GPM sample the precipitation across all local times.** Unlike the sun-synchronous orbits of its axis siblings (PACE, SWOT, SMAP), GPM flies a non-sun-synchronous orbit at roughly 407 km with a ~65° inclination, so it crosses the equator at a slowly shifting local time and samples the precipitation through the full diurnal cycle — the morning rain and the afternoon storm alike — rather than always at the same Sun angle, a design choice that lets it map the timing of rain as well as its amount.
- **Two radar frequencies see the rain and the snow differently — and together resolve the storm in three dimensions.** GPM's Dual-frequency Precipitation Radar reads the same column at Ku band (~13.6 GHz) and Ka band (~35.5 GHz); because the two frequencies attenuate and scatter differently in rain and snow, comparing them constrains the drop-size distribution and resolves the precipitation in three dimensions — the first spaceborne dual-frequency precipitation radar, where TRMM's single Ku-band radar (1997) saw only the tropics in one frequency.
- **The Core Observatory is the standard the whole constellation is measured against.** The GPM Core Observatory's well-calibrated dual-frequency radar and microwave imager serve as the reference standard against which the international GPM constellation of partner satellites' microwave radiometers are inter-calibrated; the morphing algorithm then merges their intermittent overpasses into IMERG, a half-hourly ~0.1° global precipitation map — so a single well-calibrated observer turns a scattered fleet into one continuous picture of the falling water.
- **A rain beetle whose brief flight is keyed entirely to the first heavy rains.** The Rain Beetle (*Pleocoma crinita*), a Pacific Northwest scarab of the family Pleocomidae, has males that fly only during the first heavy autumn and winter rains, often in cold rain near the snow line, while the flightless females wait in deep burrows; short-lived and non-feeding as adults, the beetle's whole above-ground life is keyed to the falling rain: a mirror of GPM measuring the precipitation falling across the whole Earth from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only; a Washington / north-of-the-Columbia rain beetle distinct from the east-of-the-Cascades arid-zone *Pleocoma oregonensis*).
- **An epiphytic moss that revives with the autumn rains and the fog it drinks from the air.** The Hanging Moss (*Antitrichia curtipendula*), a pendulous epiphytic moss draping the branches of PNW oaks (especially Garry / Oregon white oak) and conifers, is poikilohydric — drying in the summer and reviving with the autumn rains and fog, living on the water the sky gives (ecological description only; a pendulous epiphytic moss distinct from the beard lichens *Usnea* / *Alectoria* it drapes alongside, its family placement unsettled and now often placed in its own family Antitrichiaceae) — bound with the throughline of the falling water, the same rain and fog whose global measurement GPM makes from orbit.

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived GPM mission data.** The GPM shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual mission data. A future revision could render real GPM data — the measured launch trajectory, the ~407 km ~65° orbit tracks, the DPR's three-dimensional reflectivity fields, the GMI brightness-temperature swaths, or the IMERG precipitation maps — for a higher-fidelity view.
- **The GPM diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the GPM Core Observatory (with the DPR and the GMI) and the H-IIA, and a sectioned view of Earth with the ~407 km ~65° non-sun-synchronous orbit and the radar beams cutting down through a storm.
- **The launch, dual-frequency-radar, precipitation, and constellation modes are depicted rather than driven by published data.** The four shader modes are depicted but not rendered from the published GPM data (the launch ephemeris, the orbit tracks, the DPR reflectivity products, the GMI brightness temperatures, or the IMERG global precipitation maps); a future ship could drive the modes from those data sets for a quantitatively faithful reading.
- **The rain-and-snow mode is depicted rather than tied to the actual precipitation measurements.** The precipitation mode depicts the radar and imager reading the falling water at a representative level rather than tying it to the published measurements (the GPM L2/L3 precipitation products, the IMERG merged maps, the long precipitation record); a future ship could update the mode from the archived data for a faithful rendering of the falling water across the whole Earth.

## Dedication

v1.284 GPM is dedicated to the people across NASA and JAXA who built and still fly the observer that measures the falling water of the whole Earth. The teams who lifted it from Tanegashima on an H-IIA and turned it into a low orbit that crosses every latitude band at every hour, so the rain of the dawn and the storm of the afternoon are caught alike. The engineers behind the dual-frequency radar — two beams, one to read the heavy rain and one for the light rain and the snow — and behind the imager that listens to the faint microwave glow of cloud and falling ice across a wide swath, resolving the precipitation in three dimensions. And the teams who tied a scattered fleet of partner satellites to this one well-calibrated standard, merging their passes into a single half-hourly map of where the water falls. Together they measure a thing as old as rain, and make of it a record that grows by the day — the snow on the high country, the storm over the sea, the first cold rain that calls the beetles up. They watch the falling water, and the watch goes on.
