## Surprises

- **One mission orbited a giant planet for years and set a lander down on its moon.** Cassini entered orbit around Saturn on 1 July 2004 — the first craft ever to orbit Saturn — and on 14 January 2005 ESA's Huygens probe parachuted through Titan's haze to land on its surface, the first landing in the outer solar system and the most distant landing from Earth ever made.
- **A probe descended through an orange haze to a hidden surface and found a world like an early Earth.** The Huygens probe descended through Titan's thick atmosphere over about two and a half hours, reading the winds, the haze, and the surface, and returned images of a landscape carved by liquid methane — the first direct look beneath the veil of a moon in the outer solar system.
- **Icy jets sprayed a hidden sea into space.** In 2005 Cassini found active jets of water ice and vapor erupting from the south pole of Enceladus, the first detection of such plumes and the first direct evidence of a hidden subsurface ocean — a small moon spraying its hidden sea into space where the orbiter could sample it directly.
- **A spacecraft flew the gap between a planet and its rings.** From April to September 2017 Cassini flew its Grand Finale of 22 dives through the gap between Saturn and its rings, the first spacecraft ever to fly that gap, returning the closest views of the planet and its rings before its planned final descent into Saturn on 15 September 2017.
- **A salamander stays in the water its whole life, hidden beneath the streambed.** The Cope's Giant Salamander (*Dicamptodon copei*), a large salamander of cold, clear Pacific Northwest mountain streams, often remains aquatic for life (neotenic, keeping its gills) and lives hidden in and beneath the cold streambed where it is rarely seen (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of the hidden sea beneath Enceladus and the hidden surface beneath Titan's haze.
- **A deep-violet mushroom hangs a cobwebby veil over the dark forest floor.** The Violet Webcap (*Cortinarius violaceus*), a deep-violet fungus of cool, damp Pacific Northwest conifer forests, takes its genus name from the *cortina*, the cobwebby partial veil that drapes from the cap edge to the stalk in young fruitings (ecological description only) — a mirror of the veil drawn back across the ringed world.

1. **A sustain KEEPS the predecessor's shared domain vocabulary — it does not remove it.** Unlike a rotation, this is a SUSTAIN (mode=continuation): Cassini-Huygens and the Galileo / Voyager / Pioneer siblings share almost all of their domain vocabulary, so the rewrite KEPT the shared outer-planets content (the outer solar system, deep space, the giant planets, Saturn, its rings and moons, Titan, the gravity assist, the orbit insertion, the atmospheric probe, RTG power, the heliosphere) and replaced only the predecessor-specific facts — Galileo as the subject, the Space Shuttle Atlantis (STS-34) launch with the Inertial Upper Stage, the first Jupiter orbit, the first atmospheric entry probe at a giant planet, the first asteroid flyby (Gaspra) and first asteroid moon (Dactyl), and the Predaceous Diving Beetle + Watershield pairing — with Cassini-Huygens': the joint NASA / ESA / ASI structure (Cassini orbiter = NASA/JPL; Huygens probe = ESA; antenna = ASI), the 15 October 1997 Titan IVB / Centaur launch, the VVEJGA trajectory, the Saturn Orbit Insertion (1 July 2004) as the first Saturn orbiter, the Huygens descent and landing on Titan (14 January 2005) as the first landing in the outer solar system, the active plumes of Enceladus and the methane lakes of Titan, the Grand Finale and the planned final descent (15 September 2017), and the Cope's Giant Salamander + Violet Webcap pairing.
2. **A sustain SUSTAINS the axis — the obs counter advances and there are prior siblings on the same axis.** Cassini-Huygens sustains the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis at obs#6 (the fifth consecutive sustain on the axis opened by Pioneer 10, sustained by Pioneer 11, Voyager 1, Voyager 2, and Galileo), so only the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION counter advances (obs#5 → obs#6) and the substrate-axis-rotation discipline holds UNCHANGED at obs#101 (no NEW rotation observed). The single deliberate backward reference is the nav-prev to the 1.254 Galileo sibling, with a lineage thread to the Voyagers and the Pioneers.
3. **The mission is joint NASA / ESA / ASI — Huygens is an ESA probe, not a NASA probe.** The Cassini orbiter was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (like Galileo and the Voyagers, not Ames), the Huygens probe was built and operated by the European Space Agency, and the high-gain antenna and radio subsystem were contributed by ASI, the Italian Space Agency; this is the correct international structure, not a contradiction, and the catalog states it precisely throughout.
4. **Cassini-Huygens launched on a Titan IVB / Centaur (15 October 1997); it flew a VVEJGA trajectory.** Cassini-Huygens launched aboard a Titan IVB / Centaur from Cape Canaveral and flew a VVEJGA gravity-assist path (Venus 1998 and 1999, Earth 1999, Jupiter December 2000) over about seven years; the catalog states this precisely.
5. **The Saturn achievement is the first ORBIT, not the first flyby.** On 1 July 2004 Cassini became the first spacecraft to orbit Saturn; the catalog scopes this strictly to ORBIT / gravitational capture, ceding the first Saturn FLYBY to Pioneer 11 (1979) — the Pioneers and Voyagers only flew PAST, the same orbit-vs-flyby distinction Galileo holds at Jupiter (obs#5).
6. **The Titan landing is the first landing in the OUTER solar system — the qualifier is load-bearing.** On 14 January 2005 ESA's Huygens probe landed on Titan, the first landing in the outer solar system, the first landing on a moon other than Earth's Moon, and the most distant landing from Earth ever made; the catalog keeps the OUTER-solar-system qualifier essential, since landings on Earth's Moon and the inner planets (Venus, Mars) came earlier.
7. **The Enceladus result is the first DETECTION of active plumes — a discovery — and the hidden ocean is a finding.** In 2005 Cassini discovered active jets of water ice and vapor erupting from the south pole of Enceladus; the catalog scopes this as the first detection of active eruptions from the moon, with the hidden subsurface ocean described as a positive science finding.
8. **0 re-stamps — Cassini-Huygens shares the predecessors' journey but not their firsts.** Cassini-Huygens made the first Saturn orbit (NOT the first flyby — Pioneer 11) and a Jupiter gravity assist (NOT a first); it does not re-stamp Pioneer 10's outer-solar-system / belt-traverse / Jupiter-flyby / escape-trajectory / plaque firsts, Pioneer 11's Saturn-flyby / F-ring / magnetic-field firsts, Voyager 1's Io-volcanism / most-distant / first-heliopause-crossing firsts, Voyager 2's Uranus / Neptune / Grand-Tour firsts, or Galileo's first-Jupiter-orbit / first-giant-planet-atmospheric-probe / first-asteroid-flyby firsts. 10. **The Grand Finale is a celebrated exploration phase — positive framing.** From April to September 2017 Cassini flew the Grand Finale of 22 dives between Saturn and its rings, the first spacecraft to fly that gap; the catalog frames this positively as a celebrated exploration phase.
