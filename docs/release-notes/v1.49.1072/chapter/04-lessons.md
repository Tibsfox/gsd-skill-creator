1. **A sustain KEEPS the predecessor's shared domain vocabulary — it does not remove it.**
   Unlike a rotation, this is a SUSTAIN (mode=continuation): Cassini-Huygens and the Galileo / Voyager / Pioneer siblings share almost all of their domain vocabulary, so the rewrite KEPT the shared outer-planets content (the outer solar system, deep space, the giant planets, Saturn, its rings and moons, Titan, the gravity assist, the orbit insertion, the atmospheric probe, RTG power, the heliosphere) and replaced only the predecessor-specific facts — Galileo as the subject, the Space Shuttle Atlantis (STS-34) launch with the Inertial Upper Stage, the first Jupiter orbit, the first atmospheric entry probe at a giant planet, the first asteroid flyby (Gaspra) and first asteroid moon (Dactyl), and the Predaceous Diving Beetle + Watershield pairing — with Cassini-Huygens': the joint NASA / ESA /…

2. **A sustain SUSTAINS the axis — the obs counter advances and there are prior siblings on the same axis.**
   Cassini-Huygens sustains the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis at obs#6 (the fifth consecutive sustain on the axis opened by Pioneer 10, sustained by Pioneer 11, Voyager 1, Voyager 2, and Galileo), so only the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION counter advances (obs#5 → obs#6) and the substrate-axis-rotation discipline holds UNCHANGED at obs#101 (no NEW rotation observed). The single deliberate backward reference is the nav-prev to the 1.254 Galileo sibling, with a lineage thread to the Voyagers and the Pioneers.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12474_

3. **The mission is joint NASA / ESA / ASI — Huygens is an ESA probe, not a NASA probe.**
   The Cassini orbiter was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (like Galileo and the Voyagers, not Ames), the Huygens probe was built and operated by the European Space Agency, and the high-gain antenna and radio subsystem were contributed by ASI, the Italian Space Agency; this is the correct international structure, not a contradiction, and the catalog states it precisely throughout.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12475_

4. **Cassini-Huygens launched on a Titan IVB / Centaur (15 October 1997); it flew a VVEJGA trajectory.**
   Cassini-Huygens launched aboard a Titan IVB / Centaur from Cape Canaveral and flew a VVEJGA gravity-assist path (Venus 1998 and 1999, Earth 1999, Jupiter December 2000) over about seven years; the catalog states this precisely.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12476_

5. **The Saturn achievement is the first ORBIT, not the first flyby.**
   On 1 July 2004 Cassini became the first spacecraft to orbit Saturn; the catalog scopes this strictly to ORBIT / gravitational capture, ceding the first Saturn FLYBY to Pioneer 11 (1979) — the Pioneers and Voyagers only flew PAST, the same orbit-vs-flyby distinction Galileo holds at Jupiter (obs#5).
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12477_

6. **The Titan landing is the first landing in the OUTER solar system — the qualifier is load-bearing.**
   On 14 January 2005 ESA's Huygens probe landed on Titan, the first landing in the outer solar system, the first landing on a moon other than Earth's Moon, and the most distant landing from Earth ever made; the catalog keeps the OUTER-solar-system qualifier essential, since landings on Earth's Moon and the inner planets (Venus, Mars) came earlier.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12478_

7. **The Enceladus result is the first DETECTION of active plumes — a discovery — and the hidden ocean is a finding.**
   In 2005 Cassini discovered active jets of water ice and vapor erupting from the south pole of Enceladus; the catalog scopes this as the first detection of active eruptions from the moon, with the hidden subsurface ocean described as a positive science finding.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12479_

8. **0 re-stamps — Cassini-Huygens shares the predecessors' journey but not their firsts.**
   Cassini-Huygens made the first Saturn orbit (NOT the first flyby — Pioneer 11) and a Jupiter gravity assist (NOT a first); it does not re-stamp Pioneer 10's outer-solar-system / belt-traverse / Jupiter-flyby / escape-trajectory / plaque firsts, Pioneer 11's Saturn-flyby / F-ring / magnetic-field firsts, Voyager 1's Io-volcanism / most-distant / first-heliopause-crossing firsts, Voyager 2's Uranus / Neptune / Grand-Tour firsts, or Galileo's first-Jupiter-orbit / first-giant-planet-atmospheric-probe / first-asteroid-flyby firsts. Cassini-Huygens' firsts are all at the first Saturn orbit, the first landing in the outer solar system, and the first detection of active plumes from Enceladus, yielding 0 predecessor re-stamps.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12480_

9. **The Jupiter flyby (30 December 2000) is a gravity assist, not a first.**
   Cassini's Jupiter flyby was a gravity assist on the VVEJGA road to Saturn; the catalog frames it as a gravity assist, not a first, and does not re-stamp Galileo's first-Jupiter-orbit.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12481_

10. **The Grand Finale is a celebrated exploration phase — positive framing.**
   From April to September 2017 Cassini flew the Grand Finale of 22 dives between Saturn and its rings, the first spacecraft to fly that gap; the catalog frames this positively as a celebrated exploration phase.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12482_

11. **The mission is framed positively — the 15 September 2017 end of mission is a planned final state.**
   On 15 September 2017 Cassini made a planned final descent into Saturn; the catalog frames this strictly and positively as a planned final state aligned with planetary-protection guidelines, a deliberate choice to keep the spacecraft's path clear of the potentially habitable ocean moons Enceladus and Titan.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12483_

12. **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived mission imagery.**
   The Cassini-Huygens shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual mission data or imaging-science-subsystem imagery. A future revision could render a real Cassini view — the rings of Saturn, the orange haze of Titan, or the south-polar plumes of Enceladus (publicly available from the National Space Science Data Center) — for a higher-fidelity view.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12484_

13. **The Cassini-Huygens diorama is a forthcoming artifact.**
   The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the Cassini orbiter with its high-gain antenna and magnetometer boom, and the Huygens probe with its heat shield and parachute.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12485_

14. **The Saturn-orbit and Titan-descent modes are depicted rather than driven by real data.**
   The orbital tour and the Huygens descent in the second and third modes are depicted but not rendered from archived Cassini imaging and Huygens atmospheric-structure measurements; a future ship could drive the modes from the real imaging and the probe's pressure-temperature-wind time series for a quantitatively faithful reading.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12486_

15. **The Enceladus-plume and Titan-lakes findings are depicted rather than computed.**
   The south-polar plumes of Enceladus and the methane lakes of Titan are depicted rather than rendered from the published Cassini cosmic-dust, mass-spectrometer, and radar-mapper data; a future ship could drive an Enceladus sub-mode and a Titan-radar sub-mode from the real measurements for a quantitatively faithful view.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12487_
