## Surprises

- **A mission that tilts its path to see what no one had photographed.** Solar Orbiter uses repeated Venus gravity assists to climb out of the ecliptic plane — reaching ~17° relative to the solar equator after the February 2025 flyby and designed to climb toward ~33° in the extended mission — and from that tilted high-latitude vantage it captured the first images of the Sun's poles (released June 2025), a face never before photographed.
- **The closest pictures, taken inside Mercury's orbit.** From perihelion at ~0.28 AU — inside Mercury's orbit, within ~0.3 AU of the Sun — Solar Orbiter returns among the closest and highest-resolution images of the Sun's surface and corona, a far closer vantage than ecliptic-plane solar observatories, while Parker (1.266) dives far closer still (~0.04 AU) on its closest-approach and fastest-object records.
- **Ten instruments designed to link the Sun to its wind.** Solar Orbiter carries six remote-sensing imagers (EUI, PHI, SPICE, STIX, Metis, SoloHI) and four in-situ instruments (EPD, MAG, RPW, SWA), a suite designed to connect a feature imaged on the Sun's surface with the solar wind later measured at the spacecraft.
- **An ESA-led mission carrying NASA hardware.** Solar Orbiter is built by Airbus Defence and Space and operated from ESA's ESOC with ESTRACK as the primary tracking network, while NASA provided the Atlas V 411 launch, one full instrument — SoloHI, the Solar Orbiter Heliospheric Imager — and the Heavy Ion Sensor of the SWA suite, with the Deep Space Network supplementing ESTRACK.
- **A hovering thrush that scans the open ground from the air.** The Mountain Bluebird (*Sialia currucoides*), a brilliant sky-blue thrush of the open country of the interior Pacific Northwest (montane meadows, sagebrush edges, open burns), hovers in mid-air over open ground and scans below before dropping on prey — one of the few thrushes that hunts from a hovering aerial vantage (behavioral / ecological description only) — a mirror of the mission that rose to a tilted vantage to bring a hidden face into view.
- **A radiating sun-disk wildflower of the open meadow.** Blanketflower (*Gaillardia aristata*), a sun-loving composite wildflower of the aster family found across open meadows, grasslands, and dry slopes of the interior Pacific Northwest, lifts a broad radiating head of red-and-yellow ray florets around a central disk — a stylized sun-face held up to the open sky (ecological description only, distinguished from the true sunflowers and from Oregon Sunshine) — bound with Solar Orbiter's first sight of the Sun's poles by the first sight of the hidden face.
