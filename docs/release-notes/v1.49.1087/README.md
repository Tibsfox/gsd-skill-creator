## Mission Overview

Solar Orbiter is the obs#5 sustainer of the HELIOPHYSICS-EXPLORATION axis — an ESA-led mission, with NASA participation, whose defining records are the first images of the Sun's poles, among the closest and highest-resolution images of the Sun, and a remote-sensing + in-situ design that connects what it sees on the Sun's surface with the solar wind it measures. The spacecraft is built by Airbus Defence and Space (Stevenage, UK); mission control runs at ESA's European Space Operations Centre (ESOC, Darmstadt), with ESA's ESTRACK as the primary tracking network. NASA provided the launch vehicle, one full instrument — SoloHI, the Solar Orbiter Heliospheric Imager — and the Heavy Ion Sensor of the SWA suite, with the Deep Space Network supplementing ESTRACK — NOT a NASA-built spacecraft, NOT JHU APL / STScI, and the firsts concern close-up solar imaging and the poles, not the builder. Solar Orbiter launched 10 February 2020 (UTC) on a United Launch Alliance Atlas V 411 from Cape Canaveral (SLC-41) into an elliptical heliocentric orbit whose perihelion lies inside Mercury's orbit (~0.28 AU, ~42 million km); repeated Venus gravity assists progressively raise its inclination out of the ecliptic — reaching ~17° relative to the solar equator after the February 2025 Venus flyby, designed to climb toward ~33° in the extended mission — to look down on the Sun's poles. It carries ten instruments: six remote-sensing imagers (EUI, the Extreme Ultraviolet Imager; PHI, the Polarimetric and Helioseismic Imager; SPICE, spectral imaging; STIX, X-ray; Metis, a coronagraph; SoloHI, the heliospheric imager) and four in-situ instruments (EPD, energetic particles; MAG, the magnetometer; RPW, radio and plasma waves; SWA, the solar wind analyser), a suite designed to connect close-up imaging of the Sun's surface with in-situ measurement of the solar wind it produces. The story is the Atlas V launch, the cruise and the repeated Venus flybys that tilt the orbit up out of the ecliptic, the close dives to within ~0.3 AU that yield the sharpest pictures of the solar disk and corona, and the climb to a high-latitude vantage from which the spacecraft photographs the Sun's poles for the first time. The science is central: from within ~0.3 AU Solar Orbiter returns among the closest and highest-resolution images of the Sun's surface and corona, and from its tilted high-latitude orbit it captured the first images of the Sun's poles (taken March 2025, released June 2025), a face never before photographed; its combined remote-sensing and in-situ suite links a feature seen on the Sun to the solar wind measured at the spacecraft. ## Dedication

v1.270 Solar Orbiter is dedicated to the people across ESA, NASA, Airbus, and the instrument teams who built and fly the mission that rose to a tilted vantage and brought the Sun's hidden poles into view. The engineers at Airbus in Stevenage who built a spacecraft to work inside Mercury's orbit, and the flight team at ESOC in Darmstadt who guide it past Venus again and again to lift its path up out of the ecliptic. The instrument teams behind EUI, PHI, SPICE, STIX, Metis, and SoloHI, whose cameras take the closest and sharpest pictures of the Sun, and behind EPD, MAG, RPW, and SWA, whose sensors read the solar wind as it streams past — a suite built to connect what is seen on the surface with what is measured in the wind. The colleagues at NASA who provided the Atlas V from Cape Canaveral, the SoloHI imager, and the Heavy Ion Sensor, and the ESTRACK and Deep Space Network stations that keep the link open. They flew in close, tilted the orbit, and looked down on a face never before photographed. The first images of the Sun's poles, seen from a vantage held steady out of the plane.
