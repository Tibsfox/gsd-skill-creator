- ***Larus argentatus* Taxonomy and the "Silvery" Etymology.** The Herring Gull is scientifically named *Larus argentatus* — with *Larus* as the Latin-derived genus name for gulls and *argentatus* as the species name meaning "silvery" (from Latin *argentum*, silver), referring to the silvery-gray back and upperwing coloration. The species was named by **Erik Pontoppidan** (Danish bishop and naturalist) in 1763. The Herring Gull is one of the most widespread gull species in the Northern Hemisphere: breeding across North America, Europe, and parts of Asia. In North America, the species breeds across Canada, Alaska, and the northeastern United States and is the dominant large gull in eastern North America and northern Europe. In the Pacific Northwest, the Herring Gull is primarily a winter visitor; the **American Herring Gull** (*Larus argentatus smithsonianus*) subspecies is the specific taxonomic register documented for the Pacific Northwest winter populations.
- **The Herring Gull Complex and Taxonomic Hybridization.** The **Herring Gull complex** is taxonomically complicated. The American Herring Gull (*Larus argentatus smithsonianus*) is the subspecies that winters in the Pacific Northwest; European and other populations are now sometimes treated as separate species. - **The "Seagull" of Popular Culture: Cultural Image Built Primarily on This Species.** The Herring Gull is the species that most people visualize when they say "seagull" — the large, white-headed, black-wingtipped gull of English seaside towns, American fishing harbors, and parking lots adjacent to fast food restaurants. The cultural image of the gull is built primarily on this species. Adult identification characteristics include a large gull body (wingspan 54-58 inches / 137-147 cm, very similar to the Glaucous-winged Gull in size), white head and underparts, gray back and upperwing, and black wingtips with white spots (mirrors) — the classic large white-headed gull pattern. The bill is yellow with a red gonydeal spot, the eye is yellow with a yellow orbital ring, and the legs are pink. The black wingtips are the key distinction from the Glaucous-winged Gull's gray wingtips that distinguishes the two species in the mixed winter flocks that populate Pacific Northwest harbors and beaches.
- **The Triggered Alarm Chorus Acoustic Profile: Sudden Onset, Peak Intensity, Gradual De-escalation.** The Herring Gull flock alarm is a specific acoustic event produced by a specific behavioral trigger. A winter gull flock at a beach or harbor is suddenly disturbed: a **Bald Eagle** or **Peregrine Falcon** stoops overhead, a human approaches too quickly, a dog charges the flock. The birds nearest the threat are the first to alarm; their calls trigger adjacent birds in a rapid cascade. Within seconds, the entire flock is calling simultaneously, producing the overlapping kyow-kyow-kyow alarm chorus that is the defining acoustic event of the species in its wintering-flock context. The Herring Gull alarm call is loud, harsh, and repeated — higher-pitched and more emphatic than the contact mewing call, less melodic, more abrupt. The alarm is not musical; it is functional, optimized for carrying information rapidly through the flock about the nature and direction of the threat. Alarm events last 30 seconds to a few minutes before the threat passes or the flock relocates.
- **Frequency Range 800-4,000 Hz and the Overlapping Simultaneous Call Wall of Sound.** Herring Gull alarm vocalizations are concentrated approximately 800-4,000 Hz, similar to other large gull species. The alarm call is generally higher in pitch than the contact call. At a flock alarm event, the simultaneous production by many birds creates acoustic energy across a broad range within this band. In a flock of dozens or hundreds of birds, the alarm propagates in a wave but all birds reach alarm state within a few seconds; for the duration of the alarm event, many birds are calling simultaneously, and the overlapping of individual alarm calls creates a wall of sound qualitatively different from any single bird's call. A larger flock produces a more intense alarm event: more birds calling simultaneously, greater total acoustic energy. Winter beach flocks of Herring Gulls in the Pacific Northwest can range from a few dozen to several hundred birds, and at the upper end of flock size the alarm event approaches the acoustic scale of the colonial chorus at reduced duration.
- **Winter Flock Ecology: Harbors, River Mouths, Salmon Carcasses, Tidal Roosts.** Winter gull flocks in the Pacific Northwest are rarely single-species. Herring Gulls mix with **Glaucous-winged Gulls**, **Thayer's Gulls** (now recognized as **Iceland Gull** *Larus glaucoides thayeri*), **Mew Gulls** (*Larus canus*), **Ring-billed Gulls** (*Larus delawarensis*), and others depending on location. Sorting through a mixed winter gull flock is one of the more demanding exercises in Pacific Northwest birding. Herring Gulls in winter favor fishing harbors (where fish scraps are available), river mouths (where salmonid carcasses concentrate after spawning runs), beaches adjacent to shellfish operations, and refuse disposal sites. The species is an opportunistic omnivore in winter: fish, invertebrates, carrion, garbage, and the remains of spawned-out salmon. One of the most ecologically significant winter feeding behaviors of Pacific Northwest Herring Gulls is the consumption of salmon carcasses after **Chinook**, **Coho**, **Chum**, **Pink**, and **Sockeye** salmon runs spawn in Washington and Oregon rivers and streams. Large winter gull flocks gather at river mouths and lower river sections to exploit this resource.
