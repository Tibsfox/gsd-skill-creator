## Mission Overview

QuikSCAT was the obs#15 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's quick-recovery ocean-wind satellite, the mission built and flown in barely a year to restore the global ocean-surface wind record after the loss of NSCAT, the NASA Scatterometer that had ended when its host spacecraft (Japan's ADEOS-I / Midori) lost its solar array in June 1997. ★ It was a NASA Earth-science mission managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), built rapidly (~12 months, faster than any NASA mission since the 1950s) on a Ball Aerospace BCP-2000 bus, carrying a single instrument: SeaWinds, a Ku-band (13.402 GHz) conically-scanning pencil-beam radar scatterometer with a rotating ~1 m parabolic dish antenna spinning at ~18 rpm and producing two beams — an inner HH/H-polarized beam at ~46° incidence and an outer VV/V-polarized beam at ~54° incidence — across a wide ~1800 km swath. The instrument read the ocean two ways at once with a single spinning dish: as the antenna swept its two conical beams across the swath, it measured the Bragg backscatter off the wind-driven centimetre-scale waves on the sea surface, and from the way that backscatter changed with the look direction it resolved both the speed and the direction of the wind in ~25 km wind-vector cells. The mission launched 19 June 1999 (UTC) on a Titan II (Titan 23G) from Vandenberg AFB, SLC-4W, California, and reached a sun-synchronous, dawn–dusk orbit at roughly 803 km altitude and ~98.6° inclination, flying alone — not in the A-Train. The story was the Titan II launch from Vandenberg, the climb to the dawn–dusk sun-synchronous orbit, the rotating dish sweeping its two conical beams across the wide swath, the backscatter off the wind-roughened ocean resolving into wind speed and direction, and the decade-long record of ocean-surface winds gathered day after day. The science was central: SeaWinds measured ocean-surface wind vectors — both speed and direction — at ~25 km resolution over a wide ~1800 km swath, covering ~90% of the ice-free ocean each day, and those winds fed operational weather and marine forecasting (hurricane intensity and extent, marine wind warnings) as a near-real-time workhorse. ★ QuikSCAT was a RETIRED mission, framed in the past: the antenna's spin (bearing) mechanism degraded, and on ~23 November 2009 the dish stopped rotating, ending the wide-swath wind-vector record; the spun-down instrument was then held as a stable calibration reference for other scatterometers (e.g. OSCAT, ASCAT) until the mission was decommissioned ~2 October 2018. QuikSCAT's firsts were scoped carefully: the SeaWinds Ku-band rotating pencil-beam delivered near-continuous daily near-global ocean-surface wind vectors at high resolution over a wide swath; the wide ~1800 km swath mapped ~90% of the ice-free ocean each day; and the ~10-year (1999–2009) continuous wind-vector record restored the data after NSCAT and fed operational forecasting. ★ The scope is load-bearing: ocean winds had been measured from space before QuikSCAT (Seasat-A's SASS in 1978 and NSCAT in 1996 were earlier spaceborne scatterometers; ERS-1, 1991, also preceded), so it was NOT "the first scatterometer" / not "first ocean winds from space"; QuikSCAT's advance was the sustained, wide-swath, daily, decade-long high-resolution wind-vector record, plus the quick recovery after NSCAT's loss. ★★★ QuikSCAT was RETIRED (launched 1999, antenna stopped ~2009, decommissioned ~2018), so the catalog framed its whole story in the PAST (QuikSCAT orbited its sun-synchronous orbit, carried its instrument, measured and mapped the ocean-surface wind vectors, and built the decade-long record of the wind on the sea). ## Surprises

- **One spinning dish read both the speed and the direction of the wind on the sea.** SeaWinds was a single rotating pencil-beam antenna, ~1 m across, spinning at ~18 rpm and sweeping two conical beams across the wide swath; because each patch of sea was looked at from several azimuth angles as the dish turned, the way the backscatter changed with the look direction let the instrument resolve not just the wind speed but the wind direction — a full wind vector in each ~25 km cell, from one rotating dish.
- **The wind was read from the roughness it raised on the water, not from the wind itself.** SeaWinds did not feel the wind; it read the centimetre-scale capillary waves the wind raised on the sea surface. The Ku-band radar measured the Bragg backscatter off those tiny wind-driven waves, and because the roughness grew with the wind and lined up with its direction, the backscatter carried both the speed and the direction of the near-surface wind.
- **A mission built in barely a year to replace a record lost in an instant.** When NSCAT's host spacecraft (ADEOS-I / Midori) lost its solar array in June 1997, the satellite ocean-wind record stopped. NASA's JPL built QuikSCAT in roughly twelve months — faster than any NASA mission since the 1950s — on a commercial Ball Aerospace BCP-2000 bus, launched it in June 1999, and restored the wind record; QuikSCAT then extended it for a decade.
- **A North Pacific seabird that lives on the same wind QuikSCAT mapped.** The Northern Fulmar (*Fulmarus glacialis*), a stiff-winged tubenose seabird of the North Pacific and a regular non-breeding visitor far off the Pacific Northwest coast, forages over the open ocean by dynamic soaring — extracting energy from the wind-speed gradient just above the wave crests, the very near-surface ocean wind field QuikSCAT read from the roughness it raised on the sea (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A pink dune vine that holds fast where the ocean wind comes ashore.** The Beach Morning Glory (*Calystegia soldanella*, also Seashore False Bindweed), a low, mat-forming perennial vine of Pacific Northwest sandy beaches and foredunes, carries thick kidney-shaped leaves and pink funnel flowers and roots deep against the wind-blown sand and salt spray of the open coast — growing exactly where the ocean wind comes ashore, distinct from the field and hedge bindweeds — bound with the throughline of the wind over the water.

## Dedication

v1.295 QuikSCAT is dedicated to the people at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory and at Ball Aerospace, who built and flew the satellite that read the wind on the sea. The teams who, after the ocean-wind record stopped with the loss of NSCAT, designed and built a whole new spacecraft in barely a year — faster than any NASA mission since the dawn of the space age — to restore that record, and who tuned the spinning dish so well that it read both the speed and the direction of the wind in every patch of sea from the faint roughness the wind raised on the water. The crews who carried it aloft on a Titan II from Vandenberg into its dawn–dusk orbit. The forecasters who used those near-real-time winds to warn ships and to read the size and the strength of storms far out at sea, and the scientists who gathered the ten-year record of the ocean's surface winds, day after day, until the antenna came to rest. The satellite mapped the wind over most of the open ocean every day for a decade, and that record of the wind on the water belongs to them.
