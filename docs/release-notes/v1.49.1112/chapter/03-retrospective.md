## Surprises

- **One spinning dish read both the speed and the direction of the wind on the sea.** SeaWinds was a single rotating pencil-beam antenna, ~1 m across, spinning at ~18 rpm and sweeping two conical beams across the wide swath; because each patch of sea was looked at from several azimuth angles as the dish turned, the way the backscatter changed with the look direction let the instrument resolve not just the wind speed but the wind direction — a full wind vector in each ~25 km cell, from one rotating dish.
- **The wind was read from the roughness it raised on the water, not from the wind itself.** SeaWinds did not feel the wind; it read the centimetre-scale capillary waves the wind raised on the sea surface. The Ku-band radar measured the Bragg backscatter off those tiny wind-driven waves, and because the roughness grew with the wind and lined up with its direction, the backscatter carried both the speed and the direction of the near-surface wind.
- **A mission built in barely a year to replace a record lost in an instant.** When NSCAT's host spacecraft (ADEOS-I / Midori) lost its solar array in June 1997, the satellite ocean-wind record stopped. NASA's JPL built QuikSCAT in roughly twelve months — faster than any NASA mission since the 1950s — on a commercial Ball Aerospace BCP-2000 bus, launched it in June 1999, and restored the wind record; QuikSCAT then extended it for a decade.
- **A North Pacific seabird that lives on the same wind QuikSCAT mapped.** The Northern Fulmar (*Fulmarus glacialis*), a stiff-winged tubenose seabird of the North Pacific and a regular non-breeding visitor far off the Pacific Northwest coast, forages over the open ocean by dynamic soaring — extracting energy from the wind-speed gradient just above the wave crests, the very near-surface ocean wind field QuikSCAT read from the roughness it raised on the sea (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A pink dune vine that holds fast where the ocean wind comes ashore.** The Beach Morning Glory (*Calystegia soldanella*, also Seashore False Bindweed), a low, mat-forming perennial vine of Pacific Northwest sandy beaches and foredunes, carries thick kidney-shaped leaves and pink funnel flowers and roots deep against the wind-blown sand and salt spray of the open coast — growing exactly where the ocean wind comes ashore, distinct from the field and hedge bindweeds — bound with the throughline of the wind over the water.
