## Mission Overview

GEDI is the obs#19 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's full-waveform laser altimeter aboard the International Space Station, the first space mission built to measure the three-dimensional structure of Earth's forests. ★ It is a NASA Earth-science mission: a NASA Earth Venture Instrument, competitively selected in 2014 and cost-capped at about $94M, led by the University of Maryland (principal investigator Ralph Dubayah) with NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center (deputy PI and instrument scientist J. Bryan Blair); GEDI is an instrument hosted on the International Space Station rather than a free-flying satellite. GEDI carries a single instrument and profiles the world's forests from the station's orbit: a full-waveform lidar with three lasers (two full-power and one split, optically dithered to four beams and eight parallel ground tracks) firing at 1064 nm in ~25 m footprints (about 60 m along-track and 600 m across-track), recording the full returned waveform to retrieve canopy height, the vertical canopy profile, and aboveground biomass. The mission launched 5 December 2018 on a SpaceX Falcon 9 Block 5 — the CRS-16 Dragon cargo flight — from Cape Canaveral SLC-40, and was mounted on the JEM-EF (the Japanese Experiment Module Exposed Facility, "Kibo") of the ISS, which flies at roughly 51.6° inclination, so GEDI's coverage spans about 51.6°N to 51.6°S — the tropical and temperate forests, not the high latitudes or the poles. The story is the Falcon 9 launch from Cape Canaveral, the Dragon berthing and the mounting of the instrument on the JEM-EF, the three lasers split into eight ground tracks firing 1064 nm pulses down through the canopy, the full returned waveform resolving canopy height and vertical layers and ground, and the global map of tropical and temperate forest aboveground biomass being gathered from above. The science is central: from the station's orbit GEDI fires its lasers down through the canopy — three lasers producing eight parallel ground tracks of ~25 m footprints — and reads the full returned waveform to retrieve canopy height, the vertical layering of the forest, and the aboveground biomass that holds the forest's carbon. ★★★ GEDI is OPERATING, so the catalog frames its whole story in the PRESENT (GEDI flies with the station, fires its lasers, profiles the canopy, measures the forest, and maps its biomass); the mission collected science from 2019, was stowed on 17 March 2023 (moved from EFU-6 to EFU-7 because its JEM-EF slot was needed), was reinstalled to EFU-6 on 22 April 2024, and resumed nominal three-laser science on 11 June 2024 — a PAST stow→resume sub-sequence — and continues. GEDI's firsts are scoped carefully: it is the first spaceborne mission dedicated to measuring forest canopy three-dimensional structure and aboveground biomass, it performs the full-waveform lidar profiling of the canopy from the ISS, and it maps the global tropical and temperate forest aboveground biomass. ★ The scope is load-bearing: lidars flew in space before GEDI — LITE on the Shuttle in 1994, ICESat/GLAS in 2003, CALIPSO in 2006, and ICESat-2 in 2018 — so GEDI is NOT "the first spaceborne lidar"; its distinction is the dedicated forest-structure and biomass full-waveform lidar. ## Surprises

- **Three lasers become eight ground tracks across the canopy.** GEDI's full-waveform lidar uses three lasers — two full-power and one split — optically dithered to produce four beams and eight parallel ground tracks of ~25 m footprints. The eight tracks are the point: eight parallel swaths of canopy profiles laid down across the forest from one instrument on the station's exposed facility, each footprint reading the full returned waveform from canopy top to ground.
- **The full waveform resolves the whole vertical forest, not just the top.** Rather than a single elevation, GEDI records the full returned waveform of each 1064 nm pulse, so the canopy is resolved layer by layer — the top of the canopy, the vertical structure between, and the ground beneath — a three-dimensional reading of the forest from above, the basis for the aboveground-biomass estimate.
- **A mission stowed and brought back to service.** GEDI collected science from 2019, was stowed and hibernated on 17 March 2023 (moved from EFU-6 to EFU-7 because its JEM-EF slot was needed), was reinstalled to EFU-6 on 22 April 2024, and resumed nominal three-laser science on 11 June 2024 — a mission taken offline and returned to service, and now profiling the forest again, a landmark mission, the first to measure the three-dimensional structure of Earth's forests from space.
- **A PNW old-growth swift that nests and roosts inside the tall hollow trees GEDI profiles from above.** The Vaux's Swift (*Chaetura vauxi*), a small, fast-flying swift of Pacific Northwest old-growth conifer forests, nests and communally roosts inside large hollow old-growth trees and snags (and, on migration, in tall chimneys), depending on the big-old-tree structure of mature forest — the vertical forest structure GEDI's lidar measures from above (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A forest fern that carpets the structured floor beneath the tall canopy.** The Spreading Wood Fern (*Dryopteris expansa*, Spreading Wood Fern), a PNW conifer-forest fern of cool, moist old-growth woods, raises finely divided fronds from the deep duff and from mossy nurse logs and stumps on the structured forest floor beneath the tall canopy — distinct from the sword ferns and lady ferns — bound with the throughline of the structure of the old-growth forest.

## Dedication

v1.299 GEDI is dedicated to the people at the University of Maryland, at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, and across the GEDI science team who built and flew the first space mission made to measure the three-dimensional structure of Earth's forests. To Ralph Dubayah and the Maryland team who imagined reading the forest's height and layers from above, and to J. Bryan Blair and the Goddard engineers who built a full-waveform laser altimeter compact enough to ride a cargo flight to the station. The crews who carried the instrument aloft on a Falcon 9 in December 2018 and mounted it on Kibo's exposed facility, and those who later stowed it and brought it back to service so it could fire again. The scientists who turn three lasers and eight beams of light, footprint by footprint across the tropics and the temperate forests, into maps of canopy height and aboveground carbon. From the station the lasers still reach down through the canopy to the forest floor, and the long record of the living forest — its height, its layers, its carbon — belongs to them.
