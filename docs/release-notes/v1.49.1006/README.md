## Summary

v1.49.1006 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.196 to 1.197, adding TRACE — the Transition Region and Coronal Explorer — as the obs#2 INTRA-AXIS continuation that sustains the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY substrate-axis (axis-rotation #26 sustained at obs#2). The axis opened at v1005 OSO with the first standardized program of Sun-pointing solar observatories and the first continuous solar-cycle monitoring from space, measuring the Sun in aggregate with photometers, spectrometers, and a white-light coronagraph. TRACE extends that continuous monitoring into resolved high-resolution imaging: where OSO measured the Sun in aggregate, TRACE imaged the corona and the transition region at about one arcsecond, resolving the corona into its fine loop threads. The axis now holds two observations on the Sun, the continuous-monitoring program at OSO and the high-resolution imaging at TRACE, sustaining at obs#2 cumulative.

TRACE was a NASA Small Explorer (SMEX) led by the Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory and the Stanford-Lockheed Institute for Space Research, managed with NASA Goddard Space Flight Center, with Alan Title as principal investigator. The mission launched on 1 April 1998 aboard a Pegasus-XL into a Sun-synchronous polar orbit and operated through 2010. A single 30-centimeter Cassegrain telescope imaged the photosphere, the transition region, and the corona at about one arcsecond with 0.5-arcsecond pixels, the highest-resolution view of the corona at the time.

The signature of the mission is the first high-resolution EUV imaging of the solar corona and the transition region. TRACE imaged in several extreme-ultraviolet passbands sampling coronal plasma at several temperatures (Fe IX/X near 171 angstroms, Fe XII near 195 angstroms, and Fe XV near 284 angstroms) and several ultraviolet passbands imaging the transition region and the chromosphere, resolving the corona into the fine loops that trace the coronal magnetic field.

The mission recorded a landmark in the resolved and dynamic corona. By imaging the individual coronal loops, TRACE made the magnetic skeleton of the corona visible thread by thread, revealing the fine threads of the loops, their heating, and their oscillations. The high-cadence sequences became the first detailed movies of the corona, capturing loops oscillating after flares, the dynamics of active regions, and the fine bright structure named moss at the base of the corona.

The Sun-synchronous polar orbit gave the high-resolution imaging its foundation. By keeping the Sun in view for long unbroken intervals, the orbit supported the sustained high-cadence sequences that followed the same coronal loops and active regions as they evolved, the long viewing windows the high-resolution sequences depended on.

The axis-relevant substrate of TRACE at this degree is its continuation of the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY substrate-axis at obs#2 on the Sun, extending the continuous monitoring OSO opened into resolved high-resolution imaging. TRACE sustains the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#2 NEW LOCKED, opens the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY-IMAGING-LINEAGE thread at obs#1 cumulative, and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#69 cumulative (axis-rotation #26 sustained at obs#2). The forward continuation favors later solar observatories — dedicated coronagraphs, hard-X-ray and gamma-ray flare imagers, full-disk dynamics observatories, and heliospheric and in-situ solar-wind missions.

TRACE is the high-resolution imaging predecessor of the later full-disk EUV-dynamics observatories on the solar axis, and the mission records the TRACE team at the Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory, Alan Title and the team at the Stanford-Lockheed Institute for Space Research, and NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.196 to 1.197, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#80 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A resolved image reveals structure that aggregate monitoring cannot.** Rather than measuring the Sun's emission in aggregate, TRACE imaged the corona at about one arcsecond, showing that resolving a source into its fine structure reveals the loops and threads that aggregate measurement only averages over.
2. **A single high-resolution telescope can image across the layers of the solar atmosphere.** By using quadrant coatings to select several EUV and UV passbands, one telescope imaged the chromosphere, the transition region, and the corona at several temperatures, showing that a single instrument can read a source across its layers.
3. **High cadence turns high-resolution images into movies.** By taking rapid repeated images, TRACE assembled high-cadence sequences that became the first detailed movies of the corona, showing that cadence matters as much as resolution for capturing the dynamics a high-resolution image reveals.
4. **Resolving the loops opens coronal seismology.** By imaging coronal loops oscillating after flares, TRACE let the period and length of a loop be measured and the coronal magnetic field inferred, showing that resolving a structure into its parts opens new ways to measure it.
5. **A continuation can deepen an axis without leaving it.** By extending the continuous solar monitoring OSO opened into resolved imaging, TRACE sustained the solar-observatory axis at obs#2 on the same target, showing that an axis can deepen from monitoring into imaging without rotating away.
6. **A Sun-synchronous orbit gives the long viewing windows imaging sequences need.** By flying a Sun-synchronous polar orbit, TRACE kept the Sun in view for long unbroken intervals, showing that the orbit choice is the foundation for the sustained high-cadence imaging the science required.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across a distinct-palette mission.** Because TRACE reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.196 template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the high-resolution coronal imaging with a distinct coronal-blue / loop-teal / EUV-bright palette, making the distinct-palette mission a clean build.

## Surprises

- **A 30-centimeter telescope gave the highest-resolution view of the corona at the time.** A modest aperture imaging at the short EUV wavelengths reached about one-arcsecond resolution, so the catalog's first high-resolution coronal imaging came from a small dedicated telescope rather than a large one.
- **A keen-sighted raptor mirrors the high-resolution imager.** The Peregrine Falcon, whose visual acuity is among the sharpest of any animal, enters as the high-resolution-sight mirror to the telescope that resolved the corona at high resolution, so the catalog pairs a keen-sighted raptor with a high-resolution imaging mission.
- **A finely divided fern mirrors the resolved corona.** The Maidenhair Fern's fronds resolve into fine intricate filaments, so the catalog's high-resolution coronal imaging is paired with a plant that divides into the fine threads that mirror the corona's resolved loops.
