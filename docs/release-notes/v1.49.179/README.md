### Part B: American Goldfinch -- THE UNDULATION

Full deep research covering the American Goldfinch as the Washington State Bird, the Parulidae streak-breaker, the *Spinus* reunion, and the undulation:

- **Parulidae streak broken -- five consecutive warblers end at degree 43.** Five consecutive Parulidae (degrees 38-42) -- the engine's longest same-family consecutive run, matching the swallow quintet. The streak told a complete story: displacement cascade (38-40), disturbance ecology (41), generalist triumph (42). Specialist to generalist. Drawl to shimmer. It ends here. Degree 43 is Fringillidae. The finches bookend the warbler era: Fringillidae at 34 (Red Crossbill), three hummingbirds (35-37), five consecutive Parulidae (38-42), Fringillidae at 43 (American Goldfinch). The recommendation to break the streak, made at every degree from 38 through 42, is finally achieved.
- **Washington State Bird -- first official state symbol in the engine.** The American Goldfinch has been Washington's official state bird since 1951. Schoolchildren voted for it in 1928 by a wide margin. The legislature took twenty-three years to act. Three lenses on the same bird: the legislative name ("Willow Goldfinch"), the scientific name (*Spinus tristis* -- "sad thorn-bird"), and the folk name ("Wild Canary"). Each sees something different. The first government-designated symbol in a 360-degree survey of Washington's soundscape. Washington shares the American Goldfinch with Iowa and New Jersey.
- **Spinus genus reunion -- degrees 12 and 43, 31 degrees apart.** Pine Siskin (*Spinus pinus*, degree 12): E=1, irruptive, electronic zhreee, cryptic streaked brown, ascending sawtooth sweep. American Goldfinch (*Spinus tristis*, degree 43): E=4, summer resident, melodic per-chick-o-ree, brilliant lemon yellow, undulating coupled call. Same genus, opposite in energy, season, sound, plumage, and behavior. The 31-degree gap exceeds the next-widest genus gap (*Leiothlypis* at 21 degrees) by ten. Between them: the entire warbler quintet, all three hummingbirds, both waxwings, both other finches, the grosbeak, the tanager, the robin, all five swallows. The engine transformed between *Spinus* entries. The genus spans the transformation.
- **Taxonomy: the sad thorn-bird.** *Spinus* from Latin "thorn bush." *Tristis* from Latin "sad." Full translation: "Sad thorn-bird." One of the most ironic scientific names in North American ornithology: the brilliant lemon-yellow bird with the jaunty black cap and cheerful song, named "sad" because Linnaeus heard the plaintive quality in certain call notes, particularly the soft descending *tee-yee* or *dear-me*. The first species epithet in the engine that names an emotion rather than a physical feature, place, or person. Four Fringillidae now span four genera: *Spinus* (recurring), *Haemorhous*, *Loxia*, and *Spinus* again. Fringillidae ties Trochilidae at four members.
- **The sound: the per-chick-o-ree flight call and the canary song.** The flight call -- *per-chick-o-ree* or *po-ta-to-chip* -- is 3-4 downslurred notes at approximately 4 kHz, delivered at the bottom of each undulation in bounding sine-wave flight. The sound and motion are coupled. The bird rises, falls, and calls -- the flight path IS the vocalization's rhythm section. The canary song is a long, complex, rambling warble -- bright, high-pitched, phrase after phrase without obvious repetition, lasting several seconds to over a minute, the source of the "wild canary" nickname. American Goldfinches retain call learning into adulthood. Paired birds develop pair-specific flight calls -- mates assume almost identical per-chick-o-ree patterns for pair recognition. Five vocalization categories: flight call (per-chick-o-ree), complex song (canary warble), disjunct song (spaced phrases), contact call (tee-yee -- the "sad" call Linnaeus heard), and aggressive call (bee-ee for territory defense). Frequency range 2-7 kHz.
- **The undulation -- 44th acoustic element (species side), locomotion-vocalization coupling.** The undulation is the coupling of vocalization to locomotion. The per-chick-o-ree is not just a call but a call embedded in a flight pattern, locked to the sine-wave rhythm of bounding flight, delivered at the trough of each arc. The undulation opens a new acoustic category: coupled sound -- vocalization rhythmically synchronized to physical movement. Previous elements were purely vocal or purely mechanical (thrum, sonic boom). The undulation is both: a vocal call whose timing is set by the physics of bounding flight. Named "the undulation" for four resonances: flight call rhythm, canary song contour, sine-wave flight path, and annual plumage cycle.
- **Biology: the bird that waits for thistles.** One of the latest-nesting songbirds in North America. Delays breeding until thistles and composites produce mature seeds. Feeds nestlings regurgitated seeds rather than insects -- exceptional among passerines. Nest lined with thistle down so dense it holds water (vulnerability: unattended nest can drown nestlings in rain). The only cardueline finch in North America to molt twice per year: complete prebasic molt (fall, brilliant to drab) and partial prealternate molt (spring, drab to brilliant). The twice-yearly molt is energetically expensive and contributes to late nesting. Three traits form one integrated system: thistle timing controls breeding, molt timing controls breeding, seed diet controls cowbird parasitism. Cowbird chicks starve within 3-5 days on the all-seed diet -- accidental immune system made of thistle seeds.
- **Habitat: the weedy field -- first human-created habitat in engine.** The weedy field is the engine's first explicitly human-created habitat. Before European settlement, Puget Sound lowlands were dense coniferous forest -- unsuitable for goldfinches. Forest clearing for agriculture created the open, weedy fields where goldfinches thrive. The same logging that threatened the Spotted Owl (degree 4) and the Hermit Warbler (degree 40) created goldfinch habitat. The state chose a bird that prospers from the state's own transformation of the landscape. Vertical position 0-5 m -- dramatic descent from the warbler quintet's canopy heights (10-50 m). The lowest regular singing position since the Common Yellowthroat on its cattail stalk at degree 17.
- **Conservation: IUCN Least Concern, population ~44 million.** Global population approximately 42-47 million mature individuals. Partners in Flight estimates 44 million. BBS trend approximately -0.6% per year between 1966 and 2019. Some regional populations stable or increasing. Protected under Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918. Threats: habitat loss (development), pesticides, window strikes, domestic cat predation -- none at population-threatening levels.
- **Energy: E=4 continues at 20 degrees (45.5%).** E=4 for the twentieth time. Six consecutive E=4 across two families: Parulidae (38-42) then Fringillidae (43). The energy level did not change when the family changed. The *Spinus* genus brackets: E=1 at degree 12 (Pine Siskin), E=4 at degree 43 (American Goldfinch). Lower three-fifths of the energy spectrum absent for the entire second quarter of the engine. Dynamic range compression at critical severity.
- **Fourth Fringillidae ties Trochilidae at four members.** Fringillidae rises to four members: Pine Siskin (12), Cassin's Finch (33), Red Crossbill (34), American Goldfinch (43). But Fringillidae has greater diversity: four genera (three unique plus *Spinus* recurring), energy range E=1 to E=4 (the widest within any family), two irruptive and two summer species. Parulidae still leads with ten members. Eighteen families total.
- **Late nesting as integrated life history strategy.** American Goldfinch breeds June-September, later than nearly all North American passerines. The late timing is not accidental -- it is locked to thistle seed maturation (Cirsium, Carduus species), which peak in late summer. Three coupled traits: (1) seed-only nestling diet, (2) thistle-down nest construction, (3) twice-yearly molt timing. Each trait forces late timing and each is reinforced by the others. The all-seed diet also incidentally defeats Brown-headed Cowbird brood parasitism -- cowbird chicks starve on it. A defense that costs nothing because it is a side effect of the timing strategy.
- **Three names, three registers.** The "Willow Goldfinch" (legislative), "Spinus tristis" (scientific), "Wild Canary" (folk) -- the same bird holding three semantic registers. The legislature named the bird by its most visible breeding habitat. Linnaeus named the bird by its plaintive call. The public named the bird by its bright sustained song. Each name captures a different slice of the bird's presence: its place, its hidden voice, its obvious voice. Future state-symbol entries (if any) should be examined for similar multi-register naming.

- **The Parulidae streak break lands at the right moment.** After five consecutive warblers that told a complete ecological narrative (specialist to generalist), degree 43 achieves maximum contrast: new family, new habitat, new genus return. The break feels earned because the warbler quintet completed its story at degree 42. Had the break arrived at degree 41 or 40, the narrative arc would have been truncated; at degree 43 the streak concluded naturally and the Fringillidae return registered as a paradigm shift rather than a mechanical change.

## Lessons Learned

- **E=6 is structural, not inflationary.** The second E=6 was not assigned to validate a commercial career -- it was earned by a fifty-five-year trajectory of sustained maximum output that included instrument redefinition, barrier-breaking, and cultural permanence. E=6 appears only when an artist transforms what their instrument can be. Two E=6 modes, both Rock, both Seattle: this is a structural pattern, not a coincidence. The engine should track whether E=6 appears again and whether it remains exclusive to Seattle and to Rock.
- **The genre map reaches rock only when the lineage is complete.** Forty-four degrees of jazz, soul, funk, R&B, blues, neo-soul, blues rock, and gospel preceded rock-primary. This was not avoidance -- it was honest sequencing. The engine documents what flows through Seattle, and the Black American musical traditions that preceded rock in Seattle's history had to be documented first. Rock enters the record only after its ancestors are in the record.
- **Family changes do not guarantee energy changes.** The family changed from Parulidae to Fringillidae at degree 43, but the energy remained E=4 -- six consecutive E=4 across two families. The lesson: taxonomic diversity does not automatically produce dynamic diversity. The engine needs to track energy independently of taxonomy.
- **State symbols add cultural depth beyond pure taxonomy.** The children's vote of 1928, the legislative act of 1951, the three names (Willow Goldfinch, *Spinus tristis*, Wild Canary), and the ecological paradox of state-chosen habitat-created species bring layers pure ornithology cannot. Future degrees should identify any additional state or federal symbol designations and add the civic-history layer when they apply.
- **The lifer pattern was overdue.** Forty-three degrees of artist-city patterns, and the most obvious relationship -- the artist who simply lived in the city their entire career -- had not been named until now. Sometimes the simplest connection is the hardest to see because it is so fundamental. The lesson: check the taxonomy for gaps that feel like they should already be filled.
