### THE TOWNSEND'S GROUND SQUIRREL: THE GRASSLAND COLONIST

The Townsend's Ground Squirrel is a small, colonial ground squirrel of the **open grasslands** of the Pacific Northwest interior -- sagebrush steppe, bunchgrass prairie, and agricultural fields of eastern Washington, Oregon, and Idaho. At 120-300 grams (4-10 oz), it is dramatically smaller than the three marmots that precede it (75-76-77), but it shares their family (Sciuridae) and their colonial social structure.

This is the engine's **FOURTH consecutive Sciuridae** (Yellow-bellied Marmot 75, Hoary Marmot 76, Olympic Marmot 77, Townsend's Ground Squirrel 78) -- the **Sciuridae quartet**, which **matches the Mustelidae quartet** (63-66) as the longest family run in the engine. From marmots (75-77) to ground squirrel (78) -- same family, different genus (*Marmota* to *Urocitellus*), dramatically different habitat (alpine to grassland).

This is also the engine's **first true grassland species** -- a landmark habitat shift. After four consecutive alpine degrees (74-77), degree 78 descends to open grassland. The alpine run ends. A new habitat opens.

### TAXONOMY: *Urocitellus townsendii*

- **Urocitellus** -- from Greek *oura* ("tail") + Latin *citellus* ("ground squirrel," diminutive of *citus*, "swift"). The genus was split from the broader *Spermophilus* in 2009 based on molecular phylogenetics.
- **townsendii** -- honors **John Kirk Townsend** (1809-1851), a Philadelphia naturalist who explored the Columbia River region in 1834 with botanist Thomas Nuttall on the Nathaniel Wyeth expedition. The ground squirrel was described by Bachman in 1839 from Townsend's Columbia River specimens.
- **Common names:** Townsend's Ground Squirrel. Sometimes locally called "picket pin" for its habit of standing erect like a picket pin.

Classification:
- **Class:** Mammalia (17th mammalian entry; 15th species)
- **Order:** Rodentia -- FIFTH Rodentia in the engine
- **Family:** Sciuridae -- FOURTH CONSECUTIVE (75-76-77-78). The Sciuridae quartet. Ties Mustelidae (63-66) for longest family run
- **Genus:** *Urocitellus* -- NEW GENUS. Ground squirrels, not marmots. The shift from *Marmota* (75-77) to *Urocitellus* (78)
- **Species:** *U. townsendii* -- distributed across the Columbia Basin and Snake River Plain

Four Sciuridae. Four acoustic signatures. The quartet spans alpine to grassland.

### THE SOUND: CHIRPING TRILL ALARM -- 79th Species-Side Acoustic Element

The Townsend's Ground Squirrel's alarm call is a **chirping trill** -- a rapid series of short, sharp chirps delivered in quick succession:
- **Frequency range:** approximately 4,000-8,000 Hz
- **Duration:** 0.5-2.0 seconds for a trill burst. Individual chirps within the trill: 0.05-0.1 seconds each
- **Structure:** Rapid repetitive chirps -- a stuttering, machine-gun quality. Unlike the marmots' sustained or musical calls, the ground squirrel's alarm is a rapid-fire burst
- **Amplitude:** **E=4** -- moderate to loud. Audible at 100-200 meters across open grassland. Flat, treeless habitat allows sound to carry
- **Function:** Colony alarm. One squirrel's trill triggers colony-wide alert response -- all visible squirrels dive for burrows. Distributed early warning network

### ECOLOGY: THE GRASSLAND COLONIST

**Habitat -- First True Grassland Species:** Open grassland, sagebrush steppe, bunchgrass prairie, agricultural fields. Elevation 200-1,500 meters. The OPPOSITE of alpine. Vegetation: sagebrush (*Artemisia*), bunchgrass, agricultural crops. Range: Columbia Basin and Snake River Plain -- eastern Washington, eastern Oregon, southern Idaho, northern Nevada, northern Utah. The sagebrush steppe is one of the most threatened ecosystems in North America.

**Double Dormancy -- Estivation + Hibernation:** Active only 4-5 months per year (approximately February-July). Estivates in late summer when vegetation dries and temperatures rise. Hibernates through autumn and winter. Combined dormancy: 7-8 months total. Contrast with marmots: marmots hibernate in winter but are active in summer. The Townsend's Ground Squirrel estivates in summer AND hibernates in winter -- unique in the engine.

**Colonial Structure:** Dense colonies, hundreds of individuals per hectare in suitable habitat. Complex underground burrow networks. Multiple entrances. The chirping trill alarm system functions as a distributed sensor network.

**Keystone Prey Species:** Supports multiple predators already in the engine -- Red Fox (71), Short-tailed Weasel (67), Long-tailed Weasel (68), plus raptors, badgers, and coyotes. Colonial density makes it a reliable food source for entire predator communities.
