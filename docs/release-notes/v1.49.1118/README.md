## Mission Overview

Sentinel-6 Michael Freilich is the obs#21 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — the US-EU NASA/NOAA + ESA/EUMETSAT/CNES ocean-surface-topography reference radar-altimetry mission, a free-flying satellite that measures the height of the sea surface to within a few centimetres and is the current prime reference for global mean sea level. ★ It is a NASA Earth-science mission flown as a US-EU partnership: NASA and NOAA on the US side and ESA, EUMETSAT, and CNES on the European side, part of the EU Copernicus programme; it is the operational successor that took the reference-altimetry baton from Jason-3 in 2022 and carries forward the TOPEX/Poseidon (1992) → Jason-1 (2001) → Jason-2 (2008) → Jason-3 (2016) → Sentinel-6 (2020) reference altimetry record. Sentinel-6 carries the Poseidon-4 nadir-looking Ku-band and C-band radar altimeter — operating in SAR (synthetic-aperture / delay-Doppler) mode interleaved with conventional low-resolution mode (LRM), which sharpens the along-track resolution over Jason-3's LRM-only Poseidon-3B — the Advanced Microwave Radiometer for Climate (AMR-C) for the wet-tropospheric correction, DORIS, GNSS, and a Laser Retroreflector Array (LRA) for precise orbit determination, and a GNSS-Radio-Occultation (GNSS-RO) receiver profiling atmospheric temperature and humidity alongside the altimeter — together yielding sea-surface height to within a few centimetres. The mission launched 21 November 2020 on a SpaceX Falcon 9 (Block 5) from Vandenberg SLC-4E, and flies as a free-flying satellite in the TOPEX/Jason reference orbit (~1336 km altitude, 66° inclination, ~10-day repeat). The story is the Falcon 9 launch from Vandenberg, the ascent to the ~1336 km reference orbit, the Poseidon-4 Ku/C-band pulse going straight down to the sea surface in SAR mode interleaved with low-resolution mode and the echo returning, the range measurement corrected for the ionosphere (by the dual frequency), the wet troposphere (by the AMR-C), and the orbit (by DORIS/GNSS/LRA) to yield sea-surface height, the GNSS-RO receiver sounding the atmosphere alongside, and the global map of ocean surface topography and rising mean sea level being gathered from above as the current reference. The science is central: from the reference orbit Sentinel-6 ranges the sea surface — the Poseidon-4 altimeter firing a nadir Ku/C-band pulse down to the water in SAR mode interleaved with LRM and timing the echo — and turns the corrected range into sea-surface height, ocean circulation, and the long record of global mean sea-level rise. ★★★ Sentinel-6 is OPERATING, so the catalog frames its whole story in the PRESENT (Sentinel-6 orbits the Earth, ranges the sea surface, measures sea-surface height, and maps the rising global sea); it launched 21 November 2020, entered service in mid-2021, and became the prime Reference Altimetry Mission for global mean sea level in 2022 (the science-team recommendation on 24 March 2022, the formal declaration on 7 April 2022, with Jason-3 moving to an interleaved orbit) — a PAST sub-event — and the mission continues today as the prime reference, with a twin, Sentinel-6B, scheduled to follow to extend the record. ## Surprises

- **A new SAR-mode altimeter on the same old reference orbit.** Sentinel-6 flies the same ~1336 km / 66° / ~10-day reference orbit as Jason-3, the same orbit TOPEX/Poseidon and the whole Jason series have flown — but its Poseidon-4 altimeter runs in SAR (synthetic-aperture) mode interleaved with the conventional low-resolution mode the earlier missions used, sharpening the along-track resolution while staying tied to the very same reference track. The continuity is in the orbit and the record; the advance is in the instrument.
- **One satellite carries an altimeter and an atmosphere-sounder at once.** Sentinel-6 is a sea-surface altimeter, but it also carries a GNSS-Radio-Occultation receiver that tracks GNSS signals as they set behind the Earth's limb and bends them into profiles of atmospheric temperature and humidity — a second, quite different measurement riding alongside the altimetry on the same free-flyer.
- **A reference record stitched across five missions and three decades.** Sentinel-6 is not measuring the sea-level rise alone — it is the fifth link in a single reference record, TOPEX/Poseidon (1992) → Jason-1 → Jason-2 → Jason-3 → Sentinel-6, each cross-calibrated to the last on the same reference orbit so the three-decade global-mean-sea-level time series stays consistent. The point is the continuity: a single, stable reference track of the sea's height across more than thirty years, now carried by the current prime reference.
- **A mission that took the lead and kept its predecessor flying.** Sentinel-6 launched in November 2020 and flew in tandem with Jason-3; in 2022 it became the prime Reference Altimetry Mission for global mean sea level (the science-team recommendation 24 March 2022, the formal declaration 7 April 2022), and Jason-3 moved to an interleaved orbit — the new mission taking the lead from the old one and then flying alongside it, interleaved and tandem, to densify the coverage, with a twin Sentinel-6B scheduled to follow.
- **A PNW marine mammal of the sea surface and the rocky shore the rising sea reaches.** The Steller Sea Lion (*Eumetopias jubatus*), the largest of the sea lions, a North Pacific marine mammal, hauls out on rocky islands and surf-washed reefs along the Pacific Northwest coast and forages in coastal waters, rising to the surface to breathe between dives — its life lived at the sea surface and on the rocky shore the sea washes, the very surface whose height Sentinel-6 maps from above (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A foredune wildflower at the top of the beach the rising sea slowly approaches.** The Yellow Sand-verbena (*Abronia latifolia*, Yellow Sand-verbena), a PNW coastal foredune wildflower of the open sandy beach, spreads a low trailing mat with bright yellow flower-heads that anchors the sand at the top of the beach just above the reach of the tide — a plant of the foredune that the rising global sea, the sea-level rise Sentinel-6 tracks, slowly approaches — bound with the throughline of the edge of the rising sea.

## Dedication

v1.301 Sentinel-6 Michael Freilich is dedicated to the people at NASA, NOAA, ESA, EUMETSAT, and CNES, and across the ocean-altimetry science teams, who built and fly the satellite that carries the long record of the height of the sea forward as the current reference. To the engineers in Europe who built the platform and the Poseidon-4 altimeter that ranges in synthetic-aperture mode, and to the NASA and JPL teams who built the radiometer, the radio-occultation receiver, and the precise-orbit hardware that turn a single pulse into a sea-surface height good to a few centimetres. The crews who carried the satellite aloft on a Falcon 9 from Vandenberg in November 2020 and the controllers who keep it on its reference orbit. The scientists who stitch TOPEX/Poseidon, Jason-1, Jason-2, Jason-3, and Sentinel-6 into one consistent reference record, cycle by cycle, and who flew Sentinel-6 alongside Jason-3 and then handed it the reference role in 2022. And to Michael Freilich, for whom the mission is named. From the reference orbit the altimeter still reaches down to the moving surface of the sea, and the long record of the rising global ocean — its height, its circulation, its slow rise — belongs to them.
