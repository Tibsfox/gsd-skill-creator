- **The Pacific Northwest Continental Shelf and Canyon Geology:** The **Pacific Northwest continental shelf** is the underwater geologic terrain that extends westward from the coastline of **Washington**, **Oregon**, and northern **California** before dropping off into the deeper abyssal basin of the Pacific Ocean. The shelf is incised by submarine canyons cut during Pleistocene sea-level lowstands when rivers delivered their sediment load directly to what was then the shelf edge, and the canyons are maintained by ongoing turbidity current and sediment density flow driven by the seasonal wind patterns, the Columbia River sediment delivery, and the California Current. The canyon systems are mapped by NOAA bathymetric surveys, studied by the **University of Washington School of Oceanography**, the **Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution**, and the **USGS Pacific Coastal and Marine Science Center**, and form a major component of the Pacific Northwest's marine-geologic infrastructure.
- **Quinault Canyon offshore of the Olympic Peninsula:** The **Quinault Canyon** lies offshore of the **Olympic Peninsula** roughly due west of the **Quinault Indian Reservation** and **Lake Quinault**. The canyon was cut during Pleistocene lowstands by the ancestral Quinault River flow and is presently maintained by sediment input from the Quinault River plume and the coastal currents that carry Olympic Peninsula runoff sediment southward and offshore. The Quinault Canyon's acoustic signature contributes to the northern-shelf component of the infrasonic acoustic field, and its position offshore of reservation lands anchors the canyon in the cultural and treaty geography of the **Quinault Indian Nation** whose treaty-reserved ocean fishing rights extend across the canyon's surface waters.
- **Willapa Canyon south of Grays Harbor:** The **Willapa Canyon** lies south of **Grays Harbor** and **Willapa Bay**, cut by the ancestral Willapa River and related regional drainage during Pleistocene lowstands, and presently maintained by coastal sediment flux and episodic turbidity currents. The Willapa Canyon's relatively shallow shelf incision reflects the modest modern sediment delivery from the Willapa River system compared to the much larger Columbia River system to the south. The canyon's acoustic signature contributes the central-shelf component of the Pacific Northwest infrasonic acoustic field.
- **Astoria Canyon south of the Columbia River Mouth:** The **Astoria Canyon** lies south of the **Columbia River** mouth and is the deepest and most active of the three Pacific Northwest shelf canyons. The canyon is maintained by the Columbia River's enormous sediment output — approximately 10 million metric tons per year from the continental interior to the river mouth — which during Pleistocene lowstands passed directly into the canyon head and which during modern highstands is partially intercepted by the Columbia River estuary before reaching the canyon. The Astoria Canyon's acoustic signature is the largest per-canyon component of the Pacific Northwest infrasonic acoustic field and is the most frequently studied of the three canyons in the oceanographic literature.
- **Turbidity Current as Primary Acoustic Source Mechanism:** The primary acoustic source mechanism for the submarine canyon infrasonic flow is the **turbidity current** — a density-driven downslope flow of water-plus-sediment that travels along the canyon axis from the shallow canyon head to the deep canyon mouth. Turbidity currents are density-driven rather than momentum-driven: the gravitational pull on the elevated-density sediment-water mixture exceeds the gravitational pull on the surrounding clearer water, producing a downslope flow that propagates as a gravity current until the sediment deposits out of suspension and the density contrast dissipates. The flow itself generates acoustic energy through particle-particle collision, particle-wall interaction, and the overall fluid-dynamic turbulence of the flow body. The acoustic energy is radiated into both the overlying water column (measurable by hydrophones) and the underlying seafloor sediments (measurable by ocean-bottom seismometers).
- **Infrasonic Frequency Regime and Sub-Audible Propagation:** The submarine canyon acoustic signature occupies the infrasonic regime — frequencies below approximately **20 Hz**, below the conventional lower edge of human hearing. The measurement infrastructure for this regime is correspondingly different from audible-range instrumentation: hydrophone arrays for water column propagation, ocean-bottom seismometers for seafloor sediment propagation, specialized infrasonic-band acoustic analyzers for signal processing. At 20 Hz and below, the acoustic wavelengths in water are long (75 meters at 20 Hz, 750 meters at 2 Hz), and the signals propagate across continental-shelf distances with modest attenuation. The canyon signal is therefore detectable across tens to hundreds of kilometers from the canyon itself, integrated with signals from other canyons and other shelf-wide acoustic sources into the overall Pacific Northwest infrasonic acoustic field.
- **E=2 Placement and the Arc's Low-Energy Midpoint:** **Submarine Canyon Infrasonic Flow** lands at **E=2**, the lowest amplitude in the geophysics arc and among the lowest amplitudes in the engine's SPS catalog. The E=2 placement is structurally deliberate: the arc's valley-floor position corresponds to its lowest-amplitude entry, and the E=2 register is the engine's designated low-energy-but-structurally-real amplitude zone where the documented process is genuine, continuous, and geophysically significant but modest in per-moment energy compared to the high-amplitude peaks elsewhere in the engine's record. The placement is not a diminishment — the submarine canyon is a genuine Pacific Northwest acoustic feature at its own appropriate amplitude — but an honest accounting of the energy register at which the process operates.
