## Summary

**Skin Yard** arrive at E=7 -- the Seattle proto-metal band founded in 1985 by Jack Endino (guitar), Ben McMillan (vocals), Matt Cameron (drums, before Soundgarden), and Daniel House (bass, later of Malfunkshun and Catbutt). Skin Yard are one of the founding bands of the Seattle sound: not the most celebrated, not the most commercially successful, but among the most structurally important to what followed. Their contribution to the history of Seattle rock is documented in two ways: through their own music -- four albums of dense, slow, heavy rock that sat at the boundary between metal and what would become grunge -- and through Jack Endino, their guitarist, who became the defining record producer of the Seattle scene's formative period. E=7 for Skin Yard reflects the weight and density of their catalog: slow-tempo proto-metal with thick production and genuine menace, not as explosively high-energy as Mudhoney at E=8 but consistently heavier and more deliberate in its approach than the E=6 bands of the sequence.

Jack Endino is the most important producer in Seattle rock history. He recorded and produced the albums that defined the sound of the Pacific Northwest in the late 1980s and early 1990s: Nirvana's *Bleach* (1989), Mudhoney's *Superfuzz Bigmuff* (1988), Soundgarden's *Screaming Life* (1987), Green River's *Dry as a Bone* (1987), the U-Men, Skin Yard's own records, and dozens of other releases on Sub Pop and independent labels. The Endino production aesthetic -- raw, low-budget, maximally honest, built around the actual sound of a band playing in a room rather than a constructed studio illusion -- is the aesthetic of Sub Pop's early catalog, and it influenced the global sound of alternative rock in ways that extended far beyond the Pacific Northwest. He recorded *Bleach* for approximately $606 over thirty hours at Reciprocal Recording in Seattle. That $606 is one of the most significant investments in the history of popular music. The engine documents Skin Yard at degree 264 in full acknowledgment that the band is, in part, a vehicle for documenting Endino's contribution. It is not a diminishment of the band -- Skin Yard's music stands on its own -- but it is an honest accounting of what degree 264 is commemorating.

Matt Cameron's appearance in Skin Yard's early lineup is a further document of how interconnected the Seattle scene was in the mid-1980s: Cameron would go on to join Soundgarden in 1986 and Pearl Jam in 1998, becoming the only drummer to hold a full-time position in two bands that simultaneously achieved stadium-level commercial success. His work in Skin Yard, before any of that, is where the Seattle 360 engine first encounters him on record.

On the soundscape side, **Submarine Canyon Infrasonic Flow** arrives at E=2 -- the infrasonic acoustic signature produced by sediment and water movement through the submarine canyon systems of the Pacific Northwest continental shelf. Submarine canyons -- the Quinault Canyon, the Willapa Canyon, the Astoria Canyon south of the Columbia -- are deep underwater gorges cut by ancient river systems and maintained by ongoing turbidity currents and sediment flows. The sound produced by these processes is infrasonic: propagating at frequencies below human hearing through the water column and into the seafloor sediments, measurable by hydrophones and ocean bottom seismometers but not directly audible to human ears. E=2 is the correct assignment: the process is real, continuous, and geophysically significant, but it operates at the extreme low-frequency edge of the acoustic record, and its energy at any given moment is modest. The Osceola lahar at E=7 was the most energetic geophysical event the engine has documented thus far in the arc; the submarine canyon at E=2 is the most subtle. Both are genuine Pacific Northwest acoustic events; the engine treats them with equal seriousness.

The pairing at degree 264 -- the proto-metal density of Skin Yard alongside the deep, slow, continuous infrasonic flow of a submarine canyon -- is structurally honest. Skin Yard's music is the acoustic equivalent of geological process: heavy, slow, deliberate, operating at the lower end of the frequency range where weight accumulates rather than velocity.
