This is what *Leaves Turn Inside You* is. This is why it occupies the final position in the Seattle 360 engine's 360-degree arc.

Unwound's relationship to the Pacific Northwest music tradition is not the relationship of the bands that defined the tradition's commercial peak or its critical reputation in the wider world. They were not Nirvana or Soundgarden or Pearl Jam or Sleater-Kinney in terms of audience scale or critical visibility at the time of their activity. They were a band that made records of increasing structural ambition and decreasing commercial accessibility, moving from the post-hardcore urgency of *New Plastic Ideas* (1994) through the noise-rock density of *Repetition* (1996) and *Challenge for a Civilized Society* (1998) and *Sound as Time: Temporary Surroundings* (1995) into the expanded, patient, melancholy architecture of *Leaves Turn Inside You* -- a record that operates at a slower tempo than everything Unwound had previously made, in a wider dynamic range, with more space inside the sound and more weight in that space, as if the band knew at some level that they were making a record that needed to hold everything that had come before it.

*Leaves Turn Inside You* is two discs, twenty tracks, seventy-eight minutes. The production (by Unwound and Steve Fisk, Kill Rock Stars) is deliberately underdense in the way that late-career records by bands approaching dissolution sometimes are: there is more silence inside the sound than on any previous Unwound record, more room between guitar and bass and drums and Trosper's voice, as if the band's sonic architecture had begun to let air in. Trosper's voice on *Leaves Turn Inside You* has moved away from the hardcore-adjacent delivery of the early records toward something quieter and more defeated, not in the sense of artistic defeat but in the sense of a voice that has finished fighting and begun simply speaking. "Off This Century." "December." "Below the Salt." "The Jungle."
