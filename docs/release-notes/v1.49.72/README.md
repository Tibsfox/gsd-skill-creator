## Summary

The 72nd Research project and the fifth radio station entry. KUB (KUBE 93.3 FM Seattle) traces 62 years of the 93.3 MHz frequency — from KBLE-FM's 1964 sign-on through its transformation into Seattle's first rhythmic CHR station in 1991, the hip-hop era that launched Sir Mix-A-Lot to a Grammy, and the corporate era that exiled the format three times (2016, 2018, 2022) before sending the KUBE call letters to Pueblo, Colorado while the frequency became KJR-FM sports.

Four modules map the arc: the technical infrastructure (6,600W to 100kW on Cougar Mountain, Blue Optimod audio chain, 2016 signal loss analysis), the people (Nasty Nes Rodriguez's FreshTracks and NastyMix Records, Sir Mix-A-Lot from the Central District, the mentorship chain from Washington Hall), the hip-hop era (1991 format shift, "Baby Got Back" at #1, the Central District ecosystem, the cassette tape economy), and the corporate era (Clear Channel acquisition, three format exiles, portfolio optimization mechanics, the HD2 afterlife).

KUB completes the five-station radio sub-cluster alongside C89 (student/electronic), KSM (border/classic rock), WLF (country), and KPZ (heritage pop). Together they map the full spectrum of PNW broadcasting: different formats, different ownership models, different relationships to community, same fundamental lesson — the frequency outlasts every format programmed on it.

Named "Three Exiles" — because KUBE's rhythmic format was removed three times (2016, 2018, 2022) by corporate owners optimizing for portfolio metrics rather than community service. Each time the format returned because the audience demanded it. The third exile was permanent — the call letters were sent to Colorado.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

1. **Four modules match this station's story density.** KUBE's arc is tight: one frequency, one pivotal format, one corporate trajectory. Four modules cover it without padding. The radio sub-cluster now has studies at 4, 5, and 6 modules — the count matches the subject.

2. **The Nasty Nes biography anchors the people module.** A Filipino immigrant who created Seattle's first hip-hop radio show, founded the label that launched Sir Mix-A-Lot, and whose 2025 death was mourned across the PNW music community — Nes's story IS the story of KUBE. Without him, the station was just a frequency.

3. **The three exiles reveal corporate radio mechanics.** Each exile follows the same pattern: portfolio analysis determines that a different format generates higher CPM in the same demographic. The community response (listener petitions, social media campaigns) is ignored because the decision model doesn't include community sentiment as a variable. The pattern is structural, not personal.

### What Could Be Better

1. **The Central District ecosystem deserves its own study.** The neighborhood that produced Sir Mix-A-Lot, connected to military bases, sustained the cassette economy, and served as the geographic heart of Seattle hip-hop culture is only partially mapped in Module 03. A dedicated Central District cultural study would strengthen the entire radio sub-cluster.

### Lessons Learned

1. **Corporate ownership and community service are structurally incompatible in radio.** KUBE's three exiles demonstrate that portfolio-optimized ownership will always sacrifice community-serving formats when the numbers favor a different use of the frequency. The counter-models (C89.5's school district ownership, KISM's Saga Goldilocks strategy) survive because they have different optimization functions.

2. **The frequency persists after the identity is stripped.** 93.3 MHz on Cougar Mountain still broadcasts at 100kW. The KUBE call letters are in Pueblo. The format is sports. The Central District community that KUBE served still exists. The lesson: infrastructure without community governance becomes extractable. Same lesson as WLF's bankruptcy module, from a different angle.
