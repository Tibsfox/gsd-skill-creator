   New Horizons sustains the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis at obs#8 (the seventh consecutive sustain on the axis opened by Pioneer 10, sustained by Pioneer 11, Voyager 1, Voyager 2, Galileo, Cassini-Huygens, and Juno), so only the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION counter advances (obs#7 → obs#8) and the substrate-axis-rotation discipline holds UNCHANGED at obs#101 (no NEW rotation observed). 3. **New Horizons is a NASA New Frontiers mission operated by the Johns Hopkins APL — NOT JPL.**
   The mission is the first of NASA's New Frontiers program, built and operated by the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory (an intra-axis management distinction, like the Ames-versus-JPL distinction among the earlier siblings), with principal investigator Alan Stern of the Southwest Research Institute, and it is powered by a single radioisotope thermoelectric generator; the catalog states this precisely throughout.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12505_

4. **New Horizons launched on an Atlas V 551 (19 January 2006) at the fastest Earth-departure speed ever; it used a Jupiter gravity assist (28 February 2007).**
   It launched aboard an Atlas V 551 with a STAR 48B third stage from Cape Canaveral and used a Jupiter gravity assist on 28 February 2007 to speed the journey by roughly three years; the catalog states this precisely and notes that the Jupiter flyby was a gravity assist, NOT a first (Pioneer 10 was first, in 1973). Juno also launched on an Atlas V 551 but on a different date; the fastest-launch record is attributed to New Horizons.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12506_

5. **The Pluto achievement is the first to fly by / explore Pluto, not "the first to a dwarf planet.**
   On 14 July 2015 New Horizons became the first spacecraft to fly by Pluto; the catalog scopes this strictly to Pluto (it flew by, never orbited) and explicitly does NOT call it the first to a dwarf planet — the Dawn mission reached the dwarf planet Ceres in March 2015, about four months earlier. New Horizons characterized the small moons up close but did NOT discover them (Hubble found Styx and Kerberos during the cruise).
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12507_

6. **The Kuiper Belt achievement is the first close flyby of a small / cold-classical Kuiper Belt object — distinct from the Pluto anchor.**
   On 1 January 2019 New Horizons flew by Arrokoth; the catalog scopes this as the first close flyby of a small, primordial cold-classical Kuiper Belt object and the most distant object ever explored up close, kept distinct from the Pluto anchor (Pluto is itself a Kuiper Belt object and dwarf planet). Arrokoth is a two-lobed contact binary.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12508_

7. **New Horizons holds the fastest LAUNCH speed — not the fastest spacecraft ever.**
   It left Earth at the highest launch / Earth-departure speed ever achieved; the catalog scopes this STRICTLY to the launch speed and explicitly does NOT call it the fastest spacecraft ever (the Parker Solar Probe reaches far higher speeds via solar gravity assists).

9. **The mission is framed present-tense per verified status, with the long hibernation noted.**
   New Horizons continues its extended mission through the Kuiper Belt (now about 60 AU from the Sun); as of 2025–2026 it is operating but in a long hibernation between encounters (begun August 2025), and is expected to continue operating into the 2040s. The catalog frames it present-tense while noting the hibernation and does not imply continuous active observation right now.
