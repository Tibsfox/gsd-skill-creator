## Mission Overview

New Horizons is the obs#8 sustain of the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA deep-space mission to Pluto and the Kuiper Belt whose defining achievements are being the first spacecraft to fly by and explore Pluto, the first to fly by a Kuiper Belt object, and the fastest spacecraft ever launched from Earth. It is the first mission of NASA's New Frontiers program, built and operated by the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory (APL — NOT JPL; APL designed, built, and operates it), with principal investigator Alan Stern of the Southwest Research Institute. A compact, fast-cruising flyby probe powered by a single radioisotope thermoelectric generator, it launched aboard an Atlas V 551 (with a STAR 48B third stage) from Cape Canaveral (Space Launch Complex 41) on 19 January 2006, leaving Earth at the highest launch speed ever achieved and passing the Moon's distance in about nine hours. A Jupiter gravity assist on 28 February 2007 (about 2.3 million km from Jupiter) sped the journey by roughly three years; that flyby was a gravity assist, NOT a first flyby of Jupiter (Pioneer 10 was first, in 1973). New Horizons carried a focused remote-sensing payload: the Long Range Reconnaissance Imager (LORRI) telescopic camera; the Ralph imager (the MVIC color/panchromatic mapper and the LEISA infrared spectral imager); the Alice ultraviolet imaging spectrometer; the Radio Science Experiment (REX); the Solar Wind Around Pluto (SWAP) plasma instrument; the Pluto Energetic Particle Spectrometer Science Investigation (PEPSSI); and the Venetia Burney Student Dust Counter (SDC). The story is the long fast cruise outward and the two great encounters at the frontier. On 14 July 2015 New Horizons made its closest approach to Pluto — about 7,800 km / 4,800 miles above the surface, near 11:49 UT — becoming the first spacecraft to fly by and explore Pluto and returning the first close-up data on Pluto and its moons (Charon, Styx, Nix, Kerberos, and Hydra). The scoping is load-bearing: this is the first to fly by / explore PLUTO, NOT "the first to a dwarf planet" (the Dawn mission reached the dwarf planet Ceres in March 2015, about four months earlier), and New Horizons characterized the small moons up close but did NOT discover them (Hubble found Styx and Kerberos during the cruise, in 2012 and 2011). On 1 January 2019 it made its closest approach to Arrokoth (formerly 2014 MU69; closest approach about 3,500 km), at about 44 AU from the Sun — the first close flyby of a small, primordial cold-classical Kuiper Belt object and the most distant object ever explored up close; Arrokoth is a two-lobed contact binary. The launch speed is the highest Earth-departure speed ever achieved by a spacecraft (about 58,000 km/h, roughly 36,000 mph relative to Earth) — scoped strictly to the LAUNCH speed, NOT "the fastest spacecraft ever" (the Parker Solar Probe reaches far higher speeds via solar gravity assists). New Horizons continues its extended mission through the Kuiper Belt (now about 60 AU from the Sun); as of 2025–2026 it is operating but in a long hibernation period between encounters (begun August 2025), framed present-tense per its verified status while noting the hibernation. ## Surprises

- **One spacecraft left Earth faster than anything before it.** New Horizons launched on 19 January 2006 aboard an Atlas V 551 with a STAR 48B third stage, leaving Earth at the highest launch / Earth-departure speed ever achieved (about 58,000 km/h, roughly 36,000 mph relative to Earth) — fast enough to pass the Moon's distance in about nine hours, the fastest launch speed ever set.
- **A flyby probe returned the first close-up reconnaissance of Pluto.** On 14 July 2015 New Horizons flew about 7,800 km / 4,800 miles above Pluto's surface, returning the first close-up data on Pluto and its moons (Charon, Styx, Nix, Kerberos, and Hydra) — the first reconnaissance of the Pluto system, revealing the heart-shaped plain (Tombaugh Regio) and the icy mountains.
- **A spacecraft explored a small, pristine body at the far edge of the Kuiper Belt.** On 1 January 2019 New Horizons flew within about 3,500 km of Arrokoth (formerly 2014 MU69), at about 44 AU from the Sun — the first close flyby of a small, primordial cold-classical Kuiper Belt object and the most distant object ever explored up close, a two-lobed contact binary preserved from the early solar system.
- **The small moons of Pluto were found from Earth before the flyby, not by the spacecraft.** New Horizons characterized Charon, Styx, Nix, Kerberos, and Hydra up close, but Styx and Kerberos were discovered by the Hubble Space Telescope during the cruise (in 2012 and 2011), so the spacecraft characterized rather than discovered the small moons.
- **A swift tree bat travels far through the dark of the forest night.** The Silver-haired Bat (*Lasionycteris noctivagans*), a swift, agile tree bat of Pacific Northwest forests and a monotypic genus, migrates long distances and forages on the wing through the dark, roosting alone in loose bark, cavities, and foliage (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of New Horizons reaching swiftly and far across the dark of the outer solar system.
- **A small cushion wildflower clings at the cold far upper edge of where plants can grow.** Tolmie's Saxifrage (*Micranthes tolmiei*), a small mat- or cushion-forming alpine wildflower of cold, rocky high Pacific Northwest summits, talus, and snowbeds (the Cascades and Olympics), holds the cold far upper edge of where plants can grow, with small white star-flowers and fleshy succulent leaves (ecological description only) — a mirror of New Horizons reaching the coldest, most distant small worlds at the frontier of the planetary system.

v1.257 New Horizons is dedicated to the people across NASA, the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory, the Southwest Research Institute, and the mission's many partners who built and flew a small fast probe to the edge of the planetary system and made the first reconnaissance of two distant worlds. The engineers who gave the spacecraft its single radioisotope generator so it could work far from the Sun; the launch team who lit the way off Cape Canaveral aboard an Atlas V 551 in January 2006, sending it off Earth faster than anything before it; the navigators who threaded the Jupiter gravity assist in 2007 and aimed the long cruise across the outer solar system; the scientists behind the cameras and spectrometers who returned the first close views of Pluto and its moons in July 2015, the heart-shaped plain and the icy mountains; the teams who guided the New Year's flyby of the small two-lobed Arrokoth on 1 January 2019, the most distant world ever seen up close; and the operators who keep watch over the probe as it travels onward through the Kuiper Belt. They reached the cold far edge, swiftly, and looked.
