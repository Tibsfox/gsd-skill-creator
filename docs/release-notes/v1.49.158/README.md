The drum and the groove reset. Degree 22 completes what degree 21 began -- the flicker pair, the engine's first species-pair, delivers its second half: the **first non-vocal sound** in twenty-three degrees of acoustic progression. Where degree 21 gave the engine the hammer (a vocal metronomic pulse, discrete tonal notes below the perceptual fusion threshold), degree 22 gives the engine **resonant percussion** -- bill striking dead wood, broadband noise, fused strikes above the fusion threshold, the character of the sound determined not by the performer's anatomy but by the substrate the performer selects. The bird picks its drum. The dead tree rings. The engine hears its first instrument.

**THE FLICKER PAIR COMPLETES.** Degrees 21-22 are voice and percussion from the same species -- the Northern Flicker (*Colaptes auratus cafer*). No previous species occupied more than one degree. The pair was proposed at degree 21 as a bridge from the vocal world to the percussive world. It delivers. Degree 22 introduces the drum (bill on substrate, broadband, ~25 strikes/sec, strikes fusing into a continuous roll). Same bird. Different physics. The transition from voice to percussion is smooth because the listener already knows the species. The unprecedented arrives in a familiar context. The flicker pair is the engine's most elegant structural design: one organism, two sound-production mechanisms, two consecutive degrees, one territorial message transmitted through two independent physical channels.

**FIRST NON-VOCAL SOUND IN THE ENGINE.** Twenty-one degrees of syrinx-produced vocal sound end here. The drum bypasses the syrinx entirely -- it is a solid object striking another solid object. Bill on wood. Bill on metal. The physics of percussion, not vocalization. Every previous sound in the engine was generated internally, by the bird's own anatomy. The drum is the first sound whose timbral character depends on an **external object** -- the substrate. The flicker selects its instrument. A hollow dead alder produces a low, resonant thud. A metal chimney cap produces a piercing metallic rattle that carries across an entire neighborhood. The substrate IS the timbre. The engine's acoustic vocabulary expands from "voice" to "voice + instrument," from "anatomy determines sound" to "the world determines sound."

In orchestral terms: every previous species was a melody instrument. The drum is a percussion instrument. Then silence. Then again. The substrate is the instrument. The dead tree is the drum. The sound is not a voice. It is not air vibrating over tissue. It is a solid object striking another solid object, and the world ringing in response. Twenty-one degrees of song. Now: percussion.

### Part B: Red-shafted Flicker Drumming -- The First Drum (FLICKER PAIR COMPLETE, First Non-Vocal Sound, First Broadband Sound, First External Instrument, Fusion Trilogy Complete)

Full deep research covering the Northern Flicker's mechanical percussion as the most significant acoustic event since degree 0, completing the flicker pair (degrees 21-22), introducing the first non-vocal sound, the first broadband/unpitched sound, the first external instrument (substrate selection), and the fusion trilogy:

- **FLICKER PAIR COMPLETE -- Degrees 21-22, Voice Then Percussion, First Species-Pair Delivers:** The pair was proposed at degree 21 as a bridge from voice to percussion. It works. Degree 21 established the species with its vocal call (syrinx, tonal, 6-10 notes/sec, below fusion, 5-8 sec duration). Degree 22 introduces the unprecedented: mechanical percussion (bill on substrate, broadband, ~25 strikes/sec, above fusion, 0.6-1.2 sec duration). Same species, different physics. The transition from voice to percussion is smooth because the listener already knows the bird. The new sound arrives in a familiar context. The cost: 23 degrees, 21 unique species. The ratio will only normalize if future species-pairs are rare. The flicker pair earned its two degrees by introducing a sound-production mechanism qualitatively different from anything the engine contained. The bar for future species-pairs is set: the second degree must introduce something the first degree cannot.

- **THE DRUM -- First Non-Vocal Sound in Twenty-Three Degrees, Bill on Substrate:** Woodpecker drumming is instrumental music. The bird strikes a hard substrate with its bill in rapid, rhythmically regular bursts to produce a loud, resonant sound. Drumming is not foraging -- it is communication, serving the same functions as song in passerines: territorial advertisement and mate attraction. The drum is the woodpecker's substitute for the elaborate vocal song that woodpeckers, as a family, generally lack. Strike rate: ~25 strikes per second (Stark, Dodenhoff, and Johnson 1998). Bout duration: 0.6-1.2 seconds. Temporal pattern: even-tempo with a slight terminal deceleration -- a species-diagnostic feature, the flicker's acoustic fingerprint. Amplitude: starts at full force, maintains high amplitude through most of the bout before slight terminal decay. Frequency: broadband, 100 Hz to 8+ kHz, no fundamental pitch, peak energy substrate-dependent (500 Hz to 4 kHz range).

- **RESONANT PERCUSSION -- 23rd Acoustic Element (Part B Side), Substrate-Dependent Broadband Mechanical Percussion:** The 23rd element in the engine's acoustic progression. The first sound whose timbral character is determined by an external object rather than the performer's anatomy. The flicker selects its instrument -- hollow snag, metal chimney cap, cedar utility pole, house fascia board -- and the substrate shapes the sound. Resonant percussion is substrate-dependent, broadband, mechanically produced, rhythmically structured noise at or above the perceptual fusion threshold, amplified by the resonant properties of the struck surface. Distinct from the hammer (degree 21, vocal metronomic pulse of discrete identical notes) in every dimension: source (bill on substrate vs. syrinx), pitch (broadband vs. tonal), fusion state (above vs. below), timbre control (variable/substrate-determined vs. fixed/anatomical), duration (short burst vs. long series), information content (identity + territory + substrate quality vs. identity + territory).

- **SUBSTRATE SELECTION AS CREATIVE ACT -- The Bird Picks Its Drum, Maximally Resonant Surfaces:** Northern Flickers preferentially select maximally resonant surfaces for drumming (Dodenhoff, Stark, and Johnson 2001; Kilham 1959). They do not drum on whatever surface is nearest -- they actively seek and return to substrates that produce the loudest, most resonant sound. Hollow dead alder (low-mid resonance, woody thud). Metal chimney cap (high resonance, metallic rattle, dramatically louder -- carries hundreds of meters). Cedar utility pole (low-mid, deep resonant knock). House fascia board (mid, wooden rap amplified by house structure). Metal has a higher Q factor (quality factor of resonance) than wood -- it rings longer and at higher amplitude. Flickers return to the same metal surface day after day, defending it as a drumming station. The bird has discovered, empirically, that metal outperforms wood. This is not random behavior. The substrate IS the instrument.

- **FUSION TRILOGY COMPLETE -- Three Points on the Perceptual Fusion Continuum:** Degree 11 (Oregon Junco trill): at the fusion threshold, ~10-20 notes/sec, notes blur into continuous buzz. Degree 21 (Northern Flicker wick call): below fusion, 6-10 notes/sec, each note heard individually -- the hammer. **Degree 22 (Northern Flicker drum): above fusion, ~25 strikes/sec, individual strikes merge into a continuous roll.** The same species maps two of the three positions. The progression from degree 11 (blur) to degree 21 (clarity) to degree 22 (blur again, through a different physical channel) maps the entire fusion continuum. Three perceptual states from rate variation alone.

- **BROADBAND SOUND ENTERS THE ENGINE -- First Unpitched, Wide-Spectrum Percussive Transient:** Twenty-one degrees of tonal, pitched sound. The drum is the first broadband, unpitched sound -- energy spread across the spectrum without a dominant fundamental frequency. In music theory terms: every previous species was a melody instrument. The drum is a percussion instrument. The engine now contains both pitched and unpitched sound sources.

- **DUAL-CHANNEL COMMUNICATION -- Voice and Percussion, Redundant Channels:** The Northern Flicker at degrees 21-22 is the first organism in the engine to communicate through two fundamentally different physical channels: air-borne vocal sound (wick call) and substrate-borne mechanical percussion (drum). These are two different mechanisms serving overlapping but not identical functions. The wick call says "I am here, this is my territory, I am fit." The drum says "I am here, this is my territory, I am fit, **and listen to this substrate** -- this is good habitat." The drum carries a layer of information the call cannot: habitat quality. In recording engineering terms, this is parallel compression -- one clean/detailed path (the call) and one compressed/powerful path (the drum), blended for a territorial signal with both clarity and impact.

- **FOREST SNAG ECOLOGY -- First Dead-Wood Ecosystem, The Instrument IS the Habitat:** A snag is a standing dead or dying tree -- not debris but a keystone habitat structure. The engine has documented living trees across twenty-one degrees. Degree 22 introduces the first ecosystem defined by dead wood. The forest snag is not just the flicker's nesting substrate (covered at degree 21); it is the flicker's instrument. Without dead, resonant wood, there is no drum -- only the wick call. Mature and old-growth forests: 8-20 snags per hectare. Managed stands: <3 snags per hectare. Snag removal eliminates drumming habitat. Where you hear flicker drumming, dead wood is present. The instrument disappears, and with it, the music.

- **DAWN DRUM SESSION -- Temporal Pattern of Territorial Display:** Spring breeding season (February-June), peak drumming at dawn. The male flies to a preferred drumming station and delivers 20-50 drumming bouts over 15-30 minutes, each bout separated by 10-30 second pauses. Counter-drumming between neighboring males: one drums, the other responds within seconds, exchanges continue in alternation -- the percussive equivalent of countersinging in passerines. The louder, more resonant drum wins.

- **BIOMECHANICS OF PERCUSSION -- The Skull as Drumstick:** ~25 strikes/sec subjects the skull to repeated deceleration forces of approximately 100-300 g per impact (Van Wassenbergh et al. 2022). The stiff skull hypothesis: the woodpecker skull functions as a rigid hammer -- transmitting maximum energy to the substrate rather than absorbing shock. The skull IS the drumstick. The spongy bone provides some vibration damping while maintaining structural rigidity. The hyoid apparatus (the same tongue-support structure that wraps around the skull for ant extraction) may function as a tension band during drumming, stabilizing against lateral displacement. Fast-twitch muscle fibers in the neck power each strike. Brief bout duration (0.6-1.2 seconds) prevents muscle fatigue. The 10-30 second pause between bouts allows recovery.

- **SPECIES-SPECIFIC DRUMMING SIGNATURES -- Acoustic Fingerprint:** Drumming patterns function as species identifiers. PNW woodpecker community: Northern Flicker (~25/sec, even with terminal deceleration), Downy Woodpecker (~15-17/sec, even, short), Hairy Woodpecker (~25/sec, even, shorter than flicker), Pileated Woodpecker (~15-17/sec, accelerating then decelerating), Red-breasted Sapsucker (irregular, stuttering), Three-toed Woodpecker (~8-12/sec, slower, deliberate). A trained listener can identify the species by drum alone.

- **NON-PASSERINE MOMENTUM MAINTAINED -- Second Consecutive Piciformes, But No New Non-Passerine Species:** The Northern Flicker holds two degrees but is still one species. The engine has 21 unique species across 23 degrees, with only 2 non-passerines (heron and flicker). The 20:2 ratio has not changed since degree 21. More non-passerine species (not more degrees of existing non-passerines) are needed. Still no marine/shoreline species, no raptor, no corvid. The flicker pair is justified by the structural significance of introducing mechanical percussion, but the next species must be new.

### The Degree 22 Parallel: Self-Released Percussion

The resonance between Foxy Apollo and the Red-shafted Flicker drumming operates on four primary axes:

1. **Self-released percussion.** Foxy Apollo is self-released -- independent, outside the label system, making music with available instruments and distributing directly. The Northern Flicker is self-amplified -- finding resonant surfaces, broadcasting directly without institutional infrastructure. Both operate outside the establishment. The DIY percussionist.

2. **Instrument selection as creative act.** Foxy Apollo selects production tools, timbres, and sonic palette. The flicker selects drumming substrates for resonance. In both cases, the choice of instrument IS the creative act. The sound is shaped by what you play as much as how you play it. A hollow snag produces a woody thud; a metal chimney cap produces a piercing rattle. A garage-funk EP sounds different from a studio-produced LP. The substrate matters.

3. **Energy 4 sustained.** Second consecutive degree at E=4 in both engines. Not a spike but a new baseline. The energy escalation introduced at degree 21 persists. Polyrhythmics (ensemble-kinetic) gave way to Foxy Apollo (collective-kinetic). The wick call (vocal energy) gave way to the drum (mechanical energy). The level holds.

4. **The pair transition.** Polyrhythmics (degree 21) to Foxy Apollo (degree 22): same city, same energy, different project. The 8-piece band gives way to the founder-led collective. This mirrors the flicker pair: the ensemble voice (wick call, long, tonal) gives way to the solo percussion (drum, short, broadband). Both transitions move from larger-scale, sustained, tonal production to smaller-scale, burst-form, broadband production.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **The flicker pair structure delivers on its promise.** The pair was proposed at degree 21 as a bridge from voice to percussion. It works. Degree 21 establishes the species, degree 22 introduces the unprecedented sound type. The bridge is smooth. The contrast is clear. The dual-channel concept emerges naturally. The controlled experiment in acoustic physics -- same species, different mechanism, everything else held constant -- produces the engine's cleanest structural comparison.

- **Resonant percussion is a genuinely new acoustic concept with four unprecedented properties.** Not just "fast repetition" (that was the trill at degree 11) or "loud repetition" (that was the hammer at degree 21). Resonant percussion introduces: (1) substrate selection (external instrument), (2) broadband sound (unpitched), (3) above-fusion percussive rolls, and (4) substrate-dependent timbre. Four new concepts in one element. The 23rd acoustic element earns its position.

- **The consecutive same-genre analysis produced the most productive structural finding on the Part A side.** The polarity between Polyrhythmics' groove-sovereign and Foxy Apollo's groove-as-platform reveals something fundamental about genre compounds that the engine had not previously documented: same genre, different music. This finding is general -- it will apply to all future genre repetitions.

- **The generational transfer analysis connects naturally to the scene ecology documented across degrees 18-22.** Maktub (workshop), Fly Moon Royale (collective), Whitney Monge (street laureate), Polyrhythmics (ensemble anchor), Foxy Apollo (inheritor) -- five consecutive degrees documenting different aspects of how a music scene operates, sustains, and reproduces itself. The funk/soul quadrant is becoming a scene ecology study.

- **The documentation gap was handled honestly.** Rather than padding the research with speculation or inflating thin sources, the gap was acknowledged as a structural finding. The research explains why the gap exists (youth, grassroots career stage, research methodology) and treats it as data, not failure.

- **The substrate-as-instrument concept adds a new dimension to the engine.** Every previous sound was anatomically fixed. The drum introduces the variable of the external world. What you play matters as much as how you play it. This is a paradigm shift for the engine's acoustic framework.

- **The fusion trilogy (degrees 11, 21, 22) forms a clean three-point mapping.** At fusion, below fusion, above fusion. Three perceptual states from rate variation alone. The same species spanning two of the three positions is an elegant demonstration.

- **Forest snag as ecosystem is ecologically precise.** The engine's ecosystem vocabulary gains its first dead-wood habitat. The distinction between the living open woodland (degree 21) and the dead standing wood within it (degree 22) is sharp and meaningful. The instrument IS the habitat.

- **Bandcamp is the most reliable source for emerging artists.** Complete discography, track listings, credits, genre tags, release dates, and pricing from a single, artist-controlled platform. For Foxy Apollo, Bandcamp provided more reliable data than any other source.

### What Could Be Better

- **Still only two non-passerines in twenty-three degrees (one species at two degrees).** The Northern Flicker holds two degrees but is still one species. 21 unique species, 20:2 ratio unchanged since degree 21. More non-passerine species needed.

- **Still no marine or shoreline species.** Twenty-three degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound" and still nothing from the Sound itself. Gulls, murrelets, cormorants, auklets, scoters -- all absent. The engine's most persistent geographic gap.

- **Still no raptor, still no corvid.** No hawks, eagles, falcons. No Steller's Jay, crow, or raven. Two of the engine's most conspicuous absences persist.

- **Energy 5 remains absent.** E=4 is sustained but not exceeded. The engine's scale extends to 5. Raptor screams, corvid territorial calls, or goose honking could reach E=5.

- **Two consecutive degrees consumed by one species.** Justified by the structural significance of introducing mechanical percussion, but the roster stands at 21 unique species through degree 22 instead of 22. The next species must be new.

- **Forest interior species still underrepresented.** The last six unique habitats have all been edge, open, or dead-wood habitats. Deep forest interior (old-growth, mature closed-canopy conifer) has not appeared since degree 13.

- **Sam Ashkenazy's high school is unspecified.** No source names the school where Foxy Apollo formed. The formation story cannot be connected to a specific institutional pipeline.

- **TyeLeah Nanto's role on *Sweet Talk* is undocumented.** Her specific contribution (vocals? keys? other?) is unclear. She is potentially the only female performing member in the band's history.

- **The Hendrix comparison's depth is uncertain.** The Daily UW's Hendrix comparison is external observation, not confirmed self-identified influence.

- **Gender gap resumes and widens.** Distribution at 3:20 after Whitney Monge's single-degree breakthrough at degree 20. The fourteen-degree gap was closed only to resume a new gap.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A genre compound is a container, not a prescription.** Jazz/Funk at degree 21 is instrumental polyrhythmic Afrobeat. Jazz/Funk at degree 22 is vocal psychedelic pop-funk. Same compound, different music. The lesson applies to all genre labels: they describe the ingredients, not the recipe. Track all future consecutive same-genre pairs to see whether the polarity pattern (opposite poles of the same genre) recurs or whether consecutive pairs can also be stylistically similar.

2. **The species-pair concept solves the introduction-of-the-unprecedented problem -- and should be used sparingly.** The flicker pair works because degree 22 introduces something qualitatively different from degree 21 (mechanical percussion vs. vocal call). If repeated frequently, species-pairs would slow the species count and reduce taxonomic breadth. The bar: the second degree must introduce something the first cannot. Apply this test to all future species-pair proposals.

3. **External instrument selection (the substrate-as-instrument concept) opens a new dimension in the engine's acoustic framework.** Twenty-one degrees of anatomically-determined sound established that the performer's body shapes the sound. One degree of substrate-dependent percussion establishes that the world also shapes the sound. The lesson: when a single entry introduces a genuinely new dimension, document it exhaustively. The substrate concept will apply to future non-vocal sounds (wing displays, winnowing, tail sounds).

4. **Scene ecology emerges from consecutive degrees documenting the same ecosystem.** Degrees 18-22 (Maktub, FMR, Monge, Polyrhythmics, Foxy Apollo) unintentionally produced a five-degree study of how a music scene operates: workshop, collective, individual, anchor, inheritor. The lesson: consecutive degrees in the same genre zone produce structural insights that no single degree can. The funk/soul quadrant's scene ecology emerged from the data, not from planning.

5. **The documentation gap is itself structural data.** Foxy Apollo's thin documentation (21 sources vs. 24-38 range) is not a failure -- it is evidence of the emerging-artist tax. The engine's research methodology is optimized for established artists. Emerging artists exist primarily in social media, Bandcamp, and local event listings. The lesson: when the engine encounters more emerging artists, supplement web search with Bandcamp deep dives, social media analysis, and local event archive databases.

6. **The ADSR envelope's second cycle begins at non-zero.** The groove reset at degree 22 confirms that the engine does not restart from silence after completing one envelope. Everything accumulated in the first cycle (twenty-two degrees of vocabulary, patterns, cross-references) persists. The second cycle inherits the first. The lesson: macro-structural cycles in the engine are not loops that return to origin -- they are spirals that begin each cycle higher than the last.

7. **Dispersed geography may be the new normal for younger artists.** The I-5 corridor identity at degree 22 reflects real economic and cultural forces: real estate costs pushing musicians to peripheral cities, digital distribution reducing proximity requirements, regional touring circuits. The engine should track whether subsequent degrees continue the dispersal trend or return to neighborhood-specific. The geographic schema may need a permanent regional category.
