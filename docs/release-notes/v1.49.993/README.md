The spacecraft launched 2006-01-19 at 19:00 UTC on an Atlas V 551 vehicle with a Star 48B solid upper stage from Cape Canaveral Space Launch Complex 41. It was the fastest launch to date, and the high launch energy placed New Horizons directly onto a solar-escape trajectory, so the spacecraft left Earth already on a path out of the solar system. The compact, lightweight, three-axis- and spin-capable spacecraft is built around a triangular aluminum body with a 2.1-meter parabolic high-gain dish antenna and body-mounted instruments, powered by a single radioisotope thermoelectric generator that supplies power independent of sunlight. Because the instruments are body-mounted, the spacecraft is pointed as a whole to aim them, and it can be three-axis stabilized for high-resolution imaging or spin-stabilized for the long cruise and the energetic-particle measurements.

New Horizons achieved a series of firsts that carried close-range exploration to the most distant ground of the solar system. It reached Jupiter with closest approach on 2007-02-28 and used a Jupiter gravity assist to add speed and shorten the cruise to the Pluto system by years, while exercising its instruments on the Jovian system as a working rehearsal. It became the first spacecraft to reconnoiter the Pluto system, with closest approach on 2015-07-14, returning the first detailed observations of Pluto and its moons from the Ralph imaging spectrometer, the LORRI high-resolution imager, and the Alice ultraviolet spectrometer, revealing a varied surface and a layered atmosphere. It then flew past the Kuiper Belt object Arrokoth on 2019-01-01, the farthest flyby of any solar-system object, returning the first detailed observations of a small, primitive contact-binary trans-Neptunian world and extending close-range reconnaissance into the Kuiper Belt for the first time.

Beyond Arrokoth the mission entered its extended outer-heliosphere phase as the contemporary outbound frontier. New Horizons carries the Solar Wind Around Pluto plasma instrument and the Pluto Energetic Particle Spectrometer Science Investigation outward through the heliosphere, the vast bubble of solar-wind plasma and magnetic field that the Sun blows into the surrounding interstellar medium, returning solar-wind and energetic-particle data from progressively greater distances. As the only spacecraft actively gathering outer-heliosphere plasma data during the IBEX and IMAP imaging era, New Horizons links the in-situ and remote-sensing methods in the present epoch, extending the in-situ baseline forward in time from the Voyager and Pioneer precursors and anticipating a future heliopause crossing of its own. The contemporary dataset complements the Voyager in-situ heliopause crossings and the IBEX and IMAP remote energetic-neutral-atom maps with a present-day outbound plasma record.

The fastest launch and the direct solar-escape trajectory are central to understanding how New Horizons reached the distant Pluto system. The launch on the Atlas V 551, the most powerful configuration of that vehicle with five solid rocket boosters, combined with a Star 48B solid upper stage and a lightweight spacecraft, delivered the highest launch energy of any launch to date and placed the spacecraft directly onto a solar-escape trajectory. Where the prior axis entries gained escape velocity through planetary flybys or were placed into Earth orbit or the inner solar system, New Horizons left Earth already moving fast enough to leave the solar system, crossing the orbit of the Moon in well under a day and reaching Jupiter in barely more than a year. The direct-escape launch was a deliberate mission-design choice, the great distance to the Pluto system requiring a fast trajectory to arrive within a decade, and it is what set the spacecraft on the outbound path that carries it now through the outer heliosphere.

The first Pluto reconnaissance returned a wealth of first observations. The Ralph imaging spectrometer mapped the surface composition and color, the LORRI high-resolution imager returned the sharpest images of Pluto and its largest moon, and the Alice ultraviolet spectrometer studied the thin atmosphere, while the spacecraft also observed the smaller moons of the system. Because the spacecraft flew past at high speed at a great distance from Earth, the encounter unfolded as a tightly choreographed sequence around closest approach, with the spacecraft pointing its body-mounted instruments in turn at each target, and the data were then returned to Earth over many months because of the low data rate at that distance. The first reconnaissance transformed a distant point of light into a mapped world, and the Arrokoth flyby that followed revealed a gently assembled contact binary that offers a window into the building blocks from which the planets formed.

The instrument suite carries both a planetary-science role and a heliophysics role. The Ralph imaging spectrometer and the Alice ultraviolet spectrometer, with team leadership at Southwest Research Institute, and the LORRI high-resolution imager, with team leadership at the Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory, performed the remote sensing at Pluto and Arrokoth. The SWAP plasma instrument, with team leadership at Southwest Research Institute, and the PEPSSI energetic-particle spectrometer, with team leadership at the Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory, measured the plasma and energetic-particle environment at Pluto and now in the outer heliosphere. The Student Dust Counter, built and operated by students at the University of Colorado Boulder, records the interplanetary dust along the trajectory, a science instrument and an education program in one. The suite gave the mission a planetary-science harvest at the encounters and a deep-space measurement role across the long extended cruise.

The continued operation of New Horizons depends on the NASA Deep Space Network, the system of large radio antennas that command the spacecraft and receive its faint signal through the 2.1-meter high-gain dish antenna in the X-band. As the distance grew across the journey to Pluto and the outbound cruise, the round-trip light time lengthened and the data rate fell with the square of the distance, so the wealth of Pluto-encounter data, gathered in the brief window around closest approach, had to be stored on board and returned over many months. The reception of data from a spacecraft now far beyond the Kuiper Belt is a measure of how far New Horizons has traveled along its outbound trajectory and of the capability of the network that tracks it. Operating the spacecraft across the widening gulf required careful planning: command sequences had to be uploaded well in advance, instrument operations had to be managed against the gradually declining radioisotope power budget, and the spacecraft pointing had to be maintained so that the high-gain antenna stayed aimed at Earth.
