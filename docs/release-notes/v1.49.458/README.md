## Part A: Temple of the Dog — Grunge Supergroup at E=7, Seattle 1990-1991, A&M Records, Chris Cornell as Primary Writer and Vocalist, Eddie Vedder's First In-Person Meeting With the Seattle Scene, Rick Parashar Production at London Bridge Studio, and the Andrew Wood Memorial Record That Contained Two Futures

- **Temple of the Dog Records at E=7 Because Tribute-Record Function Overrides Supergroup-by-Personnel Register.** Temple of the Dog arrives at E=7 as the Post-Summit Cooldown's opening S36 entry at degree 317. The band is a supergroup by personnel — Chris Cornell (Soundgarden), Matt Cameron (Soundgarden), Stone Gossard (the soon-to-be Pearl Jam), Jeff Ament (the soon-to-be Pearl Jam), Mike McCready (the soon-to-be Pearl Jam), and Eddie Vedder (the soon-to-be Pearl Jam) at the same time combines members of two of the Big Four's four entries (Soundgarden at degree 312, Pearl Jam at degree 314) in one studio — but the project-function register is a tribute record rather than a career band. Cornell wrote the songs for Andrew Wood. The sessions were recorded over a compressed window in 1990. No tour supported the 1991 release. The album's delayed Platinum certification in 1992-1993 came via MTV's heavy rotation of "Hunger Strike" after Pearl Jam became globally famous. The E=7 placement is grounded in the tribute-record register rather than in the personnel-roster register. The engine's documentation vocabulary distinguishes between the supergroup-by-who-is-in-the-room register and the tribute-record-by-function register, and E=7 is the correct reading for the latter register even when the former would otherwise suggest a higher placement.
- **Andrew Wood's March 19, 1990 Death Is the Catalyst for the Entire Record.** Andrew Wood, vocalist of Malfunkshun (documented at degree 295) and Mother Love Bone (degree 296), died of a heroin overdose on March 19, 1990 at age 24. Mother Love Bone had completed recording *Apple* (released posthumously July 19, 1990 on Polygram/Stardog), but Wood did not live to see it released. Wood's death catalyzed Chris Cornell's direct memorial songwriting within weeks of the event. Cornell and Wood had shared a Capitol Hill house at various points in the late 1980s; their friendship was direct and specific. The Temple of the Dog album's eleven tracks — "Say Hello 2 Heaven" opens the album addressed directly to Wood, "Reach Down" as the emotional center, "Wooden Jesus," "Pushin Forward Back," "Call Me a Dog," "Times of Trouble," "Wooden Jesus," "Four Walled World," "All Night Thing," and "Hunger Strike" — are all Cornell compositions written in direct response to Wood's death. The memorial character is not a branding decision; it is the album's compositional origin.
- **The Personnel Assembly Is the Most Significant Overlap in Seattle Music History.** The Temple of the Dog lineup combines Chris Cornell and Matt Cameron from Soundgarden (vocals and drums, respectively) with Stone Gossard, Jeff Ament, Mike McCready, and Eddie Vedder from what would become Pearl Jam. Gossard and Ament were the Mother Love Bone rhythm-section continuation after Wood's death; McCready was already working with them on the project that would become Pearl Jam; Vedder was newly arrived from San Diego after the audition-cassette routing. The lineup combines members of two of the Big Four (Soundgarden at degree 312, Pearl Jam at degree 314) plus the immediate post-Mother Love Bone continuation (Gossard, Ament) in a single studio at a single time. No other moment in the Seattle grunge canon assembles this personnel configuration.
- **The Eddie Vedder Audition Cassette and the First Meeting.** Eddie Vedder (born December 23, 1964 in Evanston, Illinois; living in San Diego working in gas stations in 1990) received Stone Gossard's instrumental demo tapes through drummer Jack Irons (former Red Hot Chili Peppers drummer connecting the Los Angeles-San Diego and Seattle music networks). Vedder recorded vocals over Gossard's instrumentals in a single session in San Diego — the takes that became "Alive," "Once," and "Footsteps" — and sent the tape back. Gossard played the tape for Cornell. Cornell was struck by Vedder's voice and invited him to Seattle. The Temple of the Dog studio sessions in 1990 were Vedder's first in-person meeting with Cornell, Gossard, Ament, McCready, and Cameron. The studio was the first meeting point. Vedder arrived in Seattle for Temple; he stayed for Pearl Jam.
- **"Hunger Strike" Emerged From the Session as the Pre-Breakthrough Cornell-Vedder Duet.** The duet between Cornell (upper range) and Vedder (baritone) on "Hunger Strike" was not planned as the lead single. It emerged from the session when the two voices, in that configuration, on that arrangement, produced something that stood apart from the rest of the album. "Hunger Strike" became the most commercially successful track from Temple of the Dog when it entered heavy MTV rotation in 1992 after Pearl Jam's *Ten* commercial breakthrough. The 1990 recording precedes both vocalists' commercial peaks by approximately twelve months — Pearl Jam released *Ten* on August 27, 1991, and Soundgarden released *Badmotorfinger* on October 8, 1991. "Hunger Strike" is the pre-breakthrough document of what Cornell and Vedder sounded like before the industry had labeled and packaged what they were doing. Degree 317 carries the pre-breakthrough version of those voices into the post-summit, not the commercially successful version.
- **Rick Parashar Produced at London Bridge Studio and Established Sonic Continuity With *Ten*.** Rick Parashar (1963-2014) produced Temple of the Dog in 1990 and went on to produce Pearl Jam's *Ten* in 1991 at the same studio — London Bridge Studio at 2010 4th Avenue West in Seattle — with overlapping personnel (Gossard, Ament, McCready, Vedder on both records). The sonic continuity between Temple of the Dog and *Ten* is partly a function of shared production and partly a function of shared personnel. Parashar's production style on both records — dry, dynamic, guitar-forward, vocal-centered — established the sonic signature of the early Pearl Jam catalog. Parashar's 2014 death removed from the Seattle scene's history a figure whose recorded documentation of the pre-commercial Seattle grunge moment is as structurally important as any single musician's contribution. Degree 317 documents the producer context at first-class resolution rather than treating the production register as a peripheral concern.
- **No Tour Supported the 1991 Release Because the Personnel Had Already Separated.** Temple of the Dog was released on A&M Records in March 1991. The album was not supported by a tour at the time of release. The reason is structural: by the time the record came out, its personnel had separated into two different bands at the peak of their commercial emergence. Soundgarden was completing *Badmotorfinger* for October 1991 release; Pearl Jam was recording and preparing *Ten* for August 1991 release. Both albums became enormous. There was no organizational structure or collective intent to support the Temple record in the conventional way. The album's commercial performance accelerated dramatically in 1992 as Pearl Jam became globally famous and "Hunger Strike" entered heavy MTV rotation, reaching Platinum certification in 1992-1993 via the delayed recognition rather than via conventional release-window support.
- **Temple of the Dog as the Pre-Commercial Snapshot Before Both Lineages Became Globally Famous.** The Temple of the Dog sessions in 1990 occurred at the exact intersection of Soundgarden's lineage and Pearl Jam's lineage before either band had achieved commercial breakthrough. The sessions assembled personnel who would separately define the grunge era across the 1990s, but at the moment of assembly none of them had yet achieved the commercial scale they would reach within twelve months. The album documents the Seattle underground at the moment before the commercial moment, assembled in grief and recorded with the directness that grief makes possible. Degree 317 carries this pre-commercial snapshot into the post-summit arc as the post-summit's opening S36 entry, a tribute-record-by-function placement that documents the scene at its pre-famous moment rather than at its famous moment.

## Part B: Humpback-Orca Same-Channel Vocalization — Multi-Species Simultaneous Event at E=8, San Juan Islands, *Megaptera novaeangliae* + *Orcinus orca*, Summer (June-September) Peak July-August, the 100-500 Hz Shared Overlap Zone, the First Multi-Species Vocalization Entry in the Engine, and the Salish Sea's Most Acoustically Complex Multi-Species Encounter

- **The Humpback-Orca Same-Channel Event Is the First Multi-Species Vocalization Entry in the Engine.** The Humpback-Orca Same-Channel Vocalization at degree 317 establishes multi-species simultaneous vocalization as a first-class event category in the engine's SPS documentation vocabulary. Prior SPS entries have documented species-specific acoustic events (single-species solo calls, single-species herd bugles) and inter-species territorial overlap events like the Cougar-Wolf Encounter at degree 313 where the two species vocalized in territorial adjacency rather than in same-channel simultaneity. The Humpback-Orca event documents the simultaneous same-channel vocalization of two different cetacean species at the 100-500 Hz shared overlap zone. The new category opens at E=8 and establishes the register for subsequent multi-species events.
- **The Humpback Whale's Song Architecture Operates at a Scale No Other Animal Vocalization Matches.** The male Humpback whale (*Megaptera novaeangliae*, family Balaenopteridae, 40-50 feet, 25-40 tons) produces the most structurally complex animal vocalization known: song cycles of 10-30 minutes in duration, repeated continuously for hours, organized into units (the smallest acoustic element), phrases (unit sequences), themes (phrase patterns), and songs (theme cycles). Individual singers produce songs that evolve across breeding seasons in ways that spread through populations as acoustic culture. Primary acoustic energy runs 20-2,000 Hz with biologically central elements in the 30-500 Hz range and infrasonic lower components at 20-30 Hz detected at long distances by other whales. The Humpback song at the degree 317 event brings a vocal architecture built over millions of years of cetacean evolution and propagated through acoustic culture across breeding seasons.
- **The Southern Resident Killer Whale Dialect Is Pod-Specific and Culturally Transmitted.** The Southern Resident Killer Whale population in the San Juan Islands consists of three pods — J, K, and L — totaling approximately 73 animals as of 2024. Each pod produces call types so specific that researchers can identify pod membership from acoustic data alone; the dialect is not learned individually but transmitted culturally within the pod across generations. The SRKW acoustic catalog documents 100+ distinct call types. Discrete social calls operate at 1-20 kHz, whistles at 1-18 kHz, echolocation clicks at 20-100 kHz, and lower-frequency calls at 100-500 Hz. The 100-500 Hz range is where the SRKW's vocalization shares acoustic space with Humpback song at the same frequencies. The Orca vocal component of the overlap event is not generic whale sound but a specific cultural inheritance, a pod's acoustic identity audible in the same water as the Humpback's structurally complex song.
- **The 100-500 Hz Overlap Zone Is the Convergence Region Where Two Vocal Systems Coexist.** The Humpback whale's primary song energy (20-2,000 Hz with central biological elements 30-500 Hz) and the Orca's lower-frequency calls (100-500 Hz range) share acoustic space at 100-500 Hz. This is the convergence zone where the two species' vocalizations coexist. The overlap is partial rather than complete — the Humpback's infrasonic components (20-30 Hz) and upper song elements (500-2,000 Hz) lie outside the Orca's typical lower-frequency range, and the Orca's higher-frequency calls (discrete at 1-20 kHz, echolocation at 20-100 kHz) lie above the Humpback's song range. Research documents vocal-behavior modification in the presence of simultaneous same-channel events: in some studies, Orcas reduce low-frequency call rates when Humpbacks are actively singing; in others, Orcas and Humpbacks appear to vocalize at different times in a turn-taking pattern. The research on multi-species acoustic coordination is a frontier.
- **The San Juan Islands as Multi-Species Habitat Produces the Simultaneity.** Both species are reliably present in the San Juan Islands during summer. The Southern Resident Killer Whales are year-round residents of the Salish Sea with highest concentration in the San Juans during summer months (June-September) when Chinook salmon runs provide their primary prey. The Humpback whales arrive in the San Juans in late May or early June following their northward migration from tropical breeding grounds in Hawaii and Mexico, and remain through October feeding intensively on herring, sand lance, and krill. The San Juans' productive waters support multiple Humpback individuals simultaneously; whale-watching research documents daily sightings of 5-15 individuals during peak summer. The geographic concentration of both species in the same archipelago during the same summer months produces the structural possibility of simultaneous same-channel vocalization events.
- **The Humpback Recovery Post-1966 Made the Event Structurally Possible.** *Megaptera novaeangliae* was listed as endangered globally following commercial whaling. North Pacific Humpback populations have recovered significantly since commercial whaling ended in 1966. The Salish Sea / San Juan Islands area has seen increasing Humpback presence over the past two decades as the recovery has progressed. Both species in the same channel simultaneously is partly a product of successful recovery — the Humpback-Orca same-channel event at degree 317 depends on a Humpback population size large enough to produce reliable daily sightings of singing individuals during the summer feeding window. Prior to the 1966 commercial-whaling closure and the subsequent recovery, the simultaneous event would have been significantly less probable at the population-density level.
- **The Acoustic Character of the Simultaneous Event: Humpback Foundation Plus Orca Overlay.** When Humpback and Orca are simultaneously active in the San Juan Islands, the Humpback's song provides a low-frequency sonic landscape — moans and cries at 20-500 Hz create an omnipresent tonal background that carries across kilometers of water. This is not the sharp, burst-pulse sound of Orca calls but a sustained, tonal, quasi-musical sequence. The SRKW vocalizations — discrete calls, whistles, burst-pulse sounds — occur at higher frequencies and with shorter temporal structures, more staccato and more socially contextual. When the Orca calls overlap with the Humpback song background, the Orca calls stand out as distinct events against the Humpback's tonal foundation. The two species' vocalizations coexist in the same water column but occupy different frequency regions and different temporal rhythms, producing a layered complexity that no single species can generate alone.
- **E=8 Is the Complexity-Maximum Rather Than the Intensity-Maximum Register.** The E=8 placement for the Humpback-Orca same-channel event reflects the complexity-maximum register rather than the intensity-maximum register. The Orca Greeting Ceremony at degree 311 registered at E=9 because it was a single species at its maximum social vocalization intensity during pod convergence. The Humpback-Orca same-channel event at degree 317 registers at E=8 because the multi-species combination generates a different kind of acoustic richness — not intensity-maximum but complexity-maximum, two independent vocal architectures simultaneously present in the same geographic space at the 100-500 Hz overlap zone. E=8 is the correct reading for a phenomenon that is extraordinary not because it is the loudest thing in the Salish Sea but because it is the most acoustically layered. The engine's SPS documentation vocabulary distinguishes between single-species peak intensity (E=9) and multi-species peak complexity (E=8) at first-class resolution rather than conflating the two registers.
