## Armstrong Before Apollo

Neil Armstrong at degree 44 is not the Apollo 11 commander. He is a 35-year-old test pilot on his first spaceflight, a former X-15 pilot (7 flights, Mach 5.74, 207,500 feet) and Korean War veteran (78 combat missions). Gemini 8 is the proving ground. The stuck thruster is the test:

- **The problem:** After docking, the combined Gemini-Agena begins an unexpected roll. Armstrong undocks, suspecting the Agena. The roll accelerates -- it was Gemini's Thruster #8, stuck open.
- **The physics:** At one revolution per second, the crew has seconds before loss of consciousness. The OAMS (Orbital Attitude and Maneuvering System) cannot overcome the stuck thruster.
- **The decision:** Armstrong activates the RCS -- the reentry control system, a backup never intended for orbital use. It stops the roll. But mission rules require abort once RCS fuel drops below 75%.
- **The outcome:** Emergency splashdown after 10 hours 41 minutes. The first docking lasted 27 minutes.

Three years later, Armstrong will make the same kind of decision -- switching to manual control during the Eagle's descent when the computer overflows, flying past boulders to land with 25 seconds of fuel remaining. The pattern is set at degree 44.

## Gemini Docking: The Missing Piece

After degree 42's rendezvous, docking completes the two-step process Apollo requires:

1. **Rendezvous** (Gemini 6A, degree 42) -- find and approach another vehicle in orbit
2. **Docking** (Gemini 8, degree 44) -- physically connect two vehicles

For Apollo, the sequence is:

1. CSM/LM separate in lunar orbit
2. LM descends to lunar surface
3. LM ascends from lunar surface
4. LM rendezvous with CSM
5. LM docks with CSM
6. Crew transfers back to CSM

Every step after separation depends on rendezvous and docking. Gemini 6A proved the first. Gemini 8 proved the second -- and proved that emergencies can be survived with the right pilot.

## The Lesser Goldfinch: Mimicry and Adaptation

The Lesser Goldfinch (*Spinus psaltria*) is the smallest true finch in North America at 8-11 grams -- smaller than most warblers. Despite its size, it is a prolific mimic, incorporating phrases from other species into its song. This is unusual among finches (Fringillidae are typically not mimics). The species is a year-round resident in Pacific Northwest urban areas, frequenting nyjer seed feeders alongside its larger cousin, the American Goldfinch (degree 43).

Two Fringillidae in consecutive degrees (43-44): the American Goldfinch (Washington State bird, strict vegetarian, late breeder) and the Lesser Goldfinch (smallest finch, mimic, year-round urban resident). Family continuity before the break to Passerellidae at degree 45.
