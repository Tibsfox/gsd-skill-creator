## Mission Overview

GRACE-FO is the obs#9 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — a joint NASA-German Earth-observing mission whose twin satellites orbit the planet and weigh the water of the world, sensing month to month the minute shifts in the distance between the two spacecraft as the mass of Earth passes below and mapping where the water goes. ★ It is a joint mission of NASA (Jet Propulsion Laboratory) and its German partners — GFZ (German Research Centre for Geosciences, Potsdam) as science lead, with DLR — and the follow-on to the original GRACE (NASA + DLR/GFZ, 2002–2017). Two satellites (GRACE-FO 1 & 2) fly in tandem some 220 km apart in a near-polar ~490 km orbit (~89° inclination); a K/Ka-band microwave ranging instrument measures the inter-satellite range to micron precision, and a Laser Ranging Interferometer (LRI) — the first inter-satellite laser interferometer flown in space, a technology demonstrator — measures it to nanometre precision, with accelerometers, GNSS, and star cameras completing the payload. The pair launched 22 May 2018 on a SpaceX Falcon 9 from Vandenberg (Space Launch Complex 4E), a rideshare with five Iridium NEXT satellites, and reached a near-polar low-Earth orbit: two satellites in tandem, weighing the changing water below. The story is the launch from Vandenberg, the two satellites settling into tandem some 220 km apart, the microwave and laser links measuring the distance between them by microns and nanometres, the time-variable gravity field traced month to month as mass passes beneath, the maps of groundwater and ice and ocean mass change built scan by scan, and the long watch over the changing water that began with the original GRACE and continues with the follow-on today. The science is central: as mass — mostly water — below pulls on each satellite, the inter-satellite distance changes minutely, and tracking that change month to month maps Earth's time-variable gravity field, the redistribution of water mass: groundwater and aquifer depletion, ice-sheet and glacier mass loss (Greenland, Antarctica), ocean mass and sea level, terrestrial water storage, drought and flood. GRACE-FO continues the time-variable-gravity / water-mass record begun by the original GRACE; its Laser Ranging Interferometer is the first inter-satellite laser interferometer flown in space; and it maps the monthly redistribution of water mass across groundwater, ice, and ocean. ★ The continuation result is load-bearing: GRACE-FO continues the time-variable-gravity / water-mass record begun by the original GRACE (from 2002) — NOT "the first to measure Earth's gravity / mass change from space," since the original GRACE did and CHAMP (2000) and GOCE (2009) also measured Earth's gravity. ★ The instrument-first result is equally load-bearing: the Laser Ranging Interferometer is the first inter-satellite laser interferometer flown in space — a technology demonstrator, a precursor for LISA-class gravitational-wave detection. ★ The survey result is load-bearing: GRACE-FO maps the monthly redistribution of water mass across groundwater, ice, and ocean. ★★ GRACE-FO is STILL OPERATING (launched 22 May 2018; the prime five-year mission completed May 2023; now in extended mission, with lifetime extended toward ~2028 and a GRACE-C follow-on planned ~2028), so the catalog frames its launch and completed milestones in the PAST (GRACE-FO launched 22 May 2018, reached its near-polar orbit, and completed its prime mission) while its ongoing role is framed in the PRESENT (GRACE-FO orbits Earth, weighs the water, and continues to map the changing mass). ## Surprises

- **Two satellites become a single instrument.** Rather than carrying a gravity sensor aboard one spacecraft, GRACE-FO splits the measurement across two satellites flying in tandem some 220 km apart in the same near-polar orbit — and the instrument is the changing distance between them. As the leading satellite passes over a concentration of mass below, it is tugged forward slightly before the trailing satellite, and the inter-satellite range opens and closes by microns; tracking that change month to month recovers the time-variable gravity field across nearly all the planet.
- **A microwave link to microns and a laser link to nanometres.** GRACE-FO's primary K/Ka-band microwave ranging instrument tracks the inter-satellite distance to micron precision — and alongside it the Laser Ranging Interferometer (LRI), the first inter-satellite laser interferometer flown in space, tracks the same distance to nanometre precision, a thousand-fold finer, demonstrating a technique that points toward LISA-class gravitational-wave detection while sharpening the gravity measurement itself.
- **The mass that is measured is mostly water.** What changes the gravity field month to month, on the scales GRACE-FO senses, is the redistribution of water — the groundwater drawn from aquifers, the ice lost from the sheets and glaciers, the mass moving through the oceans, the water stored on the land. GRACE-FO weighs that water, in equivalent water height, mapping where the water goes across groundwater, ice, and ocean — drought and flood read as the comings and goings of mass.
- **A spadefoot that waits out the dry below the ground.** The Great Basin Spadefoot (*Spea intermontana*), a small spadefoot toad of the family Scaphiopodidae of the arid Pacific Northwest interior (the Columbia Basin shrub-steppe of eastern Washington and Oregon, southward through the Great Basin), spends most of the year buried underground, riding out the drought below the surface, and emerges to breed explosively in the temporary pools that gather after spring rain and snowmelt — its tadpoles racing to mature before the water vanishes: a creature of the water that comes and goes, the small-scale, living version of the hidden and ephemeral water that GRACE-FO weighs from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A calicoflower that lives and dies with the vernal pool.** The Common Calicoflower (*Downingia elegans*), a small annual of vernal pools, pond margins, and seasonally flooded meadows of the Pacific Northwest interior, germinates underwater as the pool fills and flowers in a ring of blue-and-white blooms across the mud as the water draws down and the pool dries (ecological description only; distinct from the meadowfoams and popcornflowers it grows among) — bound with the throughline of the weight of the water, living and dying with the same coming-and-going water that GRACE-FO maps from orbit.

The teams who carried forward the work begun by the original GRACE, sending up two new spacecraft to fly in tandem around the poles of a turning planet. The engineers who tracked the distance between the pair by microwave to a width finer than a hair, and then, for the first time between two free-flying satellites, by laser finer still. And the scientists who, from the way that distance opens and closes as the satellites cross the planet, weigh the groundwater drawn from the aquifers, the ice lost from Greenland and Antarctica, and the mass that moves through the seas — reading where the water of the Earth has gone, month after month. They measure what the eye cannot see: not the surface of the water, but its weight; the hidden water below and the water that comes and goes. Two satellites became one instrument, and the watch goes on.
