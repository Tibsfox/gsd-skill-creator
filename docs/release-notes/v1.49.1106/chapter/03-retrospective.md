## Surprises

- **Two satellites become a single instrument.** Rather than carrying a gravity sensor aboard one spacecraft, GRACE-FO splits the measurement across two satellites flying in tandem some 220 km apart in the same near-polar orbit — and the instrument is the changing distance between them. As the leading satellite passes over a concentration of mass below, it is tugged forward slightly before the trailing satellite, and the inter-satellite range opens and closes by microns; tracking that change month to month recovers the time-variable gravity field across nearly all the planet.
- **A microwave link to microns and a laser link to nanometres.** GRACE-FO's primary K/Ka-band microwave ranging instrument tracks the inter-satellite distance to micron precision — and alongside it the Laser Ranging Interferometer (LRI), the first inter-satellite laser interferometer flown in space, tracks the same distance to nanometre precision, a thousand-fold finer, demonstrating a technique that points toward LISA-class gravitational-wave detection while sharpening the gravity measurement itself.
- **The mass that is measured is mostly water.** What changes the gravity field month to month, on the scales GRACE-FO senses, is the redistribution of water — the groundwater drawn from aquifers, the ice lost from the sheets and glaciers, the mass moving through the oceans, the water stored on the land. GRACE-FO weighs that water, in equivalent water height, mapping where the water goes across groundwater, ice, and ocean — drought and flood read as the comings and goings of mass.
- **A spadefoot that waits out the dry below the ground.** The Great Basin Spadefoot (*Spea intermontana*), a small spadefoot toad of the family Scaphiopodidae of the arid Pacific Northwest interior (the Columbia Basin shrub-steppe of eastern Washington and Oregon, southward through the Great Basin), spends most of the year buried underground, riding out the drought below the surface, and emerges to breed explosively in the temporary pools that gather after spring rain and snowmelt — its tadpoles racing to mature before the water vanishes: a creature of the water that comes and goes, the small-scale, living version of the hidden and ephemeral water that GRACE-FO weighs from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A calicoflower that lives and dies with the vernal pool.** The Common Calicoflower (*Downingia elegans*), a small annual of vernal pools, pond margins, and seasonally flooded meadows of the Pacific Northwest interior, germinates underwater as the pool fills and flowers in a ring of blue-and-white blooms across the mud as the water draws down and the pool dries (ecological description only; distinct from the meadowfoams and popcornflowers it grows among) — bound with the throughline of the weight of the water, living and dying with the same coming-and-going water that GRACE-FO maps from orbit.
