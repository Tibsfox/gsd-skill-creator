## Summary

Three surveys complete in parallel at degree sixty-one. Lunar Orbiter 5 completes the photographic survey of ninety-nine percent of the lunar surface on behalf of NASA's five-mission program (1966-1968, five-of-five success). Death Cab for Cutie's 2003 *Transatlanticism* completes the band's cataloguing-approach lyric-voice survey on behalf of its eleven-album Bellingham-to-Portland catalog. North Pacific humpback whales complete an annual four-thousand-kilometer migration survey on behalf of their recovering twenty-one-thousand-individual population, and — via Noad et al. 2000 east-Australian song revolution — periodically complete cross-population cultural-transmission revolutions. Three scales, one principle: comprehensive coverage brought to closure, and the closure itself the beginning of the next survey. Each thread mirrors the others at its own tempo. The same way the Lunar Orbiter program built five spacecraft to image the lunar substrate at meter-scale resolution and stopped at the right place, Death Cab for Cutie built one breakthrough album that completed the cataloguing-songwriting register that Ben Gibbard had been developing across the Bellingham years, and the humpback population built a forty-thousand-kilometer migration arc that completes itself every year between Alaska and Hawaii. Both Lunar Orbiter 5 and Death Cab for Cutie operated as carriers transmitting a survey signal forward; each one terminated at the completion of its survey rather than at an arbitrary halt.

Lunar Orbiter 5 launched from Cape Canaveral Launch Complex 13 at 22:32:00 UTC on August 1, 1967, carrying a 385.6-kilogram truncated-cone spacecraft built at Boeing Space Division in Seattle, Washington under NASA Langley Research Center management. Four days later it entered a selenocentric orbit at eighty-five degrees inclination — more polar than any Lunar Orbiter before. For twelve days (August 6-18, 1967), dual-resolution 610-mm and 80-mm cameras exposed 844 photographic frames (633 high-resolution and 211 medium-resolution) onto Eastman Kodak 70-mm aerial film, which was bimat-processed on-orbit, mechanically slit-scanned line by line, and transmitted as analog video via S-band at 2295 MHz. Resolution reached two meters at closest approach. Cumulative coverage across LO1-LO5 reached ninety-nine percent. On January 31, 1968 at 07:58:08 UTC, LO5 was commanded to impact the lunar surface at 2°47'S 83°01'W to prevent RF interference with Apollo 8's December 1968 translunar communications. The Lunar Orbiter program closed with a five-of-five success rate. Lee R. Scherer at Langley Research Center had managed all five missions and would go on to manage Viking Mars and direct Kennedy Space Center. Leo R. Day held the LO5 project office. The Boeing Space Division team in Seattle, approximately two hundred engineers at peak, built all five spacecraft on the same architectural lineage — a lineage that runs forward in parallel through Pioneer Venus Orbiter (1978), Galileo (1989), Cassini-Huygens (1997), Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter (2005), and Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (2009). Every Apollo landing site was selected from LO-imaged candidates.

Death Cab for Cutie formed in 1997 in Bellingham, Washington — at Western Washington University, while Ben Gibbard was a WWU student — when Nick Harmer, a year older, met Gibbard while booking campus gigs. Chris Walla joined on guitar and production. Their debut *Something About Airplanes* (1998, Barsuk Records, Seattle independent label) established the band's sound; the fourth album *Transatlanticism* (October 7, 2003, still on Barsuk) was the breakthrough — RIAA Platinum, Billboard 200 debut, 500,000+ US sales in two years, and one of the first indie-rock albums to achieve mass-market reach without major-label backing. They signed Atlantic Records in November 2004. *Plans* (2005) hit Billboard 200 #4 and reached 1.2 million US sales. *Narrow Stairs* (2008) debuted at Billboard 200 #1 — the band's only #1 album. Chris Walla played his final show in Calgary on September 13, 2014. Jason McGerr has been drumming since 2003 (twenty-three continuous years by 2026). In January 2026 DCFC signed with ANTI- Records, ending the twenty-two-year Atlantic relationship; their eleventh studio album *I Built You a Tower* (2026) is the first ANTI- release. The Bellingham anchor is the first Whatcom County anchor in the S36 engine and the first Western Washington University connection — both first-instance declarations at v1.61 alongside the first Barsuk-Records-three-exemplar stabilization (after v1.53 Fleet Foxes + v1.57 Brandi Carlile).

The humpback whale (*Megaptera novaeangliae*, described by Georg Heinrich Borowski in 1781 as *Balaena novaeangliae*, converted to the genus *Megaptera* by John Edward Gray in 1846 — the same Gray who at v1.60 gave us *Phoca vitulina richardii* for the harbor seal) is the first Balaenopteridae and second Cetacean in the sixty-one-degree corpus. Adult body length 13.5-17.4 m (male typical-max) to 15.0-18.6 m (female typical-max); up to forty metric tons; pectoral fins four-to-five meters (longest of any cetacean, giving the genus name "big-wing"). North Pacific population approximately twenty-one thousand (2024 estimate), recovered from a 1960s low of approximately five thousand at a 4.2× global recovery factor — second MMPA/IWC-recovery-arc exemplar after v1.60 harbor seal. Both recovery arcs run in parallel at the marine-mammal scale; each side mirrors the others as a different species recovering from a different mid-twentieth-century low under a different statutory regime, with the convergent outcome of a North-Pacific-basin population restored to a viable level by the late twentieth century. Annual four-thousand-kilometer migration Alaska/British-Columbia summer feeding to Hawaii/Mexico winter breeding completes itself every year. Salish Sea presence recovering from ~0 sightings in the 1990s to hundreds annually in the 2020s per Cascadia Research Collective photo-identification. ESA most distinct-population-segments delisted September 2016; Central America DPS remains Endangered.

Male humpback breeding-season songs are the canonical cross-population cultural-transmission exemplar in marine mammal biology. Payne and McVay 1971 (*Science* 173:585) first characterized the hierarchical song structure: subunits → units → subphrases → phrases → themes. A complete song is four to thirty-three minutes long; Hawaiian recording sessions have captured continuous singing bouts up to seven hours. Dominant frequencies one hundred to four thousand Hertz with harmonics reaching twenty-four kilohertz. Source levels up to 188 dB re 1 μPa at 1 m. Noad et al. 2000 (*Nature* 408:537) documented the 1996-1998 east-Australian population's wholesale adoption of the west-Australian song across approximately two years — the first rapid cross-population cultural revolution in a non-human mammal. Garland et al. 2011 (*Current Biology* 21:687) extended cultural transmission to ocean-basin scale across six populations. Humpback song is the **third cultural-transmission axis exemplar** after v1.58 Bewick's Wren (neighborhood scale) + v1.59 SRKW (matriline scale), stabilizing the axis at three species across three scales. Each scale mirrors the others — neighborhood, matriline, ocean basin — with the convergent property that acoustic-signal repertoires propagate via social learning rather than genetic transmission. The same way the §6.6 CHANNEL-PARALLELISM alignment variant stabilizes at three exemplars (v1.58 Mariner 5 + v1.59 Surveyor 4 + v1.61 LO5) at v1.61, the cultural-transmission axis stabilizes at three species at the same degree. 3. **Faust compile-check pre-ship.** 2/2 DSPs verified compile-clean before the release pipeline kicked off.
4. **Cache-bust tags.** `?v=1.61.1` on audio HTML runners — prevents stale-browser-cache hits when visitors revisit the v1.61 page after a subsequent DSP iteration.
