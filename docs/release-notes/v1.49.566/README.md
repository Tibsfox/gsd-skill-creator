## Summary

Three surveys complete in parallel at degree 61. Lunar Orbiter 5 completes the photographic survey of 99% of the lunar surface on behalf of NASA's five-mission program (1966-1968, 5-of-5 success). Death Cab for Cutie's 2003 *Transatlanticism* completes the band's cataloguing-approach lyric-voice survey on behalf of its 11-album Bellingham-to-Portland catalog. North Pacific humpback whales complete an annual 4,000-kilometer migration survey on behalf of their recovering ~21,000-individual population, and — via Noad et al. 2000 east-Australian song revolution — periodically complete cross-population cultural-transmission revolutions. Three scales, one principle: comprehensive coverage brought to closure, and the closure itself the beginning of the next survey.

Lunar Orbiter 5 launched from Cape Canaveral Launch Complex 13 at 22:32:00 UTC on August 1, 1967, carrying a 385.6-kilogram truncated-cone spacecraft built at Boeing Space Division in Seattle, Washington under NASA Langley Research Center management. Four days later it entered a selenocentric orbit at 85 degrees inclination — more polar than any Lunar Orbiter before. For twelve days (August 6-18, 1967), dual-resolution 610-mm and 80-mm cameras exposed 844 photographic frames (633 high-resolution and 211 medium-resolution) onto Eastman Kodak 70-mm aerial film, which was bimat-processed on-orbit, mechanically slit-scanned line by line, and transmitted as analog video via S-band at 2295 MHz. Resolution reached 2 meters at closest approach. Cumulative coverage across LO1-LO5 reached 99%. On January 31, 1968 at 07:58:08 UTC, LO5 was commanded to impact the lunar surface at 2°47'S 83°01'W to prevent RF interference with Apollo 8's December 1968 translunar communications. The Lunar Orbiter program closed with a 5-of-5 success rate.

Death Cab for Cutie formed in 1997 in Bellingham, Washington — at Western Washington University, while Ben Gibbard was a WWU student — when Nick Harmer, a year older, met Gibbard while booking campus gigs. Chris Walla joined on guitar and production. Their debut *Something About Airplanes* (1998, Barsuk Records, Seattle independent label) established the band's sound; the fourth album *Transatlanticism* (October 7, 2003, still on Barsuk) was the breakthrough — RIAA Platinum, Billboard 200 debut, 500,000+ US sales in two years, and one of the first indie-rock albums to achieve mass-market reach without major-label backing. They signed Atlantic Records in November 2004. *Plans* (2005) hit Billboard 200 #4 and reached 1.2 million US sales. *Narrow Stairs* (2008) debuted at Billboard 200 #1. Chris Walla played his final show in Calgary on September 13, 2014. Jason McGerr has been drumming since 2003 (23 continuous years by 2026). In January 2026 DCFC signed with ANTI- Records, ending the 22-year Atlantic relationship; their eleventh studio album *I Built You a Tower* (2026) is the first ANTI- release.

The humpback whale (*Megaptera novaeangliae*, described by Georg Heinrich Borowski in 1781 as *Balaena novaeangliae*, converted to the genus *Megaptera* by John Edward Gray in 1846 — the same Gray who at v1.60 gave us *Phoca vitulina richardii* for the harbor seal) is the first Balaenopteridae and second Cetacean in the 61-degree corpus. Adult body length 13.5-17.4 m (male typical-max) to 15.0-18.6 m (female typical-max); up to 40 metric tons; pectoral fins 4-5 m (longest of any cetacean, giving the genus name "big-wing"). North Pacific population approximately 21,000 (2024 estimate), recovered from a 1960s low of approximately 5,000 at a 4.2× global recovery factor — second MMPA/IWC-recovery-arc exemplar after v1.60 harbor seal.

Male humpback breeding-season songs are the canonical cross-population cultural-transmission exemplar in marine mammal biology. Payne & McVay 1971 (*Science* 173:585) first characterized the hierarchical song structure: subunits → units → subphrases → phrases → themes. A complete song is 4-33 minutes long; Hawaiian recording sessions have captured continuous singing bouts up to 7 hours. Dominant frequencies 100-4000 Hz with harmonics reaching 24 kHz. Source levels up to 188 dB re 1 μPa at 1 m. Noad et al. 2000 (*Nature* 408:537) documented the 1996-1998 east-Australian population's wholesale adoption of the west-Australian song across approximately 2 years — the first rapid cross-population cultural revolution in a non-human mammal. Garland et al. 2011 (*Current Biology* 21:687) extended cultural transmission to ocean-basin scale across six populations. Humpback song is the **third cultural-transmission axis exemplar** after v1.58 Bewick's Wren (neighborhood scale) + v1.59 SRKW (matriline scale), stabilizing the axis at three species across three scales.
