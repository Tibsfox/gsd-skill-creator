## Summary

**PLT is the first micro-climate-intelligence Research project and the release is structurally about timing, not about plants.** The 80th Research project ships five modules totaling 1,762 research lines — Weather Intelligence (322), Cool-Season Crop (359), Warm-Season Planning (358), Orchard Management (332), and Defense & Companion Ecology (391) — plus a 990-line LaTeX mission-pack source (`pnw-planting-mission.tex`), a 176,143-byte pre-rendered PDF (`pnw-planting-mission.pdf`), a mission-pack landing page (454 lines, `mission-index.html`), and a four-file site shell (`index.html` 91 lines, `page.html` 199 lines, `mission.html` 69 lines, `style.css` 73 lines). Cumulatively the release lands +3,714 lines across 14 files in a single content commit (`4f84bf83d`), a single docs commit (`ed5de3d90`), and a merge commit (`5dd468e4b`). The module that matters most is Module 01 (Weather Intelligence): it takes the seven-day forecast, ENSO cycle overlay, soil-temperature-by-bed-type measurements, and frost-risk maps for six PNW locations (Seattle, Portland, Mukilteo, Everett, Bellingham, Olympia) and reveals them as the timing substrate every other module depends on. The rest of PLT is downstream of that substrate: cool-season crops are scheduled against soil temperature (Module 02), warm-season crops are gated on soil temperature (Module 03), orchard work is sequenced against dormant-period frost dynamics (Module 04), and companion ecology is timed against pollinator-emergence windows that track soil and air temperature together (Module 05).

**The "May Mistake" protocol names a universal PNW gardening failure mode and turns tacit knowledge into a transferable rule.** Every PNW gardener has planted tomatoes on May 1st — because the nursery stock arrives, because the calendar says spring, because neighboring regions are planting already — and watched those tomatoes die in a late frost or sulk through a cool May that never crossed the 60°F soil threshold warm-season crops require. The pattern is so universal it has earned a name. Module 03 (Warm-Season Planning) walks the protocol: do not plant tomato, pepper, or eggplant transplants until the soil is consistently 60°F at four-inch depth; in most PNW locations that date is not May 1st but June 1st, and in Mukilteo and Bellingham it can be June 15th. The protocol starts backward from the transplant date to calculate indoor-start timing (6-8 weeks for tomatoes, 8-10 weeks for peppers, 10-12 weeks for eggplant) and includes a two-week hardening schedule (gradually increasing outdoor exposure, wind, and temperature variation). Naming the mistake and writing down the protocol turns collective PNW gardening experience into explicit knowledge the next gardener does not have to re-learn through crop loss. The same pattern — name the failure mode, document the protocol — is the Research-series methodology at work: what experienced practitioners know in their bones gets written down so a beginner can start where a veteran leaves off.

**The five-module structure maps the PNW growing year, not a linear syllabus.** Module 01 (Weather Intelligence) establishes the atoms: seven-day forecast reading, ENSO cycle context (El Niño tending warmer/drier winters and cooler springs, La Niña tending cooler/wetter), soil temperature measurements by bed type (raised beds warming first, in-ground beds slowest, black-plastic-mulched beds in between), frost risk profiles for Seattle (last frost ~April 15, first frost ~November 1), Portland (last ~April 1, first ~November 15), Mukilteo (last ~April 25, first ~October 25), Everett (last ~April 20, first ~October 30), Bellingham (last ~May 10, first ~October 15), and Olympia (last ~April 25, first ~November 5), plus snowpack context for the Cascade and Olympic rain-shadow dynamics that determine regional water availability through the growing season. Module 02 (Cool-Season Crop) catalogs 28 crops across five categories — legumes (peas, fava beans), greens (lettuces, spinach, chard, kale, arugula, mustards), roots (radish, carrot, beet, turnip, parsnip), alliums (onion, shallot, leek, garlic), and herbs (cilantro, dill, parsley, chervil) — each with planting depth, spacing, days-to-maturity, and PNW-specific timing. Module 03 (Warm-Season Planning) walks indoor-start timing, the May Mistake protocol, variety recommendations tuned to PNW short warm season (Stupice tomato, Early Girl tomato, Glacier tomato, Gypsy pepper, Patio Baby eggplant), and the hardening schedule. Module 04 (Orchard Management) documents dormant-season work — dormant pruning for apple, pear, plum, cherry; dormant oil spray for scale and mite suppression; codling moth trap deployment timing; fire blight inspection protocol for apple and pear; bare-root tree planting window — the winter work that determines the summer harvest. Module 05 (Defense & Companion Ecology) ships the 15-plant companion matrix, six slug defense strategies ranked by effectiveness (iron phosphate bait, copper barriers, diatomaceous earth, beer traps, hand-picking at night, duck tractoring), nitrogen-fixing legumes and mineral-accumulating comfrey/yarrow documentation, pollinator timeline keyed to bloom phenology, and hardy annual flowers for season-long pollinator support (calendula, borage, phacelia, alyssum, bachelor button).

**Micro-climate intelligence is infrastructure intelligence, and PLT documents a pattern that applies far beyond gardening.** The seven-day forecast overlay, the ENSO context, the soil-temperature-by-bed-type measurements, and the six-location frost-risk map are not only gardening inputs — they are the same data a FoxFiber node-placement analyst would consult for conduit-freeze risk, the same data a solar panel installer would consult for orientation and shading, the same data a building-envelope designer would consult for insulation R-value targets, and the same data any PNW infrastructure project would consult at siting. PLT's deliberate framing of the data layer as general-purpose micro-climate intelligence (rather than as gardening-specific data) is the pedagogical move that makes the project reusable. The same principle drives the Research series more broadly: every project ships with a data layer that generalizes beyond the immediate subject, because infrastructure depends on the same micro-climate data regardless of whether the infrastructure is a raised bed, a fiber conduit, a photovoltaic array, or a building envelope. The pattern is explicit rather than implicit and future PNW-infrastructure Research projects will cite PLT's Module 01 as the canonical micro-climate data layer.

**The 15-plant companion matrix documents mechanisms, not folklore, and turns gardening into applied ecology.** Module 05's companion matrix is not a repetition of the usual "plant basil near tomatoes" advice — it is a mechanism-level map of the 15 interactions the PNW garden consistently exhibits. Nitrogen fixation (peas and fava beans hosting Rhizobium bacteria in root nodules; measured ~30-50 lb/acre equivalent nitrogen contribution over a season). Mineral accumulation (comfrey taproots reaching to 6-10 feet for potassium and calcium; yarrow similarly for phosphorus and magnesium; chop-and-drop cycle returning accumulated minerals to surface soil). Pest confusion (aromatic interplanting with basil, oregano, thyme, rosemary, sage, lavender, and chive masking tomato and brassica volatile signatures from specialist herbivores). Pollinator attraction (sequential bloom timing across calendula → borage → phacelia → cosmos → alyssum → bachelor button maintains pollinator presence from April through October). Trap-cropping (nasturtium drawing aphids off brassicas and cucumbers). Each interaction is documented with its mechanism, its species list, its seasonal window, and its measured outcome where data exists. The garden IS an ecology — the matrix makes the ecology legible in a way a beginner can implement and a veteran can refine.

**The weather-intelligence module is load-bearing for the entire project and for any derivative PNW-infrastructure work.** Module 01's 322 lines walk four distinct data layers: the seven-day forecast (wind, precipitation, temperature range, cloud cover with PNW-specific notes on the marine-layer morning pattern that delays soil warming in coastal areas), the ENSO overlay (with the current 2025-2026 La Niña context driving a cooler and wetter spring prediction), the soil-temperature progression (measured Seattle bed temperatures from March through June at 4" depth, with raised-bed data running two to three weeks ahead of in-ground), and the frost-risk-map data for six PNW locations. The frost-risk data in particular is the piece most commonly missing from general gardening references, because "last frost date" varies not only by latitude but by elevation, proximity to salt water, proximity to Puget Sound's thermal mass, rain-shadow position relative to the Olympics, and Cascade convergence zone effects for cities like Mukilteo and Lynnwood. Module 01 captures all of those modifiers with specific dates for specific cities and a reading guide for other PNW locations not on the list. The module is cited by every subsequent PLT module and will be cited by future PNW-infrastructure Research projects that need the same data layer.

**Orchard management is the Research series' first dormant-season study and walks a cycle most gardening references skip.** Module 04 covers the three to four months (late November through early March) that most gardening references treat as fallow. In fact the dormant season is when the next year's apple, pear, plum, and cherry harvest is determined: dormant pruning sets the tree architecture and next year's fruiting-spur distribution; dormant oil spray (a horticultural mineral oil applied at delayed-dormant phase) suppresses scale, mite, and overwintering aphid populations by 50-80% without harming pollinators that arrive post-bloom; codling moth pheromone trap deployment in early March catches the first generation-1 flight and calibrates spray timing for the season; fire blight inspection (for apple and pear, scanning for overwintering cankers on scaffold limbs) removes next year's inoculum source; bare-root tree planting (best done in late January through early March) establishes new trees with a four-to-six-week root-window before bud-break. The module ends with a cross-reference to forthcoming orchard-specific Research projects that will go deeper on single-species orchard management.

**The defense module ships six slug strategies ranked by effectiveness and documented with PNW-specific data.** Slugs are the signature PNW garden pest, and most gardening references either over-recommend iron phosphate bait or moralize about "natural" methods without efficacy data. Module 05 ranks six strategies: (1) iron phosphate bait (Sluggo-style, ~80% population reduction when applied weekly starting at first emergence, safe for pets and pollinators, breaks down to iron fertilizer), (2) copper barriers (50-70% reduction, high installation cost, effective indefinitely once installed), (3) diatomaceous earth (40-60% reduction, must stay dry to work, reapply after every rain — marginal in PNW), (4) beer traps (30-50% reduction, labor-intensive, concentrated local effect), (5) hand-picking at night with headlamp and salt cup (25-45% reduction depending on dedication), and (6) duck tractoring (70-90% reduction where feasible, requires livestock infrastructure). The ranking is with measured data where available and with farmer and master-gardener interview data where measurement is thin. The point is to give the reader a decision framework — effort vs effect vs cost — rather than a single "correct" answer, because the correct answer depends on the garden and the gardener.

**The color palette is a deliberate agricultural-ecology register — forest green, river blue, emerald.** PLT uses a three-color palette — forest green `#1B5E20`, river blue `#1565C0`, emerald `#2E7D32` — that reads as PNW-forest and PNW-river rather than as abstract-infrastructure or corporate-agriculture. Forest green is the dominant color, carrying the conifer-canopy association that is central to PNW identity. River blue is the accent, invoking the Puget Sound basin's water network and the rivers (Skagit, Snohomish, Duwamish, Willamette) that drain the Cascades into the basin. Emerald is the accent-to-the-accent, catching the understory and new-growth register and pairing with the gardener's-hand-in-soil tactility the subject warrants. The three-color discipline continues from COI (v1.49.58, ritual palette), ATC (v1.49.64, celestial palette), K8S (v1.49.69, infrastructure palette), and OTM (v1.49.74, creative-classroom palette) and extends naturally to agriculture-and-ecology subject matter. Palette signals register to the reader before the text does any work.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **Module 01's micro-climate-intelligence framing is load-bearing and generalizes cleanly beyond gardening.** Framing the weather-and-soil-data layer as general-purpose micro-climate intelligence (rather than as gardening-specific data) lets the module be cited by future PNW-infrastructure projects without any semantic adaptation. The seven-day forecast, the ENSO overlay, the soil-temperature-by-bed-type data, and the six-city frost-risk map read the same whether the reader is planting a tomato or siting a FoxFiber node. The decision to frame the data layer as general-purpose is the release's single most load-bearing editorial decision and the piece that gives PLT downstream reach.
- **The "May Mistake" protocol names a universal PNW experience and converts tacit knowledge into transferable rule.** Every PNW gardener has planted tomatoes too early and watched them fail; naming that pattern and writing down the protocol (wait for 60°F soil at 4" depth, harden for two weeks, plant after June 1st in most PNW locations) converts collective experience into explicit knowledge. The pattern — name the failure mode, document the protocol — is the Research-series methodology distilled into a single module. Future projects on subjects with universal-but-unspoken failure modes should follow PLT's pattern.
- **The 15-plant companion ecology matrix documents mechanisms rather than folklore.** Module 05's matrix walks nitrogen fixation, mineral accumulation, pest confusion, pollinator attraction, and trap-cropping with species lists, seasonal windows, and measured outcomes where data exists. The garden IS an ecology and the matrix makes the ecology legible. The pattern is the same pedagogical pattern OTM (v1.49.74) used to name the Outside Assistance rule: identify the mechanism under the practice and document it explicitly.
- **The three-commit atomic-content discipline continues to pay off.** Content commit `4f84bf83d` + docs commit `ed5de3d90` + merge commit `5dd468e4b` means the v1.49.79..v1.49.80 window contains exactly one meaningful state transition. Bisect finds it instantly. Forensic recovery from the repo alone is trivial. The discipline was formalized around v1.49.43 (WYR) and has held across every Research project since; PLT is no exception.
- **The three-color agriculture-and-ecology palette matches the subject's native vocabulary.** Forest green `#1B5E20`, river blue `#1565C0`, and emerald `#2E7D32` read as PNW-forest-and-river rather than as abstract corporate agriculture. The three-color discipline continues from COI (v1.49.58), ATC (v1.49.64), K8S (v1.49.69), and OTM (v1.49.74), extending naturally to agriculture-and-ecology subject matter. Palette signals register to the reader before the text does any work.
- **The six-strategy slug ranking with measured effectiveness gives readers a decision framework rather than a single "correct" answer.** Iron phosphate bait at 80% reduction, copper barriers at 50-70%, duck tractoring at 70-90% where feasible — each strategy is ranked with effort, effect, and cost so the reader can match a strategy to their garden and their tolerance. This framing (decision framework over prescription) is the pattern most gardening references miss and the pattern that makes Module 05 reusable across very different PNW gardens.

### What Could Be Better

- **The microclimate-variation-within-a-city discussion is thin.** The six-city frost-risk map works at city granularity, but within a single city the frost date can shift by 10-20 days (Mukilteo convergence zone, Seattle's Duwamish-valley cold-pocket, Portland's east-of-I-205 cooler-than-downtown pattern). Module 01 gestures at the variation but does not walk it with maps. A follow-on project or revision should add micro-map overlays for the three largest cities to close the intra-city variation gap.
- **The cultivar-level specificity in Module 02 is uneven.** The 28-crop master table lists species but only partially cites cultivars; a gardener reading the table sees "lettuce" without knowing whether Buttercrunch, Black-Seeded Simpson, Rouge d'Hiver, or Red Sails is the PNW-tested recommendation. A revision pass should add cultivar-level PNW recommendations for the top-10 cool-season crops, sourced against WSU and OSU Extension trials.
- **The orchard-management module covers four species and leaves a gap for the PNW-specific sixth or seventh species a gardener might plant.** Module 04 walks apple, pear, plum, and cherry. PNW gardeners also commonly plant fig (edge-of-zone in northern PNW, reliable in Portland), kiwi (hardy kiwi for northern locations, fuzzy kiwi south of Olympia), Asian pear, and quince. The orchard module's species list should be extended in a future revision to cover the full PNW orchard palette.
- **The cost-and-equity dimension is under-addressed.** A 28-crop garden with orchard trees, drip irrigation, row cover, bait and trap supplies, and seed-starting equipment is a multi-year capital investment that excludes the gardener who cannot pay up front. PLT documents the technical knowledge without walking the capital structure; a future project on community-garden economics, plot-sharing, and tool-library models would complement PLT's technical depth with the access-and-equity layer the subject requires.

### What Needs Improvement

- **The ENSO overlay lacks a current-year-updated reading guide.** Module 01 cites the ENSO cycle but does not walk how to update the reading as new ENSO data lands (NOAA's monthly CPC ENSO discussion, for example, or the BOM weekly update). A follow-on revision should include a reading-and-update protocol so gardeners can keep their ENSO context current across growing seasons rather than reading the module as static reference.
- **The soil-temperature data is Seattle-specific and the generalization to other PNW cities is left to the reader.** Module 01 provides explicit soil-temperature progressions for Seattle raised beds and in-ground beds from March through June, then says "adjust for your location." The adjustment rule is implicit. A revision pass should add explicit temperature-offset tables (Bellingham typically 2-3 weeks behind Seattle, Portland typically 1-2 weeks ahead, Mukilteo typically 1-2 weeks behind due to marine influence) so the reader has a concrete offset rather than a hand-wave.
- **The companion ecology matrix does not quantify pollinator outcomes.** Module 05's matrix names pollinator attraction as a mechanism but does not measure the outcome — bee visits per square meter per hour during peak bloom, for example, or fruit-set percentage differentials for plants within versus outside the bloom corridor. The data exists in the literature (USDA ARS studies, OSU Extension trials) and a revision pass should pull the quantitative outcome data into the matrix.
- **The mission pack LaTeX source uses inline cultivar lists that don't cross-reference the markdown modules.** Cultivar recommendations appear in both the LaTeX source (`pnw-planting-mission.tex`) and Module 03 but are maintained separately, creating drift risk when one source is revised and the other is not. A future build-discipline revision should centralize cultivar tables in a single source file and render into both the LaTeX and the Markdown at build time.
- **The release-notes chapter auto-generation policy should be formalized.** PLT, like every Research project since the parser landed, ships with parser-generated chapters at confidence 0.95. This README uplift rewrites the README to A-grade depth while leaving the chapters as DB-navigation substrate. The policy works but is not written down; an explicit policy document in `.planning/missions/release-uplift/` is overdue and would let future uplifts skip the rediscovery step.

## Lessons Learned

- **Micro-climate intelligence is infrastructure intelligence.** The same weather patterns, soil conditions, and frost dynamics PLT documents for gardening apply directly to any PNW infrastructure project. FoxFiber node placement, solar panel orientation, building insulation targets, and habitat-restoration site selection all depend on the same micro-climate data layer. Framing the data explicitly as general-purpose micro-climate intelligence (rather than as gardening-specific data) lets the module be cited across subjects without adaptation. Future Research projects on subjects with a shared data layer should foreground the data layer as general-purpose to maximize downstream reach.
- **The best documentation maps what people already know but haven't written down.** Every experienced PNW gardener knows the May Mistake, the slug pressure curve, and companion planting mechanisms; PLT's value is making that tacit knowledge explicit, structured, and transferable. The same principle drives the Research series itself: document what exists so others can find it. Every future Research project on a practice-intensive subject should audit for tacit knowledge that the practitioner community shares but that is not written down, and treat that tacit knowledge as the first target of documentation.
- **Timing is the hidden variable in every practical-knowledge domain, and foregrounding the timing layer transforms the domain's legibility.** PLT is structured around two facts — first frost date, last frost date — and the rest of the content is derived from those two dates. Cool-season timing, warm-season timing, orchard dormant-season timing, and pollinator-emergence timing all cascade from the frost window. Foregrounding the timing layer (Module 01's weather intelligence) rather than leading with a crop list (the common gardening-reference structure) makes the whole subject legible because the crops become consequences of the timing rather than independent atoms. The pattern generalizes: when a subject has a hidden timing variable, foregrounding it reorders the content in a way that reveals the structure.
- **Mechanism-level documentation beats folklore-level documentation.** "Plant basil near tomatoes" is folklore. "Basil emits a volatile compound mix that masks tomato-signature volatiles from the hornworm moth at a measurable reduction in ovipositing" is mechanism. Module 05's 15-plant matrix walks mechanisms rather than prescriptions, and the mechanism-level framing is what makes the matrix useful for PNW gardens whose conditions vary from the typical garden-advice source region (usually northeastern US or British Isles). Future ecology-or-practice-subject Research projects should default to mechanism over prescription, because the mechanism transfers across contexts and the prescription does not.
- **Ranked decision frameworks are more useful than single "correct" answers for practice subjects.** Six slug strategies ranked by effort, effect, and cost is more useful than a single "do this" recommendation because the right answer depends on the garden, the gardener's tolerance, and the surrounding infrastructure (are there chickens? pets? pollinator gardens next door?). The ranked framework lets the reader match strategy to context; the single answer does not. Every Research project on a practice subject should default to ranked decision frameworks, not prescriptions.
- **The three-commit atomic-content discipline is invisible until forensics are needed.** Content commit `4f84bf83d` + docs commit `ed5de3d90` + merge commit `5dd468e4b` is the same shape every Research project has shipped under since v1.49.43. The discipline produces no visible benefit until a repo-forensics pass becomes necessary — a corrupted working tree, a lost feature branch, a bisect across a many-release window.
