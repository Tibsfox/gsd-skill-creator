## Mission Overview

GRAIL is the obs#2 sustainer of the ROBOTIC-LUNAR-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's Discovery-program twin lunar gravity orbiters, two nearly identical spacecraft that flew in precise formation in a low near-polar orbit and measured the tiny changes in the distance between them to map the Moon's gravity field at unprecedented resolution, and from it the Moon's crustal thickness and interior structure. The principal investigator was Maria Zuber of MIT; the mission was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory; the two spacecraft were built by Lockheed Martin. GRAIL was the eleventh Discovery mission (★ NOT the first NASA Discovery mission to the Moon — Lunar Prospector holds that). GRAIL-A and GRAIL-B, renamed Ebb and Flow by Montana schoolchildren (fourth-graders at Emily Dickinson Elementary School in Bozeman) in a nationwide contest tied to the mission's MoonKAM student-camera education project led by Sally Ride, launched together on 10 September 2011 (UTC) on a single Delta II 7920H-10 — the most powerful Delta II configuration — from Cape Canaveral SLC-17B, cruised a long, fuel-efficient low-energy trans-lunar trajectory through the Sun-Earth Lagrange region for about three and a half months, and entered lunar orbit in a two-spacecraft sequence about a day apart — Ebb about 31 December 2011 and Flow about 1 January 2012. They flew in precise tandem in a low, near-circular, near-polar orbit — near fifty-five kilometres mean altitude during the primary mission, lowered to about twenty-three kilometres in the extended mission: a dedicated gravity and interior-structure mapper. The story is the launch from Cape Canaveral, the long low-energy cruise to the Moon, the two orbiters circling pole to pole one following the other, the inter-spacecraft range stretching and compressing by microns over buried mass, and the planned final descent to a mountain near the lunar north pole. The science is central: the distance between the two spacecraft changed slightly as the leading one passed over regions of greater or lesser mass; that inter-spacecraft range was measured to within microns by a Ka-band ranging link, and the range-rate variations were inverted into the gravity field (Zuber et al. 2013). ★ The satellite-to-satellite tracking technique was pioneered by the GRACE mission at Earth in 2002; GRAIL flew it for the first time at the Moon. From the highest-resolution gravity field of the Moon ever obtained, GRAIL produced the first global map of the Moon's crustal thickness and interior structure — a crust thinner than thought (about 34 to 43 kilometres average), highly porous and fractured (a bulk crustal porosity near twelve percent), and threaded with ancient buried linear gravity anomalies read as giant magmatic dikes from the early expansion of the Moon (Wieczorek et al. 2013; Andrews-Hanna et al. 2013). The scoping is load-bearing throughout: the HIGHEST-RESOLUTION gravity field of the Moon — distinct from Lunar Prospector's FIRST (coarse Doppler) global gravity field; the first global map of the Moon's crustal thickness and INTERIOR structure — distinct from Lunar Prospector's surface GEOCHEMICAL maps; and the first twin-spacecraft formation flight in lunar orbit — the technique flown at Earth first by GRACE, not the first formation flight ever. ★★ The mission ended in December 2012 (launched 10 September 2011, entered lunar orbit about 31 December 2011 and 1 January 2012, mapped the Moon's gravity for about a year, ended 17 December 2012 with a planned final descent onto a mountain near the north pole), so the catalog frames the story in the PAST tense throughout (GRAIL launched, orbited, mapped, revealed, ended). ★ The dates are kept distinct: launch 10 September 2011, lunar-orbit insertion about 31 December 2011 (Ebb) and 1 January 2012 (Flow), end of mission 17 December 2012. ## Surprises

- **Two spacecraft mapped the Moon's gravity by measuring the distance between themselves to within microns.** GRAIL's Ebb and Flow flew in precise tandem in a low near-polar orbit, and the tiny stretching and compressing of the distance between them — by microns — as the leading spacecraft passed over buried mass became the gravity signal, inverted into the highest-resolution gravity field of the Moon ever obtained.
- **The Moon's crust turned out thinner and more porous than thought.** From the gravity field GRAIL mapped the Moon's crustal thickness and interior structure for the first time globally, revealing a crust about 34 to 43 kilometres thick — ten to twenty kilometres thinner than prior estimates — and highly porous and fractured, with a bulk crustal porosity near twelve percent to depths of at least a few kilometres.
- **Ancient buried dikes from the early expansion of the Moon.** GRAIL's gravity gradiometry revealed long, straight, buried linear gravity anomalies threading the lunar crust — interpreted as giant magmatic dikes intruded during an early expansion of the Moon — structures hidden beneath the surface and read only through their gravitational pull.
- **A fossorial mole that reads its hidden underground world by touch.** Townsend's Mole (*Scapanus townsendii*), the largest North American mole, a Pacific Northwest native of moist lowland meadows and forest edges west of the Cascades in Washington and Oregon, is nearly blind and lives almost entirely underground in an extensive tunnel system, sensing its hidden world by touch and vibration and pushing up the soil it cannot see — a mirror of GRAIL reading the Moon's hidden interior by sensing the pull of mass it could not see.
- **A wildflower that keeps its life in a corm buried beneath the soil.** Western Spring Beauty (*Claytonia lanceolata*), a small spring-ephemeral wildflower of PNW mountain meadows, stores its life through the year in a small round corm buried beneath the soil, sending up leaves and pale pink-veined flowers for only a few weeks as the snow retreats before dying back underground (ecological description only) — bound with the throughline of reading what lies hidden beneath the surface.

## Dedication

v1.277 GRAIL is dedicated to the people across MIT, NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, and Lockheed Martin who built and flew the twin orbiters that read the Moon from within. The principal investigator, Maria Zuber, who carried a precise gravity mission to flight, and the team at JPL who managed it. The engineers at Lockheed Martin who built two nearly identical spacecraft and the Delta II that lifted both together, and the flight team who held the long cruise and settled Ebb and Flow into a low orbit pole to pole around the Moon. The teams behind the Ka-band ranging link, who measured the distance between the two spacecraft to within microns, and the scientists who inverted that signal into the highest-resolution gravity field of the Moon and the first global map of its crustal thickness and hidden interior. The students of Bozeman who named the orbiters Ebb and Flow, and Sally Ride, who led the MoonKAM cameras and whose name marks the mountain near the north pole where the twins came to rest. They read a world from the pull of mass unseen, and learned what lies hidden beneath its surface.
