## Summary

**Alice in Chains** arrives at E=8 -- the engine's maximum, tied only by The Sonics (241) and Jimi Hendrix (245). The E=8 assignment makes Alice in Chains the third entry to reach the ceiling and the second in three degrees, a concentration of maximum energy that reflects the engine's position in one of the most significant passages of the PNW music canon. Alice in Chains formed in Seattle in 1987. Jerry Cantrell, Layne Staley, Mike Inez, and Sean Kinney -- the classic lineup -- produced *Facelift* (1990), *Dirt* (1992), *Jar of Flies* (1994), and *Alice in Chains* (1995) before Staley's increasing seclusion effectively ended the band's active phase. Layne Staley died on April 5, 2002. Alice in Chains reformed with vocalist William DuVall in 2005 and has continued recording and touring since.

Alice in Chains is one of the **Big Four of grunge** -- Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Alice in Chains -- the four bands that constituted the original Seattle sound as it was understood internationally in the early 1990s. Of the four, Alice in Chains is the one that did not fit cleanly. The other three had clear antecedents in punk or classic rock. Alice in Chains came from metal -- from the same tradition as Queensrÿche (documented at degree 236) and from the harder end of the 1980s rock spectrum. *Facelift* is a metal album. *Dirt* is something else: a document of addiction and grief that became one of the most critically respected albums of the 1990s and one of the darkest records ever to sell in the millions. Cantrell's down-tuned guitar, Staley's harmonized vocals, and the band's commitment to a sound that was heavy rather than melodic placed them outside the clean grunge narrative, but they were unambiguously part of it.

On the soundscape side, **Waterfall Sol Duc** enters -- **Sol Duc Falls** in Olympic National Park, a forked waterfall where the Sol Duc River splits around a central basalt island and falls approximately 50 feet into a narrow canyon. The **canyon roar** -- the acoustic effect of a waterfall in an enclosed rock channel, where the sound reflects off the canyon walls and returns amplified, filling the space with a sustained reverberation that is different from an open-air waterfall -- registers at E=7. Sol Duc Falls is a different acoustic environment from Snoqualmie Falls (degree 246): smaller in height, but confined, and the confinement changes the sound.

The Sol Duc watershed is within the core of the Olympic Peninsula -- one of the wettest places in the continental United States, receiving 140 or more inches of precipitation annually in some areas. The Hoh Rain Forest and the Sol Duc valley represent the Pacific temperate rainforest at its most intact. Olympic National Park protects approximately 922,000 acres of this landscape. Sol Duc Falls is accessible via a 0.8-mile trail from the Sol Duc Hot Springs area and is one of the most visited points in the park. The basalt formation that splits the river at the falls is the result of volcanic activity during the Cenozoic -- the same geological forces that built the Cascades are visible here in the canyon walls.
