## Summary

The fiftieth paired release and the degree where everything happens on the first try. On September 12, 1966, a Titan II launched Gemini 11 from Launch Complex 19 at Cape Kennedy with Pete Conrad commanding and Dick Gordon as pilot -- Gordon's first spaceflight, Gordon born in Seattle, Gordon a graduate of the University of Washington with a degree in chemistry, class of 1951. Eighty-five minutes later -- before completing a single revolution of the Earth -- Conrad had rendezvoused and docked with the Agena target vehicle. First orbit. First try. No multi-orbit chase. No cautious approach over hours. The M=1 rendezvous profile: the technique Apollo would need when the Lunar Module ascended from the Moon's surface with limited consumables and no time for the leisurely fourth-orbit rendezvous of earlier Gemini flights. Conrad proved it worked. Launch to docking in 85 minutes. The shortest distance between two spacecraft in the Gemini program. Both the mission planners at Houston and the flight controllers at the Mission Operations Control Room recognized that this compression was the single most important orbital-mechanics demonstration of the entire Gemini program -- the profile Apollo would inherit wholesale.

Then they went higher than any human had gone before. The Agena's Bell 8247 engine fired for approximately 26 seconds, boosting the docked stack to an apogee of 1,374 km -- more than three times higher than the International Space Station's typical orbit, nearly twice the altitude Young and Collins had reached on Gemini 10 two months earlier (763 km). From that altitude, Conrad and Gordon could see the curvature of the Earth extending to the horizon in all directions. Conrad reported being able to see from Africa to Australia in a single glance. The spacecraft passed through the inner Van Allen belt. The record would stand for 26 months -- until Frank Borman, Jim Lovell, and Bill Anders flew Apollo 8 to the Moon in December 1968. Dick Gordon, the Seattle kid who had studied chemistry at the University of Washington sixteen years earlier, was now the highest-flying human in history, 1,374 km above a planet that included, somewhere below the clouds, the campus where he had learned about molecular bonds. The altitude record and the songwriting commitment mirror each other at degree 49: both sides of the paired release reach their respective maxima at the same time, the altitude record and the energy record synchronized across the dual tracks.

Then they did something no one had ever attempted: tethered spaceflight. Gordon had attached a 30-meter Dacron tether between Gemini 11 and the Agena during a brutally difficult EVA -- straddling the Agena's smooth nose, visor fogging from exertion, sweat pooling in his eyes, Conrad talking him through it from the cockpit with steady calm. The tether was attached. Conrad undocked, backed away until the line was taut, and fired thrusters perpendicular to the tether. The dumbbell configuration rotated at approximately one revolution every six minutes. Accelerometers detected 0.00015 g -- about one-six-thousandth of Earth's surface gravity. Tiny. But real. The first artificial gravity ever generated in space. The 30 meters of Dacron rope that connect Gemini 11 to every space elevator proposal, every rotating habitat concept, every tethered-spaceflight experiment in the sixty years since. Finally, Gemini 11 performed the first fully automatic reentry -- the onboard computer managing the trajectory without manual input, landing 4.5 km from the target, within visual range of the recovery carrier USS Guam. The most accurate Gemini splashdown. Four firsts in three days, executed by a 5'6" Navy test pilot from Philadelphia who had been rejected from Mercury for refusing psychological tests and a Seattle native who had set the transcontinental speed record in an F-8U Crusader.

Pete Conrad would fly twice more: Apollo 12 in November 1969 -- third crewed lunar landing, Ocean of Storms, first words on the Moon: "Whoopee! Man, that may have been a small one for Neil, but that's a long one for me!" winning a $500 bet with journalist Oriana Fallaci that NASA did not script astronaut words -- and Skylab 2 in May 1973, where his crew saved the entire Skylab program by freeing a stuck solar panel and deploying a sunshade. Four flights. Walked on the Moon. Saved a space station. Left NASA in 1973. Died July 8, 1999, in Ojai, California, from injuries sustained in a motorcycle accident. He was 69. The astronaut who proved you didn't have to be solemn about spaceflight. The irreverence was not carelessness -- it was the confidence of a pilot who had done the math and knew the margins.

Dick Gordon would fly once more: as Command Module Pilot on Apollo 12, orbiting the Moon alone in Yankee Clipper while Conrad and Alan Bean walked on the surface. Originally assigned to command Apollo 18, which was cancelled in September 1970 as part of NASA's budget cuts. Gordon never walked on the Moon. The Seattle native who flew higher than any human before him -- who set the transcontinental speed record, who attached the first tether in space while his body failed him -- never set foot on the lunar surface. He left NASA in 1972. Died November 6, 2017, in San Marcos, California. He was 88. Gordon and Conrad both became Apollo 12 crewmates after Gemini 11 proved the first-orbit rendezvous profile; the same two-pilot team flew together again three years later, the same voice in Gordon's ear through the tether EVA guiding him through the Lunar Module Pilot's lunar-orbit solo pass.

Kimya Dawson does not do cautious either. She emerged from The Moldy Peaches -- the band she co-founded with Adam Green around 1994 in New York City's anti-folk scene at the Sidewalk Cafe on the Lower East Side, the band that defined anti-folk by refusing every convention the folk establishment held sacred. Tuning was approximate. Lyrics were uncomfortably personal. Performances happened in costume, in living rooms, on the street. When Dawson moved to Olympia, Washington, in November 2006, she carried that refusal with her. K Records. Calvin Johnson's label, founded in 1982 at Evergreen State College's KAOS-FM. The spiritual ground zero of Pacific Northwest DIY. Her solo albums are raw in a way that makes "lo-fi" sound like a production choice rather than a philosophy. She records in her home. She sings about anxiety, parenthood, mortality, bodily functions, and the specific quality of love between friends who have seen each other at their worst. The folk punk label fits not because she plays fast -- her tempo is often gentle -- but because the punk is in the refusal to wait for permission, to polish, to present anything other than exactly what she is in the moment of singing. Conrad's first-orbit rendezvous eliminated the multi-orbit buffer. Dawson's K Records work eliminates the production buffer. Both trust that the first revolution -- the first raw attempt -- carries what matters. The parallel is not thematic garnish; both sides of this degree operate on the same operational principle: maximum commitment on the first attempt, no intermediary allowed between the intent and the artifact.

The Juno paradox is the structural heart of the Dawson story. In 2007, Ellen Page's Juno arrived in theatres with six Kimya Dawson tracks on the soundtrack. The film grossed $231 million. The soundtrack debuted #1 on the Billboard 200 chart. "Anyone Else But You" -- a duet with Adam Green from a Moldy Peaches album no major label had wanted to release -- reached #91 on the Hot 100. The Grammy for Best Compilation Soundtrack followed. Every ticket to every major label, every opening-night spot on every late-night show, every brand-partnership agent in Los Angeles found its way to Dawson's voicemail in the months after the film. She did not sign. She released *Alphabutt*, a children's album of songs about bodily functions and transportation and going to bed, through K Records in 2008. She continued to live in Olympia. She continued to drive a minivan. She continued to perform in living rooms and small theatres, often with her daughter Panda and often in collaboration with Aesop Rock as The Uncluded. The Juno paradox: an artist who achieved the commercial exposure most musicians spend their entire careers pursuing, and who then used that exposure to make the same kind of raw, intimate, DIY music she had been making before, for audiences of the same approximate size as before, through the same tiny label as before. The paradox is not cynicism or modesty -- it is commitment to the first revolution, the raw take, the refusal to let the compression ratio of the second revolution distort the signal that the first revolution captured.

And deeper into the thicket than any bird in the quintet has gone, where the salal grows head-high and the blackberry closes overhead and the light does not reach the ground -- a sound. First: a violent rustling in the leaf litter, loud enough to hear from ten meters. The double-scratch. Then a sharp *tsik* from inside the brush. And then, drifting out of the darkest corner of the thicket -- a song. Rich. Warm. Full. Eight clear whistled notes, slow and measured, each one carrying weight. *Passerella iliaca.* The Fox Sparrow. Fifth consecutive Passerellidae -- a new record, surpassing the swallow quintet at degrees 27-31. The largest sparrow in the quintet. The highest energy (E=3). The richest song. The bird that forages with maximum commitment on every attempt: both feet, full force, hundreds of times per foraging bout, excavating trenches in the leaf litter that other species follow. Conrad docked on the first orbit. Dawson records on the first take. The Fox Sparrow scratches with both feet on the first lunge. Three acts of maximum commitment. Three first revolutions. The gravity is small, but it is real. The paired structure of the engine produces this kind of triangulation almost automatically: when the NASA track, the S36 music track, and the SPS organism track each converge on first-attempt commitment as their defining behavior, the paired release has found a genuine structural resonance.

The Fox Sparrow is taxonomically the most volatile species the engine has yet documented. The American Ornithological Society recognizes one species -- *Passerella iliaca* -- containing four subspecies groups: Red (*iliaca* group, boreal forest), Sooty (*unalaschcensis* group, coastal Pacific), Slate-colored (*schistacea* group, interior western mountains), and Thick-billed (*megarhyncha* group, Sierra Nevada). The IOC and BirdLife International have proposed splitting these four groups into four separate species, a proposal rejected by a split AOS vote in 2003 and still under active reconsideration. The Pacific Northwest subspecies -- *Passerella unalaschcensis unalaschcensis*, the Sooty Fox Sparrow -- is the dark-chocolate-brown bird of dense coastal thickets from southeast Alaska through the San Juan Islands to the western Washington coast. A single species that may be four species. A single genus (*Passerella*) that is monotypic (only this species) -- and may, if the split is accepted, suddenly contain four species. The taxonomic volatility mirrors Dawson's artistic volatility: one artist whose discography contains The Moldy Peaches and Kimya Dawson solo and *Alphabutt* and *The Uncluded*, each effectively a distinct project, each performed under the same name, the unity provided by the performer's voice and the refusal of intermediary polish. Both sides of this degree operate on the same identity problem: what counts as one versus four, at what point does diversity of expression exceed the bounds of a single species or a single artist, and how do the listeners (conspecific rivals for the sparrow, fans for the artist) decide when to recognize continuity versus when to recognize rupture.

The Seattle-Olympia axis is the degree's third major synchrony. Dick Gordon was born in Seattle in 1929. He graduated from the University of Washington in 1951 with a bachelor's degree in chemistry. He joined the Navy, became a test pilot at Patuxent River, set the transcontinental speed record in an F-8U Crusader in 1961 (Bendix Trophy winner), and was selected for NASA's third astronaut group in 1963. Kimya Dawson was born in Bedford Hills, New York, in 1972, co-founded The Moldy Peaches in New York City around 1994, and moved to Olympia, Washington, in November 2006 to align her geography with her values. Seattle at the north end, Olympia sixty miles south, both anchoring the southern arm of Puget Sound. Gordon carried Seattle to the highest point any human had reached. Dawson carried anti-folk to the city that K Records and Riot Grrrl had been holding open for her since 1982. Both transplants -- Gordon from Seattle out into space, Dawson from New York down into Olympia -- are acts of geographic commitment that shape the rest of the career. The engine's geography pulls forward: Seattle (Jurado at 45, Votolato at 46, Pedro the Lion at 47, Cahoone at 48) plus Olympia (Dawson at 49) now form a two-city PNW structure. The folk-adjacent streak of five degrees and the Passerellidae streak of five degrees both terminate their current counts at degree 49, and the same geography -- the Seattle-Olympia axis along I-5 and Puget Sound's southern arm -- anchors them both.

## Part A: Kimya Dawson -- THE RAW TAKE

- **Bedford Hills Origins.** Born November 17, 1972, in Bedford Hills, New York, in a nineteenth-century farm cottage that also operated as a daycare. Grew up adjacent to Manhattan but outside its gravity. The rural-suburban New York origin informed her later geographic choice of Olympia -- small town, intentional community, distance from the industry -- as continuous with her earliest environment rather than a reaction against it.
- **Moldy Peaches and the Anti-Folk Scene.** Co-founded The Moldy Peaches with Adam Green around 1994 at the Sidewalk Cafe on New York City's Lower East Side, the anti-folk venue that cultivated Lach's Monday night open stages. The band defined anti-folk through a rolling catalogue of refusals: approximate tuning, uncomfortable personal lyrics, costume performances in living rooms, no production budget. The debut *Moldy Peaches* (2001) was recorded on a four-track in Dawson's apartment.
- **Olympia Transplant, November 2006.** Moved from New York City to Olympia, Washington, in November 2006, three years into her solo career, one year before the Juno soundtrack broke. Olympia was not a marketing choice. Olympia was an alignment decision: K Records, Riot Grrrl's birthplace, Evergreen State College's KAOS-FM, the DIY infrastructure that matched her commitments. The radical transplant as principle rather than career strategy.
- **K Records Signing.** Signed with K Records, Calvin Johnson's Olympia-based label founded in 1982 at KAOS-FM. K Records is the spiritual ground zero of Pacific Northwest DIY and a spiritual kin to SST, Dischord, and Kill Rock Stars -- independent labels whose artist relationships assume the artist retains creative control. Dawson's post-Juno catalogue remains with K Records.
- **The Juno Paradox (2007).** Six Kimya Dawson tracks on the Juno film soundtrack. Film grossed $231 million worldwide. Soundtrack debuted #1 on the Billboard 200. "Anyone Else But You" (Moldy Peaches duet with Adam Green) reached #91 on the Hot 100. Grammy Award for Best Compilation Soundtrack. Dawson did not sign a major label, did not hire a manager, did not change her touring style, did not move out of Olympia. The Juno paradox is her defining structural feature: maximum exposure met with minimum infrastructure change.
- ***Alphabutt* and the Children's Album.** Released *Alphabutt* (2008, K Records), a children's album produced after Juno's commercial peak. Songs about bodily functions, transportation, going to bed, alphabet silliness. Recorded in her home with her daughter Panda and other children singing. The timing of *Alphabutt* -- immediately after the Juno moment -- is the clearest operational signal that Dawson did not intend to capitalize on visibility in the industry sense.
- **The Uncluded with Aesop Rock (2013).** Collaborative album with Aesop Rock released 2013, a folk-punk/indie-hip-hop hybrid that neither artist could have produced alone. The project demonstrates her tether-as-collaboration pattern: every Dawson project is a partnership (The Moldy Peaches with Adam Green, K Records with Calvin Johnson, *Alphabutt* with children, The Uncluded with Aesop Rock), and the music exists in the tension between the two connected artists.
- **Performance Model: Living Rooms and Small Theatres.** Dawson's touring practice emphasizes intimate spaces -- living rooms, basement shows, small independent venues of 50-300 capacity -- rather than the club-to-theatre-to-arena progression most artists pursue after breakthrough moments. Her fans are regulars, not audiences. The performance model is continuous with The Moldy Peaches' Sidewalk Cafe origin.
- **Nonbinary Identity and Language.** Dawson uses she/they/grandpa pronouns and is the first openly nonbinary artist in the S36 engine. Language flexibility extends to performance: on-stage monologues, audience conversation, casual profanity, direct address in songs. The identity commitment is part of the same refusal-of-polish that defines the raw take aesthetic.
- **Genre Stage 30 Opens.** Dawson's Folk Punk / Anti-Folk introduces Genre Stage 30 to the S36 ledger. Before degree 49, the engine's 49 artists had moved through folk, indie folk, grunge, indie rock, electronic, jazz, soul, hip-hop, and many other categories -- but no punk-adjacent genre. Dawson's work opens the punk territory; The Sonics at degree 55 will push it further with the first garage rock / proto-punk.

## Part B: Fox Sparrow -- THE DOUBLE-SCRATCH

- **Taxonomic Complexity.** *Passerella iliaca* is a single species (AOS, eBird) or four species (IOC, BirdLife International). The four subspecies groups -- Red (*iliaca*, boreal forest), Sooty (*unalaschcensis*, Pacific coast), Slate-colored (*schistacea*, interior western mountains), Thick-billed (*megarhyncha*, Sierra Nevada) -- differ in plumage, song, bill morphology, and mitochondrial DNA. A 2003 AOU proposal to split was rejected on a split vote and remains under active reconsideration. The most taxonomically volatile species in the SPS engine.
- **The Sooty PNW Form.** The Pacific Northwest Fox Sparrow is *Passerella iliaca unalaschcensis* -- the Sooty subspecies group. Dark chocolate-brown plumage (rather than the Red group's rusty streaking), large body, heavy bill. Breeds in dense coastal thickets from southeast Alaska through the San Juan Islands to western Washington. The bird the engine is documenting is the PNW-specific Sooty form.
- **Monotypic Genus.** *Passerella* is a monotypic genus (contains only *Passerella iliaca*), a structural rarity in Passerellidae. If the IOC split is accepted, the genus would suddenly contain four species. The monotypic-to-quadritypic potential mirrors Dawson's single-artist-to-many-projects identity structure.
- **The Double-Scratch Foraging Technique.** Fox Sparrows forage with the signature double-scratch: plant both feet simultaneously, lunge forward, then rake backward with explosive force, excavating leaf litter and exposing seeds and invertebrates. Audible 20 meters away in dense forest. The trench the double-scratch creates becomes foraging habitat for less-kinetic species. The Spotted Towhee (degree 46) also double-scratches but with less force and less frequency -- the Fox Sparrow is the family's most committed double-scratcher.
- **Rich Melodious Song.** The Fox Sparrow's song is one of the richest in Passerellidae: 5-12 song types per male, clear whistles, slurred notes, trilled segments, 2-8 kHz frequency range. Comparable to Hermit Thrush for musical quality. Each male has a repertoire of song types that he uses in context-dependent sequences -- introduction songs, territorial songs, courtship songs. The richest singer in the degree-45-to-49 Passerellidae quintet.
- **Song Culture: Shared Elements, Individual Recombination.** Neighboring Fox Sparrow males share song elements across territorial boundaries -- young males learn from multiple adult tutors in their natal area and weave the neighborhood's acoustic palette into individually distinctive repertoires. This is cultural transmission as creative recombination, distinct from the White-crowned Sparrow's (degree 48) rigid dialect transmission. The fluid-repertoire model for avian song culture.
- **Habitat: Dense Coastal Thickets.** The Sooty Fox Sparrow occupies dense thickets -- salal, blackberry, sword fern, willow, and alder understory -- where the light barely penetrates. This is the densest habitat of any species in the Passerellidae quintet. The Song Sparrow (45) uses marsh edges; the Spotted Towhee (46) uses thickets and edges; the Dark-eyed Junco (47) uses open floor and coniferous edges; the White-crowned Sparrow (48) uses shrub-edge and roadside; the Fox Sparrow (49) goes deeper than any of them.
- **Size and Physical Dominance.** The largest Passerellidae in the engine streak (18-19 cm length, ~32 g mass). Heavier bill than other sparrows. Larger feet with larger claws, adapted for the double-scratch. The highest-energy forager and the loudest singer in the quintet simultaneously -- both maxima converge on this species.
- **Altitudinal and Latitudinal Migration.** Northern Fox Sparrows are long-distance migrants (Alaska and Canada to the southern US); southern forms are short-distance altitudinal migrants (Sierra Nevada down to lowland valleys). Multiple migration strategies coexist within the species complex. PNW Sooty Fox Sparrows are primarily short-distance migrants or residents in milder coastal habitats.
- **Acoustic Signature of the Thicket.** The Fox Sparrow's song emerges from inside dense vegetation where the singer is invisible. The song is the bird's signature -- a listener walking through Fox Sparrow habitat hears the song but almost never sees the bird. This projected-from-inside acoustic presence mirrors Kimya Dawson's recording-in-the-home practice: the singer is inside the dense material (the thicket, the rental house in Olympia) and the song projects outward while the body stays hidden.

The engine now has a reference pattern for high-density mission documentation: each first gets its own paragraph with a specific quantitative value (85 minutes, 1,374 km, 0.00015 g, 4.5 km), and the paragraphs stack chronologically rather than thematically.

A future PNW-aviation degree or a dedicated Gordon tribute at Apollo 12 should restore the detail.
- **The Fox Sparrow species split is under-explored as a taxonomic process.** The four-way IOC/BirdLife split proposal is one of the most active species-complex debates in New World Passerellidae taxonomy, and the engine has an opportunity to document ongoing taxonomic revision as it happens. Future Fox Sparrow references (if any of the four groups enters the engine at a later degree) should cite the current status of the split decision.
- **Marine species remain critically overdue.** Forty-nine degrees of "Sound of Puget Sound," zero species ON the Sound. Twelve consecutive Passeriformes (degrees 38-49). The existential mismatch between the series title and the taxonomic coverage grows more acute with every terrestrial passerine. Degree 50 or degree 51 must address this or the omission becomes a defining structural irony rather than a passing gap.

## Lessons Learned

1. **First-orbit rendezvous achieved.** Conrad closed the distance to the Agena in one orbit -- 85 minutes, launch to docking. The M=1 profile Apollo would need for Lunar Module ascent rendezvous, proven operationally viable. The fastest rendezvous of the Gemini program. The technique that made lunar orbit rendezvous feasible without redesigning Apollo's consumables budget. Every subsequent LOR mission inherits this demonstration.
2. **Highest crewed altitude: 1,374 km.** The Agena PPS burn boosted the docked stack to an apogee nearly twice the Gemini 10 record (763 km). More than three times the ISS orbit. The spacecraft passed through the inner Van Allen belt. Conrad could see from Africa to Australia in a single glance. The record stood for 26 months until Apollo 8 flew to the Moon in December 1968 and left Earth orbit entirely.
3. **First tethered spaceflight generates first artificial gravity.** The 30-meter Dacron tether between Gemini 11 and the Agena, rotating at approximately one revolution per six minutes, produced 0.00015 g -- tiny but real, the first artificial gravity ever generated in space. Every subsequent tethered-spaceflight proposal (TSS-1, TSS-1R, space elevator concepts, rotating-habitat designs) traces back to this: 30 meters of Dacron rope and two spacecraft spinning slowly above the Earth.
4. **First fully automatic reentry.** The onboard computer managed reentry without manual input, achieving 4.5 km accuracy -- the most precise Gemini landing, within visual range of the recovery carrier USS Guam. Validated the automated guidance approach Apollo would use for the more demanding return from lunar distance. Automated reentry became the default after this mission.
5. **Dick Gordon's first flight -- Seattle native, UW class of 1951.** The deepest PNW thread in the engine's NASA track. Born in Seattle. Educated at UW with a chemistry degree. Bendix Trophy winner (1961, transcontinental speed record in an F-8U Crusader). Future Apollo 12 Command Module Pilot -- orbited the Moon alone in Yankee Clipper while Conrad and Bean walked. Assigned to command the cancelled Apollo 18. Never walked on the Moon.
6. **Fifth consecutive folk-adjacent ties the swallow quintet record.** Folk (45, Jurado), Folk (46, Votolato), Indie Folk (47, Pedro the Lion), Folk (48, Cahoone), Folk Punk (49, Dawson). Five degrees, five distinct production paradigms, two cities (Seattle, Olympia), three labels (Sub Pop, Barsuk, K Records). The longest genre-family run in the engine, now tied with the swallow quintet. Degree 50 determines whether the record breaks or the engine shifts genre territory.
7. **Fifth consecutive Passerellidae sets a NEW RECORD.** Song Sparrow (45), Spotted Towhee (46), Dark-eyed Junco (47), White-crowned Sparrow (48), Fox Sparrow (49). Five genera, five habitats, five acoustic strategies. Surpasses the swallow quintet (degrees 27-31) for the longest single-family streak in engine history. The Fox Sparrow -- largest, loudest, richest song -- is the climax of the sequence, the most-committed member of the family.
8. **First Olympia degree opens the second PNW city.** The Seattle-Olympia axis is now structural. Seattle provides scale (Sub Pop, KEXP, grunge). Olympia provides ideology (K Records, Riot Grrrl, Evergreen State College). Sixty miles apart, connected by Interstate 5 and the southern arm of Puget Sound. The engine's geographic architecture expands from one pole to two.
9. **First punk-adjacent genre (Folk Punk, Genre Stage 30).** Forty-nine degrees of jazz, soul, R&B, funk, blues, rock, gospel, electronic, and folk -- punk in any form was absent. Dawson's folk punk / anti-folk opens a new genre territory. The punk is not in the tempo but in the refusal: no polish, no permission, no production buffer. The Sonics at degree 55 will push further with the first full garage rock / proto-punk.
10. **Gemini program: 9 of 10 crewed flights complete.** One flight remains: Gemini 12 (Lovell/Aldrin, November 1966), where Buzz Aldrin will solve the EVA problem with underwater neutral-buoyancy training and the innovation of handholds and foot restraints everywhere. The program that proved every technique Apollo needed is approaching its final flight. After Gemini 12, the engine enters the Apollo-reconnaissance-only window until the Block II Command Module is ready.

## The Gemini 11 Mission

| Phase | Date | Event |
|-------|------|-------|
| Agena Launch | Sep 12, 1966 | Atlas-Agena launches GATV-11 from LC-14, 97 minutes before Gemini |
| Launch | Sep 12, 1966 13:42 UTC | Titan II launches Gemini 11 from LC-19 with Conrad and Gordon |
| First-Orbit Rendezvous | Sep 12, T+85 min | Conrad achieves M=1 rendezvous and docking -- first orbit, 85 minutes, fastest Gemini rendezvous |
| Gordon's EVA | Sep 13 | Hatch EVA to attach 30m Dacron tether; visor fogging, exhaustion, terminated early at ~33 min |
| Standup EVA | Sep 13 | 2h 8m standup EVA for astronomical photography -- more successful |
| Agena PPS Burn | Sep 13 | Bell 8247 fires ~26 seconds; apogee climbs to 1,374 km -- highest crewed altitude |
| High-Altitude Observation | Sep 13 | View from 1,374 km: Earth's curvature in all directions, Africa to Australia visible |
| Return to Low Orbit | Sep 13 | Second Agena burn lowers orbit to ~300 km for tethered experiment |
| Tethered Flight | Sep 14 | Conrad undocks, tether taut, slow rotation (~1 rev/6 min), 0.00015 g detected; ~3 hours |
| Automatic Reentry | Sep 15, 13:59 UTC | First computer-controlled reentry; splashdown 4.5 km from target, recovery by USS Guam |

Pete Conrad and Dick Gordon completed Gemini 11 in 71 hours, 17 minutes, and 8 seconds -- 44 orbits -- with four firsts and a record altitude that would stand until Apollo left Earth orbit entirely. The mission was designed to prove that first-orbit rendezvous was operationally viable, that docked propulsive maneuvers could reach high altitudes safely, that tethered operations were feasible in orbit, and that automated reentry guidance was reliable. All four objectives were met. The EVA difficulties continued the pattern from Gemini 9A and 10 -- Gordon's exhaustion while attaching the tether was the third consecutive difficult EVA, the cost of learning that working in a pressurized suit in zero gravity required underwater training and fixed handholds, lessons that Buzz Aldrin would apply definitively on Gemini 12.

The Conrad-Gordon dynamic was central to the mission's success. Both Navy test pilots from Patuxent River. Both competitive, both funny, both willing to push to the edge. Conrad's calm voice from the cockpit guided Gordon through the tether attachment when his body was failing. They would fly together again on Apollo 12 with Alan Bean -- Conrad and Bean walked on the Moon, Gordon orbited alone in Yankee Clipper. The three remained close for the rest of their lives. Conrad died in 1999. Gordon died in 2017. Bean died in 2018.

*Degree 49: the first revolution. On September 12, 1966, Pete Conrad launched from Cape Kennedy and docked with the Agena 85 minutes later -- one orbit, first try, the M=1 profile that would make Apollo's lunar rendezvous possible. Then the Agena's engine fired and pushed them to 1,374 km, higher than any human had flown, the Earth curving away in all directions, Africa and Australia visible in a single glance. Then Dick Gordon -- born in Seattle, educated at UW, the fastest man across the continent -- crawled out into the void and attached a tether while his visor fogged and his body failed him, because the mission needed the tether and Conrad's voice in his ear said keep going. The tether held. The two vehicles spun slowly. The accelerometers registered 0.00015 g. The first artificial gravity. In Olympia, Washington -- sixty miles south of where Gordon grew up -- Kimya Dawson sits in a rented house with an acoustic guitar and records a song about fear and love and mortality directly to a laptop, no studio, no producer, no buffer between the impulse and the artifact. The raw take. The first revolution in anti-folk: refuse the intermediary, trust the first attempt, let the distance between feeling and recording approach zero. She could have signed a major label after Juno. She could have been everywhere. She chose K Records and a children's album and a minivan and Olympia. The radical transplant: geography as principle, not career strategy. And in a thicket so dense the light does not reach the ground -- salal head-high, blackberry overhead, sword fern across every opening -- a large dark sparrow plants both feet and lunges forward, raking backward through the leaf litter with explosive force. The double-scratch. No tentative pecking. No cautious approach. Full commitment on the first try, every time, hundreds of times a day, excavating the forest floor with the same conviction Conrad brought to the first-orbit rendezvous. The Fox Sparrow. Largest sparrow in the quintet. Richest song in the family. The fox in the forest, the fox in the sparrow, the fox in the name. Five consecutive Passerellidae -- a new record. Five consecutive folk-adjacent degrees -- the record tied. Both quintets close at degree 49 with their most distinctive member. Both quintets demonstrate that sustained commitment to one family yields maximum diversity, not monotony. The gravity is small, but it is real. The raw take holds true. The first revolution is always the one that matters.*

*"Conrad docked in 85 minutes. Dawson records in one take. The Fox Sparrow scratches with both feet. Three acts of maximum commitment. Three first revolutions. The gravity is small, but it is real."*
