## Summary

**CRV is the most cognitively rich wildlife study in the PNW Research series and the release is structurally about the watcher-watcher relationship, not just about a family of birds.** The 78th Research project ships six modules totaling 2,115 research lines — Taxonomy & Identification (363), Cognition & Intelligence (325), Ecology & Habitat (359), Social Behavior & Communication (350), Cultural Significance (346), and Threats & Conservation (372) — plus a 1,041-line LaTeX mission-pack source, a 185,974-byte pre-rendered PDF, a mission-pack landing page (221 lines), and a four-file site shell (`index.html` 102 lines, `page.html` 201 lines, `mission.html` 71 lines, `style.css` 69 lines). Cumulatively the release lands +3,888 lines across 15 files in a single content commit (`1434f78c7`), a single docs commit (`3224faf20`), and a merge commit (`4374aa6ef`). The module that matters most is Module 02 (Cognition & Intelligence): it takes a family of birds that most field guides reduce to "smart corvids" and reveals a cognitive architecture — convergent evolution with primate intelligence, facial recognition that generalizes across observers, episodic memory that encodes what/where/when, theory of mind evidence — that reframes the whole rest of the project. Everything else follows from that architecture. The taxonomy (Module 01), the ecology (Module 03), the social behavior (Module 04), the cultural significance (Module 05), and the conservation threats (Module 06) are each downstream of the central claim that corvids are cognitive peers rather than cognitive curiosities.

**The 2020 AOS taxonomic merger is the single load-bearing structural event in Module 01, and the release names it directly.** For most of the twentieth century, North American ornithology recognized Northwestern Crow (*Corvus caurinus*) as a distinct species from American Crow (*Corvus brachyrhynchos*), with the former treated as a small coastal PNW endemic from Puget Sound up through southeast Alaska. The Slager et al. genomic evidence — published in the 2010s and consolidated through the American Ornithological Society's 2020 annual taxonomic update — dissolved the species boundary by showing that the two "species" had interbred freely along the entire contact zone for generations and that the genomic structure was better described as a single species with coastal size variation than as two species. The merger is not just a nomenclature change; it is a methodological correction that ripples across every field guide, every birding checklist, every regional wildlife database. Module 01 walks field identification for the five PNW corvid species that remain after the merger (American Crow, Common Raven, Steller's Jay, Gray Jay / Canada Jay, Black-billed Magpie at the eastern margin) and documents how the merger simplifies rather than complicates the PNW birder's life — every crow in the PNW is now one species, and the size cline is what it always was: a cline, not a boundary.

**Corvid cognition is documented at research-citation depth, not at popular-science-headline depth.** Module 02 walks four specific research programs that together establish corvids as cognitive peers. John Marzluff's lab at the University of Washington ran the dangerous-face experiments in which researchers wore masks while banding crows, and the crows learned to identify the specific mask as a threat — not just to themselves but to their offspring and to other crows through social transmission — and the memory persisted for years. Nicola Clayton and Anthony Dickinson's work on Western Scrub-Jays (a California corvid but methodologically foundational for the family) demonstrated episodic-like memory: the jays remembered not just where they cached food but what kind of food and when, and preferentially retrieved perishable caches before they spoiled. Thomas Bugnyar's lab work with ravens provided evidence for theory of mind — ravens track what other ravens can and cannot see, hide caches more carefully when observed by dominant conspecifics, and adjust their caching behavior based on the observer's line of sight. The convergent evolution framing is load-bearing: corvid brains and primate brains evolved cognitive complexity independently from a common ancestor that had neither, which means there are at least two distinct evolutionary pathways to higher cognition on Earth. The implication that the release does not labor but does name is that intelligence is not a one-off accident of the primate lineage; it is a solution the biosphere has found more than once.

**The ecological framing maps four PNW zones and names the wolf-raven partnership as a first-order ecological relationship.** Module 03 documents the corvid species distribution across the four PNW ecological zones — temperate coastal rainforest, interior montane, urban/suburban matrix, and eastern slope dry forest — with niche partitioning by species and habitat. The American Crow dominates the urban/suburban matrix and the lowland coastal zone; the Common Raven ranges through all four zones but concentrates in the interior montane and the eastern slope; Steller's Jay occupies coniferous forest at middle elevations; Gray Jay / Canada Jay takes the subalpine boreal margin; Black-billed Magpie enters from the east into the dry eastern slope. The module walks diet ecology (omnivorous generalism, caching strategy, carrion specialization in ravens versus omnivore generalism in crows), breeding strategy (cooperative breeding with helpers at the nest in American Crow versus monogamous pair breeding in Common Raven), and the wolf-raven partnership — the documented co-feeding behavior at wolf kills in which ravens spot carrion, call wolves to kills the ravens cannot open, and then share the opened carcass. The partnership is one of the clearest inter-specific cooperative relationships documented in temperate wildlife ecology, and it is the one that the PNW's returning wolf populations make regionally active again after decades of local wolf absence.

**The social behavior module treats corvid vocal repertoire and roost ecology as data systems, not as anecdotes.** Module 04 documents the UW Bothell roost — one of the largest and best-studied urban corvid communal roosts in North America, with winter counts in the tens of thousands — and the Redmond 2023-2025 roost migration in which the roost shifted several miles in response to development pressure on the historical roost site, providing a natural experiment in how corvid populations respond to habitat change. The module walks the 30+ documented raven call types (territorial calls, food calls, alarm calls differentiated by threat type, contact calls, juvenile begging variations) and the fission-fusion social dynamics in which crow flocks reorganize continuously throughout the day as individuals move between foraging groups, family units, and roost-bound aggregations. The fission-fusion framing is what connects corvid social behavior to primate social behavior: both evolved in populations with high individual recognition, long-term memory for relationships, and flexible group composition, and the convergent social structure is one of the specific pieces of evidence for the convergent-cognition thesis Module 02 develops.

**The cultural significance module names eight specific First Nations and applies OCAP/CARE data sovereignty principles rather than treating indigenous knowledge as anonymous folklore.** Module 05 walks the corvid presence in the oral traditions and material culture of eight named nations — Tlingit (Raven as Creator), Haida (Raven as trickster and transformer), Tsimshian (Raven cycle and the box of light), Kwakwaka'wakw (ceremonial regalia and mask tradition), Heiltsuk (coastal cycle variants), Coast Salish (Crow traditions), Koyukon (northern Athabaskan Raven cycle), and Inuit (northern circumpolar Raven narratives). The module documents Bill Reid's monumental Haida sculpture *The Raven and the First Men* (Museum of Anthropology at UBC) as the canonical twentieth-century formline-art expression of the Raven Creator narrative, and walks formline art as a compositional vocabulary — the ovoid, the U-form, the S-form, the positive-negative inversion — through which the corvid becomes a structural principle rather than only a subject. The OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) and CARE (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) principles are named explicitly and applied to every cited source: the module cites published scholarship and publicly shared cultural knowledge without extracting material that its source communities have asked be held within community, and the restraint is as load-bearing as the inclusion.

**The conservation module is quantitatively disciplined and names specific impacts on specific prey species.** Module 06 documents the West Nile Virus impact on PNW crow populations — a measured 45% decline across parts of the region in the first years of WNV establishment, with slow partial recovery over the subsequent decade — and walks raven predation as a conservation issue for Marbled Murrelet (Pacific Northwest old-growth-dependent seabird) and Snowy Plover (coastal nester on Pacific beaches). The raven-predation data is one of the harder conservation conversations in the region because ravens are native, are culturally load-bearing, and are benefiting from anthropogenic food subsidies (landfills, highway kills, agricultural waste) that have boosted raven populations beyond historical baselines; the prey species they impact are already under separate anthropogenic pressure. The module does not prescribe a conservation strategy but names the tension cleanly: a native species whose population has been artificially inflated is impacting other native species whose populations have been artificially suppressed, and the management question is not which species to value but which anthropogenic inputs to remove. The framing is the same framing the environmental-systems entries (v1.49.75 PGP, forthcoming climate entries) carry forward.

**The forest-wildlife color palette encodes the subject register.** CRV uses a three-color palette — forest green `#1B5E20`, river blue `#1565C0`, emerald `#2E7D32` — that reads as temperate rainforest rather than as urban or industrial register. Forest green is the dominant color, echoing the coniferous canopy that is the corvid's native PNW habitat; river blue is the accent, evoking the salmon streams and coastal estuaries that are the richest corvid foraging grounds; emerald is the tonal bracket, suggesting the understory and the moss-draped rainforest floor. The three-color discipline continues from COI (v1.49.58, ritual palette), ATC (v1.49.64, celestial palette), K8S (v1.49.69, infrastructure palette), and OTM (v1.49.74, creative-educational palette), and extends naturally to wildlife-ecology subject matter. Palette signals the register to the reader before the text does any work.

The cluster shape is the same survey-plus-deep-dive pattern that the series uses across other clusters: a broad survey entry that covers the taxonomic and ecological surface, paired with deep-dive entries that walk specific families or species at the cognitive, cultural, and conservation depth the survey cannot afford. ## Part A: Crows of the PNW

- **American Crow (*Corvus brachyrhynchos*) dominance:** After the 2020 AOS merger absorbed Northwestern Crow, the American Crow is the single crow species across the entire PNW, with body size forming a cline from larger inland populations to smaller coastal ones.
- **Slager et al. genomic evidence:** Free interbreeding across the historical contact zone, absence of reproductive isolation, and genomic structure consistent with a single species with geographic size variation — the empirical substrate for the merger.
- **Marzluff facial recognition at UW:** Masked researchers banding crows produced a mask-specific threat memory in banded individuals that generalized to unbanded bystanders through social transmission and persisted for years.
- **Cooperative breeding with nest helpers:** American Crow family groups retain prior-year offspring as helpers at the nest, a social structure closer to cooperatively breeding mammals than to typical passerine breeding systems.
- **Urban-matrix ecology:** Crows concentrate in the urban/suburban matrix where anthropogenic food subsidies and reduced predator pressure together produce population densities well above pre-development baselines.
- **Redmond 2023-2025 roost migration:** A natural experiment in roost-site plasticity — the UW Bothell communal roost's neighboring Redmond roost shifted several miles in response to development pressure, documented across multiple winter census cycles.
- **West Nile Virus impact:** A measured 45% regional crow decline in the first post-establishment years, with slow and uneven partial recovery over the following decade; crow-mortality is one of the two most sensitive WNV indicator signals in the region.
- **Fission-fusion social dynamics:** Continuous reorganization of flocks throughout the day as individuals move between foraging groups, family units, and roost-bound aggregations — convergent with primate social structure in a family of birds.

## Part B: Ravens and Jays of the PNW

- **Common Raven (*Corvus corax*) ecological range:** The raven ranges through all four PNW ecological zones but concentrates in the interior montane and eastern slope, with the largest population densities at the intersection of carrion availability and low human population density.
- **30+ documented call types:** Territorial, food, alarm (differentiated by threat type), contact, juvenile begging, pair-bond, and play vocalizations — one of the most-studied avian vocal repertoires outside of songbirds.
- **Wolf-raven partnership:** Ravens spot carrion, call wolves to kills the ravens cannot open, and then share the opened carcass — one of the clearest inter-specific cooperative relationships documented in temperate wildlife ecology, now regionally active again after PNW wolf recovery.
- **Bugnyar theory of mind:** Ravens track what other ravens can and cannot see, hide caches more carefully when observed by dominant conspecifics, and adjust caching behavior based on the observer's line of sight.
- **Steller's Jay (*Cyanocitta stelleri*) coniferous specialization:** Mid-elevation coniferous-forest specialist, the dark-blue-and-crested jay most PNW visitors recognize as the "mountain jay."
- **Gray Jay / Canada Jay (*Perisoreus canadensis*) subalpine persistence:** The boreal-margin corvid that caches food in bark crevices and persists through the coldest PNW winters without migration; the "camp robber" of backcountry tradition.
- **Black-billed Magpie eastern margin:** Enters from east of the Cascade crest into the dry eastern slope, providing the corvid-family representative in the rain-shadow ecological zone.
- **Raven predation as conservation tension:** Anthropogenic food subsidies have inflated raven populations beyond historical baselines; inflated raven populations impact Marbled Murrelet (old-growth-dependent seabird) and Snowy Plover (coastal beach nester), both already under separate anthropogenic pressure — a clean case where native-species management requires anthropogenic-input removal rather than species-level triage.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **The cognition module establishes corvids as cognitive peers, not as curiosities.** Marzluff's facial recognition, Clayton's episodic memory, and Bugnyar's theory of mind evidence — documented with specific researcher names and study citations — establishes that corvids are not just smart birds but cognitive beings with capabilities that parallel primate intelligence through convergent evolution. Naming the researchers and the specific experimental paradigms is the editorial decision that lets Module 02 support the rest of the project; without that grounding the cognition claim reads as popular-science filler, and with it the claim reads as a research synthesis.
- **The cultural significance module names specific nations and applies OCAP/CARE principles.** 8 named nations (Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian, Kwakwaka'wakw, Heiltsuk, Coast Salish, Koyukon, Inuit) rather than generic "indigenous peoples" means the module does the naming work the series has required since the earliest PNW entries. OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) and CARE (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) data sovereignty principles acknowledged explicitly, and applied to every cited source — only publicly shared cultural knowledge and published scholarship is carried into the module, and material that its source communities have asked be held within community is held within community.
- **The 2020 AOS merger is treated as methodological correction, not as nomenclatural trivia.** The Slager et al. genomic evidence that dissolved Northwestern Crow is walked at causal depth — what the evidence showed, why the AOS accepted it, how the merger simplifies rather than complicates PNW field identification — rather than as a one-line taxonomic update. The treatment sets a precedent for how the Research series handles taxonomic change: the change is a substantive knowledge claim, not a clerical update.
- **The wolf-raven partnership ties ecology to PNW wolf recovery directly.** Documenting the inter-specific cooperative relationship at kill sites and naming PNW wolf recovery as the regional re-activation of the partnership connects the corvid project to the broader wildlife-recovery arc the series documents. The partnership is one of the clearest examples of what returning apex predators do for landscape-scale ecology that is not reducible to prey-population regulation.
- **The three-commit atomic-content discipline and the mission-pack triad continue to hold.** Content commit `1434f78c7` + docs commit `3224faf20` + merge commit `4374aa6ef` produces a v1.49.77..v1.49.78 window with exactly one meaningful state transition. The mission-pack triad (LaTeX source + pre-rendered PDF + HTML landing) continues the JNS (v1.49.53) shape.

### What Could Be Better

- **The raven-predation conservation conversation could carry more dialog with First Nations perspectives.** Module 06 documents the Marbled Murrelet and Snowy Plover impact cleanly and names the anthropogenic-inputs framing, but the cultural dimension of raven conservation — the load-bearing cultural significance of Raven in PNW indigenous traditions — deserves more explicit treatment in the conservation module specifically rather than only in the cultural module. The risk of treating the two frames as separate is that the conservation conversation becomes scientifically complete and culturally thin.
- **The international comparative scope is gestured at but not walked.** Corvidae is a globally distributed family, and the cognition research cited in Module 02 draws on studies of PNW, European, and Japanese corvid populations. The module names the convergent-evolution framing but does not walk the international comparative research program in detail. A future project-variant or companion module could survey the global corvid cognition research landscape to contextualize the PNW-focused synthesis.
- **The longitudinal data on the UW Bothell roost deserves a dedicated time-series treatment.** The roost census data covers multiple decades and the Redmond 2023-2025 migration is one datapoint in a longer time series. A future revision could walk the full time series with a specific figure or table showing winter counts, roost-site shifts, and correlations with regional development and weather events. The data exist; the treatment in this release summarizes rather than exposes them.

### What Needs Improvement

- **The field-identification section in Module 01 could carry worked examples.** The 5-species identification walk is summarized at the species level (size, call, silhouette, range), but a worked example — a photograph or silhouette plate, a decision tree for common confusion pairs (American Crow vs. Common Raven at a distance, Steller's Jay vs. Blue Jay at the eastern margin) — would help PNW birders apply the taxonomy module in the field. The Research series does not ship photographs as part of the module set; a future revision could add a text-based decision tree.
- **The palette and site-shell boilerplate is retained from prior Research projects without subject-specific refinement.** The three-color palette is apt for temperate-rainforest ecology, but the site shell (`index.html`, `page.html`, `mission.html`) carries the generic card-and-page structure that every Research project uses. A corvid-specific flourish — the formline ovoid as a page-head ornament, the raven silhouette as a list bullet — would lift the site-shell register to match the subject; the uniformity across projects has editorial value (readers recognize a Research project immediately) but the subject-specific subtlety has been sacrificed for it.
- **The conservation-tension framing is honest but does not walk specific management interventions.** The module names the tension (native species with artificially inflated population impacting native species with artificially suppressed populations) and names the underlying cause (anthropogenic food subsidies) but does not walk specific management interventions — landfill covering protocols, highway-kill removal protocols, agricultural-waste handling protocols — that would reduce raven food subsidies. A revision pass could close the gap.
- **The release-notes chapter auto-generation policy should be formalized across the uplift mission.** CRV, like every Research project, ships with parser-generated chapters at confidence 0.95. This README uplift rewrites the README to A-grade depth while leaving the chapters as DB-navigation substrate. ## Lessons Learned

- **The watcher-watcher relationship is unique and worth naming explicitly.** Corvids remember individual human faces and pass that information to offspring. No other PNW wildlife species has this documented capacity. The corvid studies the human studying the corvid. This reflexive relationship is why corvids hold such cultural significance — they are the one animal that watches back. The lesson generalizes beyond corvids: wildlife projects that document reflexive relationships (species that track individual humans, species that respond to long-term human presence or absence) carry a kind of ecological-ethical weight that other projects cannot.
- **Convergent evolution is a structural argument, not a rhetorical flourish.** Corvid brains and primate brains evolved cognitive complexity independently from a common ancestor that had neither. That is a load-bearing empirical claim about intelligence as a solution the biosphere has found more than once, and the claim should be named structurally rather than decoratively. Future wildlife-cognition entries should name the convergent-evolution framing at module-header depth rather than burying it in later paragraphs.
- **Taxonomic change is a substantive knowledge claim and should be treated that way.** The 2020 AOS merger is not nomenclatural trivia — it is a methodological correction supported by the Slager et al. genomic evidence. Treating taxonomic change at causal depth (what the evidence showed, why the governing body accepted it, what downstream corrections follow) rather than at nomenclatural depth (what the species is now called) is the editorial precedent CRV sets. Future Research projects that engage taxonomic change should follow the pattern.
- **OCAP/CARE indigenous-knowledge principles are the minimum bar, not the achievement.** Naming the principles and applying them to source selection is the floor; the ceiling is ongoing relationship with source communities, review by named tradition-bearers, and willingness to revise modules when communities ask. CRV names the principles and applies them to source selection; future Research projects on subjects with indigenous-knowledge load-bearing content should take the principles as the starting point for relationship, not as the finish line for due diligence.
- **Inter-specific cooperative relationships are rarer than inter-specific competitive ones in the literature and deserve extra editorial care when they appear.** The wolf-raven partnership is documented in the ethology literature for decades, but it is one of only a handful of clearly-cooperative inter-specific relationships in temperate wildlife ecology. Entries that engage such relationships should walk the specific behavioral evidence (carrion-spotting, call-response between species, carcass-opening and co-feeding) rather than summarizing the relationship as "ravens follow wolves" — the summary loses the ecological specificity that makes the partnership interesting.
- **Palette continuity is an architectural investment, not a design preference.** Forest green + river blue + emerald signals temperate rainforest. Deep violet + energy orange + cosmic (OTM, v1.49.74) signals creative classroom. Steel + electric blue + graphite (K8S, v1.49.69) signals infrastructure. The three-color discipline has now walked five subject-specific palettes and the discipline is load-bearing: readers recognize the subject register before reading the text because the palette primes them. Palette choice belongs to the editorial surface of the project, not to the visual-design surface alone.
- **PNW wildlife projects benefit from survey-plus-deep-dive cluster shapes and the shape generalizes.** AVI (survey of 250+ bird species) paired with CRV (deep-dive into Corvidae) is the same shape a future MAM (survey) + species-level deep-dive would produce, and the same shape any survey-plus-deep-dive cluster should produce. Future cluster planning should name the survey entry and the deep-dive entries together so the cluster reads as a coherent architecture rather than as independent projects.
- **The 32-test verification matrix at 5 safety-critical / 14 core / 8 integration / 5 edge cases holds for wildlife-plus-indigenous-knowledge subjects.** The matrix pattern has now been applied across infrastructure (K8S), educational (OTM), biographical (FDR), and wildlife (CRV) subjects without breaking. The safety-critical band grows appropriately for subjects with cultural-attribution stakes (5 tests for CRV vs. 4 for OTM) — the growth is the matrix responding to subject-specific risk rather than inflating.
- **Mission-pack triads continue to justify their storage cost at the 1:1 cost / 3:1 reader-autonomy ratio that JNS established.** LaTeX source + pre-rendered PDF + HTML landing supports reference, source-tinkering, and site-navigation reader modes.
