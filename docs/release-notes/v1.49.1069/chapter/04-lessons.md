1. **A sustain KEEPS the predecessor's shared domain vocabulary — it does not remove it.**
   Unlike a rotation, this is a SUSTAIN (mode=continuation): Voyager 1 and the Pioneer siblings share almost all of their domain vocabulary, so the rewrite KEPT the shared outer-planets content (the outer solar system, deep space, the giant planets, Jupiter, Saturn, RTG power, the flyby, the gravity assist, the escape trajectory, the heliosphere, interstellar space) and replaced only the predecessor-specific facts — Pioneer 11 as the subject, the 6 April 1973 launch, the NASA Ames management and TRW, the first flyby of Saturn, the F-ring discovery, the first detection of Saturn's magnetic field, the SNAP-19 RTG framing, the Pioneer plaque as Pioneer 11's, the 1995 end, and the Ring-necked Snake + Sweetscented Bedstraw pairing — with Voyager 1's: the JPL management, the 5 September 1977 Titan…
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12429_

2. **A sustain SUSTAINS the axis — the obs counter advances and there are prior siblings on the same axis.**
   Voyager 1 sustains the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis at obs#3 (the second consecutive sustain on the axis opened by Pioneer 10 and sustained by Pioneer 11), so only the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION counter advances (obs#2 → obs#3) and the substrate-axis-rotation discipline holds UNCHANGED at obs#101 (no NEW rotation observed). The single deliberate backward reference is the nav-prev to the 1.251 Pioneer 11 sibling, with a lineage thread to Pioneer 10.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12430_

3. **The management is JPL, NOT Ames — a correct intra-axis distinction.**
   Voyager 1 was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, whereas the Pioneer siblings (obs#1/#2) were managed by Ames; this is a correct distinction within the shared axis, not a contradiction, and the catalog states JPL precisely throughout.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12431_

4. **The Io result is the first ACTIVE volcanism beyond Earth — it confirmed a prediction, it was not a total surprise.**
   Voyager 1 discovered active volcanoes erupting on Io (March 1979); the catalog scopes this as the first active volcanism beyond Earth (past/extinct volcanism on the Moon and Mars was known earlier) and notes it confirmed the tidal-heating prediction of Peale, Cassen & Reynolds (Science 203) published days earlier. The plume was first noticed by navigation engineer Linda Morabito; the discovery paper's co-authors are Synnott, Kupferman, and Collins.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12432_

5. **The most-distant result is the "most distant human-made object," the record ceded by Pioneer 10.**
   Voyager 1 overtook Pioneer 10 on 17 February 1998 (at about 69 AU); the catalog scopes this as the most distant human-made object — exactly the record Pioneer 10's page assigned to Voyager 1 — and notes Voyager 1 recedes faster than the other escaping probes and so remains the most distant.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12433_

6. **The heliopause result is the first crossing into interstellar space — the heliosphere boundary, NOT gravitational solar-system escape.**
   Voyager 1 crossed the heliopause on 25 August 2012 (at about 122 AU); the catalog scopes this strictly to the heliopause / heliosphere boundary and never says "first to leave the solar system" gravitationally (Voyager 1 is still within the Sun's gravitational domain, the Oort cloud lies far beyond), noting Voyager 2 crossed the heliopause later, in 2018.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12434_

7. **0 re-stamps — Voyager 1 shares the Pioneers' journey but not their firsts.**
   Voyager 1's Jupiter flyby is the THIRD and its Saturn flyby the SECOND (Pioneer 10 first to Jupiter, Pioneer 11 first to Saturn); it does not re-stamp the asteroid-belt traverse, the escape trajectory, the ETI plaque, the F-ring discovery, or the first detection of Saturn's magnetic field. Voyager 1's firsts are all at Io, in distance, and at the heliopause, yielding 0 predecessor re-stamps.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12435_

8. **The Golden Record is the richer audio-visual message, NOT the first message to ETI.**
   The Pioneer plaque (1972) was the first physical message sent out of the solar system; the catalog describes the Golden Record as the richer audio-visual message Voyager 1 carries, not anchored as a "first."
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12436_

9. **The Uranus and Neptune flybys belong to Voyager 2 (obs#4), not Voyager 1.**
   Voyager 1 did NOT fly by Uranus or Neptune; the catalog reserves those flybys for Voyager 2, the only craft to visit them, and keeps them off this page.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12437_

10. **The mission is framed positively, in the present tense — Voyager 1 is still operating.**
   Voyager 1 continues to operate and transmit from interstellar space (as of 2026), with its instruments being progressively powered down to conserve its declining RTG power; the catalog frames the mission positively in the present tense as the probe that made the detailed grand tour and went the farthest, still whispering faintly home.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12438_

11. **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived mission imagery.**
   The Voyager 1 shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual mission data or imaging science subsystem imagery. A future revision could render a real Voyager 1 view — Jupiter and the erupting Io plume, or Saturn and its rings (publicly available from the National Space Science Data Center) — for a higher-fidelity view.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12439_

12. **The Voyager 1 diorama is a forthcoming artifact.**
   The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the probe with its large dish high-gain antenna, its RTG booms, its scan platform, and the Golden Record on its side.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12440_

13. **The Jupiter and Saturn flyby modes are depicted rather than driven by real data.**
   The Io plume and the Titan pass in the first and second modes are depicted but not rendered from archived Voyager 1 imaging and field measurements; a future ship could drive the modes from the real imaging and fields-and-particles time series for a quantitatively faithful reading.

14. **The heliopause-crossing mode is depicted rather than computed.**
   The crossing of the heliopause in the fourth mode is depicted rather than rendered from the published Voyager 1 plasma-wave and cosmic-ray data and the heliocentric ephemeris; a future ship could drive the mode from the real interstellar-plasma time series for a quantitatively faithful view.
