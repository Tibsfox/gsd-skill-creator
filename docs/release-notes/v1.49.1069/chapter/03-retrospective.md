## Surprises

- **A 1977 probe is still operating from interstellar space in 2026.** Voyager 1 continues to operate and transmit from interstellar space, nearly half a century after launch, with its instruments being progressively powered down one by one to conserve its declining RTG power (about two science instruments still on; a 2024 data/computer glitch was recovered).
- **A spacecraft caught a moon in the act of erupting.** At Jupiter on 5 March 1979 Voyager 1 discovered active volcanoes erupting on Io — the umbrella-shaped plume first noticed by JPL navigation engineer Linda Morabito in a navigation image — the first active volcanism seen anywhere beyond Earth, confirming a tidal-heating prediction published just days before the flyby.
- **A probe became the most distant human-made object by overtaking another.** Voyager 1 overtook Pioneer 10 on 17 February 1998 (at about 69 AU) to become the most distant human-made object — the record Pioneer 10's page had ceded to it — and recedes faster than every other escaping probe, so it remains the most distant.
- **A probe crossed the boundary of the Sun's domain.** On 25 August 2012 (at about 122 AU) Voyager 1 crossed the heliopause, where the Sun's solar wind gives way to the interstellar medium, becoming the first human-made object to enter interstellar space — the heliosphere boundary, not the edge of the Sun's gravity, which lies far beyond.
- **A far-ranging seabird of the North Pacific wanders beyond the last sight of land.** The Black-footed Albatross (*Phoebastria nigripes*), a large, dark, long-winged seabird, ranges thousands of kilometres over the open ocean (including the offshore waters of the Pacific Northwest), coming ashore only to breed on remote islands (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Voyager 1 ranging far out beyond the last edge.
- **A coastal perennial holds the very seaward edge of the land.** Puget Sound Gumweed (*Grindelia integrifolia*), a tough, resinous perennial of Pacific Northwest coastal bluffs, beaches, and salt-marsh edges, tolerates salt spray with its sticky gum-tipped flower buds as it holds the threshold where the land meets the open water (ecological description only) — a mirror of the last green thing at the edge before the boundless beyond.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A sustain KEEPS the predecessor's shared domain vocabulary — it does not remove it.** Unlike a rotation, this is a SUSTAIN (mode=continuation): Voyager 1 and the Pioneer siblings share almost all of their domain vocabulary, so the rewrite KEPT the shared outer-planets content (the outer solar system, deep space, the giant planets, Jupiter, Saturn, RTG power, the flyby, the gravity assist, the escape trajectory, the heliosphere, interstellar space) and replaced only the predecessor-specific facts — Pioneer 11 as the subject, the 6 April 1973 launch, the NASA Ames management and TRW, the first flyby of Saturn, the F-ring discovery, the first detection of Saturn's magnetic field, the SNAP-19 RTG framing, the Pioneer plaque as Pioneer 11's, the 1995 end, and the Ring-necked Snake + Sweetscented Bedstraw pairing — with Voyager 1's: the JPL management, the 5 September 1977 Titan IIIE-Centaur launch, the Golden Record, the Jupiter flyby and the discovery of active volcanism on Io, the Saturn flyby and the Titan slingshot out of the ecliptic, the most distant human-made object (1998), the first crossing of the heliopause into interstellar space (2012), the continued operation from interstellar space (2026), and the Black-footed Albatross + Puget Sound Gumweed pairing.
2. **A sustain SUSTAINS the axis — the obs counter advances and there are prior siblings on the same axis.** Voyager 1 sustains the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis at obs#3 (the second consecutive sustain on the axis opened by Pioneer 10 and sustained by Pioneer 11), so only the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION counter advances (obs#2 → obs#3) and the substrate-axis-rotation discipline holds UNCHANGED at obs#101 (no NEW rotation observed). The single deliberate backward reference is the nav-prev to the 1.251 Pioneer 11 sibling, with a lineage thread to Pioneer 10.
3. **The management is JPL, NOT Ames — a correct intra-axis distinction.** Voyager 1 was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, whereas the Pioneer siblings (obs#1/#2) were managed by Ames; this is a correct distinction within the shared axis, not a contradiction, and the catalog states JPL precisely throughout.
4. **The Io result is the first ACTIVE volcanism beyond Earth — it confirmed a prediction, it was not a total surprise.** Voyager 1 discovered active volcanoes erupting on Io (March 1979); the catalog scopes this as the first active volcanism beyond Earth (past/extinct volcanism on the Moon and Mars was known earlier) and notes it confirmed the tidal-heating prediction of Peale, Cassen & Reynolds (Science 203) published days earlier. The plume was first noticed by navigation engineer Linda Morabito; the discovery paper's co-authors are Synnott, Kupferman, and Collins.
5. **The most-distant result is the "most distant human-made object," the record ceded by Pioneer 10.** Voyager 1 overtook Pioneer 10 on 17 February 1998 (at about 69 AU); the catalog scopes this as the most distant human-made object — exactly the record Pioneer 10's page assigned to Voyager 1 — and notes Voyager 1 recedes faster than the other escaping probes and so remains the most distant.
6. **The heliopause result is the first crossing into interstellar space — the heliosphere boundary, NOT gravitational solar-system escape.** Voyager 1 crossed the heliopause on 25 August 2012 (at about 122 AU); the catalog scopes this strictly to the heliopause / heliosphere boundary and never says "first to leave the solar system" gravitationally (Voyager 1 is still within the Sun's gravitational domain, the Oort cloud lies far beyond), noting Voyager 2 crossed the heliopause later, in 2018.
7. **0 re-stamps — Voyager 1 shares the Pioneers' journey but not their firsts.** Voyager 1's Jupiter flyby is the THIRD and its Saturn flyby the SECOND (Pioneer 10 first to Jupiter, Pioneer 11 first to Saturn); it does not re-stamp the asteroid-belt traverse, the escape trajectory, the ETI plaque, the F-ring discovery, or the first detection of Saturn's magnetic field. Voyager 1's firsts are all at Io, in distance, and at the heliopause, yielding 0 predecessor re-stamps.
8. **The Golden Record is the richer audio-visual message, NOT the first message to ETI.** The Pioneer plaque (1972) was the first physical message sent out of the solar system; the catalog describes the Golden Record as the richer audio-visual message Voyager 1 carries, not anchored as a "first."
9. **The Uranus and Neptune flybys belong to Voyager 2 (obs#4), not Voyager 1.** Voyager 1 did NOT fly by Uranus or Neptune; the catalog reserves those flybys for Voyager 2, the only craft to visit them, and keeps them off this page.
10. **The mission is framed positively, in the present tense — Voyager 1 is still operating.** Voyager 1 continues to operate and transmit from interstellar space (as of 2026), with its instruments being progressively powered down to conserve its declining RTG power; the catalog frames the mission positively in the present tense as the probe that made the detailed grand tour and went the farthest, still whispering faintly home.
