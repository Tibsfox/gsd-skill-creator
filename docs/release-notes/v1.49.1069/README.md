## Mission Overview

Voyager 1 is the obs#3 sustain of the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA deep-space probe whose defining achievements are the detailed grand tour of the outer planets and the journey on to the edge: the discovery of active volcanism on the moon Io (the first active volcanism beyond Earth), becoming the most distant human-made object, and the first crossing of the heliopause into interstellar space. It is managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) — NOT Ames, distinct from the Pioneer siblings. A three-axis-stabilized flyby probe with a large 3.7-m dish high-gain antenna and a scan platform of pointed instruments — not an orbiter or a lander — it launched on a Titan IIIE-Centaur from Cape Canaveral LC-41 on 5 September 1977 (after its twin Voyager 2, which launched 20 August 1977, but on a faster, shorter trajectory, hence the designation "1"), powered by three MHW radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) fueled with plutonium-238 rather than solar panels, because sunlight is far too weak in the outer solar system, and it carries the Golden Record. Its payload included the imaging science subsystem (ISS), the infrared interferometer spectrometer (IRIS), the ultraviolet spectrometer (UVS), the photopolarimeter (PPS), the planetary radio astronomy (PRA) and plasma wave (PWS) instruments, the magnetometer (MAG), the plasma (PLS), the low-energy charged particle (LECP), and the cosmic ray subsystem (CRS). The story is the detailed grand tour and the long climb to the edge: across the inner solar system on RTG power and on to Jupiter on 5 March 1979 — the third Jupiter flyby — where it returned detailed imaging of the cloud bands, the Great Red Spot, the ring, and the Galilean moons, and discovered active volcanoes erupting on Io, the umbrella-shaped plume first noticed by JPL optical-navigation engineer Linda Morabito in a navigation image, the first active volcanism seen anywhere beyond Earth, confirming the tidal-heating prediction published days earlier. The Jupiter gravity assist redirected the probe to Saturn, reached on 12 November 1980 — the second Saturn flyby — where a close, targeted pass by the giant moon Titan deflected Voyager 1 northward out of the ecliptic plane, ending its planetary tour and committing it to a climb out of the solar system. From there it continued outbound: on 17 February 1998 (at about 69 AU) it overtook Pioneer 10 to become the most distant human-made object, and on 25 August 2012 (at about 122 AU) it crossed the heliopause into interstellar space, the first human-made object to do so. The scoping is load-bearing: the Jupiter (third) and Saturn (second) flybys are described, not anchored (Pioneer 10 reached Jupiter first, Pioneer 11 Saturn first); Io is the first active volcanism beyond Earth (confirming a prediction); the most-distant record was ceded to Voyager 1 by Pioneer 10 (1998); the heliopause crossing is the first into interstellar space, scoped to the heliosphere boundary, not gravitational solar-system escape (Voyager 2 crossed later, 2018); the Golden Record is the richer audio-visual message, NOT a first (the Pioneer plaque, 1972); and Voyager 1 did NOT visit Uranus or Neptune (Voyager 2). It continues to operate and transmit from interstellar space (as of 2026, instruments being progressively powered down to conserve energy). ### What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived mission imagery.** The Voyager 1 shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual mission data or imaging science subsystem imagery. A future revision could render a real Voyager 1 view — Jupiter and the erupting Io plume, or Saturn and its rings (publicly available from the National Space Science Data Center) — for a higher-fidelity view.
- **The Voyager 1 diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the probe with its large dish high-gain antenna, its RTG booms, its scan platform, and the Golden Record on its side.
- **The Jupiter and Saturn flyby modes are depicted rather than driven by real data.** The Io plume and the Titan pass in the first and second modes are depicted but not rendered from archived Voyager 1 imaging and field measurements; a future ship could drive the modes from the real imaging and fields-and-particles time series for a quantitatively faithful reading.
- **The heliopause-crossing mode is depicted rather than computed.** The crossing of the heliopause in the fourth mode is depicted rather than rendered from the published Voyager 1 plasma-wave and cosmic-ray data and the heliocentric ephemeris; a future ship could drive the mode from the real interstellar-plasma time series for a quantitatively faithful view.

## Dedication

v1.252 Voyager 1 is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's grand tourer and sent it on the farthest, into interstellar space. The Jet Propulsion Laboratory team who managed the mission and the engineers who raised the three-axis-stabilized probe, with its great dish antenna, its pointed scan platform, and its nuclear power supply; the launch team who lit the Titan IIIE-Centaur off Cape Canaveral in September 1977; the navigators who threaded the spacecraft past Jupiter and bent its path off Saturn's moon Titan, up out of the plane of the planets and toward the edge; Linda Morabito, who first saw the volcanic plume rising off Io in a navigation image; the scientists behind the cameras, the spectrometers, the magnetometer, and the particle and plasma instruments; and the committee, chaired by Carl Sagan, who gathered the sounds and images of Earth onto the Golden Record. They sent a small craft where none had gone — across the giant planets, then on past the last boundary of the Sun's wind into the space between the stars, the most distant of all human things, still answering home. The record rides on, borne out into the boundless beyond.
