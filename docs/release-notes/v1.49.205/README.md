New Age as a genre has a complicated critical history. The term was applied broadly -- sometimes derisively -- to instrumental music that prioritized atmosphere, contemplation, and sonic beauty over rhythmic drive or lyrical content. The genre was associated with Windham Hill Records, with George Winston's solo piano albums, with the 1980s trend toward "relaxation music." At its best, New Age music represented a serious engagement with acoustic beauty -- the sound of a piano in a room, the resonance of a guitar string, the decay of a note into silence. Windham Hill's artists were technically accomplished musicians making music that prioritized the pure sound of their instruments. Many rejected the term. ### BIOGRAPHY: Philip Aaberg and the Montana Piano

**Philip Aaberg** was born in Chester, Montana -- a small town of roughly 900 people on the Hi-Line, surrounded by wheat fields and cattle ranches, the Rocky Mountain Front visible to the west, the Great Plains stretching east to the horizon. The landscape is horizontal, enormous, and quiet. The sky dominates everything. Chester is not a music town. The fact that a Harvard-trained pianist emerged from Chester reads like fiction.

Aaberg grew up with music -- his family played piano, and the musical traditions of rural Montana (fiddle tunes, hymns, popular songs) were part of the cultural air. The level of talent that led to Harvard and NEC was exceptional and unexpected. He crossed the distance from Chester to Cambridge through talent and determination.

At Harvard and NEC, Aaberg received a technical foundation that few musicians in any genre possess. ### THE OPEN PLAIN -- 66th Acoustic Element

### THE SUSTAIN PEDAL -- 65th Blue Note

The sixty-fifth blue note is **the sustain pedal** -- the blue note that sounds when a note is held past its natural life, extended by the pianist's foot on the sustain pedal, lingering in the air after the hammer has left the string. The sustain pedal is the piano's unique mechanism for fighting silence. Every other instrument can sustain a note through continuous effort. The piano cannot. Once the key is struck, the sound begins to die. The sustain pedal delays the death but does not prevent it.

The blue note in the sustain pedal is the melancholy of impermanence made audible. Aaberg's use of the sustain pedal is central to his sound -- long tones ringing across open intervals, chords bleeding into each other through sympathetic vibration, the piano's frame resonating with accumulated sound that slowly, inevitably, decays. The sustain pedal creates a halo of sound around each note -- harmonics, overtones, sympathetic vibrations.

- **The hometown stranger** (63, Loggins) is the absence of connection. The sustain pedal is the presence of a connection that is fading.

The sustain pedal connects all three E=1 artists through their shared instrument. ### TAXONOMY: *Martes americana*

- **Martes** -- from Latin *martes* (marten), genus stable since Pinel (1792). Some authorities split Pacific martens as *Martes caurina* from *Martes americana*, with the Cascades and Olympics populations potentially belonging to the Pacific form. The split is based on morphological and molecular evidence, though not universally accepted.
- **americana** -- "of America." Distinguishes from the European Pine Marten (*M. martes*), the Sable (*M. zibellina*), the Japanese Marten (*M. melampus*), and the Yellow-throated Marten (*M. flavigula*).

Classification:
- Kingdom: Animalia / Phylum: Chordata / Class: Mammalia (5th mammalian entry, 4th species)
- **Order: Carnivora** -- 3rd entry, three consecutive Carnivora (63-64-65)
- **Family: Mustelidae** -- 3rd entry, TRIO COMPLETE. 3 of 8 PNW mustelid species (37.5%). Three subfamilies now represented: Lutrinae (otter), Mustelinae (mink), Guloninae (marten)
- Genus: *Martes* -- the true martens. Species: *M. americana*.

### PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION: The Arboreal Predator

A small, cat-sized mustelid built for canopy life: 50-68 cm total length, 0.5-1.2 kg. Elongated but more proportional than the mink -- slightly less tube-like, reflecting arboreal rather than burrow-entering lifestyle. Broader, more triangular head with larger, rounded ears giving an alert, fox-like expression. Rich golden-brown to dark brown fur with a diagnostic **orange-gold throat bib** (individually distinctive, usable for camera-trap identification). Semi-retractable claws for climbing, powerful hind legs for explosive leaps, bushy tail for balance during arboreal pursuit. Fur softer and less oily than the mink's waterproof guard hairs -- no waterproofing needed because the marten rarely enters water. Winter fur becomes thicker and more luxuriant; furred soles provide insulation on snow.

### THE CHUCKLE-CHATTER -- 66th Acoustic Element

The American Marten's vocal repertoire sits between the otter's diversity and the mink's minimalism:

- **Low chuckle:** *chuh-chuh-chuh-chuh*, 2-4 notes per second, 500-2,000 Hz. Social -- mother-kit communication, close encounters, low-intensity alert. Warmer and more tonal than the mink's shrill chitter.
- **Loud chatter:** *cha-cha-cha-cha-cha*, rapid and harsh, 1,500-4,000 Hz. Territorial -- agitation, boundary disputes, alarm. Louder, harsher, more urgent than the chuckle. Carries through the forest.
- **Additional:** Screams (distress), hissing (close-range aggression), huffing (mild alarm).

The sound comes from the canopy. The otter's chuff rises from the river surface. The mink's chitter erupts from the creek bank. The marten's chuckle-chatter descends from above -- from the branches of old-growth trees. For the first time in the engine, a mammalian vocalization comes from the vertical dimension. The listener looks up instead of across.

The Mustelidae vocal gradient: diverse (otter, 6 types) to minimal (mink, 1 dominant) to moderate (marten, 2 primary registers).

### FIRST CANOPY PREDATOR -- Hunting in Three Dimensions

The American Marten hunts IN the canopy -- pursuing squirrels through branches, leaping, climbing, navigating the three-dimensional maze of old-growth forest at high speed. This is fundamentally different from the Spotted Owl (degree 4), which hunts FROM the canopy -- perching and dropping onto prey below. The owl uses the canopy as a launch platform; the marten uses the canopy as a hunting ground.

Primary prey: red squirrels and Douglas squirrels. Secondary: voles, mice, shrews (ground and subnivean), birds and eggs (canopy nests), insects, berries, and truffles. The subnivean ecology is the engine's first documentation of under-snow hunting.

### OLD-GROWTH FOREST HABITAT -- The Canopy Returns

The Marten is one of the Pacific Northwest's most old-growth-dependent mammals. Its requirements are specific: mature coniferous forest with large-diameter trees (>50 cm DBH), closed canopy, complex vertical structure, and abundant coarse woody debris. It is not found in young second-growth or clearcuts. The marten is an old-growth obligate.

The old-growth connection:
- **SPS-004: Northern Spotted Owl** -- Both old-growth-dependent, both indicators of forest health, both solitary, both threatened by logging. 61 degrees apart, connected by the same forest.
- **SPS-001: Pacific Wren** -- The wren is the forest floor; the marten is the canopy. Same old-growth forest, different vertical strata.
- **SPS-054: Pileated Woodpecker** -- Both depend on large trees. The woodpecker creates cavities the marten uses for denning. Architect and tenant.

The old-growth portfolio now spans four species, two classes, the full vertical structure of old-growth forest: floor (Wren), trunk (Woodpecker), canopy (Owl and Marten).

### CONSERVATION STATUS

The Marten is not formally listed as threatened in Washington but is a species of management concern due to strict old-growth dependence. The Pacific Northwest has lost 80-90% of pre-settlement old-growth forest. Marten populations have declined commensurately. Forest fragmentation isolates populations. Climate change threatens subnivean winter habitat through snowpack decline. Like the Spotted Owl, the marten is an indicator species -- where martens persist, old-growth structure remains.

### DUWAMISH/COAST SALISH TERRITORIAL ACKNOWLEDGMENT

The old-growth forests where American Martens hunt, den, and raise their young throughout western Washington are the ancestral and current homelands of the **Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Puyallup, Suquamish, Tulalip, Snoqualmie, Stillaguamish, Upper Skagit, Sauk-Suiattle, and other Coast Salish and Interior Salish peoples**. These forests have been managed by Indigenous communities through fire, selective harvest, and spiritual stewardship for millennia. Old-growth forest is not "unmanaged wilderness" -- it is the product of human-landscape relationships that long predate European colonization.
