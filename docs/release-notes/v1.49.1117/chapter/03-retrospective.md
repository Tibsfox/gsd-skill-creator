## Surprises

- **A single nadir pulse, corrected three ways, becomes a centimetre.** Jason-3's Poseidon-3B fires a nadir Ku/C-band pulse straight down to the sea surface and times the two-way echo, but the raw range is only the start: the dual frequency corrects the ionospheric delay, the AMR radiometer corrects the wet-tropospheric delay, and DORIS, GPS, and the LRA fix the precise orbit — and only after all three corrections does the range become a sea-surface height good to within a few centimetres.
- **A reference record stitched across four missions and three decades.** Jason-3 is not measuring the sea-level rise alone — it is the fourth link in a single reference record, TOPEX/Poseidon (1992) → Jason-1 → OSTM/Jason-2 → Jason-3, each cross-calibrated to the last on the same reference orbit so the three-decade global-mean-sea-level time series stays consistent. The point is the continuity: a single, stable reference track of the sea's height across more than thirty years.
- **A mission that handed off the lead and kept flying.** Jason-3 launched in 2016 with a five-year nominal life; ten years on it still ranges the sea surface. Sentinel-6 Michael Freilich, launched in November 2020, flew about sixteen months in tandem with Jason-3, was declared the Reference Altimetry Mission on 24 March 2022, and Jason-3 began moving to its interleaved orbit on 7 April 2022 — the older mission handing the lead to the new one and then continuing alongside it, interleaved and tandem, to densify the coverage.
- **A PNW shorebird whose feeding is governed by the very height of the sea Jason-3 measures.** The Surfbird (*Calidris virgata*), a stocky shorebird that breeds in alpine tundra but winters along the rocky Pacific coast (on the PNW outer coast), forages in the surf-splashed rocky intertidal zone where the waves break — its access to food rising and falling with the tide, the local height of the sea that Jason-3 maps from above (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A salt-marsh herb of the tide line that the rising sea reshapes.** The Seaside Arrowgrass (*Triglochin maritima*, Seaside Arrowgrass), a PNW coastal salt-marsh herb of tidal marshes and estuary edges, raises slender grass-like leaves from the tidal mud where the sea floods and drains twice each day — a plant of the tide line and the salt marsh that the rising global sea, the sea-level rise Jason-3 tracks, slowly reshapes — bound with the throughline of the height of the sea.
