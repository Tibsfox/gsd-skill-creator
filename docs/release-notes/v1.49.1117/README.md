## Mission Overview

Jason-3 is the obs#20 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — the NASA/NOAA + CNES/EUMETSAT ocean-surface-topography reference radar-altimetry mission, a free-flying satellite that measures the height of the sea surface to within a few centimetres and continues the decades-long reference record of global sea level. ★ It is a NASA Earth-science mission flown as a four-agency partnership: NASA and CNES supply the science and instruments and NASA the launch, while NOAA and EUMETSAT lead operations; it continues the TOPEX/Poseidon (1992) → Jason-1 (2001) → OSTM/Jason-2 (2008) → Jason-3 (2016) reference altimetry series. Jason-3 carries the Poseidon-3B nadir-looking dual-frequency Ku-band (13.575 GHz) and C-band (5.3 GHz) radar altimeter (the dual frequency corrects the ionospheric delay), the Advanced Microwave Radiometer (AMR-2) for the wet-tropospheric correction, and DORIS, GPS, and a Laser Retroreflector Array (LRA) for precise orbit determination — together yielding sea-surface height to within a few centimetres (the ~10-day requirement is better than 4 cm and the precise data products reach about 3 cm). The mission launched 17 January 2016 on a SpaceX Falcon 9 v1.1 — the final Falcon 9 v1.1 flight — from Vandenberg Air Force Base SLC-4E, and flies as a free-flying satellite in the TOPEX/Jason reference orbit (~1336 km altitude, 66° inclination, ~10-day repeat). The story is the Falcon 9 launch from Vandenberg, the ascent to the ~1336 km reference orbit, the Poseidon-3B Ku/C-band pulse going straight down to the sea surface and the echo returning, the range measurement corrected for the ionosphere (by the dual frequency), the wet troposphere (by the AMR), and the orbit (by DORIS/GPS/LRA) to yield sea-surface height, and the global map of ocean surface topography and rising mean sea level being gathered from above. The science is central: from the reference orbit Jason-3 ranges the sea surface — the Poseidon-3B altimeter firing a nadir Ku/C-band pulse down to the water and timing the echo — and turns the corrected range into sea-surface height, ocean circulation, and the long record of global mean sea-level rise. ★★★ Jason-3 is OPERATING, so the catalog frames its whole story in the PRESENT (Jason-3 orbits the Earth, ranges the sea surface, measures sea-surface height, and maps the rising global sea); Sentinel-6 Michael Freilich (launched November 2020) flew about sixteen months in tandem with Jason-3, was declared the Reference Altimetry Mission on 24 March 2022, and Jason-3 began moving to its interleaved orbit on 7 April 2022 — a PAST sub-event — and the mission continues, interleaved and tandem. Jason-3's firsts are scoped carefully: it continues the reference satellite record of global mean sea level, it performs the nadir dual-frequency radar altimetry of sea-surface height from the reference orbit, and it maps ocean surface topography and sea-level rise to the centimetre. ★ The scope is load-bearing: radar altimeters flew in space before Jason-3 — Skylab S-193 in 1973, GEOS-3 in 1975, Seasat in 1978, and TOPEX/Poseidon in 1992 — so Jason-3 is NOT "the first radar altimeter" and NOT "the first ocean altimetry" mission; its distinction is continuing the reference sea-level record. ## Surprises

- **A single nadir pulse, corrected three ways, becomes a centimetre.** Jason-3's Poseidon-3B fires a nadir Ku/C-band pulse straight down to the sea surface and times the two-way echo, but the raw range is only the start: the dual frequency corrects the ionospheric delay, the AMR radiometer corrects the wet-tropospheric delay, and DORIS, GPS, and the LRA fix the precise orbit — and only after all three corrections does the range become a sea-surface height good to within a few centimetres.
- **A reference record stitched across four missions and three decades.** Jason-3 is not measuring the sea-level rise alone — it is the fourth link in a single reference record, TOPEX/Poseidon (1992) → Jason-1 → OSTM/Jason-2 → Jason-3, each cross-calibrated to the last on the same reference orbit so the three-decade global-mean-sea-level time series stays consistent. The point is the continuity: a single, stable reference track of the sea's height across more than thirty years.
- **A mission that handed off the lead and kept flying.** Jason-3 launched in 2016 with a five-year nominal life; ten years on it still ranges the sea surface. Sentinel-6 Michael Freilich, launched in November 2020, flew about sixteen months in tandem with Jason-3, was declared the Reference Altimetry Mission on 24 March 2022, and Jason-3 began moving to its interleaved orbit on 7 April 2022 — the older mission handing the lead to the new one and then continuing alongside it, interleaved and tandem, to densify the coverage.
- **A PNW shorebird whose feeding is governed by the very height of the sea Jason-3 measures.** The Surfbird (*Calidris virgata*), a stocky shorebird that breeds in alpine tundra but winters along the rocky Pacific coast (on the PNW outer coast), forages in the surf-splashed rocky intertidal zone where the waves break — its access to food rising and falling with the tide, the local height of the sea that Jason-3 maps from above (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A salt-marsh herb of the tide line that the rising sea reshapes.** The Seaside Arrowgrass (*Triglochin maritima*, Seaside Arrowgrass), a PNW coastal salt-marsh herb of tidal marshes and estuary edges, raises slender grass-like leaves from the tidal mud where the sea floods and drains twice each day — a plant of the tide line and the salt marsh that the rising global sea, the sea-level rise Jason-3 tracks, slowly reshapes — bound with the throughline of the height of the sea.

## Dedication

v1.300 Jason-3 is dedicated to the people at NASA, NOAA, CNES, and EUMETSAT, and across the ocean-altimetry science teams, who built and fly the satellite that keeps the long record of the height of the sea. To the engineers at CNES and Thales Alenia Space who built the platform and the Poseidon-3B altimeter, and to the NASA and JPL teams who built the radiometer and the precise-orbit hardware that turn a single nadir pulse into a sea-surface height good to a few centimetres. From the reference orbit the altimeter still reaches down to the moving surface of the sea, and the long record of the rising global ocean — its height, its circulation, its slow rise — belongs to them.
