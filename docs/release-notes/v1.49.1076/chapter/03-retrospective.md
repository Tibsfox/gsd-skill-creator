## Surprises

- **A great telescope was lifted above the air on a Space Shuttle as its heaviest payload ever.** Chandra launched 23 July 1999 aboard the Space Shuttle Columbia on mission STS-93 — commanded by Eileen Collins, the first woman to command a Shuttle mission — and was boosted by a two-stage Inertial Upper Stage to a high orbit; together, Chandra and its IUS (about 22,780 kg / 50,162 lb) were the heaviest payload ever launched by the Space Shuttle.
- **X-rays are focused by skipping off mirrors at a shallow grazing angle.** Because X-rays would pass straight through an ordinary mirror struck head-on, Chandra's four nested Wolter Type I shells reflect them at a nearly tangent grazing angle; the iridium-coated optics are among the smoothest ever made, and they give Chandra the highest angular resolution of any X-ray observatory ever flown (sub-arcsecond, about 0.5", roughly a 10× improvement over the prior best).
- **A telescope circles far out on a 64-hour orbit, above the radiation belts.** Chandra flies in a high elliptical orbit (about 16,000 × 133,000 km), with a roughly 64-hour period, spending about 85% of the orbit above Earth's radiation belts — far above low Earth orbit and out of Shuttle reach, so it was never serviced by astronauts.
- **A sharp X-ray eye gave a direct empirical proof of dark matter.** In the merging galaxy cluster 1E 0657-56 (the Bullet Cluster), Chandra mapped the X-ray-bright hot gas, and the offset between that gas and the gravitational-lensing mass gave a direct empirical proof of the existence of dark matter (Clowe et al. 2006).
- **A sharp-eyed, fierce little songbird hunts from an open perch and caches its prey on thorns.** The Northern Shrike (*Lanius excubitor* / *Lanius borealis*), the keen-eyed "butcher bird," hunts small birds, mammals, and large insects from an exposed perch and is known for impaling prey on thorns and barbed wire to cache it — a winter visitor across the open country of the Pacific Northwest (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Chandra, the keen eye for the hot and the fierce.
- **A dry-slope wildflower lifts brilliant scarlet trumpet flowers like a flare of fire on the open ground.** The Scarlet Gilia (*Ipomopsis aggregata*), a striking wildflower of dry, open Pacific Northwest slopes and sagebrush-steppe, lifts brilliant scarlet trumpet-shaped flowers on tall stalks, pollinated by hummingbirds and hawkmoths (ecological description only) — a flare of fire-red on the dry open slope, a mirror of the fiery, violent universe that Chandra watches.
