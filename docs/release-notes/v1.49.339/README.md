**Roosevelt Elk Cow Call and the Herd-Center Acoustic Register.** The **Roosevelt Elk cow call** enters the Sound of Puget Sound engine at degree 198 as the middle entry in the three-degree elk trio spanning degrees 197-199. **Roosevelt Elk** (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*) is the largest of four North American elk subspecies and is named for **Theodore Roosevelt** by the biologist **C. Hart Merriam** in 1897. Adult bulls reach 700-1,100 pounds and cows reach 575-625 pounds, with the core geographic range centered on the **Olympic Peninsula** (highest density in the world, estimated at 5,000-8,000 individuals within Olympic National Park alone), extending south through the **Cascade Range** foothills and into western Oregon, Northern California, Vancouver Island, and coastal British Columbia. The **cow call** refers to the within-herd contact vocalizations used by cows and calves to maintain acoustic contact in the dense understory of the Olympic Peninsula rainforest. The repertoire is varied and includes the **mew** (a high-pitched, nasal, rising-falling contact call at approximately 200-500 Hz, used by both cows and calves), the **bark** (an abrupt alarm call in response to perceived threats), and a range of **grunts**, **squeaks**, and **whimpers** that fill the social-contact acoustic space within the herd. The vocalizations are fundamentally different from the bull's bugle: where the bugle is a broadcast across valleys at high amplitude with the function of between-herd challenge, the cow call is a directional, purposeful communication within the group at moderate amplitude with the function of within-herd contact. The engine's documentation at degree 198 establishes the herd-center acoustic register as a first-class communication-register vocabulary entry.

**Cow-Calf Individual Call Signatures and Maternal Recognition.** Research on elk bioacoustics has documented that **cow-calf pairs develop individually recognizable call signatures** across the first months of the calf's life. Each cow-calf pair's contact calls carry distinct spectral and temporal features — specific frequency peaks, specific rhythmic patterns, specific amplitude envelopes — that enable the mother to discriminate her specific calf from the background of other calves in the herd. This maternal-recognition acoustic phenomenon is not unique to Roosevelt Elk — it has been documented in multiple ungulate species, in pinnipeds, in bats, and across many bird taxa — but the elk case is particularly well-documented because the large herd sizes (often dozens of cow-calf pairs, occasionally more than a hundred individuals) create strong selection pressure for reliable individual-recognition mechanisms. A cow that cannot reliably locate her specific calf from among the other calves in the herd will have reduced reproductive success; evolution has accordingly refined the cow-calf individual-signature system to a high degree of reliability. ## Part B: Roosevelt Elk Cow Call — The Herd-Center Acoustic Register and the Elk Trio Middle

- **Species Baseline and Taxonomic Identity:** **Roosevelt Elk** (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*) is a member of **Order Artiodactyla / Suborder Ruminantia / Infraorder Pecora / Family Cervidae / Subfamily Cervinae / Genus Cervus / species canadensis / subspecies roosevelti**. The subspecies was named for **Theodore Roosevelt** by the biologist **C. Hart Merriam** in 1897. Roosevelt Elk is one of four North American elk subspecies, the others being **Rocky Mountain Elk** (*C. c. nelsoni*), **Manitoban Elk** (*C. c. manitobensis*), and **Tule Elk** (*C. c. nannodes*). Roosevelt Elk is the largest of the four by body mass, with adult bulls reaching 700-1,100 pounds and cows reaching 575-625 pounds, and distinguished from Rocky Mountain Elk by heavier body build, somewhat smaller antler spread for equivalent body mass, and darker coloration.
- **Geographic Range and Olympic Peninsula Densification:** The Roosevelt Elk core geographic range centers on the **Olympic Peninsula** (highest population density in the world), extending south through the **Cascade Range** foothills, into western Oregon, Northern California, Vancouver Island, and coastal British Columbia. Within **Olympic National Park** alone, the Roosevelt Elk population is estimated at 5,000-8,000 individuals, distributed across the **Hoh Rainforest** drainage, the **Queets Rainforest** drainage, the **Quinault Rainforest** drainage, the **Elwha River** drainage, and related sub-populations. The density reflects the specific habitat suitability of the temperate-rainforest ecosystem: old-growth Sitka spruce, western hemlock, and western redcedar canopies provide forest cover for summer retreat; river-floodplain areas provide forage; and winter range below the snow line enables year-round population maintenance without extreme seasonal mortality.
- **Cow Call Acoustic Characteristics:** The **cow call** refers to the repertoire of within-herd contact vocalizations used by Roosevelt Elk cows and calves. The central vocalization is the **mew** — a high-pitched, nasal, rising-falling contact call at approximately 200-500 Hz, moderate amplitude, directional, and pitched to cut through the ambient noise of the Olympic Peninsula rainforest understory (wind, rain, vegetation rustling). Additional vocalizations in the cow-calf repertoire include the **bark** (an abrupt alarm call in response to perceived threats, higher amplitude than the mew), **grunts** (lower-frequency contact sounds used in close-range communication), **squeaks** (high-frequency communications often associated with calves), and **whimpers** (low-amplitude distress signals). Together the repertoire fills the social-contact acoustic space within the herd.
- **Cow-Calf Individual Call Signatures and Maternal Recognition:** Research on elk bioacoustics has documented that **each cow-calf pair develops individually-recognizable call signatures** across the first months of the calf's life. Each pair's contact calls carry distinct spectral and temporal features — specific frequency peaks, specific rhythmic patterns, specific amplitude envelopes — enabling cows to discriminate their specific calves from other calves in herds of dozens to over a hundred individuals. The strong selection pressure from large herd sizes has refined the individual-signature system to high reliability: a cow that could not locate her specific calf from among the background of other calves would have reduced reproductive success, so evolution has accordingly tuned the cow-calf signature discrimination system. The engine's documentation at degree 198 establishes individual-call-signature recognition as a first-class individual-recognition vocabulary entry.
- **Within-Herd Contact Versus Between-Herd Challenge:** The cow call repertoire occupies the **within-herd contact** register, which is functionally and acoustically distinct from the **between-herd challenge** register occupied by the bull's bugle (degree 197) and the **close-combat** register occupied by the rut roar (degree 199). The within-herd contact calls are moderate-amplitude, directional, purposeful, and pitched to maintain acoustic bond within the group rather than to broadcast across valleys. The bugle, by contrast, is high-amplitude, omnidirectional, and pitched for maximum-distance carrying — a challenge to rival bulls across the distance between herds. The rut roar is high-amplitude but short-range, produced during physical combat at contact range. The engine's documentation at degree 198 establishes the within-herd-contact-versus-between-herd-challenge distinction as a first-class communication-register vocabulary entry.
- **Elk Trio Energy Arc and Social-Structure Acoustic Mirror:** The three-degree elk trio spans degrees 197-199 with an energy arc of **(7-5-7)** — the bugle at E=7, the cow call at E=5, and the rut roar at E=7. This arc is not incidental; it is a direct acoustic mirror of the Roosevelt Elk herd's physical social structure. Cows and calves form the **stable social core** at the center of the herd's spatial organization with continuous moderate-amplitude acoustic contact; bulls operate on the **periphery** during the autumn rut with high-amplitude challenge-and-combat vocalizations. The (loud-quiet-loud) acoustic-amplitude signature therefore encodes the (periphery-center-periphery) social-structure signature of the species. The engine's documentation at degree 198 establishes the social-structure-as-acoustic-mirror observation as a first-class taxonomic-acoustic vocabulary entry.
- **Theodore Roosevelt and the 1909 Conservation Intervention:** Roosevelt Elk is named for **Theodore Roosevelt**, the 26th U.S. President (1901-1909), whose 1909 establishment of **Mount Olympus National Monument** was motivated in part by the documented need to protect Roosevelt Elk habitat from unregulated hunting and industrial logging. The monument was expanded and redesignated as **Olympic National Park** in 1938 by Franklin D. Roosevelt. The conservation intervention has been enormously successful: the park's Roosevelt Elk population of 5,000-8,000 individuals represents the densest Roosevelt Elk population in the world. The subspecies' continued acoustic presence in the Olympic Peninsula rainforest is a direct consequence of the 1909 conservation intervention and the subsequent ongoing management by the National Park Service, USDA Forest Service, and state wildlife agencies.
- **Rainforest Understory Acoustic Optimization:** The cow call's acoustic profile is specifically optimized for the **dense understory of the Olympic Peninsula rainforest**. The mew's nasal, directional, moderate-amplitude character is pitched to cut through the ambient noise of wind moving through old-growth canopy, rain falling on leaves and ground cover, and vegetation rustling as elk and other animals move through the understory. The same vocalization in an open grassland habitat would carry for different distances with different reliability — the directional-purposeful character that the understory context requires would be a liability in an open-habitat context where omnidirectional-broadcast vocalizations would be more advantageous. The cow call's environmental fit demonstrates the co-evolution of communication and environment.
- **Lifespan, Life History, and Reproductive Biology:** Roosevelt Elk lifespan is typically 12-15 years in the wild, with some individuals reaching 20 years. Cows reach sexual maturity at approximately 2-3 years and produce a single calf per year (rarely twins) after a gestation period of approximately 240-262 days. Calves are born in late spring (May-June) weighing approximately 30-35 pounds and nursing for approximately 4-6 months before weaning to grass and browse. During the nursing period, the cow-calf individual call signatures develop and are refined through continuous contact. The herds are largely matriarchal during non-rut periods, with cows, calves, and yearlings forming stable groups while mature bulls are typically solitary or in small bachelor groups outside of the autumn rut. This social structure is the context within which the (7-5-7) elk-trio energy arc operates as a social-structure acoustic mirror.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Three-entry species clusters surface structural observations that single-degree entries cannot.** The (7-5-7) energy arc across the elk trio at degrees 197-199 encodes the Roosevelt Elk herd's social structure as acoustic amplitude, and this observation is available only when all three degrees are considered together. Future engine planning should prioritize three-entry species clusters for species with well-differentiated social-role vocalizations (pinnipeds, corvids, cetaceans with multiple call types, primates with call repertoires) as a methodology for surfacing social-structure acoustic mirror observations.

2. **Within-group versus between-group communication is a first-class communication-mode vocabulary category.** The paired coordinate at degree 198 between Pedro the Lion's confessional-within-self communication and the Roosevelt Elk cow call's within-herd communication establishes within-group communication as a first-class vocabulary category distinct from between-group challenge or broadcast communication. Future engine documentation should consider whether any given subject (artist or species) operates primarily in the within-group mode, the between-group mode, or crosses between modes, and treat this dimension with equal weight to genre, amplitude, and subject matter.

3. **Subject-matter commitment is orthogonal to aesthetic-stance commitment.** Mudhoney's sustained distortion-aesthetic, Tullycraft's sustained smallness-aesthetic, and Pedro the Lion's sustained confessional-theological subject matter are three distinct forms of sustained-commitment-as-identity that together establish the duration-to-identity vocabulary as multi-dimensional rather than single-dimensional. Future engine documentation should distinguish between aesthetic-stance commitment and subject-matter commitment when documenting sustained-commitment artists.

4. **Neighborhood-level Seattle-geography vocabulary has room for continued growth.** The Greenwood addition at degree 198 demonstrates that the engine's Seattle-geography vocabulary can continue to grow as more artists with specific neighborhood origins enter. Future engine documentation should prioritize neighborhood-specific origins over generic "Seattle" attributions where the biographical context supports specificity, building out a more fine-grained geographic vocabulary across the catalog.

5. **Cross-coast label relationships deserve explicit documentation as the exception rather than the rule.** Most Seattle 360 artists released on Seattle-area labels (Sub Pop, K Records, Up Records, Kill Rock Stars, Barsuk Records). Pedro the Lion's Jade Tree relationship is one of the relatively few transcontinental label-artist ties documented in the catalog, and this exception-status deserves explicit documentation because it situates the artist within a different label-vocabulary register than the local-label norm. Future engine documentation should explicitly note cross-coast and international label relationships as distinct from local-label relationships.

6. **Individual-recognition acoustic signatures are a cross-taxa phenomenon that improves with cross-taxa documentation.** The Roosevelt Elk cow-calf individual call signatures join the engine's broader documentation of individual-recognition acoustic phenomena across taxa, previously surfaced in bird-song contexts. The cross-taxa pattern — that individual-recognition signatures evolve convergently in lineages with social-group structure and strong mother-offspring bonds — becomes visible as a pattern only when the vocabulary is applied across taxa. Future species entries should be scanned for individual-recognition acoustic features as a first-class documentation checklist item.

7. **Environmental-context optimization shapes communication-system evolution.** The Roosevelt Elk cow call's acoustic profile is specifically optimized for the Olympic Peninsula rainforest understory, and the same vocalization in an open-grassland habitat would carry differently. The engine's documentation benefits from explicit environmental-context framing for each vocalization register, recognizing that communication systems co-evolve with the environments they operate within rather than being general-purpose broadcasts. Future species entries should include environmental-context optimization as a first-class documentation dimension.

8. **Conservation history provides essential context for species vocalization documentation.** The Roosevelt Elk cow call's continued acoustic presence in the Olympic Peninsula rainforest is a direct consequence of Theodore Roosevelt's 1909 establishment of Mount Olympus National Monument and the subsequent 1938 redesignation as Olympic National Park. Future species entries for conservation-sensitive species should include the conservation-history context as a first-class documentation dimension rather than treating the species' current acoustic presence as an uncomplicated baseline.

9. **Theological subject matter deserves explicit documentation vocabulary distinct from secular-emotional-relational subject matter.** Pedro the Lion's engagement with Protestant evangelical Christian concepts (grace, sin, salvation, fellowship, moral failure, divine judgment) in the first-person-interrogative register is a subject-matter zone that secular-emotional-relational vocabulary cannot adequately describe. Future engine documentation for artists engaging explicit religious, philosophical, or ethical-theoretical subject matter should adopt subject-matter-specific vocabulary rather than collapsing these subjects into generic "emotional" framings.

10. **Middle-position cluster entries often operate at the amplitude-quiet center of (loud-quiet-loud) arcs.** The Pedro the Lion entry at E=4 sits between the Sunny Day Real Estate E=5 and the Modest Mouse E=6 as the quiet midpoint of the emotional-sincerity cluster at degrees 197-199, mirroring the Roosevelt Elk cow call's E=5 position between the bugle E=7 and the rut roar E=7 in the elk trio at the same degree range. The recurring (loud-quiet-loud) arc pattern across multiple cluster types suggests this is a general cluster-organization principle rather than a genre-specific or taxon-specific feature. Future engine planning should consider whether three-entry clusters generally exhibit this amplitude arc and whether the cluster-middle quiet-entry is a structural regularity.
