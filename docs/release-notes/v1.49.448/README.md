## Summary

**MARK LANEGAN RETURNS. AGGREGATE SOUND. SPLASH-SLAP-CHURN. 85.3%.**

**Screaming Trees (return)** arrive at E=7 -- the Ellensburg/Seattle grunge band returning at degree 307, with Mark Lanegan (vocals), Gary Lee Conner (guitar), Van Conner (bass), and Barrett Martin (drums), the lineup that defined the band's peak period through *Sweet Oblivion* (1992) and *Dust* (1996). The Screaming Trees were not a Seattle band in the origin sense -- they came from Ellensburg, the Central Washington college town on the eastern slope of the Cascades, and their move into the Seattle orbit was a gravitational pull rather than a native condition. But their position in the grunge canon is secure: they were there from the proto-grunge period through the peak, and Lanegan's voice is one of the definitive grunge vocal instruments.

E=7 for the Screaming Trees at degree 307 is the correct reading for the band at full lineup in the grunge register. The Screaming Trees operated at E=7 as their characteristic output level: energetic without being the most explosive, melodically committed without being the most polished, vocally distinctive without the extreme energy spikes of Alice in Chains or Soundgarden. *Sweet Oblivion* is an E=7 record; *Dust* is an E=7 record with moments that reach higher. The return at degree 307 documents the band at their consistent register, two degrees before the final arc concludes.

**Salmon run full river** arrives at E=8 on the SPS track -- not a single species but an aggregate acoustic event: the full salmon run at peak density, multiple species simultaneously, the splash-slap-churn wall of sound that constitutes one of the Pacific Northwest's most distinctive large-scale acoustic phenomena. The salmon run acoustic environment is a compound: the thrashing of spawning salmon at the shallows (splash), the tail-slap of fish navigating the current (slap), and the churning of the water column at the riffles and pools where fish concentrate (churn). At peak run density -- a river like the Hoh or the Snohomish or the Skagit at the height of the October-November fall runs -- the salmon sound is not individual but aggregate: a wall of biological acoustic energy that extends for kilometers along the river.

E=8 for the salmon run full river is the aggregate acoustic event designation. No individual salmon produces an E=8 acoustic event; the aggregate run at full density is an E=8 event by accumulation. The distinction matters: the SPS track at degree 307 is not documenting a single fish but a river system in full biological acoustic expression, the salmon runs that define Pacific Northwest ecological identity and have defined it for ten thousand years of human habitation. The aggregate sound is the sound of an ecosystem at its most productive moment, acoustically expressed.

**MARK LANEGAN.** The Screaming Trees' return at degree 307 is the engine's acknowledgment of one of the most distinctive voices in Pacific Northwest music. Lanegan's baritone was the Screaming Trees' defining acoustic signature -- a voice with more range than the grunge template required, more texture than the distorted guitar typically allowed space for, and more resonance than any Seattle contemporary except Chris Cornell. The return at degree 307, one degree before the Melvins enter at degree 308, positions Lanegan's voice as the last identifiable individual vocal presence before the Melvins' sludge metal erases individual voice into collective sonic mass.

**THE RIVER AS AGGREGATE.** The salmon run sound is the apex predator arc's most ecologically complex entry because it is not a predator's sound but the prey's aggregate presence -- the same role the elk bugle played at degree 301, but at a different ecological scale. The salmon are the food base for every apex predator in the Pacific Northwest: bears at the river, eagles overhead, orcas in the estuary, seals and sea lions at the river mouth. The splash-slap-churn wall is the acoustic expression of the food base that makes every other entry in the apex predator arc possible.
