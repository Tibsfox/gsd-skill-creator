## Summary

The twenty-seventh paired release and the engine's first two-in-a-row mission failure. On November 18, 1961, three months after Ranger 1's inaugural fluid-dynamics failure at degree 25, Atlas-Agena B serial 117D rose from Launch Complex 12 at Cape Canaveral carrying Ranger 2, the second test flight in NASA's new lunar exploration program. The Atlas booster performed exactly as designed. Main engine cutoff at the programmed altitude, staging nominal, Agena B ignition nominal, first burn duration nominal. Ranger 2 entered a parking orbit at 150 by 242 kilometers with 33.3-degree inclination, the exact profile the flight plan specified. Then the guidance system began its pre-restart sequence. The propellant settling thrusters fired to push residual fuel against the pickup screens, correcting for the propellant-migration problem that had killed Ranger 1 at degree 25. The upgrade from Agena A to Agena B was working. The fluid-dynamics fix was real. And then, during the restart sequence, the roll gyroscope -- one of three gyroscopes forming the inertial reference frame that told the guidance system which way the vehicle was pointing -- was found to be inoperative. The gyroscope that should have been spinning was not. Without roll reference, the Agena B could not verify its rotational alignment around the longitudinal axis. The restart sequence required all three axes within tolerance. With the roll gyroscope dead, the tolerance check failed. The engine did not fire. Ranger 2 circled Earth for two days and reentered the atmosphere on November 20, 1961.

The same outcome as Ranger 1. Different mechanism. Ranger 1's Agena A had failed because the propellants would not settle in zero gravity -- a fluid dynamics problem, the nitrogen pressurant finding paths around the liquid methane and liquid oxygen in the tanks, gas entering the turbopump when the restart valves opened, combustion instability, shutdown. The Agena had been upgraded from A to B model between the two flights: improved engine with 71 kN thrust versus 68 kN, improved guidance with redundant inertial sensors, improved propellant management with baffles and new pickup screen geometry. The fluid dynamics fix was correct and it worked. But the roll gyroscope was a new failure mode that the upgrade had not anticipated. This is the pattern the engine has documented since Pioneer 0 through Pioneer 4 at degrees 9 through 13: fix one failure, encounter the next. Pioneer 0 exploded seventy-seven seconds into flight from a turbopump bearing failure; the bearing was fixed for Pioneer 1. Pioneer 1 fell short of escape velocity by an accelerometer bias error; the error was fixed for Pioneer 2. Pioneer 2's Able stage third stage failed to ignite; the stage was fixed for Pioneer 3. Each fix was correct and necessary, and each fix revealed the next weak link in a long serial chain. The Ranger program iterates the same way, at the same pace, with the same humbling lesson: complex systems fail in serial, and you cannot test your way past the failures you have not yet imagined. The same pattern, paired across Pioneer and Ranger, spanning five degrees on the earlier program and now two on the newer one, both programs teaching the same structural lesson through independently identical failure mechanics.
