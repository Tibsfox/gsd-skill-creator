## Mission Overview

2001 Mars Odyssey is the obs#10 sustain of the MARS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA orbiter whose defining achievements are its long, continuous watch over Mars (the longest-operating spacecraft at Mars), the first global detection of near-surface water ice from orbit, and the first characterization of the radiation environment en route to and at Mars. It is managed by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory and built by Lockheed Martin, and it is named after Arthur C. Clarke's "2001: A Space Odyssey." An ORBITER — not a lander; it did not land or traverse — Odyssey carries THEMIS (the Thermal Emission Imaging System, a visible and thermal-infrared imager, principal investigator Philip Christensen), the GRS suite (the Gamma Ray Spectrometer plus the High-Energy Neutron Detector HEND and the Neutron Spectrometer NS, principal investigator William Boynton), and MARIE (the Mars Radiation Environment Experiment). It launched on a Delta II 7925 from Cape Canaveral SLC-17A on 7 April 2001 (15:02 UTC) and entered Mars orbit on 24 October 2001 (UTC), then used aerobraking — repeatedly dipping into the upper atmosphere from late October 2001 into January 2002 (about 76 days) and using drag to shed energy — to shrink and circularize its orbit into a near-circular, Sun-synchronous polar mapping orbit (~400 km), beginning its science mapping on 19 February 2002. The science is the subsurface, the surface, the radiation, and the long watch of the whole planet: the GRS suite, and especially the neutron detectors HEND and the Neutron Spectrometer, detected abundant hydrogen in the shallow (top ~1 m) subsurface in 2002, interpreted as vast deposits of water ice concentrated at high latitudes poleward of roughly ±60° — the first global detection and mapping of near-surface hydrogen → water ice from orbit (gamma-ray/neutron remote sensing), distinct from Phoenix's first in-situ TOUCH of ice in 2008 (Odyssey's detection guided the choice of Phoenix's landing site) and never "first water on Mars." THEMIS imaged the surface in visible and thermal-infrared light, mapping temperature, mineralogy, and morphology at higher spatial resolution than TES — described as imaging and mapping, NOT a re-claim of MGS's TES first global thermal-IR mineral survey at obs#9. MARIE made the first dedicated measurements of the energetic-particle (cosmic-ray and solar) radiation environment during the cruise to Mars and in orbit at Mars, characterizing the radiation hazard to future human explorers, and collected data through 2003 until a large solar particle event ended it. Odyssey has circled Mars in its Sun-synchronous polar orbit for about 25 years and more than 105,000 orbits as of 2026 — the longest-operating spacecraft at Mars (a record it took on 15 December 2010, surpassing MGS's 3,340-day record; scope as "at Mars / at any planet beyond Earth," since the Voyagers operate longer in deep space) — and it is still active, serving as the enduring telecom relay backbone for Spirit, Opportunity, Phoenix, Curiosity, and Perseverance. ## Surprises

- **A spacecraft has kept its watch over Mars for about 25 years.** 2001 Mars Odyssey has circled Mars in its Sun-synchronous polar orbit in continuous operation since 2001 — about 25 years and more than 105,000 orbits as of 2026, far outliving its prime mission — the longest-operating spacecraft at Mars, a record it took on 15 December 2010 by surpassing MGS's 3,340-day mark, and it is still active.
- **A gamma-ray and neutron suite read the buried water ice of a planet from orbit.** Odyssey's GRS suite, and especially its neutron detectors, detected abundant hydrogen in the shallow (top ~1 m) subsurface in 2002 — interpreted as vast deposits of water ice concentrated poleward of roughly ±60° in both hemispheres, the first global detection and mapping of near-surface water ice from orbit, a result that later helped guide the choice of Phoenix's 2008 landing site.
- **A radiation experiment charted the hazard of Mars space for future explorers.** Odyssey's MARIE made the first dedicated measurements of the energetic-particle (cosmic-ray and solar) radiation environment during the cruise to Mars and in orbit at Mars, characterizing the radiation hazard to future human explorers, and collected data through 2003 until a large solar particle event ended it.
- **An imager mapped the surface in visible and thermal-infrared light day and night.** Odyssey's THEMIS imaged the surface in visible and thermal-infrared light at higher spatial resolution than TES, mapping surface temperature, mineralogy, and morphology — described as imaging and mapping, not a re-claim of MGS's TES first global thermal-IR mineral survey.
- **A sturgeon is an ancient-lineage fish that lives a century or more.** The White Sturgeon (*Acipenser transmontanus*), the largest freshwater fish in North America, an ancient-lineage species of the Pacific Northwest's large rivers (the Columbia, the Fraser) and coastal waters, lives very long — commonly several decades and the longest past a century (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Mars Odyssey enduring at Mars far beyond its prime mission.
- **A cedar endures for centuries on the southwest-Oregon coast.** The Port Orford Cedar (*Chamaecyparis lawsoniana*), a tall, long-lived evergreen conifer commonly living 500+ years, native to a limited range in southwest Oregon and northwest California (the Klamath / coastal Pacific Northwest), stands for centuries as a long-enduring presence on the landscape (ecological description only) — a mirror of Odyssey's long, steady watch over Mars.

## Dedication

v1.247 2001 Mars Odyssey is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's long-watch orbiter and gave Mars its first global reading of buried ice and radiation. The Jet Propulsion Laboratory teams who manage the mission; the Lockheed Martin engineers who built the orbiter and steered it to Mars; the instrument teams who built the imager, the gamma-ray and neutron suite, and the radiation experiment; the navigators who fired the engines into Mars orbit on an October night in 2001; and the aerobraking team who dipped the craft into the top of the atmosphere, pass after pass, to circle it into a Sun-synchronous polar mapping orbit. Year after year, a single craft has circled high above the planet and kept its long, steady watch — sensing the hydrogen of buried ice toward the high latitudes, reading the surface in visible and thermal-infrared light, and charting the particle radiation of Mars space for the explorers who may follow. It has caught the signals of the rovers below and passed them on to Earth, and it keeps its station still — a patient sentinel watching over Mars far beyond its appointed span, the longest watch yet at the red planet.
