**The Genus Limosa and the Godwit Lineage.** **Limosa** is the genus of long-billed, slightly-upturned-billed tidal-flat shorebirds — the godwit lineage — named from the Latin *limosus* (muddy) in reference to the mudflat habitat. The genus contains four species globally: **Marbled Godwit** (*L. fedoa*, North American Great Plains breeder), **Hudsonian Godwit** (*L. haemastica*, Arctic breeder with trans-equatorial migration), **Bar-tailed Godwit** (*L. lapponica*, Arctic breeder holding the record for non-stop migration distance at ~12,000 km), and **Black-tailed Godwit** (*L. limosa*, European and Asian breeder). The engine's entry at degree 172 opens the genus with the Marbled Godwit and positions Limosa as the companion lineage to **Numenius** (the curlew genus that opened at degree 170 with Whimbrel and continued at 171 with Long-billed Curlew). **The Slightly Upturned Bill and the Lateral Sweeping Technique.** The Marbled Godwit's **slightly upturned bill** is the defining morphological signature of the genus Limosa and the counter-curve to the Numenius decurved bill. The bill measures **10-13 centimeters** — long for a shorebird but shorter than the Long-billed Curlew's up to 21 centimeters — and curves gently upward, especially in the distal half. The bill is **bicolored**: pink-orange at the base transitioning to black at the tip, a distinctive field-identification feature. The upward curvature is functionally adapted for **lateral sweeping** through shallow water and soft mud: the godwit sweeps its bill side to side across the substrate, detecting prey (marine worms, small crustaceans, mollusks) by touch and by the movement of the bill against the mud. The upturned geometry keeps the bill tip near the surface during sweeping, in contrast to the curlew's downcurved geometry which pushes the bill tip deep into burrows. The two feeding strategies — godwit lateral-sweeping vs. curlew deep-probing — occupy different prey zones and demonstrate that the Scolopacidae family contains diverse feeding specializations even within the long-billed shorebird cohort. The engine's documentation at degree 172 preserves the slightly upturned bill as a **morphological first** entering the anatomical vocabulary and establishes the lateral-sweeping-vs.-deep-probing feeding-strategy comparison as a first-class observation.

The bird feeds in mixed flocks with **Willets**, **Long-billed Curlews**, **Whimbrels**, and other large shorebirds, and the **lateral sweeping feeding technique** distinguishes it visually from the curlew-lineage deep-probing behavior.
