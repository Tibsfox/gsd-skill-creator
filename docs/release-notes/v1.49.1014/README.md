## Summary

v1.49.1014 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.204 to 1.205, adding the Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory (Swift) — the dedicated gamma-ray-burst observatory — as the obs#2 INTRA-AXIS continuation that SUSTAINS the GAMMA-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis (axis-rotation #28 sustained at obs#2). The gamma-ray-astronomy axis opened at v1013 Compton with the first comprehensive full-sky gamma-ray survey and the all-sky burst monitor whose coarse positions Swift turns into precise localizations; the forward continuation extends the axis to a second observation that is substrate-form-distinct. Swift catches each gamma-ray burst, fixes its position within seconds, slews onto it within about a minute, and follows the fading afterglow across several bands of light, turning the gamma-ray-burst population Compton's monitor established into precisely located, multiwavelength-followed events.

Swift was built as an international collaboration of the United States, the United Kingdom, and Italy, managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, and renamed in 2018 for Neil Gehrels, its first principal investigator. It reached low Earth orbit on 20 November 2004, carried aloft by a Delta II rocket from Cape Canaveral, and it continues to operate as an active, productive observatory. The observatory carries three co-aligned telescopes — the Burst Alert Telescope (BAT), the X-ray Telescope (XRT), and the Ultraviolet/Optical Telescope (UVOT) — that span hard X-rays, soft X-rays, and ultraviolet-to-optical light.

The signature of the mission is the rapid precise localization of each burst. Swift's wide-field hard-X-ray telescope catches a burst and fixes its position to a few arcminutes within seconds, then the X-ray telescope refines it to a few arcseconds, where the earlier all-sky monitor could give only a coarse direction.

The autonomous rapid slew made early-afterglow study possible. On detecting a burst, Swift autonomously turns the whole spacecraft to bring its narrow-field telescopes onto the source within about a minute, capturing the early afterglow before it fades.

The multiwavelength afterglow follow-up connected the high-energy flash to its longer-wavelength afterglow. Swift's three co-aligned telescopes span hard X-rays, soft X-rays, and ultraviolet-to-optical light, so a burst and its fading afterglow are observed together across several bands from the same spacecraft.

The first afterglows of the short-duration bursts distinguished the two families of bursts by their origins. Swift's rapid follow-up caught the afterglows of the short bursts for the first time, locating them in their host galaxies and tying the short bursts to the merging of compact stellar remnants.

The detection of the most distant gamma-ray bursts made the bursts beacons for the early universe. Swift located some of the most distant bursts known, reaching back to when the universe was very young, among the most distant individual objects yet found.

The axis-relevant substrate of Swift at this degree is its sustaining of the GAMMA-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#2, the rapid precise burst localization and multiwavelength afterglow follow-up. Swift sustains the GAMMA-RAY-ASTRONOMY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#2 NEW LOCKED, sustains the ALL-SKY-GAMMA-RAY-BURST-MONITOR-LINEAGE thread at obs#2 cumulative, and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#77 cumulative (axis-rotation #28 sustained at obs#2). The forward continuation may favor a later gamma-ray observatory that sustains the axis at obs#3 — especially a large high-energy gamma-ray telescope of far greater sensitivity than EGRET — or a substrate-axis rotation #29 to an under-served axis.

Swift sustains the gamma-ray way of seeing the sky on the catalog, and the mission records the Swift team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, the BAT, XRT, and UVOT teams, and the United Kingdom and Italy partners. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.204 to 1.205, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#88 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A precise position turns a flash into a source that can be studied.** By fixing each burst to a few arcseconds within minutes, Swift turned the bursts from points on a coarse map into sources that could be followed up across the spectrum, showing that precise localization is what unlocks detailed study of a transient.
2. **A rapid response catches what a slow one would miss.** By slewing onto each burst within about a minute, Swift reached the early afterglow, the part that fades the fastest and carries the most information, showing that response time is set against the steep early decay of the target it is chasing.
3. **Several bands together connect a flash to its afterglow.** By following each burst across hard X-rays, soft X-rays, and ultraviolet-to-optical light, Swift connected the high-energy flash to its longer-wavelength afterglow, showing that a multiwavelength view traces the physics of an event one band alone cannot.
4. **Locating an event in its host reveals its origin.** By locating the short bursts in their host galaxies, Swift tied them to the merging of compact stellar remnants, showing that the setting of an event distinguishes its origin from a similar-looking one.
5. **An axis sustains at a substrate-form-distinct second observation.** By localizing and following up each burst rather than only catching it, Swift sustained the gamma-ray-astronomy axis at obs#2, showing that an INTRA-AXIS continuation extends a domain with a distinct form rather than repeating the first.
6. **A brief, bright transient can be a beacon for the distant universe.** By locating the most distant bursts, Swift used events that briefly outshine their host galaxies to probe the early universe, showing that a bright transient can light up what is too faint to see directly.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across an INTRA-AXIS continuation.** Because Swift reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.204 Compton template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the burst follow-up while keeping the warm palette, making the axis-sustaining continuation a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The same wide watch that catches a burst also points the way to its afterglow.** Swift's wide-field telescope was built to catch the bursts, and the precise position it computes is what lets the whole observatory turn onto the source, so the catch and the follow-up are one continuous relay rather than two separate steps.
- **A small, swift falcon mirrors the rapid catch-and-close of the burst follow-up.** The Merlin, the compact falcon that spots a fleeting target and overtakes it in a swift rush, enters as the detect-and-close-fast mirror to Swift catching a burst and turning onto it within a minute, so the catalog pairs a bird that seizes a fleeting target with an observatory that seizes a fading afterglow.
- **The fastest-growing hardwood mirrors the seize-the-fleeting-opening of the rapid response.** Black cottonwood, whose wind-borne seeds settle on a fresh opening and grow fast to claim it before it closes, enters as the seize-the-fleeting-opening mirror, so the catalog's burst-follow-up observatory is paired with a tree that races to seize a fresh chance before it is gone.

## Dedication

v1.205 Swift is dedicated to the teams that built and fly NASA's dedicated gamma-ray-burst observatory: the Swift team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center who built and manage the observatory; the BAT team at NASA Goddard who catch each burst and fix its position; the XRT team at the Pennsylvania State University, the University of Leicester, and the Brera Astronomical Observatory who refine the position and image the X-ray afterglow; the UVOT team at NASA Goddard and the Mullard Space Science Laboratory who image the longer-wavelength afterglow and help fix its distance; and the United Kingdom and Italy partners whose collaboration made the mission an international one. It is dedicated as well to Neil Gehrels, the first principal investigator, for whom the observatory was renamed in 2018. Swift catches each gamma-ray burst, turns swiftly onto it, and follows its fading afterglow across several bands of light, and it continues to operate.
