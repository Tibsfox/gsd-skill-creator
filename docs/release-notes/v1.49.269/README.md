## Pat O'Day -- The Original Broadcast King

Pat O'Day (born Paul Berg) was Seattle radio. Not a piece of it -- the whole thing. From 1959 through the mid-1980s, O'Day was the voice of **KJR 95 AM**, the station that dominated the Seattle market during the golden age of AM Top 40. At its peak, KJR captured a market share that would be unthinkable today -- a single station commanding the attention of an entire metropolitan area.

The context matters. The KCMU radio trio (degrees 126-128) documented underground, college, and independent radio -- stations that served communities of taste, that curated for subcultures. Pat O'Day is the **opposite pole**. Mainstream. Mass market. AM. The DJ as entertainer, not as tastemaker. O'Day didn't break indie bands; he played the hits. But he played them with a personality and energy that made KJR the station Seattle listened to.

Beyond the microphone, O'Day built the **Pacific Northwest concert industry**. He promoted shows, managed venues, created the infrastructure that allowed touring acts to play Seattle. The DJ-to-promoter pipeline. Radio as the entry point for a vertically integrated entertainment business. Concerts at the Seattle Center, dances, events -- O'Day turned radio listenership into physical attendance. The live music infrastructure that later supported the indie and grunge scenes was built, in part, on the commercial foundations O'Day laid.

E=4. Higher than the KCMU infrastructure figures (Poneman E=3, Pavitt E=3) because O'Day was a performer. The voice, the energy, the personality-driven broadcast style. Radio as performance art. Not the quiet curation of college radio but the amplified showmanship of AM Top 40.

The engine now has **four consecutive radio/media figures** (126-129): Nasty Nes Rodriguez (hip-hop radio), Jonathan Poneman (indie radio/label), Bruce Pavitt (indie radio/label), Pat O'Day (mainstream AM radio). Four figures, four different eras, four different relationships between radio and music culture. The progression from mainstream AM (O'Day, 1960s-80s) to college FM (KCMU, 1980s-90s) to hip-hop FM (Nasty Nes, 1980s-90s) maps the fragmentation of broadcast culture -- from one station for everyone to many stations for many communities.

## Canada Goose -- The Deep Honk

The Canada Goose (*Branta canadensis*) produces the most recognizable waterfowl call in North America -- a deep, resonant honk that carries across lakes, fields, and urban parks. The V-formation flight honking is one of the defining sounds of seasonal migration, audible from hundreds of feet in the air. In flock chorus, the overlapping honks create a dense, layered soundscape -- dozens of voices, each slightly different in pitch and timing.

E=5. The Canada Goose honk has genuine power -- deep, loud, carrying. Higher energy than the quiet ducks (Bufflehead E=3, Barrow's Goldeneye E=3, Canvasback E=3) but lower than the Trumpeter Swan's bugling (E=6). The goose occupies the middle register of waterfowl volume -- louder than ducks, not quite as resonant as swans.

**Goose comparison pair opens.** The Canada Goose and the Cackling Goose (degree 130) were considered a single species until **2004**, when the American Ornithologists' Union officially split them. The split was based on size, morphology, and genetics -- the smaller forms became Cackling Goose (*Branta hutchinsii*), the larger forms remained Canada Goose (*Branta canadensis*). The acoustic difference: Canada Goose produces a deep honk; Cackling Goose produces a higher-pitched, shorter cackling call. Same genus (*Branta*), adjacent degrees, one comparison pair. The taxonomic split becomes an engine split.

**12th consecutive Anatidae.** The streak: Harlequin Duck (118), Common Merganser (119), Hooded Merganser (120), Wood Duck (121), Bufflehead (122), Barrow's Goldeneye (123), Common Goldeneye (124), Greater Scaup (125), Canvasback (126), Tundra Swan (127), Trumpeter Swan (128), Canada Goose (129). Twelve degrees, twelve species. The engine has now covered mergansers, diving ducks, sea ducks, dabbling ducks, swans, and geese -- the full breadth of Anatidae in the Pacific Northwest. Lake and grass habitat -- the Canada Goose is equally comfortable on water and on land, grazing in parks, fields, and golf courses.
