- **THE DYNAMICS -- BIOGRAPHY AND THE TEEN-R&B ARC, Seattle's Premier 1960s Teen-R&B Band, Nine-Year Arc from Garage to National Distribution to Dissolution:** The Dynamics formed circa 1959 around founding vocalist Tom Larson, with Terry Afdem on keyboards, Jeff Afdem on saxophone, Pete Borg on bass, Wally Griffin on guitar, Tracy Townsend on drums, and additional vocalists Jerry Strong and Bob Johnston. Their debut single, "Baby" / "Aces Up" (Penguin Records #1006, July 1959), was described by the Northwest Music Archives as "a joyfully raw single pairing up two 'Kansas City'-type originals" and received national distribution through Guaranteed Records in New York. Tom Larson approached Tom and Ellen Ogilvy of Seafair Records with the second single, "Onion Salad" / "Lonesome Llama" (Seafair #100, June 25, 1960). Their son, Jimmy Hanna, would soon join as lead vocalist and become the band's defining voice. In 1961, the lineup underwent critical reformation: Jimmy Hanna joined on vocals, bringing what reviewers described as "rich, smooth -- almost Bobby Bland-like" vocal tone. Larry Coryell arrived from Yakima's Checkers and Marcus Doubleday on trumpet. Ron Woods replaced Tracy Townsend on drums. This lineup -- Hanna, Coryell, Doubleday, the Afdem brothers, Borg, and Woods -- recorded the band's most celebrated material. In September 1964, Seafair-Bolo released *The Dynamics with Jimmy Hanna* (BLP-8001), a live remote recording captured at Parker's (17001 Seattle-Everett Highway), engineered by Ray Van Patten and C.A. (Chet) Noland. The album was colloquially known as "The Dynamics At Parker's" and is considered the definitive document of 1960s Northwest teen-R&B. "Busybody" and "Leavin' Here" received national distribution through Atlantic Records -- the label of Ray Charles, Aretha Franklin, and the Drifters. Jimmy Hanna's cover of Eddie Holland's "Leavin' Here" (written by Holland-Dozier-Holland at Motown) was described as ranking "just behind 'Louie Louie' as a Northwest classic." By 1965, Coryell departed for New York ("the rest is jazz history," Peter Blecha wrote in *The Rocket*). Harry Wilson from The Casuals replaced him on guitar. Hanna left circa 1966 to front his own 11-piece blues big-band, eventually relocating to San Antonio, Texas. The remaining members signed with Jerden Records, releasing "I'll Be Standing There" / "All She Said" (Jerden #800, April 1966). The band then revamped as The Springfield Rifle, pivoting from teen-R&B toward pop-rock, scoring airplay with "Will You Love Me Tomorrow" (1967) and "That's All I Really Need" (1968). In 1983, the archival album *Memory Bank of Early N.W. Sounds* (Seafair-Bolo BSLP 8003) was released, compiled from live tracks mixed by Kearney Barton -- Seattle's legendary recording engineer who had defined the "Northwest sound" at Audio Recording studios. A CD reissue followed in 2009. On July 28, 2000, The Dynamics reunited for the Grand Opening of the Experience Music Project (now Museum of Pop Culture / MoPOP) at Seattle Center, alongside The Wailers and The Viceroys -- institutional recognition arriving thirty-six years after the peak album.

- **CENTRAL DISTRICT GENRE BRIDGE -- Dave Lewis "J.A.J." Cover, Birdland at 21st and East Madison, Two Parallel Music Circuits, Race and Repertoire Across the Racial Divide:** The Central District genre bridge is the mechanism by which Black R&B from the Central District's club ecosystem was transmitted into the white suburban teen-dance circuit, with The Dynamics as the primary conduit. Two parallel circuits operated in 1960s Seattle: the Central District clubs (Birdland, the Black and Tan, the Golden Crown, the Black Elks Club) were liquor-licensed adult venues serving a predominantly Black audience; the teen-dance circuit (Spanish Castle, Parker's, school dances) was all-ages, alcohol-free, and served a predominantly white suburban audience. The same songs played in both worlds. Dave Lewis was the king of Seattle R&B, an African American organist and bandleader based in the Central District who had been the house band leader at Birdland. Lewis had famously sneaked into Birdland as a teenager to catch Solomon Burke and James Brown. The Dynamics' 1962 cover of Lewis' "J.A.J." (Seafair-Bolo #45-730) received national distribution -- a genre bridge act, a predominantly white teen band channeling the CD's Black R&B tradition into the suburban circuit. This is not a simple appropriation narrative: the band played the music they loved, in the venues available to them, for the audiences who showed up. But the structural effect was that teen bands profited commercially from Black compositions performed for audiences who may never have heard the Central District originals. The distribution system that gave The Dynamics' cover national reach while Dave Lewis remained a local legend was structurally inequitable. The ethical complexity is characteristic of American popular music in the pre-civil-rights era and does not reduce to simple categories. The Dynamics' Dave Lewis covers were respectful and compositionally credited, but the system was not.

- **THE PAT O'DAY TEEN-DANCE CIRCUIT -- KJR Radio's 37% Audience Share, Spanish Castle Ballroom, Parker's on Aurora, Parallel Music Infrastructure:** The Dynamics' performing career was built on the teen-dance circuit created by Pat O'Day (1934-2020), KJR radio's legendary DJ and promoter. O'Day transformed Seattle's live music infrastructure in the late 1950s by renting the Spanish Castle Ballroom (on Highway 99 between Seattle and Tacoma) and organizing all-ages dances featuring local bands. KJR commanded a 37% audience share at its peak -- unprecedented for a single radio station. The teen-dance circuit was a parallel infrastructure to the Central District nightclub scene, separated by race and age but sharing musical repertoire. The Dynamics shared stages with The Beach Boys, Roy Orbison, Bobby Freeman, Jan & Dean, The Astronauts, and Terry Black, as well as Pacific Northwest bands: The Wailers, The Viceroys, The Sonics, The Frantics, The Casuals, and The Checkers (Coryell's pre-Dynamics band from Yakima). The Dynamics were the teen-dance circuit's premier R&B band, which means they were the primary vehicle through which Central District R&B reached suburban white teenagers. The band did not create the music, and the audience did not know its origins, but the musical energy traveled from one world to the other through The Dynamics' performances.

The taxonomy and the common name align perfectly with the visual reality.

- **THE SWALLOW TRIO'S MIDDLE MOVEMENT -- Maximum Within-Family Divergence, Every Axis Inverted from the Purple Martin:** The Violet-green Swallow is the Purple Martin's opposite on nearly every measurable axis while sharing the same family, bauplan, and lifestyle. Mass: 14 g vs. 45-60 g. Coloniality: solitary/paired vs. obligate colonial. Pitch center: 5-8 kHz peak vs. 2-6 kHz peak. Sound character: discrete liquid chips (staccato) vs. continuous liquid gurgle (legato). Nest dependency: natural cavities vs. managed nest boxes. Altitude range: valley to alpine vs. sea level only. Conservation need: none vs. critical management. Plumage: green above/white below (maximum contrast) vs. all dark (uniform). Known to public: poorly vs. moderately well. Same wing plan, same syrinx, same gape, same migration pattern -- different size, different pitch, different sociality, different everything else. The bauplan is the constant. Everything else varies. The Tree Swallow at degree 28 will occupy the middle ground, completing the trio with bookend extremes and a centrist in between.

- **THE TACHYCINETA CONSECUTIVE PAIR -- Degrees 27-28, Same-Genus Adjacency, Nested Taxonomic Hierarchy:** The Violet-green Swallow shares genus *Tachycineta* with the Tree Swallow (*T. bicolor*) at degree 28. This creates the engine's tightest same-genus adjacency. Parallels the Catharus pair at degrees 18-19 (Swainson's Thrush and Veery). Both pairs explore within-genus acoustic variation. But the Tachycineta pair is nested within the Hirundinidae family cluster (26-28), creating a taxonomic hierarchy expressed in sequential degrees: family (3 degrees) contains genus (2 degrees) contains species (1 degree each). The engine zooms to its finest taxonomic resolution and learns to express nested classification in sequential time.

- **THE LIQUID TWITTER -- Twenty-Eighth Acoustic Element (Part B), The Rain to the Martin's Stream, Staccato Liquid vs. Legato Liquid:** Rapid descending FM chips -- each chip a discrete acoustic event (15-30 ms, 1-2 kHz frequency drop) with onset, peak, and offset. Chip rate: 10-20 per second, faster than the martin's 8-15 per second, but where the martin's notes merge at high rates, the Violet-green's chips remain individually separable. Peak energy at 5.0-8.0 kHz (the martin peaked at 2.0-6.0 kHz). Call duration 0.5-2.0 seconds (the martin sustained 3-6 seconds). The twitter is organized as rapid similar chips with a terminal note differing in pitch or duration -- a burst, not a flow. Timbre: thin, high, liquid, crystalline. The stream/rain metaphor is precise: both are water, both are liquid, different time-domain structure. The engine now holds two "liquid" textures demonstrating that a single timbral adjective can produce different acoustic structures depending on temporal organization. The word for degree 27 is "articulation" -- the acoustic difference between degrees 26 and 27 is how notes are separated: not at all (martin, legato) vs. individually (Violet-green, staccato). The adjective needs a verb.

- **NATURAL CAVITY INDEPENDENCE -- Size as Starling-Proofing, No Nest Box Dependency, Self-Sufficient Conservation Status:** The Violet-green nests in natural cavities: woodpecker holes, natural tree hollows, cliff crevices, rock outcrops, and occasionally human structures. But it does not require human infrastructure. Its small body (14 g) fits cavity entrances too small for European Starlings (which need approximately 4.5 cm or larger). A Violet-green can squeeze through a hole that excludes its primary cavity competitor. This natural starling-proofing is the reason the Violet-green has never suffered the population collapse that devastated the Purple Martin. Size -- which seems like a disadvantage in a large-animal world -- is the key to housing security. One morphological variable (body mass) drives five ecological outcomes: pitch, prey size, cavity access, flight agility, and conservation status. The engine has never had a species where a single parameter explains this much. After the martin's story of managed dependence, the Violet-green shows the alternative: solve it with your body.

- **ALTITUDE RANGE -- Valley Floor to Alpine Treeline, Widest Altitudinal Range of Any Swallow:** The Violet-green breeds from sea level to over 2,500 meters (8,000+ feet) in the Cascades. The martin is a sea-level specialist. The Tree Swallow is a lowland to mid-elevation species. The Violet-green goes higher than either. In the high Cascades, Violet-green Swallows forage over alpine meadows, along cliff bands, and above timberline lakes -- habitats where neither the Purple Martin nor the Tree Swallow is regularly found. The cliff-nesting habit enables this altitudinal range: at high elevations where trees are sparse and snags are few, cliff crevices provide abundant nest sites.

- **AERIAL INSECTIVORY -- The Lightweight Fighter, Low Wing Loading, Tight Turning Radius, Small Prey Specialization:** The Violet-green is the fighter jet of the swallow trio where the martin is the heavy bomber. Low wing loading (small mass relative to wing area) gives exceptional maneuverability -- tight turns, abrupt direction changes, rapid altitude adjustments. The flight is restless, flickering, almost nervous compared to the martin's powerful sweeping cruises. Prey: small flying insects (midges, gnats, small flies, mosquitoes, winged aphids) -- too small for the martin to bother with. The two species partition the aerial insect resource by size, enabling coexistence. Resource partitioning within the same foraging guild.

- **THE WESTERN SPECIALTY PARADOX -- 20:1 More Common Than the Purple Martin, Far Less Known, Self-Sufficiency Is Less Newsworthy Than Dependency:** Approximate PNW breeding population: tens of thousands of pairs (vs. the martin's 800-1,000). Continental population approximately 7.7 million breeding individuals (Partners in Flight). More common, less known. Four reasons: habitat (cliff crevices vs. conspicuous harbors), conservation story (none vs. compelling near-extirpation recovery), sound (thin twitter easily overlooked vs. loud distinctive gurgle), size (small enough to be generic "little bird" vs. large enough to attract attention). The engine records the unrecognized majority: the quiet, common, self-sufficient species are the acoustic dark matter -- present everywhere, noticed almost nowhere.

- **PLUMAGE -- Iridescent Green and Violet, Structural Coloration, Maximum Dorso-Ventral Contrast, Species-Diagnostic White Patches:** Males: crown and nape iridescent violet-bronze, back and rump iridescent green, underparts clean white, distinctive white patches above and behind the eye and on the sides of the rump. The green iridescence in sunlight shifts through the same range as sunlit ocean water -- the color that earned the name *thalassina*. White underparts create the strongest visual signal in flight of any PNW swallow: bright white viewed from below against blue sky, visible to conspecifics during aerial social interactions. The white facial patches and rump patches are species-diagnostic -- no other PNW swallow has white extending above the eye. Structural coloration (thin-film interference in feather nanostructure) shared with the Purple Martin, but producing different colors: the martin is purple-black, the Violet-green is green-violet-white. Maximum plumage contrast vs. minimum plumage contrast across two species in the same family.

- **MIGRATION -- Neotropical Migrant, Spring Arrival Mid-March, Mixed-Species Swallow Migration Spectacle:** Breeds PNW April through August, winters Mexico and Central America. Spring arrival typically mid-March to early April in Puget Sound lowlands, with higher-elevation breeders arriving later (May-June at alpine sites). Migrates during the day in loose flocks mixed with other swallow species. Mixed-species swallow migration through mountain passes and river corridors is one of the great spectacles of PNW birding, and the Violet-green is typically the most numerous participant.

- **OPEN WATER ECOSYSTEM -- Air Column Over Water and Cliff Faces, Third Consecutive Open/Aerial Ecosystem:** The ecosystem progression: degree 25 (lawn and woodland, terrestrial surface), degree 26 (open sky near water, air column above water), degree 27 (open water, air column over water surface extending to cliff faces and montane meadows). The vegetation structure that defined every ecosystem through degree 24 has now been absent for three consecutive degrees. The engine is in its most open phase: water, rock, and air.

Self-sufficiency and foundation-laying are less newsworthy than dependency and headline acts.

## Lessons Learned

**On temporal regression:** The engine is not a timeline. It is a map. Maps do not progress chronologically -- they fill in territory. The first backward temporal step at degree 27 reveals that the engine's degree sequence is organized by genre/energy/neighborhood, not by era. As the soul/R&B zone develops, it naturally reaches back to the historical R&B that preceded soul as a distinct genre. The regression is a structural feature, not a bug. Expect the engine to continue oscillating in time as it fills in the complete picture.

**On significance through dispersal:** The Dynamics challenge the assumption that a band's significance equals its own output. Sometimes a band is a laboratory -- its significance is what the members become after they leave. The Yardbirds analogy holds: Clapton to Cream, Page to Led Zeppelin, Beck to Jeff Beck Group. Coryell to fusion, Doubleday to Electric Flag, Woods to Hendrix. The band itself is the shell. The dispersed musicians are the seeds. Track dispersal patterns at subsequent ensemble degrees.

**On within-family contrast:** Placing the Purple Martin and Violet-green Swallow in consecutive degrees creates the engine's clearest before/after comparison on the axis of body size. Same family, same lifestyle, opposite everything else. One morphological variable (mass) drives five ecological outcomes (pitch, prey, cavity access, flight agility, conservation status). The engine has never had such clean demonstration that size is the master variable. Use this lens for future within-family comparisons.

**On the genre bridge:** The Central District genre bridge is the mechanism by which Black R&B crossed the racial divide in 1960s Seattle. The same songs played in both worlds -- the Black adult nightclubs and the white teen-dance venues -- but through different performers for different audiences with different commercial outcomes. The Dynamics were the conduit. This is not simple appropriation and it is not simple tribute -- it is the complex structural reality of American popular music in a racially segregated society. The research documents the mechanism without reducing it to simple categories. Honest complexity is better than tidy narratives.

**On two liquid textures:** The engine's vocabulary refines. "Liquid" alone is insufficient to describe a sound. The martin's liquid is legato (stream). The Violet-green's liquid is staccato (rain). The timbral adjective needs a temporal verb. This principle extends: every timbral quality can be expressed in multiple temporal structures, and the temporal structure changes the perceptual meaning. The engine gains a vocabulary distinction it needed.

**On the unrecognized majority:** The Violet-green Swallow outnumbers the Purple Martin 20:1 in the PNW but is far less known. Self-sufficiency is less newsworthy than dependency. The quiet, common, independent thing lives in the shadow of the loud, rare, managed one. This applies to music history too: The Dynamics were foundational -- they gave Coryell and Doubleday and Woods their stage -- but the bands those musicians joined afterward (fusion groups, Electric Flag, Hendrix sessions) are the ones that received the recognition. The laboratory is forgotten; the results are famous. The engine documents what was there first.
