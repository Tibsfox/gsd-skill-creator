## Mission Overview

Stardust is the obs#2 sustainer of the COMET-AND-ASTEROID-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's fourth Discovery-program mission, a comet sample-return mission whose defining firsts are the first samples of a comet returned to Earth, the first capture-and-return of contemporary interstellar dust, and the fastest reentry of a human-made object into Earth's atmosphere. The spacecraft was built by Lockheed Martin Astronautics (Denver, CO) and the mission was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), with principal investigator Donald Brownlee of the University of Washington (Seattle, a PNW tie). Stardust launched 7 February 1999 on a Delta II (7426) from Cape Canaveral, collected contemporary interstellar dust on the opposite (B-side) of its collector during two cruise periods, flew past the small asteroid 5535 Annefrank on 2 November 2002 as an engineering rehearsal, and flew through the dust-and-gas coma of comet 81P/Wild 2 on 2 January 2004 (closest approach ~237 km; NASA also cites ~250 km), capturing thousands of cometary dust grains in silica aerogel behind a Whipple shield. The capture medium was the science: silica aerogel ("frozen smoke," one of the lowest-density solids) on a tennis-racket-shaped collector, used to decelerate and trap hypervelocity dust grains (~6.1 km/s relative at Wild 2) without vaporizing them. The story is the launch from Cape Canaveral, the cruise with the interstellar-dust collection and the 5535 Annefrank rehearsal flyby, the flight through Wild 2's coma with the aerogel collector out, the Sample Return Capsule released and reentering over Utah, and the parachute landing at the Utah Test and Training Range (15 January 2006) — the first cometary samples returned to Earth. The science is central: Wild 2 grains contained high-temperature minerals (refractory silicates and CAI-like material, e.g. the grain "Inti") that must have formed close to the early Sun — evidence of large-scale radial mixing in the early solar nebula; the interstellar-dust collector yielded ~7 candidate contemporary interstellar grains in later ISPE / Stardust@home analysis. 81P/Wild 2 is a Jupiter-family comet deflected inward by a close Jupiter encounter in 1974, a relatively "fresh" comet. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: Stardust is a COMET / small-body mission on the comet & asteroid axis (NOT a solar / heliophysics mission, and it re-stamps NO heliophysics-axis anchor); it was NASA's FOURTH Discovery mission (NOT the first — NEAR holds that); it returned the first COMET samples (earlier comet missions only flew PAST comets, returning no material); it was the first CAPTURE-AND-RETURN of interstellar dust (Ulysses and Galileo only DETECTED it in situ); it is NOT the first sample return from beyond the Moon (Genesis returned solar-wind samples in 2004, before Stardust's 2006 return). ★★ Stardust ended in 2011 (the extended Stardust-NExT mission flew by comet 9P/Tempel 1 on 15 February 2011 UTC and the spacecraft was decommissioned 24 March 2011), so the catalog frames the mission in the PAST tense throughout (Stardust flew, captured, returned, read). ★ The four dates are kept distinct: launch 7 February 1999, Wild 2 flyby 2 January 2004, sample return 15 January 2006, Tempel 1 (Stardust-NExT) 15 February 2011 UTC. ## Surprises

- **A solid one of the lowest density ever made caught dust travelling at hypervelocity intact.** Stardust trapped cometary dust grains arriving at ~6.1 km/s relative velocity in silica aerogel ("frozen smoke," one of the lowest-density solids), which decelerated each grain over a long stopping track and trapped it in a carrot-shaped cavity without vaporizing it — capturing thousands of grains intact for the labs to extract and read.
- **The first samples of a comet brought home.** Earlier comet missions had only flown PAST their targets (Giotto, ICE, the Vega/Sakigake/Suisei Halley armada), returning no material; Stardust flew through the coma of comet 81P/Wild 2 on 2 January 2004 and returned the first cometary samples to Earth when the Sample Return Capsule landed at the Utah Test and Training Range on 15 January 2006.
- **High-temperature minerals from a cold comet.** The Wild 2 grains contained high-temperature minerals (refractory silicates and CAI-like material, e.g. the grain "Inti") that must have formed close to the early Sun — surprising in a comet from the cold outer solar system — evidence of large-scale radial mixing in the early solar nebula; the B-side collector also yielded ~7 candidate contemporary interstellar grains in later ISPE / Stardust@home analysis.
- **The fastest human-made reentry, and a spacecraft re-targeted to a second comet.** The Sample Return Capsule reentered at ~12.9 km/s on 15 January 2006 — the fastest reentry of a human-made object into Earth's atmosphere (a record at the time, still standing as of the mid-2020s, ahead of OSIRIS-REx's ~12.4 km/s reentry in 2023) — and the still-healthy spacecraft was re-targeted as Stardust-NExT to comet 9P/Tempel 1, flying by on 15 February 2011 (UTC) and imaging the crater left by the 2005 Deep Impact experiment.
- **A solitary bee that gathers the finest pollen and seals it safely.** The Mason Bee (*Osmia lignaria*, the orchard / blue mason bee), a solitary native bee of the Pacific Northwest (the western form *O. lignaria propinqua*), gathers pollen and nectar and carries mud, packing each provision into a sealed brood cell in a narrow cavity and capping it with mud so the collected material overwinters safely until the next generation emerges (behavioral / ecological description only) — a mirror of the mission that gathered the finest grains of a comet and sealed them into a capsule to carry home.
- **A leafless orchid that lives by an unseen partner and casts dust-fine seeds.** Spotted Coralroot (*Corallorhiza maculata*), a leafless, chlorophyll-poor forest orchid of PNW conifer woods, lives by drawing nutrients from an unseen mycorrhizal fungal partner and scatters thousands of dust-fine, nearly weightless seeds on the air (ecological description only) — bound with Stardust's gathering and carrying of the finest motes home.

## Dedication

v1.272 Stardust is dedicated to the people across NASA, the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, and Lockheed Martin who built and flew the first mission to gather the grains of a comet and carry them safely home. The engineers in Denver who built a small spacecraft to fly through the dust of a comet behind a Whipple shield, and the flight team at JPL who guided it past the asteroid 5535 Annefrank and through the coma of comet 81P/Wild 2. The teams behind the silica aerogel, that "frozen smoke" that caught grains travelling at thousands of meters a second and held them whole. The navigators who flew the Sample Return Capsule back to a soft landing on the salt flats of Utah at the fastest reentry yet flown, and the lab scientists who opened the aerogel and read the tiny grains — the high-temperature minerals that formed near the early Sun, and the seven candidate motes of interstellar dust. The mission carries the work of Donald Brownlee, who set out to bring a comet to a microscope. They swept up a comet's finest dust and brought it home across the years. The first samples of a comet ever returned to Earth.
