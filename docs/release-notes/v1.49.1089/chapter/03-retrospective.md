## Surprises

- **A solid one of the lowest density ever made caught dust travelling at hypervelocity intact.** Stardust trapped cometary dust grains arriving at ~6.1 km/s relative velocity in silica aerogel ("frozen smoke," one of the lowest-density solids), which decelerated each grain over a long stopping track and trapped it in a carrot-shaped cavity without vaporizing it — capturing thousands of grains intact for the labs to extract and read.
- **The first samples of a comet brought home.** Earlier comet missions had only flown PAST their targets (Giotto, ICE, the Vega/Sakigake/Suisei Halley armada), returning no material; Stardust flew through the coma of comet 81P/Wild 2 on 2 January 2004 and returned the first cometary samples to Earth when the Sample Return Capsule landed at the Utah Test and Training Range on 15 January 2006.
- **High-temperature minerals from a cold comet.** The Wild 2 grains contained high-temperature minerals (refractory silicates and CAI-like material, e.g. the grain "Inti") that must have formed close to the early Sun — surprising in a comet from the cold outer solar system — evidence of large-scale radial mixing in the early solar nebula; the B-side collector also yielded ~7 candidate contemporary interstellar grains in later ISPE / Stardust@home analysis.
- **The fastest human-made reentry, and a spacecraft re-targeted to a second comet.** The Sample Return Capsule reentered at ~12.9 km/s on 15 January 2006 — the fastest reentry of a human-made object into Earth's atmosphere (a record at the time, still standing as of the mid-2020s, ahead of OSIRIS-REx's ~12.4 km/s reentry in 2023) — and the still-healthy spacecraft was re-targeted as Stardust-NExT to comet 9P/Tempel 1, flying by on 15 February 2011 (UTC) and imaging the crater left by the 2005 Deep Impact experiment.
- **A solitary bee that gathers the finest pollen and seals it safely.** The Mason Bee (*Osmia lignaria*, the orchard / blue mason bee), a solitary native bee of the Pacific Northwest (the western form *O. lignaria propinqua*), gathers pollen and nectar and carries mud, packing each provision into a sealed brood cell in a narrow cavity and capping it with mud so the collected material overwinters safely until the next generation emerges (behavioral / ecological description only) — a mirror of the mission that gathered the finest grains of a comet and sealed them into a capsule to carry home.
- **A leafless orchid that lives by an unseen partner and casts dust-fine seeds.** Spotted Coralroot (*Corallorhiza maculata*), a leafless, chlorophyll-poor forest orchid of PNW conifer woods, lives by drawing nutrients from an unseen mycorrhizal fungal partner and scatters thousands of dust-fine, nearly weightless seeds on the air (ecological description only) — bound with Stardust's gathering and carrying of the finest motes home.
