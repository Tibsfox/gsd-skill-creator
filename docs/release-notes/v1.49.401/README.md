## Summary

**Nancy Wilson** arrives at E=6 -- the guitarist and co-vocalist of Heart, the other half of the Wilson partnership that has sustained Pacific Northwest hard rock for more than fifty years, documented here as a solo artist at degree 260 following Ann Wilson's appearance at degree 259. Nancy Wilson's contribution to the Pacific Northwest music canon is distinct from her sister's: where Ann Wilson's defining instrument is her voice, Nancy Wilson's is her guitar -- acoustic and electric, classical-trained, technically accomplished in a way that is frequently obscured by the rock band context in which her work appears. Her solo records, her film scores, her acoustic work and her contributions to the Heart catalog all document an artist whose range extends well beyond the hard rock designation of her primary band. E=6 for the solo context reflects the broader and more varied register of her work outside of Heart's peak intensity.

Nancy Wilson's placement at degree 260, one degree after Ann Wilson, closes the Wilson Sisters Solo arc (degrees 259-260) and completes the engine's documentation of Heart as both a collective and as two distinct individual artists. The arc is the most explicit example in the engine's history of the decision to document a musical partnership at both the band level and the individual level: Heart at degrees 234-235, Ann Wilson at degree 259, Nancy Wilson at degree 260. The full picture requires all four entries.

On the soundscape side, **Lightning Strike Tree** arrives at E=9 -- the **NEW ABSOLUTE SPS MAXIMUM**, exceeding the engine's previous E=8 ceiling for the first time in the SPS track's history. A lightning strike on a tree -- a direct strike to a tall conifer, a Douglas fir or Ponderosa pine on a Cascade ridge -- produces one of the most violent acoustic events in the natural Pacific Northwest: the instantaneous explosive crack of the lightning channel itself, which at close range exceeds 130 decibels and is followed by the concussive report of the steam explosion inside the tree trunk as the lightning converts the tree's moisture to steam in microseconds. The tree may split, may fragment, may simply shed its outer bark in a spiral pattern, but the acoustic event is always the same: a sharp, explosive crack followed by the bass report of the structural failure. There is no buildup. There is no warning sound. There is silence, then the crack.

E=9 is the new absolute maximum for the SPS track. It has been assigned once. It documents a sound that cannot be anticipated, cannot be replicated, and occurs at the boundary of what the Pacific Northwest's electrical systems can produce. The storm arc closes at degree 260 with the single loudest event the engine has documented in the SPS track. The arc began at the coast with a wave on a beach and ends with a tree exploding on a ridge. The storm has been moving inland for five degrees. It arrived.
