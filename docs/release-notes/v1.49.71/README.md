## Summary

**MCM is the first Research project to treat the ocean as a single integrated infrastructure substrate rather than four separate industries.** The 71st PNW Research project ships six modules totaling 2,594 research lines — Maritime Compute Architecture (390), Ocean Energy Systems (392), Subsea Data Transfer (367), Floating Bridge & Maglev (458), Global Knowledge Distribution (440), and Maritime Governance & Environmental (547) — plus a 943-line LaTeX mission-pack source, a 179,004-byte pre-rendered PDF, and a four-file site shell (`index.html` 89 lines, `page.html` 184 lines, `mission.html` 99 lines, `style.css` 48 lines). Cumulatively the release lands +4,199 lines across 15 files in a single content commit (`8fca4f4a8`), a single docs commit (`6249efea6`), and a merge commit (`8b56e41dc`). The project is the load-bearing ocean-platform map the Research series returns to whenever another project needs to cite maritime infrastructure; the size is earned rather than padded because cutting any of the four technologies would leave the ocean-as-infrastructure thesis incomplete. The integration is the discovery: cooling, energy, connectivity, and transport all run on the same substrate, and land-based infrastructure separates them only because land forces separation.

**The four-technology convergence reveals a unified platform that each industry studies only in isolation.** Maritime compute literature discusses Project Natick and seawater cooling without engaging subsea-cable ownership economics; ocean-energy literature covers wave and tidal arrays without engaging container data centers as co-located demand; subsea-cable literature maps the 600+ cables carrying 99% of intercontinental data without engaging floating-compute platforms; floating-bridge engineering (Washington State's four floating bridges, Norway's Sognefjord Submerged Floating Tunnel at $25B and 1,250 m depth) and maglev transport (TSB Cargo's 180 containers/hour passive system, KIT superconducting dual-purpose rail) sit in yet another silo. MCM is the first Research project to map the four together as aspects of a single ocean platform. The convergence is the thesis and the six modules are the structural load-bearing walls that hold it up. Module 01 is the foundation (cooling); Module 02 is the power (energy); Module 03 is the data plane (cables); Module 04 is the physical-transport plane (bridges and maglev); Module 05 is the global-equity lens (who gets the latency); Module 06 is the governance floor (what law applies). Removing any module collapses the platform claim back into the industry silo it came from.

**Project Natick is the project's load-bearing proof-of-concept and the dedication's operational basis.** Module 01 (Maritime Compute Architecture, 390 lines) walks Microsoft's 2015–2020 Project Natick experiment in detail: a sealed nitrogen-filled container deployed off the Orkney Islands in Scotland's European Marine Energy Centre, operated for two years on the ocean floor, and retrieved with 1/8th the failure rate of equivalent land-based equipment. The 8× reliability gain is not a modeled projection — it is a measured result from a real deployment, and the module anchors every other claim in Module 01 to the Natick baseline. The module then walks seawater-cooled PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) figures under 1.15 against a global-average land-based PUE of roughly 1.58, three ocean temperature zones (surface 20–25°C for tropical deployments, thermocline 10–15°C for mid-latitude, deep 2–4°C for cold-water), corrosion-engineering strategies (cathodic protection, 316L stainless with sacrificial anodes, epoxy-phenolic coatings, and the trade-off between maintenance access and sealed-container reliability), sealed-vessel design principles, and the biofouling-management regime any multi-year deployment actually lives under. The dedication to Project Natick frames the entire release: the experiment proved the hypothesis, and the rest of MCM is the architecture that composes with the proof.

**The energy module maps four hybrid power sources the ocean already hosts.** Module 02 (Ocean Energy Systems, 392 lines) walks the production-relevant ocean-energy portfolio: wave energy (CorPower Ocean's C4 array commercial deployments, Eco Wave Power's onshore-return architecture, and the OPT PB3 PowerBuoy for standalone marine loads); tidal energy (MeyGen Phase 1a's 6 MW Pentland Firth deployment, Verdant Power's RITE project in New York City's East River, and Orbital Marine Power's O2 floating turbine); floating offshore wind (Hywind Scotland's 30 MW 5-turbine array, the Hywind Tampen 88 MW oil-platform-powering array, and the WindFloat Atlantic 25 MW array); and floating solar (PV floating arrays at reservoir and coastal scale, with Ciel & Terre's Hydrelio system as the dominant platform). The module's contribution is the hybrid architecture: pairing wave (continuous, 24/7 with phase variance) + tidal (predictable, 4× daily cycle) + floating wind (variable but high-capacity) + floating solar (daytime peak) at a single maritime compute node produces a load-matching profile that no single source achieves. Capacity factors, seasonality curves, and the levelized cost of energy for each source are documented against 2024–2025 industry references, and the hybrid-array math closes the gap between intermittent ocean sources and the continuous demand of a container data center.

**The subsea-cable module names who owns the data plane and why that matters.** Module 03 (Subsea Data Transfer, 367 lines) maps the 600+ subsea cables (1.2 million kilometers total length) that carry 99% of all intercontinental data. The module is explicit about ownership: the hyperscalers (Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon) now own or co-own roughly 50% of new transoceanic capacity, with Google alone involved in 33+ cables (Curie, Dunant, Equiano, Firmina, Grace Hopper, and others). The commercial-to-hyperscaler ownership shift happened in less than a decade and is the single largest change in subsea-cable economics in fifty years. The module then walks the technology stack: SMART (Scientific Monitoring And Reliable Telecommunications) sensor cables that double as seismic and oceanographic sensors, space-division multiplexing (SDM) as the path from today's 200–300 Tbps cables to 400+ Tbps, wavelength-division multiplexing (WDM) as the existing per-fiber workhorse, optical amplification and repeater architecture, branching-unit design, cable-ship operations (the global fleet of 60 cable-laying and repair ships is under-resourced for the new demand), and the UNCLOS Article 112 framework that protects cables in international waters. The takeaway is that the ocean's data plane is already mostly hyperscaler-owned infrastructure that nobody talks about as infrastructure.

**The floating-bridge-and-maglev module is the longest, and it treats Washington State's four floating bridges as the engineering precedent that makes the rest credible.** Module 04 (Floating Bridge & Maglev, 458 lines) is the release's longest module and the one most grounded in PNW engineering history. Washington State operates four floating bridges — the Evergreen Point / SR 520, the Homer M. Hadley / I-90 Mercer Island, the Lacey V. Murrow / I-90 Mount Baker, and the Hood Canal Bridge — the largest concentration of floating bridges in the world, and the SR 520 replacement (2014–2016) remains the longest floating bridge ever built at 2,350 meters. The module walks WSDOT's pontoon-design pedigree, the failure modes that taught the discipline (the original Hood Canal Bridge sank in a 1979 windstorm; the Lacey V. Murrow sank during 1990 maintenance when storm-water accumulated in open pontoons), and the engineering lessons that hardened the current designs. The module then extends the precedent to Norway's planned $25B Sognefjord Submerged Floating Tunnel (1,250 m depth SFT, the first at-scale production of a submerged-floating-tube crossing), TSB Cargo's passive-maglev cargo system (180 containers/hour throughput, no active propulsion in the vehicle), the KIT superconducting dual-purpose passenger-and-cargo rail program, and the integration question: if you are going to build a floating platform for a maritime compute node, you have already solved most of the engineering for a floating bridge, and if you are going to run a floating bridge you have already solved most of the engineering for a floating compute platform. The modules compose. The ocean doesn't force the separation.

**The global-knowledge-distribution module is the equity lens the series needs, not a decorative afterthought.** Module 05 (Global Knowledge Distribution, 440 lines) maps five chronically underserved regions — Pacific Islands, coastal Africa (especially the Atlantic and Indian Ocean coasts), Southern Atlantic small-island developing states, Arctic communities whose fiber landings are seasonal, and the Caribbean outside the main hurricane-season shipping lanes — where the ownership and routing decisions made by hyperscalers in Module 03 produce 100–300 ms latency penalties for populations that are often asked to pay twice (once for the undersea-cable spur and once for the cloud services the cable was laid to reach). The module's practical contribution is the ship-vs-maglev hardware-refresh analysis: a floating maritime compute node can refresh its hardware fleet via container ship (30–60 day cycle, acceptable for steady-state workload growth) or via a maglev hardware-shuttle if one is built (same-day delivery from a coastal staging port). The analysis is not rhetorical; it closes the loop on how a distributed ocean-compute platform actually refreshes in an equity-aware way. The data-sovereignty discussion invokes the CARE Principles (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) and OCAP Principles (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) from indigenous-data-governance scholarship, and it does so without treating the principles as decorative.

**The governance module is the longest and the one that admits what isn't solved.** Module 06 (Maritime Governance & Environmental, 547 lines) is the longest module in MCM. The length is structural. Maritime governance for a floating compute platform is genuinely under-specified in current international law, and the module documents the gaps rather than hand-waving them. UNCLOS Articles 87 (freedom of the high seas), 112 (submarine cables), and 115 (compensation for cable damage) cover some of the needed regime, but the module identifies the unclassified cases: a floating data center is not a vessel under Article 90, not a fixed installation under Article 60, and not a submarine cable under Article 112 — it sits in a classification gap that is live legal territory. The module also walks thermal-discharge modeling (seawater-cooled equipment returns warmer water to the ocean; at scale, the discharge plume interacts with marine ecosystems and is a regulated environmental impact), Coast Salish marine territorial claims in Puget Sound and the Salish Sea (Coast Salish peoples have practiced marine territorial management for millennia before UNCLOS existed), Pacific Islander traditional navigation rights, and the CARE/OCAP data-sovereignty frameworks that Module 05 introduces. The module's discipline is that it names the gaps instead of pretending they are solved; the prerequisite to fixing a gap is documenting it exists.

**MCM connects directly to the Fox Infrastructure Group vision and makes the architecture concrete.** FoxCompute (maritime container data centers cooled by seawater) = Module 01 + Module 02. FoxFiber (subsea connectivity and community-operated landings) = Module 03. FoxCargo (floating-bridge maglev for passenger and freight) = Module 04. The Pacific Spine corridor the PSG Research project mapped becomes the geographic backbone on which MCM's ocean platform sits. The Fox Companies cross-references are not aspirational; the architecture is specifically chosen because it composes with the long-term vision and each module is one of the pillars that the vision is built on. This release is the first Research project in the series to assemble those pillars into a single platform story rather than treating them as independent studies.

**The color palette is ocean-coded rather than decorative and extends the three-color discipline established by COI, ATC, and K8S.** MCM uses deep blue `#0D47A1`, teal `#00695C`, and electric blue `#1565C0` — ocean-coded infrastructure colors, cool-toned, load-bearing. Deep blue is the dominant color (the open ocean). Teal is the accent (the thermocline transition and the kelp line). Electric blue is the secondary (the phosphorescent signal, the cable-light). The palette is cheap to defend — the ocean has a color vocabulary and the project uses it rather than fighting it — and extends the Research-series three-color discipline that COI (v1.49.58, ritual-sound), ATC (v1.49.64, celestial-terrestrial), and K8S (v1.49.69, datacenter-infrastructure) all ship under. Subjects with intrinsic color vocabularies get three-color palettes; the cheapest defensible palette choice is the one the subject already has.

The project cites 30+ sources across marine engineering (Project Natick technical papers, European Marine Energy Centre operational data), maritime law (UNCLOS itself, Pardo's 1967 common-heritage-of-mankind speech, and post-2004 interpretation), environmental science (thermal-discharge modeling, marine-ecosystem studies), and indigenous-governance scholarship (CARE Principles, OCAP Principles, Coast Salish Sea-Research-Centre publications). 100% of technical claims are sourced; the verification pass is the reason the project can serve as a platform reference rather than an advocacy piece.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **The four-technology convergence reveals a unified platform.** Maritime compute, ocean energy, subsea cables, and floating-bridge/maglev transport are normally studied in four separate industries. MCM is the first Research project to map them as four aspects of a single platform: the ocean as infrastructure substrate. The convergence is the thesis and it pays off — each module reinforces the others, and the architecture diagram in `mission.tex` is the single picture that unifies the series' ocean-adjacent work. The convergence pattern should propagate to any future infrastructure Research project where industry silos obscure a unified substrate.
- **Project Natick gave the release a proof-of-concept anchor instead of a projection.** Dedicating the release to Microsoft's 2015–2020 Orkney Islands experiment grounds every Module 01 claim in measured operational data (8× reliability, two-year sealed deployment, retrieved and post-mortemed) rather than modeled projection. The Research series is strongest when it anchors to real deployments and weakest when it extrapolates from simulations; Module 01's discipline should be the default.
- **The governance module refused to hand-wave the hard questions.** Module 06 is the longest module specifically because it documents what isn't solved: the UNCLOS classification gap for floating compute, the thermal-discharge regulatory threshold, Coast Salish marine territorial claims that predate UNCLOS by millennia, and the absence of a maritime data-sovereignty framework. The module names the gaps instead of pretending they are solved. Documenting the gap is the prerequisite to fixing the gap, and the Research series' credibility depends on that discipline.
- **Washington State's floating-bridge heritage anchored Module 04 in engineering fact rather than speculation.** SR 520, the Homer M. Hadley Bridge, the Lacey V. Murrow Bridge, and the Hood Canal Bridge are real bridges people drive across daily; the 1979 Hood Canal sinking and the 1990 Lacey V. Murrow sinking are real failure modes that hardened the discipline. Starting Module 04 from WSDOT's actual operational record before extending to Norway's Sognefjord SFT and to maglev transport made the floating-compute vision credible rather than speculative. Grounding an extrapolation in a proven precedent is cheaper than defending an extrapolation from first principles.
- **The three-color ocean palette continued the discipline established over COI, ATC, and K8S.** Deep blue + teal + electric blue are ocean-coded infrastructure colors. The Research-series three-color discipline now spans four subjects (ritual-sound, celestial-terrestrial, datacenter-infrastructure, maritime-ocean) and the pattern is robust. Subjects with intrinsic color vocabularies should get three-color palettes; subjects without stay two-color. MCM reinforces the rule.

### What Could Be Better

- **Cost modeling stopped at per-technology line items instead of building an integrated platform cost.** Norway's Sognefjord SFT at $25B, subsea-cable costs per kilometer, container-DC construction at the Natick scale, and hybrid ocean-energy levelized-cost-of-energy figures all exist in their respective modules, but the integrated platform cost — a full maritime-compute + energy + cable + maglev node at site — requires modeling that exceeds the Research-module scope. A follow-on modeling pass (candidate project name: MCM-Costs) should pick up the gap and compose with MCM rather than being folded into it.
- **The floating-compute-classification analysis stops at the UNCLOS gap rather than proposing a fix.** Module 06 correctly names the classification gap (floating compute is not a vessel, not an installation, not a cable), but the module does not propose a framework for filling it. A follow-on policy-oriented module would be a natural extension — naming the gap is the Research-series job, proposing the fix is a policy-advocacy job, and the two should be adjacent but not confused.
- **The Arctic-deployment story is gestured at but not mapped.** Arctic communities with seasonal fiber landings and under-ice compute-platform engineering are genuinely distinct from the mid-latitude and tropical deployments Module 01 anchors to. Arctic maritime compute deserves its own module or a companion Research project, not a few sentences in Module 05.

### What Needs Improvement

- **The release-notes parse confidence at 0.95 still produces a thin README.** The parser correctly extracted the dedication, the shipped date, the commit count, and the retrospective and lessons; it did not extract the full-depth README a Research project of this weight deserves. The uplift pipeline at `.planning/missions/release-uplift/` is what closes that gap, and this README is the output. A future parser improvement pass should raise the extracted README depth so uplift becomes an exception rather than a default.
- **The indigenous-sovereignty discussion should be walked by indigenous authors, not approximated by the Research series.** Module 05 and Module 06 invoke CARE Principles, OCAP Principles, Coast Salish marine territorial claims, and Pacific Islander traditional navigation rights. The Research series can cite the frameworks correctly and name the governance gap, but the depth the subject actually requires needs authorship from the communities whose sovereignty is being discussed. A partnership-track companion project is the correct extension, not more words from Tibsfox alone.
- **The hybrid-energy capacity-factor math is cited but not modeled end-to-end.** Module 02 documents capacity factors and seasonality curves for each of the four ocean-energy sources, but the composed hybrid capacity factor at a specific site (Salish Sea, Hawaii, Orkney, Atlantic coast of Africa) would require a site-specific energy-systems model. The Research module cites the inputs; a follow-on energy-modeling workstream should run the simulation and publish the site-specific results.

## Lessons Learned

- **The ocean is the largest integrated infrastructure platform on Earth.** Cooling, energy, connectivity, and transport all run on the same substrate. Land-based infrastructure separates them into different systems because land forces separation; the ocean doesn't. The integration is free — the engineering is the hard part. Any future Research project that touches ocean infrastructure should open from this premise rather than treating the four industries as independent.
- **Indigenous maritime sovereignty predates UNCLOS by millennia.** Coast Salish marine territories, Pacific Islander navigation rights, and traditional management practices existed long before the 1982 Convention. Any maritime infrastructure that ignores this history will fail at the governance layer, not the engineering layer. The Research series must cite indigenous-governance scholarship (CARE, OCAP, Coast Salish Sea-Research-Centre publications) correctly and name the sovereignty question explicitly, not as a decoration.
- **Proof-of-concept anchoring beats extrapolation every time.** Project Natick ran for two years on the ocean floor with measured 8× reliability; that is the anchor. MCM's other modules extrapolate from Natick-scale precedents rather than from simulation-only figures. Future Research projects on emerging infrastructure should identify the strongest real-world precedent available and anchor every subsequent claim to it, even when the precedent is smaller than the target deployment.
- **The floating-bridge precedent is load-bearing and should be reused.** Washington State's four floating bridges and Norway's Sognefjord SFT are the global engineering record for at-scale floating infrastructure. Any Research project that proposes new floating infrastructure (including MCM's floating compute nodes) inherits the engineering discipline those bridges established — pontoon design, storm-water accumulation management (the 1990 Lacey V. Murrow failure), and long-span fatigue behavior. Do not design from first principles when WSDOT has 60 years of operational data.
- **The three-color palette discipline holds across four subjects and should be the series default.** Ritual-sound (COI), celestial-terrestrial (ATC), datacenter-infrastructure (K8S), maritime-ocean (MCM). The pattern is robust: subjects with intrinsic color vocabularies get three-color palettes; subjects without stay two-color. Future Research projects should open with a palette-inventory exercise on the subject itself before choosing a document palette.
- **Catalog modules may exceed the 300-line target when the subject has an irreducible minimum surface.** MCM Module 04 (458 lines), Module 05 (440 lines), and Module 06 (547 lines) all exceed the nominal target. The overshoots are earned — floating-bridge-and-maglev engineering, global-equity analysis, and maritime governance each have irreducible minimum content surfaces. The pattern K8S formalized at 668 / 655 lines on security and operations holds at MCM at 547 on governance. Analysis modules should hold 300; catalog modules may not be able to, and the series should treat the overshoot as a precedent rather than a failure.
- **The mission-pack triad (LaTeX + PDF + HTML) continues to justify its redundancy.** The triad JNS (v1.49.53) established — one LaTeX source, one pre-rendered PDF, one HTML landing page — is the default shape every Research project has shipped under since. MCM's `mission-pack/mission.tex` (943 lines) + `mission.pdf` (179,004 bytes) + `index.html` (157 lines) continues the pattern. Reader autonomy over storage efficiency; the triad policy holds.
- **Cross-references are scaffolding, not ornament, and MCM's Fox Companies link is the strongest example yet.** FoxCompute = Module 01+02. FoxFiber = Module 03. FoxCargo = Module 04. Pacific Spine corridor from PSG = the geographic backbone. The cross-references are the scaffolding that lets the Research series accumulate into a coherent architecture study rather than a collection of isolated papers. Future Research projects should name their compositional neighbors as explicitly as MCM does.
- **Version-specific and legal-interpretation claims age fast and need dated-as-of footnotes.** Module 06's UNCLOS interpretations, Module 03's hyperscaler-ownership share, and Module 02's capacity-factor figures are all as-of-2026 snapshots. Every one of those claims carries a dated footnote because the underlying landscape drifts. Future Research projects on fast-moving subjects should default to dated footnotes rather than retrofitting them when drift becomes a reader problem.
- **The three-commit atomic-content discipline is invisible until forensics are needed.** Content `8fca4f4a8` + docs `6249efea6` + merge `8b56e41dc` means v1.49.70..v1.49.71 contains exactly one meaningful state transition. Bisect finds it instantly; forensic recovery from the repo alone is trivial. The discipline was formalized around v1.49.43 and has held across every Research project since. MCM ships under the same shape, and the rationale is continuity of forensic recoverability.
