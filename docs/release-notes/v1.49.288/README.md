**Part B: The Black Swift — The Waterfall Ghost and the Last Nest Discovered.** The **Black Swift** (*Cypseloides niger*) enters the engine at degree 148 as the **second Apodidae** (completing a contrast pair with Vaux's Swift at 147) and as **the last North American bird species to have its nest discovered**. The species was known to science from the mid-1800s — a large, dark, powerful swift seen in flight in the western mountains — but where it went to breed remained one of the longest-running mysteries in North American ornithology. The first confirmed nest was found in **1901** by **A.G. Prill** behind a waterfall in Colorado, with **A.G. Vrooman** independently documenting a Santa Cruz County, California nest the same year. Both discoveries revealed the species' unusual habitat requirement: the nests are built as shallow moss-and-mud cups on damp cliff ledges behind or beside waterfalls, in locations so unexpected and inaccessible that they had evaded discovery for half a century of patient searching. The waterfall concealment, the high-elevation terrain (1000-3000 meters), and the minimal nest structure (often nearly invisible against damp rock) conspired to hide the species' breeding biology. Even today new Black Swift nesting sites continue to be discovered, and the species is so secretive that undiscovered colonies almost certainly exist in inaccessible mountain terrain across the western United States and Canada. The waterfall requirement is one of the most unusual habitat specifications of any North American bird: the nesting site must be consistently moist from spray or seeping cliff moisture, the falling water provides a natural predator barrier (few terrestrial predators can navigate the cascade), and the thermal mass of water moderates temperature extremes at the nest site. The adults navigate through or around the cascade to reach the nest, and the chick develops in a constantly damp environment.

## Part B: The Black Swift — The Waterfall Ghost, the Last Nest Discovered, and the Second Apodidae

- **The last North American bird nest discovered marks the most recent major breeding-biology discovery in North American ornithology.** The **Black Swift** (*Cypseloides niger*) nest discovery in **1901** by **A.G. Prill** behind a Colorado waterfall (with **A.G. Vrooman**'s independent Santa Cruz County, California discovery the same year) marks the most recent major breeding-biology discovery in North American ornithology. The species had been known to science from the mid-1800s — observed in flight as a large, dark swift in the western mountains — but its breeding biology remained unknown for half a century. The 1901 discoveries closed the gap between description and breeding-biology documentation and established that even in well-studied avifaunas there can be substantial gaps between species recognition and ecological knowledge. The Black Swift nest discovery is the last of any North American bird species, and its place as the final major breeding-biology discovery is a first-class structural milestone on the species axis.
- **The waterfall-nesting habitat is one of the most unusual habitat requirements of any North American bird.** The Black Swift's nesting sites are located behind or beside waterfalls on damp cliff faces at elevations between 1000 and 3000 meters, and the habitat requirement is one of the most unusual specifications of any North American bird. The waterfall provides three distinct functions: **moisture** for the moss-and-mud cup nest and for the developing chick, **predator barrier** through the physical concealment and acoustic masking of the falling water, and **thermal moderation** through the thermal mass of water that buffers temperature extremes at the nesting site. The adults must navigate through or around the cascade to reach the nest, the chick develops in a constantly damp environment, and the colonial-to-solitary nesting structure (5-20 nests per site) distinguishes the species from the communal thousands-strong chimney roosts of Vaux's Swift at degree 147.
- **The Black Swift is the largest swift in North America.** At **40-55 grams** and **18-20 centimeters** in length with a **38-44 centimeter wingspan**, the Black Swift is significantly larger than **Vaux's Swift** (17-20 g, 11-12 cm). The species' size distinguishes it from all other North American swifts and enables the powerful sustained high-altitude flight that defines its daily foraging ecology. Plumage is entirely dark blackish-brown, the darkest swift in North America. In flight, the Black Swift shows long broad wings, a forked tail, and powerful deliberate wingbeats that contrast with the flickering erratic flight of Vaux's Swift. The size-and-flight contrast with Vaux's provides the second axis of the Apodidae contrast pair at degrees 147-148, complementing the communal-versus-solitary ecology contrast.
- **The daily disappearance at high altitude makes the Black Swift a ghost in the sky for most observable hours.** The Black Swift's daily routine is one of the great mysteries of North American ornithology. Adults leave the nesting site at dawn, fly upward and outward quickly, and reach altitudes where they disappear from ground-based observation. For most of the day — the midday hours — Black Swifts are unobservable. They are presumed to be feeding at high altitude (1000-3000 meters above ground), but direct observation of their daytime activities was extremely limited until the 2010s when satellite tracking began to reveal daily movement patterns. The birds appear to fly enormous distances during the day, potentially hundreds of kilometers from the nest, foraging at high altitude and returning at dusk. Adults return to the nesting site at dusk — often the last birds visible in the fading light — and approach the waterfall at speed, entering the spray zone and landing directly on the nest ledge. The high-altitude daily disappearance register is a first-of-its-kind structural feature on the species axis.
- **The wintering-grounds mystery was solved only in the 2010s via satellite telemetry.** Black Swift wintering grounds were one of the longest-running mysteries in North American ornithology, unknown for over a century after the 1901 nest discovery. Recent **satellite telemetry** studies (**DeLuca, W.V. et al. 2015**) finally documented the birds' migration routes and wintering habitat. The species winters in **western Brazil and Bolivia** in the western Amazon basin, traveling over **6,000 kilometers** from Pacific Northwest breeding sites. The migration route and wintering habitat were confirmed only in the 2010s, marking the second great Black Swift mystery to be solved late (after the 1901 nest discovery). The two mysteries together — where it nests, where it goes in winter — give the species a unique historical position as a bird whose breeding biology was discovered last among North American species and whose wintering grounds were confirmed latest among well-studied migratory species.
- **The second Apodidae completes the swift contrast pair at matched E=4 with opposite ecology.** The Apodidae family's two engine entries — Vaux's Swift at degree 147 and Black Swift at degree 148 — register at matched E=4 but represent radically opposite ecological strategies. **Vaux's Swift**: small (17-20 g), chimney-roosting (human-built substrate), communal (up to 35,000 in a single evening vortex), public spectacle, visible from urban vantage points, acoustically loud through collective high-pitched chipping amplification. **Black Swift**: large (40-55 g, largest swift in NA), waterfall-cliff-nesting (natural substrate), solitary-to-colonial (5-20 nests), hidden mystery, visible only to those who climb to the waterfall, acoustically soft low chipping heard against the waterfall white-noise backdrop with environmental amplification from cliff-alcove acoustics. The two species match in E=4 but achieve the same energy rating through fundamentally different acoustic mechanisms, and the contrast pair demonstrates that the engine can document matched-energy pairs with opposite ecological strategies at consecutive degrees.
- **The family-progression arc 135-148 completes the ecological gradient from perch predators to pure aerial specialists.** Degree 148 completes a **fourteen-degree family-progression arc** that began at **degree 135** with the Strigidae owls. The 10+2+2 structure of Strigidae (10 degrees, 135-144) → Caprimulgidae (2 degrees, 145-146) → Apodidae (2 degrees, 147-148) documents a complete ecological gradient from perch-predator vertebrate hunters through ground-and-aerial insectivores to pure-aerial-specialist insectivores. The progression's structural elegance is reinforced by the matched-pair interior: the two Caprimulgidae form a ground-versus-aerial contrast pair (Common Poorwill sit-and-hawk vs. Common Nighthawk aerial-booming), and the two Apodidae form a communal-versus-solitary contrast pair (Vaux's chimney-vortex vs. Black Swift waterfall-cliff). The fourteen-degree arc is the most sustained ecological progression documented on the species axis and establishes that the engine can construct coherent multi-family progressions across adjacent degrees.
- **The low chipping colony calls create an acoustic environment unique in North American ornithology.** The Black Swift's acoustic contribution to the engine is **environmental acoustics** — the sound of a bird heard within the dramatic soundscape of a waterfall cliff. Individual calls are low soft chip notes ("chip" or "tchip") at approximately 3000-5000 Hz, lower-pitched and softer than Vaux's Swift's high chipping. Each chip lasts 0.03-0.06 seconds. At nesting colonies multiple birds chip simultaneously creating a soft chorus audible against the waterfall roar. The waterfall itself is a constant white-noise backdrop that partially masks the swift's chipping, and hearing the calls requires standing near the colony and listening through the water sound. The concave cliff-alcove surfaces where nests are placed act as natural acoustic reflectors, projecting the calls outward and amplifying the colony chorus. Most calling occurs during dawn departure and dusk return — the two brief windows when adults are at the colony — and during midday the colony is silent. The acoustic environment is unique among North American bird species.
- **The continuing discovery of new Black Swift nesting sites suggests additional undiscovered colonies.** New Black Swift nesting sites continue to be discovered in the twenty-first century, and the species is so secretive and its nesting habitat so specific that undiscovered colonies almost certainly exist in inaccessible mountain terrain across the western United States and Canada. The ongoing discovery is itself first-class documentation content: the bird's breeding biology remains incompletely mapped over a century after the 1901 discovery, and the engine's placement of the species at degree 148 acknowledges that the Black Swift's story is not yet closed. Future nest discoveries may expand the known range, reveal new colony structures, or document previously unrecognized habitat variations, and the species' ongoing mystery-content provides a living example of how breeding-biology documentation is a process rather than a fixed state.
