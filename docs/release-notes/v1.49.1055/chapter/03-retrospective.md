## What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived mission imagery.** The Viking shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading an actual Viking lander panorama or orbiter mosaic. A future revision could render a real Viking surface panorama or a Viking-orbiter global mosaic (publicly available from the Planetary Data System) for a higher-fidelity view.
- **The Viking diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the lander on its three legs with the surface-sampler arm and the high-gain antenna, and of the orbiter with its solar arrays.
- **The biology experiment is described but not simulated.** The three-experiment biology package and the GCMS detection threshold are described but not rendered as an interactive experiment figure; a future ship could integrate an archived Labeled Release time series and a GCMS spectrum into the shader's life-search mode for a historically accurate view.
- **The entry-descent-landing sequence is depicted rather than computed.** The aeroshell-parachute-terminal-descent sequence is depicted in the shader but not rendered from the real EDL velocity/altitude profile; a future ship could drive the descent mode from the archived Viking entry trajectory for a quantitatively faithful animation.

## Surprises

- **A 1975 mission made the first fully successful, sustained landing on another planet — and far outlived its design.** Viking's landers were built for a ~90-sol design life, yet the Viking 1 lander operated until 13 November 1982 (about six years), returning the first sustained, multi-year surface science from Mars; the orbiters together returned 52,663 images mapping about 97 percent of the planet.
- **The first in-situ search for life returned a chemically active but biologically inconclusive result — a founding astrobiology surprise, not a failure.** The biology package saw chemical activity (including a positive-looking Labeled Release signal), but no unambiguous detection of life, while the GCMS detected no organics at its 1976 threshold. The result stands as the founding result of in-situ astrobiology — neither a detection of life nor a disproof of it.
- **The organics null turned out to be an instrument-threshold story, not a verdict on Mars.** Decades later, the Phoenix lander's 2008 discovery of perchlorates (Hecht et al.) explained how organics could have been destroyed during sample pyrolysis, and Curiosity/SAM later detected indigenous Martian organics — so the Viking GCMS null is read today as "no organics at its 1976 threshold," not "no organics on Mars."
- **The orbiters were as transformative as the landers.** Beyond certifying the landing sites and relaying lander data, the Viking orbiters mapped about 97 percent of Mars (some areas to ~8 m), mapped atmospheric water vapor and surface temperature, and imaged Phobos and Deimos at close range — the first fully successful Mars orbiter+lander system, with the orbiter half delivering a near-global picture of the planet.
- **A vertebrate persists at the upper limit of where life holds on, in the most Mars-like terrestrial terrain.** The Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch (*Leucosticte tephrocotis*) breeds in the Cascades above ~6,000 feet and the Olympics — possibly the highest-altitude breeder of any North American bird — foraging on wind-scoured talus and the bare ground at melting snowfield edges and nesting in rock crevices, a behavioral analogue of life at the barren limit that Viking went to Mars to probe (the finch is never said to "see" or "live on" Mars).
- **A lichen colonizes bare exposed stone where almost nothing else grows.** Rock Tripe (*Umbilicaria* sp.), a foliose fungus–alga symbiosis anchored to bare siliceous rock by a single central holdfast and highly desiccation-, cold-, and UV-tolerant, lives at the mineral limit of the Pacific Northwest's barren rock — the same coldest, driest, most mineral habitat that makes lichens AS A CLADE (Xanthoria, Rhizocarpon, Circinaria, Buellia, Pleopsidium) the recognized Mars-analog organisms flown in EXPOSE/BIOMEX, mirroring Viking's first reach onto the cold, dry, rusted surface of Mars.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A rotation replaces ALL of the predecessor's topical vocabulary.** Viking shares almost no subject vocabulary with the X-ray-astronomy predecessor; the rewrite removed the entire X-ray axis vocabulary (telescopes, detectors, energy bands, the focusing-imaging branch) and replaced it with Mars-exploration vocabulary (Mars, the red planet, regolith, Chryse/Utopia Planitia, the soft landing, the aeroshell/parachute/terminal-descent engines, in situ, astrobiology, habitability), keeping only neutral/shared spaceflight vocabulary and a single deliberate nav-prev lineage note to Suzaku.
2. **The four "first" traps must be stated in every claim of primacy.** The first SOFT landing on Mars was Mars 3 (1971); the first TRANSMISSION from the surface was Mars 3; the first Mars ORBITER was Mariner 9 (1971); Mars 2/3 were ALSO orbiter+lander systems. Viking owns the first FULLY SUCCESSFUL, sustained versions of the landing, the clear surface images, and the orbiter+lander system — every primacy claim carries the qualifier.
3. **The life-search result is scoped, not summarized.** The GCMS detected no organics at its 1976 threshold (a null later explained by perchlorate-driven pyrolysis destruction; Phoenix 2008; Curiosity/SAM indigenous organics) — never "no organics on Mars." The biology result is chemically active but biologically inconclusive — never "found life," never "ruled out life," never a "failure." It is a founding astrobiology result and a scientific surprise.
4. **NASA / U.S.-mission framing is an accuracy requirement here, not a qualifier.** Viking was a wholly NASA / United States program (NASA Langley + JPL + Martin Marietta) with no foreign partner — the foreign-partner caveats belonged to the X-ray axis the catalog rotates away from, and attaching one to Viking would be an error.
5. **Lander power and landing system are precise facts, not generic ones.** The landers were RTG-powered (two SNAP-19 plutonium-238 generators each), the orbiters solar-powered, and the landing was propulsive on three legs (aeroshell → parachute → terminal-descent engines) — NOT airbags, which belong to Pathfinder and the Mars Exploration Rovers.
6. **The paired lichen is scoped honestly to the clade.** The lichens actually flown in orbital exposure (EXPOSE-E/R2) and Mars-simulation chambers (BIOMEX) were *Xanthoria, Rhizocarpon, Circinaria, Buellia,* and *Pleopsidium* — not *Umbilicaria*. Rock Tripe (*Umbilicaria*) is a same-form Pacific Northwest bare-rock extremophile cited alongside the flown lichens as context about the clade, never claimed to have flown or been Mars-chamber-tested.
7. **The orbiter and lander are two halves of one architecture.** Viking is not "an orbiter" or "a lander" but a fully integrated orbiter+lander system: the orbiter certified the landing site, relayed lander data, and mapped the planet (52,663 images, ~97% of Mars, Phobos/Deimos), while the lander read the surface, soil, atmosphere, and possible biology in situ. The architecture is the unit of analysis.
8. **A rotation references its predecessor exactly once, deliberately.** The only correct predecessor reference is a single nav-prev lineage note to v1.237 Suzaku (the X-ray axis the catalog rotates away from), plus a lineage reference to Mariner 4 (1.36, the first Mars flyby). Everything else from the X-ray axis is a leak.
9. **Memorial-station naming is a distinct-pair fact.** The Viking 1 lander is the Thomas A. Mutch Memorial Station; the Viking 2 lander is the Gerald A. Soffen Memorial Station. The two are not interchangeable.
10. **Clone source is Suzaku (a JAXA/ISAS X-ray observatory, five XRT + XIS/HXD/XRS, M-V, 2005); Viking is a NASA Mars landing program (two orbiter–lander spacecraft, Titan IIIE–Centaur, 1975–1976).** The most structural replacement in the rewrite is removing ALL "Suzaku / ASTRO-EII," "the Vermilion Bird of the South," "JAXA / ISAS," "M-V-6 / Uchinoura," "XIS / HXD / XRS / XRT," "thin-foil X-Ray Telescopes," "175 foil shells," "micro-calorimeter," "60 mK," "lowest-background broadband sensitivity," "ICM to the virial radius," "X-ray CCDs," "Wolter Type-I," "grazing incidence," "keV bands," "Kokubun/Kelley/George 2007–2009," and "the Western Tanager + Western Red Columbine pairing," and replacing with Viking's two orbiter–lander spacecraft, NASA Langley / JPL / Martin Marietta, the Titan IIIE–Centaur launch, the Chryse/Utopia landings, the RTG-powered propulsive landing, the lander surface laboratory and the orbiter mapper/relay, the first clear surface images, the first in-situ search for life, the 52,663-image ~97% map, COSPAR 1975-075A/C and 1975-083A/C, and the Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch + Rock Tripe pairing.
