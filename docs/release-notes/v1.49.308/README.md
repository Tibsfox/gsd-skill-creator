**The Winnowing Mechanism — Tail Feathers as Instrument.** The **winnowing** is one of the most studied mechanical sounds in ornithology, and the mechanism is anatomically and experimentally precise. During display, the male Wilson's Snipe flies in large circles at **60-100 meters altitude**, then enters a steep dive (approximately **45-degree angle**) with the tail spread. The **outer tail feathers** — specifically **rectrices 7 and 8** on each side, modified with stiff narrow vanes — extend beyond the rest of the tail, projecting into the airstream. As the bird dives, air rushes past the extended outer tail feathers, and the aerodynamic forces cause these feathers to vibrate rapidly. The vibration frequency is approximately **25 Hz** — at the threshold of human pitch perception — and the resulting sound is a pulsating, wavering, tremolo hum often transcribed as "whoo-whoo-whoo-whoo." The sound lasts **1-2 seconds per dive**, as the bird pulls out of the dive and begins to climb again for the next circuit. Multiple dives produce a rhythmic pattern: dive-winnow, climb, circle, dive-winnow. Display sessions extend for **30+ minutes** of repeated circuit-dive-winnow cycles. Critically, the winnowing is **NOT produced by the syrinx** (vocal organ) — it is produced entirely by the physical structure of the tail feathers interacting with airflow. The mechanical nature was demonstrated experimentally in **wind tunnel studies** that attached snipe tail feathers to a small rotating arm and recreated the winnowing sound in controlled airflow conditions. The feathers alone, exposed to appropriate airflow, produce the characteristic tremolo. The bird provides the airspeed; the feathers provide the sound. The engine's documentation at degree 168 preserves the wind-tunnel-experimental proof as a first-class empirical anchor for the mechanical-sound claim, and the 5TH MECHANICAL SOUND designation is established rigorously rather than by ornithological consensus alone.

**The Crepuscular Display Window — Twilight as Context.** The Wilson's Snipe winnowing occurs in a specific temporal and behavioral context that amplifies its acoustic impact beyond its physical volume. **Timing** is primarily crepuscular — the display begins in late afternoon and continues through dusk, and resumes before dawn, with some winnowing in moonlit nighttime conditions. **The "Snipe Hunt" — Real Bird, Real Metaphor.** The Wilson's Snipe is the cultural origin of the **"snipe hunt"** — the classic North American fool's-errand prank in which pranksters send an unsuspecting target to "hunt" for snipes, usually at night, in a marsh, with a bag and a flashlight. The target wanders in the marsh finding nothing. The joke is that snipes are supposedly impossible to find. The irony that makes the metaphor powerful is that Wilson's Snipes are absolutely real — they are common in wet meadows across North America. But they are extraordinarily difficult to see. The **cryptic plumage** (brown, striped, matching dead vegetation) and the habit of freezing in place until nearly stepped on make the snipe essentially invisible on the ground. The bird is common, real, and present — but you will almost never see one until it **flushes explosively** from underfoot, launching in a rapid zigzagging flight with a sharp nasal **"SCAIP!"** vocal call. The flush experience — of a bird being immediately present but totally invisible — is what made "snipe hunt" a metaphor for futility. The winnowing completes the mystery: the sound fills the twilight sky but the source is a small bird flying high in dim light, nearly impossible to see. The sound seems to come from everywhere and nowhere. If you cannot see the bird on the ground (cryptic plumage) and cannot see it in the sky (small, high, twilight), the "snipe" becomes an invisible, perhaps imaginary creature. The engine's documentation at degree 168 preserves the "snipe hunt" metaphor as a first-class cultural-context register grounded in the real bird's extraordinary cryptic invisibility, and the cultural idiom enters the engine's cultural-context vocabulary alongside earlier cultural framings of species (such as the Sandhill Crane's Japanese tancho symbolism at degree 165).

The **cryptic plumage** (brown, striped, matching dead vegetation) and the habit of freezing in place until nearly stepped on make the snipe essentially invisible on the ground. The winnowing completes the mystery: the aerial sound fills the sky from a source the listener cannot see, compounding the on-ground invisibility with the aerial invisibility and producing a creature that seems to exist everywhere and nowhere. The engine's documentation at degree 168 preserves the "snipe hunt" as a first-class cultural-context register.
- **The ecological setting is wet meadows with soft muddy substrate supporting cryptic concealment and probing foraging.** The Wilson's Snipe inhabits **wet meadows**, **boggy fields**, and **marshy areas** with soft muddy substrate for probing-bill foraging. The habitat provides both the dense sedge and grass cover for cryptic concealment and the open airspace above for aerial winnowing circuits. Across North America, Wilson's Snipe is a widespread resident and migrant breeding from southern Alaska and Canada south to the northern United States, and wintering across the southern United States, Mexico, and Central America. In the PNW, Wilson's Snipe is a resident breeder in wet-meadow habitats year-round, with some local migration following seasonal wetland conditions. The bill morphology — extremely long and flexible at the tip with sensory nerve endings — represents one of the most specialized probing bills in the Scolopacidae family, and the probing feeding ecology detects invertebrate prey by touch in soft mud. The engine's documentation at degree 168 preserves the wet-meadow ecology as a first-class ecological register and anchors the Scolopacidae family entry in the PNW shorebird fauna.
- **The flush-call "SCAIP!" and perched "chip-a chip-a" vocalizations exist but are acoustically secondary to the winnowing.** The Wilson's Snipe has vocal calls in addition to the mechanical winnowing. The **flush call** is a sharp nasal **"SCAIP!"** given when the bird is flushed from the ground, typically delivered once or twice during the explosive zigzag flight away from the disturbance.
