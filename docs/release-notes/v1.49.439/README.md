- ***Accipiter gentilis* Nest-Defense Behavior.** The Northern Goshawk's nest-defense behavior is documented extensively in the raptor-behavioral literature. **Accipiter gentilis** routinely strikes humans, horses, and dogs who approach nest sites during the breeding season (approximately April through July in the Pacific Northwest), with the nest-defense strike delivered at speeds approaching 40-50 mph directly to the head and upper body of the intruder. The strikes are not territorial displays — they are physical attacks that can produce serious injury. Field biologists working on Goshawk nest surveys wear hardhats and protective gear during nest-proximity visits, and multiple first-person accounts from Pacific Northwest field workers document riders losing their hats and researchers receiving scalp lacerations from Goshawk strikes. The acoustic character is not louder than the single alarm per pulse — the per-pulse amplitude is similar — but the sustained duration and the repetition density produce a cumulative acoustic pressure that is qualitatively different from the single call. The Pacific Northwest field recordings of Goshawk full alarm are held by the **Macaulay Library** at the **Cornell Lab of Ornithology**, and the acoustic documentation at degree 298 draws on these recordings for the E=7 placement. The *Accipiter* genus is characterized by short rounded wings adapted for rapid acceleration and turning in dense forest, long tails for precise steering, and predatory behavior focused on other birds as primary prey (with mammals as secondary prey). The Goshawk sits at the top of the Accipiter hierarchy by size and aggression. In the Pacific Northwest raptor hierarchy, the Goshawk is one of the forest-interior predators (alongside the **Great Horned Owl** *Bubo virginianus* and the **Barred Owl** *Strix varia*), and the species' ecological role as a forest-interior apex predator makes it structurally important for understanding Pacific Northwest forest ecology at the predator-level resolution.
