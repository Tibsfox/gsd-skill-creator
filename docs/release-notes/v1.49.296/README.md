**The Ventriloquial Low-Frequency Booming — How It Works.** The **deep muffled booming** of the Sooty Grouse is produced by the inflation of **cervical air sacs** — the same general mechanism as the Blue Grouse (154) — but with distinctive acoustic properties that make the display more muffled, darker, and more ventriloquial in character. The male Sooty Grouse calls from **elevated perches in dense conifers**, often **30-60 feet above the forest floor**, where the combination of acoustic height and dense conifer canopy create the conditions for low-frequency sound to propagate in all directions simultaneously. The fundamental frequency of the booming is **below 200 Hz**, and at those frequencies the sound waves have wavelengths that exceed the diameter of most tree trunks. The physical consequence is that the sound waves do not reflect cleanly off tree trunks as they would at higher frequencies but instead **diffract** around the trunks via wave bending. Diffraction is not a trick of perception but a physical consequence of the sound's wavelength: when a sound wave's wavelength is larger than an obstacle, the wave bends around the obstacle rather than being blocked or reflected. For the Sooty Grouse booming, the wavelengths (in the range of 1.7 meters at 200 Hz and larger at lower frequencies) exceed the trunk diameters of most Pacific Northwest conifers, and the sound waves bend around trunks, branches, and understory structure rather than propagating in straight lines. The auditory consequence is the **ventriloquial quality** — the sound appears to come from everywhere at once, the source direction cannot be determined by ear, and birders routinely look in the wrong direction, the wrong tree, even the wrong hillside when searching for a calling Sooty Grouse. The ventriloquial quality is a masterclass in how physics shapes communication in dense forest environments, and the engine's documentation at degree 156 preserves the acoustic-diffraction phenomenon as a first-class physical-physics register in the species vocabulary.

## Part B: Sooty Grouse — Fourth Phasianidae, Closes the Phasianidae Quartet, Ventriloquial Low-Frequency Booming via Cervical Air Sac Inflation

- **The Sooty Grouse closes the Phasianidae quartet as the fourth family entry.** The **Sooty Grouse** (*Dendragapus fuliginosus*) enters the engine at degree 156 as the **fourth Phasianidae** and closes the family quartet that opened at degree 153 with **Wild Turkey**. The four-degree quartet — Wild Turkey (153, vocal gobble, E=6), Blue Grouse / Dusky Grouse (154, subsonic pneumatic boom, E=5), Ruffed Grouse (155, accelerating mechanical wing-drum, E=5), and Sooty Grouse (156, ventriloquial muffled boom, E=5) — completes a family run that spans the full acoustic range of gamebirds across three sound production mechanisms (vocal, pneumatic, mechanical) and four species. The closing-fourth-entry structure consolidates the family as a structural block and establishes the Phasianidae quartet as the second family quartet in the engine after Columbidae (149-152).
- **The second pneumatic booming display differs from Blue Grouse in muffled ventriloquial character.** The Sooty Grouse booming is produced by **cervical air sac inflation** — the same general mechanism as the Blue Grouse (154) — but with distinctive acoustic properties that make the display darker and more muffled in character. The fundamental frequency sits below 200 Hz, matching the Blue Grouse range, but the combination of acoustic height (30-60 feet above forest floor) and dense Pacific-coastal conifer canopy structure produces a sound quality that is subjectively muffled and spatially diffuse rather than directionally anchored. The distinction is acoustic — two species using the same mechanism in different habitats produce structurally distinct acoustic outputs, and the engine's documentation at degree 156 preserves the acoustic distinction as a first-class register in the species vocabulary.
- **Ventriloquial acoustic-diffraction physics enters the engine as a first-class register.** The **ventriloquial quality** of the Sooty Grouse booming is not a trick of perception but a physical consequence of sound wavelength exceeding the size of forest obstacles. At fundamental frequencies below 200 Hz, the sound waves have wavelengths that exceed the diameter of most Pacific Northwest conifer trunks. The physical consequence is that sound waves do not reflect cleanly off tree trunks as they would at higher frequencies but instead **diffract** around the trunks via wave bending. The auditory consequence is that the sound appears to come from everywhere at once, the source direction cannot be determined by ear, and birders searching for a calling Sooty Grouse routinely look in the wrong direction, the wrong tree, even the wrong hillside. The acoustic-diffraction phenomenon enters the engine's documentation as a first-class physical-physics register and establishes the vocabulary for future species whose acoustic identity depends on wavelength-obstacle-ratio physics.
- **The elevated-perch display strategy shapes the acoustic projection.** The male Sooty Grouse calls from **elevated perches in dense conifers**, often **30-60 feet above the forest floor**, where the combination of acoustic height and dense conifer canopy create the conditions for the ventriloquial low-frequency booming to propagate in all directions simultaneously. The elevated-perch strategy differs from the ground-based Ruffed Grouse drumming log (155) and from the ground-based Blue Grouse display (154), and the height-above-forest-floor acoustic framing introduces the third vertical register in the Phasianidae quartet (Wild Turkey ground-level vocal 153, Blue Grouse ground-level pneumatic 154, Ruffed Grouse log-level mechanical 155, Sooty Grouse canopy-level pneumatic 156). The four-entry vertical-register progression establishes the quartet's spatial diversity as a structural feature of the family's acoustic display ecology.
- **Taxonomic split completion documents the 2006 AOU reclassification across two degrees.** The Sooty Grouse's placement at degree 156 completes the taxonomic split between **Dusky Grouse** (at degree 154 with the legacy "Blue Grouse" alias) and **Sooty Grouse** (at degree 156) that the 2006 American Ornithological Union reclassification made possible. Prior to 2006, the two species were treated as a single "Blue Grouse" species, and the split was based on genetic, morphological, and vocalization evidence that showed the interior-mountain and Pacific-coastal populations as distinct species. The engine's documentation preserves both the pre-2006 taxonomic history (via the Blue Grouse legacy alias at 154) and the current 2006-onward classifications (Dusky Grouse 154, Sooty Grouse 156), creating a documented taxonomic history within the species vocabulary.

The Dusky Grouse, by contrast, inhabits the drier interior-mountain forests east of the Cascade crest. The Pacific-coastal-versus-interior-mountain distinction corresponds to the 2006 taxonomic split and is structural to each species' ecology — the acoustic, dietary, and behavioral differences between Dusky Grouse and Sooty Grouse reflect the different forest ecosystems they inhabit, and the engine's documentation preserves the habitat-based distinction alongside the taxonomic split.
