## Summary

**Love Battery** arrives at E=5 -- the band that defined the psychedelic underbelly of Seattle's grunge era, and the first U-District band to enter the engine. While the rest of the world was thrashing and screaming, Love Battery was dissolving into distortion and reverb, making records that owed more to My Bloody Valentine and the Jesus and Mary Chain than to Black Sabbath or Neil Young. Ron Nine (guitar/vocals), Kevin Whitworth (guitar), Tommy Simpson (bass), and Jason Finn (drums) emerged from the University District -- the neighborhood adjacent to the UW campus that served as Seattle's off-ramp between the student world and the music world -- and recorded a catalog on Sub Pop that prioritized texture and wash over aggression. *Between the Eyes* (1992) and *Dayglo* (1994) are records full of slow, saturated guitar clouds and vocals buried like something half-remembered -- psychedelic rock at the exact moment when the genre was being reinvented by My Bloody Valentine's shoegaze revolution, and Love Battery was paying close attention. At E=5, the band occupies the warm middle register: present but not overwhelming, exploratory without being alienating. The U-District entry matters beyond geography -- it introduces a different kind of Seattle, the one that was always there beneath the flannel and the angst, the one that chose dissolution over disturbance. On the species side, the **Bald Eagle's call** registers at E=5, and with this entry the **FIRST ACCIPITRIDAE** -- the family that includes eagles, hawks, and kites -- enters the SPS engine. The Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*) is the most recognizable bird in North America, the national symbol, the apex of the raptor world in the public imagination -- but here is what the engine records at degree 221: the Bald Eagle's actual call is a thin, reedy, almost apologetic *kee-kee-kee*, a sound that would embarrass its cinematic reputation. The powerful, descending scream you hear whenever a Bald Eagle appears on screen is not the Bald Eagle's voice. It belongs to the Red-tailed Hawk. Degree 221 opens the **RAPTOR ARC** -- eight consecutive degrees of raptors -- and it opens with the engine's most important correction: the national bird sounds like a seagull having a small disagreement. The Bald Eagle is common throughout Puget Sound, nesting in old-growth snags along the shoreline, fishing from ferry-watch perches, and gathering in winter concentrations at the Skagit River delta. Its real call, once you know it, changes how you see the bird: something vast and wild reduced to a thin thread of sound.
