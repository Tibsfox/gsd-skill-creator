**The Skagit River Winter Concentration and Pacific Northwest Ecology.** **Bald Eagle** populations in Washington are concentrated along Pacific Northwest shorelines with a documented nesting population of approximately **850 pairs** — the largest in the contiguous United States and a dramatic recovery from the species' near-extirpation during the DDT-era population collapse of the 1960s and 1970s. The **Skagit River** delta and upper Skagit hosts one of the largest winter gatherings of Bald Eagles in the contiguous United States, where hundreds of eagles (counts reaching 600-800 birds during peak years) feed on spawned-out chum and pink salmon from late November through early February. Puget Sound proper hosts a year-round resident Bald Eagle population concentrated along shorelines, with preferred nesting sites in old-growth **Douglas fir** and **Sitka spruce** within 100 meters of shoreline — the structural preference reflects the species' fishing-centric foraging strategy and the need for mature nest-supporting trees. **The Bald Eagle Recovery and the Rachel Carson Connection.** The Bald Eagle's recovery from DDT-era population collapse is one of North American conservation's most successful population recoveries. **Rachel Carson** (1907-1964) published *Silent Spring* in **1962**, documenting DDT's effects on raptor populations including the Bald Eagle — specifically, the pesticide's accumulation through the food chain caused eggshell thinning that reduced reproductive success dramatically, and by the early 1970s the Bald Eagle was close to extirpation in the contiguous 48 states. The 1972 DDT ban enabled the species to begin recovering, and through the 1980s and 1990s the Bald Eagle's population climbed steadily; the species was delisted from the Endangered Species Act in **2007** after three decades of recovery. The **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife** and the **U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service** both played sustained roles in the species' recovery, with ongoing monitoring continuing through the post-delisting period. **In parallel** with Love Battery's documentation of Sub Pop's experimental edge that coexisted with commercial grunge, the Bald Eagle's recovery documents the possibility that environmental population collapses can be reversed through targeted policy intervention, and the two recovery narratives (music-scene-experimental-edge preservation and species-population-recovery) mirror each other as examples of deliberate structural preservation against dominant trajectories.

## Part B: Bald Eagle Call — The First Accipitridae, the Thin Reedy Kee-Kee-Kee, and the Hollywood-Call Correction

Preferred nesting sites are in old-growth **Douglas fir** and **Sitka spruce** within 100 meters of shoreline, reflecting the species' fishing-centric foraging strategy and the need for mature nest-supporting trees. Bald Eagles are visible from nearly every **Washington State Ferries** crossing in Puget Sound, and the species' presence in everyday Pacific Northwest life is among the most accessible wildlife-viewing experiences in the region.
