### THE BUSHY-TAILED WOODRAT: THE PACK RAT

The Bushy-tailed Woodrat is the "pack rat" -- a large nocturnal rodent famous for **collecting objects** and building **elaborate stick nests (middens)**. At 200-600 grams (7-21 oz), it is the largest North American woodrat species and significantly larger than the Deer Mouse (10-30 g) that precedes it at degree 83. It inhabits rocky outcrops -- cliff faces, talus slopes, boulder fields -- throughout western North America.

This is the engine's **SECOND CONSECUTIVE CRICETIDAE** (83-84). The Deer Mouse (83) broke the Sciuridae octet (75-82). The Bushy-tailed Woodrat continues Cricetidae. A new family run begins.

The middens are the defining feature: some are **thousands of years old** and serve as paleontological archives of ancient PNW ecology. **Amberat** -- crystallized woodrat urine -- preserves plant fragments, pollen, seeds, and insects from the time of deposition. Betancourt et al. (1990) documented midden-based reconstructions of the last 40,000 years of biotic change. One twig at a time, across millennia.

And this is the **twenty-fourth consecutive original CSV species**.

### TAXONOMY: *Neotoma cinerea*

- **Neotoma** -- from Greek *neos* ("new") + *toma* ("cutting"), referring to the sharp cutting incisors characteristic of the genus.
- **cinerea** -- from Latin "ash-colored," describing the gray pelage. Described by Ord (1815).
- **Common names:** Bushy-tailed Woodrat (standard). Pack rat (common). The "bushy-tailed" refers to the densely furred, squirrel-like tail -- unlike the scaly tails of most rats and mice.

Classification:
- **Class:** Mammalia (23rd mammalian entry; 21st mammalian species)
- **Order:** Rodentia -- ELEVENTH Rodentia in the engine
- **Family:** Cricetidae -- SECOND CONSECUTIVE (83-84). Cricetidae streak at two
- **Genus:** *Neotoma* -- NEW GENUS. Second Cricetidae genus after *Peromyscus* (83). Woodrats (pack rats)
- **Species:** *N. cinerea* -- the Bushy-tailed Woodrat. Largest North American woodrat. Rocky outcrops and montane habitats

### THE SOUND: CHATTER + TWIG GNAW -- 85th Species-Side Acoustic Element

The Bushy-tailed Woodrat produces two primary sound categories:

**Chatter:** A rapid series of tooth-clacking sounds used as a warning or territorial signal. The teeth clack together rapidly -- a mechanical, percussive sound. Approximately 2,000-8,000 Hz. Moderately loud, audible at close range.

**Twig gnaw:** The functional sounds of gnawing and manipulating sticks during midden construction. Not a vocalization but construction noise -- the sound of the animal at work. Scraping, snapping, breaking of twigs. Broadband, approximately 500-5,000 Hz. Audible in quiet nocturnal environments.

**Why E=3:** Chatter is moderately loud -- audible at close range, distinctly mechanical. Twig gnaw is audible in quiet environments. Nocturnal context but not ultrasonic -- clearly audible to human observers when nearby. E=3 reflects moderate functional sound activity -- neither silent nor loud.

**The Beaver Chewing Parallel:** The gnaw sounds are comparable to Beaver chewing (degree 62, E=4) but softer and smaller in scale. Both are rodent functional sounds -- construction noise. Beaver gnaws trees (large scale, E=4). Woodrat gnaws twigs (small scale, E=3). Both are rodent infrastructure builders producing the sounds of habitat modification.

### ECOLOGY: THE MIDDEN BUILDER

**The Middens: Time Capsules.** Built in rock crevices, cliff overhangs, and cave entrances. Sticks, bark, leaves, and collected objects cemented together with urine and feces. Some middens are thousands of years old. The oldest known middens exceed 40,000 years. Successive generations add to the same midden. Paleontologists use woodrat middens to reconstruct ancient environments. Each midden is an archive: the twigs record what plants grew nearby, the pollen records what was flowering, the insects record what was crawling. The crystallized urine preserves it all.

**Habitat: Rocky Outcrop.** Cliff faces, talus slopes, boulder fields, rock crevices. Returns to rocky terrain shared with Golden-mantled Ground Squirrel (degree 79). Two rocky-outcrop species in six degrees from different families (Sciuridae vs Cricetidae).

**Diet: Herbivore.** Leaves, shoots, fruits, seeds, fungi, bark. Caches food in and around the midden. The midden serves as both home and pantry.

**Predators:** American Marten (SPS-065) -- key predator. The Marten's prey list grows: Douglas Squirrel (80), Northern Flying Squirrel (82), Bushy-tailed Woodrat (84). Three prey species across five degrees (80-84). Cross-family predator. Also owls, weasels, coyotes, bobcats.
