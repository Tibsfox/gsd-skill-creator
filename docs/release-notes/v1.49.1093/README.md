## Mission Overview

Lunar Prospector is the obs#1 opener of the ROBOTIC-LUNAR-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's Discovery-program lunar polar orbiter, a small spin-stabilized spacecraft that mapped the whole Moon from a low polar orbit, whose defining firsts are the first strong evidence of water ice at the lunar poles, the first global geochemical maps of the Moon from a dedicated polar orbiter, and the first NASA Discovery mission to the Moon. The principal investigator was Alan Binder of the Lunar Research Institute; the mission was managed by NASA Ames Research Center; the spacecraft was built by Lockheed Martin. Lunar Prospector was the third Discovery mission and the first sent to the Moon (★ NOT the first Discovery mission — NEAR holds that). It launched 7 January 1998 (UTC) on a Lockheed Martin Athena II from Cape Canaveral, cruised for roughly 105 hours, and entered a low circular polar orbit around the Moon about 11 January 1998 — near 100 km nominal, later lowered to about 40 km in December 1998 and then to an extended-mission orbit of roughly 15–45 km in 1999. It carried five instrument packages — the Neutron Spectrometer, the Gamma-Ray Spectrometer, the Alpha Particle Spectrometer, the Magnetometer and Electron Reflectometer, and the Doppler Gravity Experiment — and no camera: a dedicated geophysics and geochemistry mapper. The story is the launch from Cape Canaveral, the cruise into a low circular polar orbit, the pole-to-pole sweep of the whole surface as the Moon turned beneath, the enhanced-hydrogen signal at the poles, and the planned descent to the lunar south pole. The science is central: the Neutron Spectrometer registered enhanced hydrogen over both poles — a suppression of epithermal and fast neutrons read as water ice held in the permanently-shadowed, perpetually-cold floors of the polar craters (Feldman et al. 1998) — the first strong, compelling evidence of lunar polar water ice; ★ the Neutron Spectrometer's instrument lead was William C. Feldman of Los Alamos, distinct from Binder, the overall mission PI. The full suite produced the first global maps of the Moon's elemental composition from nuclear (neutron and gamma-ray) spectroscopy — thorium, potassium, iron, titanium, and more — together with a refined gravity field (the Doppler experiment sharpened the mascons) and a global picture of the crustal magnetism. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: the first STRONG evidence of water ice at the lunar poles — distinct from Clementine's earlier ambiguous bistatic-radar hint (1994/1996), and not a direct confirmation (that came later, with LCROSS in 2009); the first global GEOCHEMICAL maps of the Moon — distinct from Clementine's earlier multispectral imaging and laser-altimeter topography (1994); and the first NASA Discovery mission to the Moon (the third Discovery mission, NOT the first — NEAR holds that). ★★ The mission ended in July 1999 (launched 7 January 1998, entered lunar orbit about 11 January 1998, mapped the Moon for about nineteen months, ended 31 July 1999 with a planned descent to a shadowed south-polar crater), so the catalog frames the story in the PAST tense throughout (Lunar Prospector launched, orbited, mapped, detected, ended). ★ The dates are kept distinct: launch 7 January 1998, lunar-orbit insertion about 11 January 1998, end of mission 31 July 1999. ## Surprises

- **A spacecraft found the first strong evidence of water ice on the Moon.** Lunar Prospector's Neutron Spectrometer registered enhanced hydrogen over both lunar poles in 1998 — read as water ice held in the permanently-shadowed, perpetually-cold floors of the polar craters — the first strong, compelling evidence of lunar polar water ice, where the Sun never reaches.
- **A whole world mapped from a low polar orbit with no camera.** Lunar Prospector carried five geophysics-and-geochemistry instrument packages and no camera at all, yet from its low polar orbit it produced the first global maps of the Moon's elemental composition, gravity field, and crustal magnetism — reading composition, gravity, and magnetism rather than taking pictures.
- **The mission ended where its science began — at the lunar south pole.** On 31 July 1999 Lunar Prospector ended with a planned, controlled descent into a permanently-shadowed crater near the lunar south pole (later named Shoemaker crater), a final attempt to send the spacecraft to the south pole to look for a water-vapor signature in the ejecta — closing the mission on the same question its Neutron Spectrometer had opened. No vapor plume was detected from Earth.
- **A cold-obligate insect at home only where the warmth does not reach.** The Ice Crawler (*Grylloblatta campodeiformis*), a wingless grylloblattid of the alpine talus, snowfields, and ice margins of montane Washington and the northern Rockies, is so bound to the cold that the warmth of a human hand can be fatal to it, foraging on wind-blown debris at the edge of the snow and ice — a mirror of the water ice held in the perpetually-cold polar craters where the Sun never reaches.
- **A moss that holds its moisture in the deep shade.** Broom Moss (*Dicranum scoparium*), a common moss of the shaded floors and decaying logs of Pacific Northwest conifer forests, forms dense cushions of swept-to-one-side leaves that hold moisture in the deep shade where the drying sun does not reach (ecological description only) — bound with the throughline of what the cold, sunless places keep.

## Dedication

v1.276 Lunar Prospector is dedicated to the people across NASA Ames, the Lunar Research Institute, and Lockheed Martin who built and flew the first Discovery mission to the Moon. The principal investigator, Alan Binder, who carried a low-cost lunar orbiter from idea to flight, and the team at Ames who managed it. The engineers at Lockheed Martin who built a small spinning spacecraft and the Athena II that lifted it, and the flight team who held the cruise and settled it into a low polar orbit pole to pole around the Moon. The instrument teams behind the Neutron Spectrometer, the Gamma-Ray Spectrometer, the Alpha Particle Spectrometer, the Magnetometer and Electron Reflectometer, and the Doppler Gravity Experiment — and William Feldman, whose Neutron Spectrometer read the enhanced hydrogen over the poles. The scientists who mapped the whole Moon's composition, gravity, and magnetism from that low orbit, and who read the first strong evidence of water held in the perpetually-cold, sunless floors of the polar craters. They sent a small spacecraft to map a world from pole to pole, and at the end sent it down to the south pole to look for water — finding what the cold, shadowed places keep.
