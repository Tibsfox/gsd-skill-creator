## Surprises

- **A spacecraft found the first strong evidence of water ice on the Moon.** Lunar Prospector's Neutron Spectrometer registered enhanced hydrogen over both lunar poles in 1998 — read as water ice held in the permanently-shadowed, perpetually-cold floors of the polar craters — the first strong, compelling evidence of lunar polar water ice, where the Sun never reaches.
- **A whole world mapped from a low polar orbit with no camera.** Lunar Prospector carried five geophysics-and-geochemistry instrument packages and no camera at all, yet from its low polar orbit it produced the first global maps of the Moon's elemental composition, gravity field, and crustal magnetism — reading composition, gravity, and magnetism rather than taking pictures.
- **The mission ended where its science began — at the lunar south pole.** On 31 July 1999 Lunar Prospector ended with a planned, controlled descent into a permanently-shadowed crater near the lunar south pole (later named Shoemaker crater), a final attempt to send the spacecraft to the south pole to look for a water-vapor signature in the ejecta — closing the mission on the same question its Neutron Spectrometer had opened. No vapor plume was detected from Earth.
- **A cold-obligate insect at home only where the warmth does not reach.** The Ice Crawler (*Grylloblatta campodeiformis*), a wingless grylloblattid of the alpine talus, snowfields, and ice margins of montane Washington and the northern Rockies, is so bound to the cold that the warmth of a human hand can be fatal to it, foraging on wind-blown debris at the edge of the snow and ice — a mirror of the water ice held in the perpetually-cold polar craters where the Sun never reaches.
- **A moss that holds its moisture in the deep shade.** Broom Moss (*Dicranum scoparium*), a common moss of the shaded floors and decaying logs of Pacific Northwest conifer forests, forms dense cushions of swept-to-one-side leaves that hold moisture in the deep shade where the drying sun does not reach (ecological description only) — bound with the throughline of what the cold, sunless places keep.
