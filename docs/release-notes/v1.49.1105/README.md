## Mission Overview

NISAR is the obs#8 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — a joint NASA-ISRO Earth-observing satellite that orbits the planet and watches the solid Earth move, sweeping nearly all the world's land and ice every twelve days with a dual-frequency synthetic-aperture radar and measuring how the ground deforms, the changing structure of the ice, and the biomass of the forests. ★ It is a joint mission of NASA (Jet Propulsion Laboratory) and ISRO (Indian Space Research Organisation): NASA provides the L-band synthetic-aperture radar, the ~12 m deployable mesh reflector, the high-rate telecom, and the GPS payload, and ISRO provides the S-band synthetic-aperture radar, the modified I-3K spacecraft bus, and the launch. The observer launched 30 July 2025 (12:10 UTC / 17:40 IST) on a GSLV-F16 (a GSLV Mk II flight) from the Satish Dhawan Space Centre (SDSC SHAR), Sriharikota, India, and climbed into a sun-synchronous (dawn-dusk) orbit at roughly 747 km altitude, ~98.4° inclination, with a 12-day repeat: a radar Earth observer reading the moving land below. The story is the launch from Sriharikota, the climb into the sun-synchronous orbit, the unfurling of the great ~12 m gold-plated wire-mesh reflector, the two radar beams sweeping the surface across a wide swath with the SweepSAR technique, the interferometric fringes that measure the land rising and sinking and sliding to within fractions of an inch, the ice and the forest biomass mapped across the world every twelve days, and the long watch over the moving land that begins newly aloft and continues today. The science is central: the dual-frequency synthetic-aperture radar — an L-band channel (~1.257 GHz, ~24 cm) and an S-band channel (~3.2 GHz, ~9.4 cm), fed by the ~12 m mesh reflector and swept across a >240 km swath at ~7 m azimuth resolution — measures solid-Earth deformation by interferometry (earthquakes, volcanoes, landslides, groundwater and subsidence, to sub-centimetre precision), cryosphere dynamics (ice sheets, glaciers, sea ice), and ecosystem biomass (forests, wetlands, agriculture), all-weather and day and night; NISAR is the first satellite to carry both L-band and S-band synthetic-aperture radar; it is the first major NASA-ISRO joint Earth-observing mission; and it carries out a systematic global interferometric survey of land deformation, ice, and biomass. ★ The radar-architecture-first result is load-bearing: it is the first satellite to carry both L-band and S-band synthetic-aperture radar (the first dual-frequency SAR imaging satellite) — NOT "the first radar satellite" or "the first SAR," since Seasat (1978) carried the first spaceborne synthetic-aperture radar and ERS, JERS, RADARSAT, ALOS, and Sentinel-1 followed; and NISAR's dual-frequency imaging SAR is distinct from GPM's dual-frequency precipitation radar. ★ The partnership-first result is equally load-bearing: it is the first major NASA-ISRO joint Earth-observing mission, NASA and ISRO each contributing a radar and the spacecraft and launch shared. ★ The survey result is load-bearing: NISAR carries out a systematic global interferometric survey of land deformation, ice, and biomass — sweeping nearly all the land and ice every ~12 days, the same ground revisited again and again. ★★ NISAR is NEWLY ON ORBIT (launched 30 July 2025, commissioned ~7 November 2025), so the catalog frames its launch and commissioning in the PAST (NISAR launched 30 July 2025, reached its sun-synchronous orbit, unfurled its mesh reflector, completed its ~90-day commissioning, and began its science) while its ongoing role is framed in the PRESENT (NISAR orbits Earth, sweeps the surface with its radar, maps the moving land, and is operating in early science). ## Surprises

- **A sun-synchronous 12-day-repeat orbit gives NISAR a systematic, revisiting global survey.** Rather than imaging a place once, NISAR sits in a sun-synchronous dawn-dusk orbit at roughly 747 km with a 12-day repeat cycle, so it sweeps the same ground again and again on a fixed schedule — which lets its dual-frequency synthetic-aperture radar build an interferometric record of how the land has shifted between passes, resolving the rise and sink and slide of the ground to within fractions of an inch over nearly all the world's land and ice.
- **Two radar wavelengths read the surface at once.** NISAR's instrument carries both an L-band channel (~1.257 GHz, ~24 cm) from NASA and an S-band channel (~3.2 GHz, ~9.4 cm) from ISRO, the first satellite to carry both — the longer L-band waves penetrating vegetation and probing the ground and forest structure while the shorter S-band waves read the surface, the two wavelengths together reading the deformation, ice, and biomass of the moving Earth all-weather and day and night.
- **A twelve-metre mesh reflector that unfurls on orbit and a SweepSAR wide swath.** Rather than a small fixed antenna, NISAR carries a great ~12 m gold-plated wire-mesh reflector that unfurls after launch, and uses the SweepSAR technique to open a wide swath (>240 km) — the combination that lets a single satellite sweep nearly all the land and ice every twelve days at ~7 m azimuth resolution, the engineering that makes the systematic global survey possible.
- **A fossorial rodent that turns the soil from below.** The Mazama Pocket Gopher (*Thomomys mazama*), a small fossorial rodent of the family Geomyidae of Pacific Northwest prairies and montane meadows (western Washington and Oregon), spends its life underground, digging extensive tunnel systems and pushing up mounds of fresh soil, constantly turning and mixing the earth from below: a creature of the moving earth, the small-scale, living version of the ground that NISAR watches deform from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only; a species of conservation concern in some Washington prairies).
- **A subshrub that holds the loose, recovering ground.** Cardwell's Penstemon (*Penstemon cardwellii*), a low, spreading, evergreen subshrub of the Pacific Northwest Cascades (Washington and Oregon), colonizes rocky slopes, talus, roadcuts, and freshly disturbed or burned ground, forming woody mats with deep purple-violet flowers — an early colonizer that holds the loose, recovering, changing earth (ecological description only; distinct from the cliff and rock penstemons grown elsewhere) — bound with the throughline of the moving earth, holding and greening the same disturbed, recovering land that NISAR maps from orbit.

## Dedication

v1.288 NISAR is dedicated to the people at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory and across the Indian Space Research Organisation, and to the engineers and scientists of both agencies who built and now operate the satellite that watches the solid Earth move. The teams who joined an L-band radar from one side of the planet to an S-band radar from the other, and gave them a single great mesh reflector to share, so that two wavelengths could read the moving ground at once. The engineers who folded a twelve-metre antenna small enough to fly, lifted it from Sriharikota aboard a GSLV, and unfurled it on orbit into a dish that sweeps a swath hundreds of kilometres wide. And the scientists who, by comparing one radar pass with the next, can measure how the land has risen or sunk or slid by a fraction of an inch — the slow shift of the ground beneath the cities, the creep of the glaciers, the standing forests weighed from above. Together they read what the eye cannot see: the ground itself in motion, everywhere on Earth, every twelve days. Two agencies built one instrument to watch the moving land, and the watch has begun.
