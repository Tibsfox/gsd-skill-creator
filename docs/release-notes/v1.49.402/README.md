## Summary

**Screaming Trees** arrive at E=7 -- the psychedelic rock band from Ellensburg, Washington, founded in 1985 by brothers Gary Lee Conner (guitar) and Van Conner (bass), with Mark Lanegan (vocals) and Barrett Martin (drums, replacing original drummer Mark Pickerel). The Screaming Trees are one of the most important bands in the history of Pacific Northwest rock: not the loudest, not the fastest, not the most influential on the genre that followed them, but among the most honest documents of what the Cascade foothills produced in the late 1980s and 1990s. They are from Ellensburg -- not Seattle, not Olympia, not Aberdeen -- a small city east of the Cascades in Kittitas County, and that origin is audible in their music: it carries the dryness of the east side, the open space of a high desert valley, and the rawness of a band that did not come up through any established music infrastructure. Their catalog -- *Other Worlds* (1985), *Even If and Especially When* (1987), *Invisible Lantern* (1988), *Buzz Factory* (1989), *Uncle Anesthesia* (1991), *Sweet Oblivion* (1992), *Dust* (1996) -- documents a band that spent eleven years developing a form that sat at the intersection of psychedelic rock, hard rock, and the emerging alternative sound of the Pacific Northwest without ever fully belonging to any of those categories. Mark Lanegan's voice -- a baritone of considerable weight and expressive range -- is the band's most distinctive element, and it remained among the most recognizable instruments in rock music through Lanegan's subsequent solo career and collaborative work until his death in 2022. E=7 for the Screaming Trees reflects the sustained intensity of their catalog at its peak: *Sweet Oblivion* and *Dust* are high-energy records, and the band's live energy at their peak was considerable, but E=7 rather than E=8 acknowledges the psychedelic dimension of their work -- the tempo shifts, the textural variation, the willingness to slow down -- that distinguishes them from the straight-line intensity of a band like Botch or Mudhoney.

Ellensburg enters the engine for the first time at degree 261. The Seattle 360 engine has, up to this point, documented musicians primarily from the Puget Sound basin and the western Cascades. The Screaming Trees' arrival marks the first entry of an artist from east of the Cascades, from the Kittitas Valley on the far side of the mountain range that defines the region's meteorological and cultural divide. Ellensburg is cattle country, university town (Central Washington University), rodeo country -- it is culturally distinct from Seattle in ways that are not reducible to geography, and the Screaming Trees carry that distinction into the engine.

On the soundscape side, **Volcanic Tremor Rainier** arrives at E=5 -- the continuous, low-frequency seismic signal produced by fluid movement within Mount Rainier's hydrothermal system, detectable by seismometers on the volcano's flanks. Volcanic tremor is distinct from earthquake: where an earthquake is an impulsive event, a sudden rupture, volcanic tremor is harmonic and sustained -- produced by the resonance of fluid-filled cracks and conduits in the volcanic edifice as hydrothermal water and volcanic gases move through the mountain's interior. Mount Rainier is the most glaciated volcano in the contiguous United States, and its interaction between volcanic heat and ice produces one of the most complex hydrothermal systems on the continent. The tremor generated by this system is not audible to human ears at the surface; it propagates as infrasound, below 20 Hz, and is measured by seismic instruments rather than microphones. E=5 reflects the moderate energy of a continuous, low-frequency signal: it is real, it is constant, it is geophysically significant, but it operates at the infrasonic threshold where human perception ends.

Degree 261 opens the geophysics arc -- the engine's systematic documentation of the acoustic landscape produced by Pacific Northwest geophysical processes. The arc spans degrees 261-267 and documents volcanic tremor, lahar simulation, earthquake P-waves, submarine canyon flow, glacier calving, rockslide, and mudslide in sequence. The arc opens here, with the tremor of a living volcano, and it will track the full range of what Cascade geology produces acoustically before it closes.
