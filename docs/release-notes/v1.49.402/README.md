**Ellensburg, Kittitas County, and the Columbia Plateau.** **Ellensburg** is a small city in **Kittitas County**, Washington, located east of the Cascade crest at approximately 47.0° north latitude and 120.5° west longitude, with a population near 20,000 as of the 2020 US Census. The city is organized around **Central Washington University** (founded 1891 as Washington State Normal School, a regional comprehensive university with approximately 11,000 enrolled students across its Ellensburg and satellite campuses), the **Kittitas Valley** cattle-and-agriculture economy, and the **Ellensburg Rodeo** — one of the larger professional rodeos in the Pacific Northwest and a defining cultural event since 1923. The **Columbia Plateau** climate differs sharply from the Puget Sound basin: substantially lower rainfall (Ellensburg receives approximately 9 inches of precipitation annually versus Seattle's approximately 37 inches), higher summer temperatures, colder winters, open sagebrush-and-grassland vegetation rather than coniferous rain forest, and the high-desert basalt-column geology of the **Columbia River Basalt Group**. **The Physics of Volcanic Tremor — Harmonic Resonance, Fluid Conduits, and the Below-Audible Threshold.** Volcanic tremor is the continuous, sustained, harmonic seismic signal produced by fluid movement within a volcanic edifice. It is structurally distinct from the discrete impulsive events that characterize tectonic earthquakes: where an earthquake is a sudden rupture of stressed rock along a fault surface producing a broadband transient signal, tremor is harmonic — its frequency spectrum is peaked at specific resonant frequencies — and sustained — it continues for minutes, hours, days, or longer. The generation mechanism is fluid resonance within conduits and cracks in the volcanic structure: as hydrothermal water, steam, and volcanic gases move through the plumbing system beneath the volcano, the fluid motion excites resonant oscillations in fluid-filled cracks and conduits, and the oscillations radiate seismic energy that propagates through the surrounding rock as tremor. The resonant frequencies are determined by the geometry of the fluid-filled cavities — their length, their cross-sectional area, their fluid-to-rock impedance contrast — and the frequencies that characterize volcanic tremor at Mount Rainier fall predominantly below the 20-Hz threshold of human hearing. The tremor is therefore infrasonic: it propagates in the seismic and acoustic domains but cannot be heard by unaided human ears at the surface. Detection requires seismic instruments — broadband seismometers, short-period seismometers, infrasound microbarographs — operated by the **USGS Cascades Volcano Observatory** and the **Pacific Northwest Seismic Network**. ## Part B: Volcanic Tremor Rainier — The Geophysics Arc Opens, the Mount Rainier Hydrothermal System, and the First Infrasonic SPS Entry

- **The Geophysics Arc (261-267) Opens at Volcanic Tremor:** The engine's seven-degree **Geophysics Arc** spans degrees 261-267 and documents Cascade volcanic and seismic acoustic mechanisms in sequence: **261 Volcanic Tremor Rainier** (infrasonic, E=5, the arc's opening with the continuous harmonic baseline); **262 Lahar Simulation** (debris flow); **263 Earthquake P-wave** (impulsive transient); **264 Submarine Canyon Flow** (low-frequency continuous); **265 Glacier Calving Mt Rainier** (impulsive with prolonged rumble); **266 Rockslide** (broadband transient with extended tail); and **267 Mudslide Oso-type** (broadband transient with prolonged low-frequency tail). The arc is the most geologically coherent arc the SPS track has produced, and it extends the engine's acoustic vocabulary to include the full range of Cascade-geology sounds at the infrasonic and subsonic frequencies where geophysical energy predominantly propagates.
- **Mount Rainier — the Most Glaciated Volcano in the Contiguous United States:** **Mount Rainier** is a **14,411-foot** stratovolcano in the **Cascade Range**, located approximately 55 miles southeast of Seattle, Washington. It is the most heavily glaciated peak in the contiguous United States, with twenty-five named glaciers including **Emmons Glacier**, **Winthrop Glacier**, **Carbon Glacier**, **Nisqually Glacier**, **Tahoma Glacier**, **Kautz Glacier**, **Paradise Glacier**, **Cowlitz Glacier**, **Ingraham Glacier**, and the **Puyallup Glacier**. The mountain is an active stratovolcano whose most recent eruption was approximately 1,000 years ago but whose ongoing hydrothermal activity continues at the summit and within the volcanic edifice. Mount Rainier is known as **Tahoma** to the **Puyallup** and other regional nations; the Puyallup-language name predates English-language naming by twelve thousand years of continuous regional presence.
- **The Hydrothermal System and Ice-Volcano-Thermal Interaction:** The interaction between volcanic heat and the twenty-five named glaciers of Mount Rainier's summit produces one of the most complex hydrothermal systems on the continent. Meltwater from the glaciers infiltrates into the volcanic edifice, heats by contact with hot rock and magma-derived fluids at depth, and rises back through the conduit-and-fracture plumbing system carrying dissolved minerals, volcanic gases (CO2, SO2, H2S), and thermal energy. The hydrothermal system is the geological mechanism that produces the continuous tremor signal: fluid movement through fluid-filled cracks and conduits excites resonant oscillations that radiate seismic energy as harmonic tremor. The system is unstable on geological timescales; hydrothermal alteration of the volcanic rock is a long-term contributor to the mountain's mass-wasting hazard and a factor in the **Osceola Mudflow** (approximately 5,600 years ago) and similar large-scale collapse events that define the Mount Rainier hazard context.
- **Harmonic Tremor Versus Earthquake — The Physical Distinction:** Volcanic tremor is structurally distinct from tectonic earthquake. An earthquake is an impulsive event — a sudden rupture of stressed rock along a fault surface — producing a broadband transient seismic signal with a characteristic sharp onset and decaying coda. Tremor is harmonic — its frequency spectrum is peaked at specific resonant frequencies determined by the geometry of fluid-filled cavities — and sustained — it continues for minutes, hours, days, or longer without the impulsive-onset character. The two signal types represent fundamentally different physical mechanisms: earthquake is brittle-failure-of-stressed-rock; tremor is fluid-resonance-within-fluid-filled-conduits. The engine documents tremor at degree 261 and reserves earthquake for degree 263 P-wave documentation, treating the two as distinct SPS entries with distinct acoustic characters.
- **Below-20-Hz Infrasound and the Human-Hearing Threshold:** Volcanic tremor at Mount Rainier falls predominantly below 20 Hz — the lower limit of human hearing. The tremor is therefore infrasonic: it propagates in the seismic and acoustic domains but cannot be heard by unaided human ears at the surface. Detection requires seismic instruments — broadband seismometers, short-period seismometers, infrasound microbarographs — and the tremor's acoustic signature is measured in seismic waveform records and infrasound microbarograph records rather than in sound-pressure-level decibel measurements. E=5 on the SPS amplitude scale reflects the moderate-energy character of a continuous, low-frequency, infrasonic signal whose presence is defined by sustained existence rather than by peak amplitude.
- **USGS Cascades Volcano Observatory and Pacific Northwest Seismic Network:** Mount Rainier is continuously monitored by the **USGS Cascades Volcano Observatory** (CVO, based in **Vancouver, Washington**) and the **Pacific Northwest Seismic Network** (PNSN, operated by the University of Washington). The CVO operates summit-region seismic stations, gas-emission samplers, and deformation-measurement GPS stations. The PNSN operates a broader seismic network across Washington, Oregon, and British Columbia with continuous recording at hundreds of stations. The volcanic-tremor record at Mount Rainier is continuous across multiple decades, and the tremor catalog — a structured record of tremor events with start times, durations, peak frequencies, and amplitude measurements — forms part of the mountain's scientific baseline. The monitoring provides the empirical foundation for the degree 261 documentation and for the subsequent Geophysics Arc entries.
- **The Cascade Volcanic Arc and Cascadia Subduction Zone Context:** Mount Rainier is one volcano in the **Cascade Volcanic Arc** — the chain of stratovolcanoes running from **Mount Baker** and **Glacier Peak** in Washington through **Mount Rainier**, **Mount Adams**, **Mount St. Helens**, **Mount Hood**, **Mount Jefferson**, **Three Sisters**, **Newberry Volcano**, **Crater Lake** (**Mount Mazama**), **Mount McLoughlin**, **Mount Shasta**, and **Lassen Peak**. The arc is driven by the subduction of the **Juan de Fuca Plate** beneath the **North American Plate** along the **Cascadia Subduction Zone** — a 1,000-kilometer fault system whose megathrust earthquakes and continuous aseismic slip ground the Pacific Northwest's ongoing tectonic and volcanic background. The tremor at Mount Rainier is specifically the Mount Rainier tremor signal; each Cascade volcano has its own distinct tremor signature, and the engine's Geophysics Arc documents Mount Rainier as one entry in the broader arc rather than as the sole Cascade volcanic-acoustic reference.
- **E=5 Placement — Moderate-Energy Continuous Signal Below Peak-Amplitude Register:** **Volcanic Tremor Rainier** at degree 261 lands at **E=5**. The placement reflects the moderate-energy character of a continuous, low-frequency, infrasonic signal whose presence is defined by sustained existence rather than by peak amplitude. The tremor is real, continuous, geophysically significant, but it operates at the infrasonic threshold where human perception ends, and its acoustic signature does not produce the high-amplitude peaks that characterize the storm-arc events at E=8 or the lightning-strike climax at E=9. E=5 positions the tremor in the moderate-amplitude SPS register appropriate for continuous, infrasonic, harmonic geophysical signals, and establishes the baseline amplitude for the opening of the Geophysics Arc.
