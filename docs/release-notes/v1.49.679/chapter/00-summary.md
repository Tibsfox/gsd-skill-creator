## Operational headline

The 1986 perihelion passage of comet 1P/Halley — the most-anticipated periodic comet return in history due to its 76-year orbital period and 1910 near-Earth approach — coincides with the first generation of nation-states capable of building outer-solar-system spacecraft. The IACG, founded 1981 by Roald Sagdeev (IKI), Roger Bonnet (ESA), Hubert Curien (CNES), and James Beggs (NASA), coordinates a six-spacecraft, four-agency intercept campaign across 22 days from 1986-03-06 to 1986-03-28.

**Soviet contribution:** Vega 1 + Vega 2 (4VeNeGa-1 and 4VeNeGa-2) — joint Venus + Halley dual-target missions launched from Baikonur LC-200/40 on Proton-K heavy-lift launch vehicles 1984-12-15 and 1984-12-21. Each Vega massed 4,920 kg, deployed Soviet-built landers and balloon atmospheric probes for Venus (1985-06-11 and 1985-06-15 over Aphrodite Terra; balloons aloft ~46 hours), then used Venus gravity-assist for Halley trajectory. Vega 1 closest approach 1986-03-06 11:20 UTC at 8,890 km on the sunward side; Vega 2 closest approach 1986-03-09 09:20 UTC at 8,030 km. ~1,500 images of Halley's nucleus and inner coma each.

**Japanese contribution from ISAS:** Sakigake (MS-T5; "Pioneer") launched 1985-01-07 from Kagoshima Space Center on a Mu-3SII rocket — **FIRST JAPANESE INTERPLANETARY SPACECRAFT** — distant Halley flyby 1986-03-11 at 6,990,000 km; primarily a precursor for the Suisei flight unit. Suisei (PLANET-A; "Comet") launched 1985-08-18 — Halley flyby 1986-03-08 at 151,000 km on the sunward side carrying a UV imaging photometer that mapped the 20-million-km hydrogen-Lyman-alpha corona.

**European contribution from ESA:** Giotto launched 1985-07-02 by Ariane 1 from Centre Spatial Guyanais Kourou — **FIRST ESA DEEP-SPACE SCIENCE MISSION** — 960 kg spin-stabilized spacecraft, instrumented with the Halley Multicolour Camera (HMC; PI Uwe Keller MPAE Lindau) returning the **FIRST IMAGES OF A COMET NUCLEUS FROM SPACECRAFT** from 596 km closest approach 1986-03-14 00:03:02 UTC. HMC imaged the nucleus at ~50-meter resolution, revealing a peanut-shaped body 15 × 8 × 8 km with dark albedo 0.04 and multiple active dust jets. Giotto's anti-dust shield (Whipple bumper geometry, Wilkinson + Curtis 1980-1983) sustained ~12,000 impact hits — one ~10⁻⁷ kg grain at 68 km/s caused 32-minute attitude tumble + signal loss at closest approach. Spacecraft recovered + continued post-flyby mission. ESA later extended Giotto to a second comet — 26P/Grigg-Skjellerup encounter 1992-07-10 at 200 km (first multi-comet flyby by a single spacecraft; substrate-anticipation).

**American contribution:** ICE (International Cometary Explorer) — born 1978 as ISEE-3 at Sun-Earth L1 — repurposed 1983 by trajectory engineer Robert W. Farquhar (NASA Goddard) using lunar gravity-assist phase-loop + 25-month transfer to encounter comet 21P/Giacobini-Zinner 1985-09-11 at 7,800 km — **FIRST-EVER COMET FLYBY of any spacecraft** (predating the Halley armada by 6 months). ICE Halley distant flyby 1986-03-28 at 28 million km contributed sunward-side magnetic field + plasma-wave measurements.

The IACG Pathfinder Concept used Vega 1+2 imaging of the Halley nucleus (real-time-relayed ephemeris updates via Soviet DSN Yevpatoria + NASA DSN Goldstone-Madrid-Canberra cross-ranging) to refine Halley's center-of-figure ephemeris from ±9,000 km uncertainty pre-armada to ±200 km — enabling Giotto's 596 km targeting six days after Vega 2.
