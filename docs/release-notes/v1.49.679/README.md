The 1986 perihelion passage of comet 1P/Halley — the most-anticipated periodic comet return in history due to its 76-year orbital period and 1910 near-Earth approach — coincides with the first generation of nation-states capable of building outer-solar-system spacecraft. The IACG (Inter-Agency Consultative Group; founded 1981 by Roald Sagdeev / IKI Director + Roger Bonnet / ESA Director of Science + Hubert Curien / French Minister of Research + James Beggs / NASA Administrator) coordinated a six-spacecraft, four-agency intercept campaign across 22 days from 1986-03-06 to 1986-03-28.

The Soviet contribution: **Vega 1** (4VeNeGa-1; launched 1984-12-15 from Baikonur LC-200/40 on Proton-K 8K82K; 4,920 kg) and **Vega 2** (4VeNeGa-2; launched 1984-12-21; identical configuration) — **DUAL-TARGET-VENUS-AND-HALLEY-MISSION** obs#1 first-instance NEW LOCKED. Each Vega deployed a Venera-heritage Venus lander + a French-designed atmospheric balloon (CNES + IKI joint development under Hubert Curien's authorization). The balloons descended 1985-06-11 and 1985-06-15 over Aphrodite Terra, remained airborne ~46 hours at altitudes 53.6 km in the Venus acid-cloud layer, transmitting wind speed + temperature + pressure data via the orbiter buses to the Soviet Deep Space Network at Yevpatoria. After Venus deployment, the orbiter buses used Venus gravity-assist for the trajectory to Halley. Vega 1 closest approach 1986-03-06 11:20 UTC at 8,890 km on the sunward side; Vega 2 closest approach 1986-03-09 09:20 UTC at 8,030 km. Both Vegas carried the TVS (TeleVision System) imaging package + 14 additional scientific instruments; ~1,500 images of Halley's nucleus and inner coma transmitted, contributing nucleus position measurements to the IACG Pathfinder Concept (see below).

The Japanese contribution from **ISAS (Institute of Space and Astronautical Science)**: **Sakigake** (MS-T5; "Pioneer" in Japanese; 138 kg) launched 1985-01-07 from Kagoshima Space Center on a Mu-3SII solid-fuel rocket — **FIRST JAPANESE INTERPLANETARY SPACECRAFT** obs#1 first-instance NEW LOCKED. Sakigake was primarily an engineering precursor for the Suisei flight unit, with a distant Halley flyby 1986-03-11 at 6,990,000 km measuring solar wind interaction with the coma. **Suisei** (PLANET-A; "Comet" in Japanese; 140 kg) launched 1985-08-18 from Kagoshima — Halley flyby 1986-03-08 at 151,000 km on the sunward side carrying a UV imaging photometer that mapped the 20-million-km hydrogen-Lyman-alpha corona surrounding Halley's nucleus. Sakigake + Suisei together established the substrate-anchor for the JAXA-INTERPLANETARY cumulative cohort: Hiten 1990 + Nozomi 1998 + Hayabusa 2003 + Akatsuki 2010 + Hayabusa 2 2014 + BepiColombo MMO 2018 (all substrate-anticipation forward-shadows at v679).

The European contribution from **ESA**: **Giotto** launched 1985-07-02 by Ariane 1 from Centre Spatial Guyanais Kourou — **FIRST ESA DEEP-SPACE SCIENCE MISSION** obs#1 first-instance NEW LOCKED. The 960 kg spin-stabilized spacecraft was instrumented with the **Halley Multicolour Camera (HMC; PI Prof. Uwe Keller, Max-Planck Institute for Aeronomy Lindau, West Germany)** + 9 additional scientific instruments. Giotto's closest approach 1986-03-14 00:03:02 UTC at 596 km — the closest cometary intercept of the campaign — produced the **FIRST IMAGES OF A COMET NUCLEUS FROM SPACECRAFT** obs#1 first-instance NEW LOCKED at ~50-meter resolution. HMC imaging revealed Halley's nucleus to be peanut-shaped, 15 × 8 × 8 km in extent, with dark albedo 0.04 (darker than coal — among the darkest surfaces in the solar system) and multiple active dust + gas jets emitting from a small fraction of the nucleus surface. Giotto's anti-dust shield used Whipple bumper geometry (developed Wilkinson + Curtis 1980-1983) and sustained ~12,000 dust impact hits during the 4-second peak-flux interval around closest approach. One ~10⁻⁷ kg grain at 68 km/s relative velocity struck the spacecraft at closest approach, causing 32-minute attitude tumble + telemetry signal loss. Spacecraft attitude recovered + extended mission continued. ESA later extended Giotto to a second comet — 26P/Grigg-Skjellerup encounter 1992-07-10 at 200 km closest approach — establishing **first multi-comet flyby by a single spacecraft** (substrate-anticipation at v679).

The American contribution: **ICE (International Cometary Explorer)** — born 1978 as ISEE-3 (International Sun-Earth Explorer-3) at Sun-Earth Lagrange L1 — was **repurposed 1983** by trajectory engineer **Robert W. Farquhar (NASA Goddard Space Flight Center)** using lunar gravity-assist phase-loop + 25-month transfer to encounter comet 21P/Giacobini-Zinner 1985-09-11 at 7,800 km — **FIRST-EVER COMET FLYBY of any spacecraft** (predating the Halley armada by 6 months). ICE Halley distant flyby 1986-03-28 at 28 million km contributed sunward-side magnetic field + plasma-wave measurements complementing the close encounters. The ICE repurposing established substrate-anchor for **SPACECRAFT-REPURPOSING-VIA-TRAJECTORY-INGENUITY** (later applied to Galileo Earth gravity-assist 1990/1992 + Stardust-NExT 2011 + EPOXI 2010 + Kepler-K2 2014).

The **IACG Pathfinder Concept** used Vega 1+2 imaging of the Halley nucleus (returning real-time-relayed ephemeris updates via Soviet DSN Yevpatoria + NASA DSN Goldstone-Madrid-Canberra cross-ranging) to refine Halley's center-of-figure ephemeris from ±9,000 km uncertainty pre-armada to ±200 km — enabling Giotto's 596 km targeting six days after Vega 2's encounter. This was the **first international inter-agency cooperation at a deep-space target** — substrate-form INTER-AGENCY-COOPERATION-AT-HALLEY-RANGING obs#1 first-instance NEW LOCKED.
