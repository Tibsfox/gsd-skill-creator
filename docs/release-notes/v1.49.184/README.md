The slow bloom and the dialect. Degree 48 of the Seattle 360 + Sound of Puget Sound engine completes the fourth consecutive folk-adjacent degree on Part A and the fourth consecutive Passerellidae on Part B — the longest genre-family run in the engine's history on both sides simultaneously, approaching the swallow quintet's five-consecutive record. **Sera Cahoone** is the drummer-turned-singer-songwriter whose career has unfolded across twenty-five years of quiet, incremental growth in Ballard, Seattle. **Sera Cahoone** — born August 4, 1975, in Littleton, Colorado. Daughter of Bill Cahoone, a Rocky Mountain dynamite salesman, and Judy Cahoone, a business executive. Graduated from **Columbine High School** — the school where, on April 20, 1999, twelve students and a teacher were killed, an event Cahoone watched unfold on television from Seattle, having moved there the year before. She began drumming at age twelve to combat anxiety. First stage performance at Ziggie's Saloon in North Denver during a blues open-mic night. Moved to Seattle in 1998 when a Colorado snowboarding shop opened a Washington location. Became the drummer for **Carissa's Wierd** (1998-2003), the sadcore band that produced three major subsequent careers: Ben Bridwell formed **Band of Horses** (Sub Pop, RIAA Gold), Mat Brooke formed **Grand Archives** (Sub Pop), and Cahoone became a solo singer-songwriter (Sub Pop). Played selected drum tracks on Band of Horses' *Everything All The Time* (2006, Sub Pop) — received a **Gold Record** in 2019, thirteen years after the sessions. The transition from drummer to singer-songwriter was gradual and anxiety-ridden: she forced herself through open mic nights, recorded a self-titled debut playing guitar and drums herself, and sent a demo to **John Richards** at KEXP, who played it on air and launched her solo career. **Sub Pop** signed her in 2007. *Only as the Day Is Long* (2008, Sub Pop SP0759) — NPR Tiny Desk Concert, July 10, 2008. *Deer Creek Canyon* (2012, Sub Pop SP1005, produced by Thom Monahan at Bear Creek Studios, Woodinville) — named for the canyon in Littleton, Colorado, where her mother still lives. *From Where I Started* (2017, Lady Muleskinner Records, co-produced with John Morgan Askew at Flora Recording, Portland). *The Flora String Sessions* (2018, reimagined songs with Alex Guy string arrangements). Produced Margo Cilker's *Valley of Heart's Delight* (2023). First musicians to perform on the **Space Needle rooftop** (July 11, 2013, Sub Pop Silver Jubilee, broadcast live on KEXP, alongside Mudhoney). The slow bloom across twenty-five years.

Meanwhile, at the edge of a mountain meadow where subalpine shrubs grow in dense patches between bare ground and wildflowers — a bird with a head painted in bold black-and-white stripes. *Zonotrichia leucophrys pugetensis.* "The white-browed banded-hair bird of Puget Sound." The subspecies literally named for the project's subject, described by **Robert Ridgway** in 1898 from a Puget Sound type specimen, breeding from coastal southeastern Alaska south through the Pacific Northwest to northwestern California. **Peter Marler**'s bird — the species that taught science how song is learned. Marler and Tamura's 1964 *Science* paper "Culturally Transmitted Patterns of Vocal Behavior in Sparrows" established the sensitive period, auditory template, and cultural transmission paradigm that defined behavioral ecology for half a century. The bird whose twelve Puget Sound song dialects ARE the sound of Puget Sound. The bird whose song changed when San Francisco went quiet during COVID lockdowns (**Elizabeth Derryberry** et al. 2020, *Science*) — demonstrating three temporal scales of vocal change simultaneously: evolutionary (subspecies diverge over millennia), cultural (dialects shift over decades), behavioral (individual birds adjust within days). The engine's most temporally complex acoustic subject. The fourth consecutive **Passerellidae**, tying the longest non-Hirundinidae same-family streak. **Zonotrichia leucophrys** — from Ancient Greek *zone* (band) + *thrix* (hair) = "banded hair," referring to the bold striped head pattern. *Leucophrys* from *leukos* (white) + *ophrys* (eyebrow) = "white-browed." Full translation: "the white-browed banded-hair bird" — entirely visual, no acoustic reference. The naming arc across the Passerellidae quartet is itself a progression: **Song Sparrow** *Melospiza melodia* (45) = musical-musical, **Spotted Towhee** *Pipilo maculatus* (46) = acoustic-visual, **Dark-eyed Junco** *Junco hyemalis* (47) = ecological-temporal, White-crowned Sparrow *Zonotrichia leucophrys* (48) = visual-visual. The family's names progressively abandon sound. Five subspecies of White-crowned Sparrow: *pugetensis* (PNW coastal, short-distance migrant, dark lores, yellow-orange bill — LITERALLY NAMED FOR PUGET SOUND, described by Ridgway 1898), *nuttalli* (California coast, resident), *gambelii* (western taiga, long-distance), *oriantha* (mountain west, medium-distance), *leucophrys* (eastern taiga, long-distance). The *nuttalli-pugetensis* pair forms the most genetically distinct clade within the species. And yet the cultural boundaries between dialects are not genetic: **Douglas Nelson** and colleagues (Nelson 2000, Soha et al. 2004, Poesel et al. 2017) have tracked the twelve *pugetensis* dialects along the Puget Sound coast across three decades, demonstrating that dialect boundaries are stable but not barriers to gene flow. The song is learned, not inherited.

The dialect is the organizing principle: a culturally transmitted pattern of vocal structure mapping to specific geographic areas, stable across decades, learned during the first 10-100 days of life. ## Part B: White-crowned Sparrow — The Dialect

Full deep research covering the White-crowned Sparrow as the engine's namesake bird, Peter Marler's foundational model organism for vocal learning, the completion of the Passerellidae quartet with four genera and four habitats, and the dialect as the first acoustic element defined by cultural transmission:

- **Zonotrichia leucophrys — "The White-Browed Banded-Hair Bird," Forster 1772, Four Genera in the Passerellidae Quartet.** *Zonotrichia* from Ancient Greek *zone* (band) + *thrix* (hair) = "banded hair," referring to the bold striped head pattern. *Leucophrys* from *leukos* (white) + *ophrys* (eyebrow) = "white-browed." Full translation: "the white-browed banded-hair bird" — entirely visual, no acoustic reference. The naming arc across the quartet: Song Sparrow *Melospiza melodia* = musical-musical, Spotted Towhee *Pipilo maculatus* = acoustic-visual, Dark-eyed Junco *Junco hyemalis* = ecological-temporal, White-crowned Sparrow *Zonotrichia leucophrys* = visual-visual. The family's names progressively abandon sound. Five subspecies: *pugetensis* (PNW coastal, short-distance migrant, dark lores, yellow-orange bill — LITERALLY NAMED FOR PUGET SOUND, described by Ridgway 1898), *nuttalli* (California coast, resident), *gambelii* (western taiga, long-distance), *oriantha* (mountain west, medium-distance), *leucophrys* (eastern taiga, long-distance).
- **The Pugetensis Connection — The Project Names the Bird, Described from Puget Sound Type Specimen, 12 Coastal Dialects.** *Zonotrichia leucophrys pugetensis* — the only bird in the engine's forty-nine-degree history whose subspecific epithet IS the name of the project's subject. The engine is called "The Sound of Puget Sound." The subspecies is called *pugetensis.* The Sound names the bird and the bird names the Sound. *Pugetensis* breeds from coastal southeastern Alaska south through coastal British Columbia, western Washington, western Oregon, and into northwestern California. In Washington, *pugetensis* breeds in brushy habitats from coastal scrub to mountain meadow edges. Winters in Pacific lowlands. Described by Ridgway in 1898 from a Puget Sound type specimen. Forms approximately 12 distinct song dialects along the Pacific Northwest coast, mapped and tracked across decades by Nelson, Soha, Poesel, and others. The *pugetensis* dialects literally constitute "the sound of Puget Sound."
- **Fourth Consecutive Passerellidae — Song Sparrow (45) + Spotted Towhee (46) + Dark-eyed Junco (47) + White-crowned Sparrow (48), Four Genera Four Habitats Four Song Strategies.** The Passerellidae quartet deploys four different genera — a feat no other family streak has matched. *Melospiza* (45) at wetland edge with improvised complex song. *Pipilo* (46) at dense understory with concentrated textural rasp. *Junco* (47) at forest floor with elemental reduction. *Zonotrichia* (48) at mountain meadow edge with culturally transmitted dialect. Four habitats creating a complete ground-level transect from pond to summit. Four acoustic strategies: elaboration, concentration, reduction, transmission. The quartet ties the Parulidae quintet (38-42) for longest same-family streak outside the swallow quintet (26-30). Its four different genera make it the most genus-diverse family streak in the engine.
- **The Dialect — Forty-Ninth Acoustic Element (Species-Side), First Element Defined by Cultural Transmission.** The White-crowned Sparrow's song is a multi-part structure: clear introductory whistle (species ID) + complex notes (individual ID) + buzzy trills (broadband energy) + terminal trill (dialect/population ID). Three layers of identity in 2-3 seconds. The dialect is the organizing principle: a culturally transmitted pattern of vocal structure mapping to specific geographic areas, stable across decades, learned during the first 10-100 days of life. Named "the dialect" for three resonances: (1) the bird's identity as THE model species for dialect research (Marler & Tamura 1964); (2) the Puget Sound connection — *pugetensis* forms ~12 dialects along the PNW coast; (3) the acoustic principle — a dialect is a regional variant of a shared communication system. Distinct from the preamble (45, positional structure), the rasp (46, textural intensity), and the snowbird (47, elemental reduction). The dialect is the first element defined not by the physical properties of a sound but by the social system that produces it.
- **Peter Marler's Bird — The Species That Taught Science How Song Is Learned, Marler and Tamura 1964 Science.** Marler and Tamura published "Culturally Transmitted Patterns of Vocal Behavior in Sparrows" in *Science* (1964) — one of the most cited papers in behavioral ecology. Key discoveries: young birds learn song during the first 10-100 days of life; an innate auditory template biases them toward conspecific songs; song is culturally transmitted, not genetically inherited; deafening before crystallization prevents normal development. Marler also studied Song Sparrow (degree 45), showing it has a longer, more flexible sensitive period and learns multiple song types. Two Passerellidae, two learning strategies, one researcher. The quartet's three great researchers span nearly a century: Margaret Morse Nice (Song Sparrow, 1930s-1960s, behavioral ecology), Peter Marler (White-crowned Sparrow and Song Sparrow, 1960s-2000s, vocal learning), Ellen Ketterson (Dark-eyed Junco, 1970s-present, endocrine control). The Spotted Towhee (degree 46) is the quartet's non-model species.
- **The COVID Song Experiment — Derryberry 2020 Science, San Francisco Lockdowns, Birds Sang 35% More Quietly, Three Temporal Scales Simultaneously.** During spring 2020 COVID lockdowns, White-crowned Sparrows in San Francisco rewrote their songs in real time. Derryberry documented: ~35% quieter singing (far more than the 3% Lombard effect predicts), lowered minimum frequency, widened bandwidth expanding into ranges previously masked by traffic noise, songs resembling 1970s recordings before noise increase. Paradoxically, the quieter songs traveled farther and were more attractive to females. The COVID experiment revealed a third timescale of vocal change: real-time behavioral adjustment within a crystallized song, distinct from dialect learning (cultural, decades) and subspecies divergence (evolutionary, millennia). Three temporal scales, one species, one song. The engine's most temporally complex acoustic subject.

Complex note portion changes 2.3 times more than the terminal trill. Functional differentiation: whistle = species, trill = population, complex notes = individual. Despite sharp dialect boundaries, genetic analysis reveals no tendency for within-dialect birds to be more closely related (Soha 2004, Poesel 2017). Dialect boundaries are not barriers to gene flow. - **Four consecutive folk-adjacent degrees reveal that genre-family runs expose internal diversity rather than monotony.** The engine's longest single-genre run (45-48) produces four distinct production approaches sharing one confessional root. Future sustained genre commitments should be embraced, not avoided — they are where the engine discovers what a genre actually contains.

- **The held rest extends the acoustic taxonomy into rhythm-based silence, distinct from volume-based silence.** The whisper (31) and the murmur (45) were about vocal volume. The held rest is about rhythmic structure. The engine's acoustic taxonomy now has two independent axes for silence: volume (how loudly or softly a sound is made) and rhythm (where silence sits in the beat structure). Future acoustic elements should be sorted on both axes.
- **Pugetensis is a structural gift that validates the paired-engine architecture.** The engine did not plan the *pugetensis* convergence; it discovered it. Future degrees should be attentive to unplanned alignments between Part A and Part B — they are evidence that the paired architecture surfaces real resonances rather than inventing them.
- **The dialect introduces culturally transmitted sounds as a first-class acoustic category.** The engine previously classified acoustic elements by physical properties (frequency, duration, timbre, positional structure, textural intensity). The dialect adds a new classification axis: social origin. Sounds exist because of learning systems, not only because of physics. Future degrees should watch for sounds whose identity derives from cultural transmission rather than physical production.
- **E=2 symmetry on both sides produces the strongest paired-engine resonance of the folk run.** Degrees 45 and 48 both share E=2 on both sides, and both produce the deepest cross-sync axes (quiet-prolific, cultural-transmission, slow-bloom-and-dialect, study-subject). - **The Passerellidae quartet demonstrates that genus diversity within a family commitment is possible and valuable.** Four different genera in four consecutive degrees is unprecedented. - **The CSV substitution discipline remains the gold standard for transparent departures.** Four consecutive amphibian replacements have all been documented inline, with original species named, reason stated, and preserved structural elements identified. This discipline should be applied to any future scope-rule enforcement: substitute transparently, document the original, preserve the structural load, and cite the rule.

The engine's acoustic taxonomy now spans three classes: content-based (most early elements), positional (the preamble), and cultural (the dialect).
