## Mission Overview

Suomi NPP is the obs#29 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — the NASA–NOAA free-flying polar-orbiting observatory, originally the NPOESS Preparatory Project, that images and sounds the whole Earth twice a day, by day and by night, and bridges the NASA Earth Observing System to the operational Joint Polar Satellite System. ★ Suomi NPP is a partnership of NASA (which built and launched the observatory) and NOAA (which, with NASA, operates it); it was renamed the Suomi National Polar-orbiting Partnership on 24 January 2012 to honour Verner E. Suomi, a father of satellite meteorology. ★★ Its instruments are VIIRS (the Visible Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite) — a whole-Earth cross-track imaging radiometer with 22 bands from ~0.4 to ~12.5 µm (16 moderate bands at 750 m, 5 imaging bands at 375 m, and the panchromatic Day/Night Band ~500–900 nm for low-light night imaging) over a ~3000 km swath, built by Raytheon; CrIS (the Cross-track Infrared Sounder) — a hyperspectral Michelson-interferometer infrared sounder of atmospheric temperature and moisture profiles, built by ITT; ATMS (the Advanced Technology Microwave Sounder) — a 22-channel microwave sounder, built by Northrop Grumman; OMPS (the Ozone Mapping and Profiler Suite) — a nadir mapper plus a nadir/limb profiler for total-column and vertical-profile ozone, built by Ball Aerospace; and CERES (the Clouds and the Earth's Radiant Energy System) — a broadband radiometer for Earth's radiation budget. ★ VIIRS is the operational successor to MODIS and AVHRR, CrIS the successor to AIRS, and OMPS the successor to OMI and TOMS — Suomi NPP carrying the first flight of the VIIRS/CrIS/ATMS/OMPS payload. ★ It images and sounds the whole Earth twice daily for clouds, aerosols, land surface, ocean colour, sea-surface temperature, snow and ice, active fires, vegetation, and the Day/Night-Band nightlights (VIIRS); atmospheric temperature and moisture profiles (CrIS and ATMS); total-column and profile ozone (OMPS); and the radiation budget (CERES) — feeding both climate science and operational weather forecasting; the data are open. Suomi NPP watches the whole Earth across the threshold from day into night, reading the clouds and the aerosols, the ocean colour and the sea-surface temperature, the fires and the city lights — the whole living Earth watched day and night. The mission launches into the story on 28 October 2011 (09:48 UTC) on a United Launch Alliance Delta II 7920-10C from Vandenberg Air Force Base (SLC-2W), and the observatory is renamed the Suomi National Polar-orbiting Partnership on 24 January 2012 to honour Verner E. Suomi. The story is the Delta II launch from Vandenberg, the free-flying sun-synchronous ~824 km orbit, the VIIRS cross-track scan sweeping the ~3000 km swath as the observatory flies, the 22 reflective and emissive bands and the Day/Night Band resolving the whole Earth at 375 and 750 m, and the CrIS, ATMS, and OMPS sounders reading the atmosphere below — the whole Earth imaged and sounded, twice a day, by day and by night. The science is central: from the sun-synchronous orbit Suomi NPP records the reflected sunlight and the emitted radiation of the whole Earth and turns them, by the radiometric calibration and the imaging and sounding retrievals, into the operational and climate record of the whole Earth day and night. ★★★ Suomi NPP is OPERATING, so the catalog frames the whole MISSION in the PRESENT (Suomi NPP images the whole Earth, sounds the atmosphere, records the nightlights, and observes the day-into-night threshold); it launched 28 October 2011, was renamed on 24 January 2012, and operates today — the launch and the renaming being the only past events. Suomi NPP's firsts are scoped carefully: it is the NASA–NOAA bridge from the Earth Observing System to the Joint Polar Satellite System, the preparatory mission carrying the first flight of the VIIRS/CrIS/ATMS/OMPS operational sounding-and-imaging payload, and VIIRS brought the Day/Night Band for whole-Earth day-and-night imaging. ★ The scope is load-bearing: weather satellites preceded it (TIROS-1 launched 1960), so Suomi NPP is NOT "the first weather satellite," NOT "the first polar-orbiting satellite," and NOT "the first Earth-observing satellite"; its distinction is the NASA–NOAA EOS→JPSS bridge, the first VIIRS/CrIS/ATMS/OMPS flight, and the VIIRS Day/Night Band. To the memory of Verner E. Suomi, a father of satellite meteorology, for whom the mission is named. To all who keep the data open and free and who joined this observatory with the satellites that followed it.
