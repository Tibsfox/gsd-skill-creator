## Surprises

- **A satellite that watches the whole Earth by day and by night.** Where a daytime imager sees only the sunlit half of each pass, VIIRS carries the Day/Night Band — a panchromatic low-light channel sensitive enough to image by moonlight and city-light. Suomi NPP therefore reads the whole Earth on both halves of the orbit: the daylit clouds and land on one side, and the nightlights, auroras, and fires on the other — the whole living Earth watched day and night.
- **A bridge that carried a payload from one era into the next.** Suomi NPP does not stand alone; it is the bridge from the NASA Earth Observing System (Terra, Aqua, Aura) to the operational Joint Polar Satellite System. The VIIRS, CrIS, ATMS, and OMPS instruments flew for the first time here as a preparatory / risk-reduction payload, and NOAA-20 and NOAA-21 then carried the same design forward — one observatory joining two eras of Earth observation.
- **Instruments that succeed the flagships before them.** VIIRS is the operational successor to MODIS and AVHRR, CrIS to the AIRS hyperspectral sounder, and OMPS to the OMI and TOMS ozone mappers. Suomi NPP carries a whole payload of successors — a moderate-resolution whole-Earth imager, a hyperspectral infrared sounder, a microwave sounder, and an ozone mapper — each inheriting and continuing the measurement of an Earth Observing System flagship.
- **A satellite named for a father of satellite meteorology.** The observatory launched as NPP, the NPOESS Preparatory Project, and on 24 January 2012 it was renamed the Suomi National Polar-orbiting Partnership to honour Verner E. Suomi, a father of satellite meteorology — a name given to the mission that watches the whole Earth's weather and climate day and night.
- **A nocturnal insect-hunter of the Pacific Northwest.** The Big Brown Bat (*Eptesicus fuscus*, family Vespertilionidae), a nocturnal insect-hunting bat of the forests, riparian corridors, and farmland edges of the Pacific Northwest, emerges at dusk to hunt beetles and moths by echolocation through the night that Suomi NPP watches from above with its Day/Night Band (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A night-blooming forb of the interior shrub-steppe.** The Pale Evening-primrose (*Oenothera pallida*, family Onagraceae), a low perennial of the sandy shrub-steppe and open ground of the interior Pacific Northwest, opens its pale white flowers at dusk and fades them by the next midday, pollinated by night-flying moths — a plant of the day-into-night threshold, bound with the throughline of the watch of day and night.
