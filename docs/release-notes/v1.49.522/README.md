## Summary

The twenty-second paired release and the engine's most consequential NASA mission since Freedom 7. On February 20, 1962, at Launch Complex 14 at Cape Canaveral, an Atlas LV-3B -- serial number 109-D, a pressurized stainless-steel balloon with walls thinner than a dime that would collapse under its own weight if depressurized -- ignited its three engines and lifted Mercury spacecraft number 13 toward orbit. Inside was John Herschel Glenn Jr., a forty-year-old Marine colonel from New Concord, Ohio, who had flown 59 combat missions in F4U Corsairs over the Marshall Islands in World War II, 63 combat missions in F9F Panthers and F-86 Sabres over Korea (three MiG kills in the last nine days of the war), who had made the first supersonic transcontinental flight in a Vought F8U-1P Crusader, who had been selected as one of the Mercury Seven, who had been assigned the orbital mission while Shepard and Grissom flew suborbital arcs, who had endured ten launch delays between December 1961 and February 1962 -- ten times awakened before dawn, suited up, driven to the pad, inserted into the capsule, and told to stand down. Ten times. The Atlas had a documented history of spectacular failures. Its steel-balloon construction meant that any loss of pressurization was instantly catastrophic. Two successful orbital flights preceded Glenn's -- one unmanned, one with a chimpanzee named Enos who was shocked for correct responses and kept pulling the correct lever. The qualification was statistical, not absolute. Glenn rode the pressurized balloon to orbital velocity anyway.

Scott Carpenter was the backup pilot. He had trained alongside Glenn, run the same simulations, worn the same suit, known the same procedures. Carpenter's role on launch day was CapCom at the Cape. As the Atlas cleared the tower and the clocks started, Carpenter keyed his microphone and said four words that became the most famous utterance of the Mercury program: "Godspeed, John Glenn." The Atlas burned for four minutes and fifty-three seconds. The booster engines cut off at 130 seconds and were jettisoned -- the stage-and-a-half design dropping its side engines while the sustainer continued on the same tanks, the same pressurization system, no restart capability, no redundancy. Glenn reached orbit: 161 by 265 kilometers, 88.3-minute period. He was the first American to orbit the Earth. Not the first human -- Gagarin had orbited eleven months earlier, Titov had orbited seventeen times for twenty-five hours -- but the first American, and the flight that transformed the space program from a technical experiment into a national institution. Forty million Americans watched on television. Three orbits. Four hours and fifty-five minutes. Glenn saw four sunsets and four sunrises in a single afternoon. He reported fireflies -- luminous particles floating outside the capsule window, glowing in the orbital dawn, which turned out to be ice crystals from the capsule's reaction control system sublimating in the sunlight. He saw beauty and reported data. That was Glenn.

The Atlas's Segment 51 indicator light came on during the first orbit -- a warning that the heat shield and landing bag might have come loose. If the heat shield was not locked, reentry would kill Glenn. Mercury Control debated what to do. They decided not to jettison the retropack after the retrorockets fired -- the straps holding the retropack in place might help keep the heat shield in position if it was loose. Glenn was told to leave the retropack on. He was not told why. He reentered the atmosphere with the retropack still attached, and the straps burned through during reentry, pieces of flaming debris streaming past the capsule window. Glenn reported the debris. He did not know whether it was the retropack burning or the heat shield disintegrating. He reported what he saw. "That's a real fireball outside," he said, in the same flat, factual voice he used for every other observation. The heat shield held. The indicator had been a false alarm -- a faulty sensor, not a loose shield. Glenn splashed down in the Atlantic near the recovery ship USS Noa. He was alive. The three orbits were complete. The mission was a success. The program became permanent.

Glenn was from New Concord, Ohio -- population 1,200, built around Muskingum College, a Presbyterian town where the chapel bell rang on schedule and neighbors knew each other by name. His father was a plumber and steamfitter. He married Annie Castor, his childhood sweetheart, in 1943 -- seventy-three years of marriage that lasted until his death. Annie had a severe stutter that made public speaking agonizing. She endured it through every press conference, every parade, every state dinner, every campaign. Glenn stood beside her through all of it. He was the Boy Scout of the Mercury Seven -- the one who lived his public image, who refused to participate in the after-hours culture of drinking and women that several of the others pursued, who wrote a memo suggesting the group should be more careful because the press was watching. The others found this either admirable or insufferable. Shepard was incandescent. None of it mattered when Glenn orbited the Earth three times and came home alive.
