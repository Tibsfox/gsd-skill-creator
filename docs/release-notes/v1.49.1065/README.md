## Mission Overview

The Phoenix Mars Lander is the obs#11 sustain of the MARS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA Mars lander whose defining achievements are the first landing in the Martian arctic, the first in-situ touch and confirmation of water ice on Mars, and the first detection of perchlorate in Martian soil. It was the first mission of the Mars Scout Program (MAVEN was the second and final), led by principal investigator Peter H. Smith of the University of Arizona, managed by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, with the spacecraft built by Lockheed Martin from hardware of the cancelled Mars Surveyor 2001 Lander and drawing on lessons from the lost Mars Polar Lander. A STATIONARY LANDER — not a rover and not an orbiter; it did not traverse — Phoenix carried the Robotic Arm and Robotic Arm Camera (RAC), TEGA (the Thermal and Evolved Gas Analyzer), MECA (the Microscopy, Electrochemistry, and Conductivity Analyzer, a wet-chemistry laboratory with optical and atomic-force microscopes and a conductivity probe), SSI (the Surface Stereo Imager), MARDI (the Mars Descent Imager, by Malin Space Science Systems), and a meteorological station (MET) with a Canadian-provided lidar. It launched on a Delta II 7925 from Cape Canaveral SLC-17A on 4 August 2007 (09:26 UTC) and landed on 25 May 2008 in Vastitas Borealis (the "Green Valley," ~68.22°N, ~234°E), the Martian arctic plains, by parachute followed by twelve pulsed hydrazine retro-thrusters — a propulsive soft landing (not airbags). The science is the buried ice, the soil chemistry, the weather, and the arctic sky: the robotic arm dug trenches that exposed bright sub-surface material (the "Dodo-Goldilocks" trench), the bright chunks sublimated away over several days, and TEGA heated a sample and confirmed water — the first to physically TOUCH and directly confirm water ice on Mars, in situ, distinct from Odyssey's 2002 orbital DETECTION (which guided Phoenix's landing site) and never re-claiming the Mars Exploration Rovers' ancient-mineral-water first. MECA's wet-chemistry laboratory detected perchlorate salts in the soil — the first detection of perchlorate on Mars, an unexpected oxidizer that can depress water's freezing point into brines and bears on habitability — alongside mildly alkaline soil (pH ~7.7) and calcium carbonate, while the lidar observed snow falling from water-ice clouds. Phoenix operated for about five months (a prime mission of about ninety sols, extended through the arctic summer), and its last communication came near 2 November 2008 as the arctic winter fell and the declining sunlight could no longer power the solar-powered lander; the mission was declared ended in November 2008, a successful mission that achieved its goals. ## Surprises

- **A lander reached and worked the high arctic of another planet.** Phoenix set down at ~68.22°N in Vastitas Borealis on 25 May 2008 — the first spacecraft to land in the Martian arctic, farther north than any prior lander — on a flat plain whose abundant near-surface ice had been mapped from orbit by 2001 Mars Odyssey.
- **A robotic arm uncovered buried ice and a spacecraft touched and confirmed it.** Phoenix's robotic arm dug trenches (the "Dodo-Goldilocks" trench) that exposed bright sub-surface material; the chunks sublimated away over several days, and TEGA confirmed water — the first time a spacecraft physically touched and directly confirmed water ice on Mars, in situ.
- **A wet-chemistry laboratory found perchlorate in the soil of Mars.** Phoenix's MECA detected perchlorate salts in the Martian soil (Hecht et al. 2009) — the first detection of perchlorate on Mars, an unexpected oxidizer that can lower the freezing point of water into brines, with implications for habitability and the search for organics.
- **A lidar watched snow fall from clouds on Mars.** Phoenix's meteorological station and its Canadian lidar observed water-ice clouds drifting over the landing site and snow falling from them (Whiteway et al. 2009) — the first detection of falling snow on Mars, alongside the mildly alkaline soil and calcium carbonate found in the soil.
- **An ermine is made for the cold and turns white for the snow.** The American Ermine / Short-tailed Weasel (*Mustela richardsonii*), a small, agile mustelid of the Pacific Northwest's forests, alpine meadows, and high country, is adapted to cold and snow and molts to a white winter coat to match the snowpack, staying active through the cold season (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Phoenix reaching and working the frozen Martian arctic.
- **An avens hugs the cold, exposed arctic-alpine ground.** The Mountain Avens (*Dryas octopetala*), a low, mat-forming arctic-alpine evergreen subshrub native to the cold, rocky fellfields and tundra of the high Northern Hemisphere including the high North Cascades of Washington, is a classic plant of the frozen-ground arctic-alpine zone and an early colonizer of cold, disturbed ground (ecological description only) — a mirror of Phoenix making its stand on the cold arctic ground.

## Dedication

v1.248 Phoenix Mars Lander is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's first arctic lander and gave Mars its first in-situ touch of buried water ice. The University of Arizona team led by Peter H. Smith who proposed and ran the mission; the Jet Propulsion Laboratory engineers who managed it; the Lockheed Martin builders who raised the lander from the hardware of a cancelled mission and the lessons of a lost one; the descent-and-landing team who flew the parachute and the twelve pulsed thrusters to a soft touchdown on the flat northern plain; and the instrument teams who built the robotic arm, the ovens, the wet-chemistry cells, the microscopes, the cameras, and the weather station. For one short arctic summer a lander stood at the far north of Mars, reached into the frozen ground, and confirmed by touch the ice that had been sensed from orbit — and it read the salts of the soil and watched the snow fall from the clouds before the winter closed in. The name "Phoenix" was well chosen: out of two earlier missions, one cancelled and one lost, a working lander rose, reached the arctic, and did what it set out to.
