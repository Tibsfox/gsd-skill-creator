## Surprises

- **A lander reached and worked the high arctic of another planet.** Phoenix set down at ~68.22°N in Vastitas Borealis on 25 May 2008 — the first spacecraft to land in the Martian arctic, farther north than any prior lander — on a flat plain whose abundant near-surface ice had been mapped from orbit by 2001 Mars Odyssey.
- **A robotic arm uncovered buried ice and a spacecraft touched and confirmed it.** Phoenix's robotic arm dug trenches (the "Dodo-Goldilocks" trench) that exposed bright sub-surface material; the chunks sublimated away over several days, and TEGA confirmed water — the first time a spacecraft physically touched and directly confirmed water ice on Mars, in situ.
- **A wet-chemistry laboratory found perchlorate in the soil of Mars.** Phoenix's MECA detected perchlorate salts in the Martian soil (Hecht et al. 2009) — the first detection of perchlorate on Mars, an unexpected oxidizer that can lower the freezing point of water into brines, with implications for habitability and the search for organics.
- **A lidar watched snow fall from clouds on Mars.** Phoenix's meteorological station and its Canadian lidar observed water-ice clouds drifting over the landing site and snow falling from them (Whiteway et al. 2009) — the first detection of falling snow on Mars, alongside the mildly alkaline soil and calcium carbonate found in the soil.
- **An ermine is made for the cold and turns white for the snow.** The American Ermine / Short-tailed Weasel (*Mustela richardsonii*), a small, agile mustelid of the Pacific Northwest's forests, alpine meadows, and high country, is adapted to cold and snow and molts to a white winter coat to match the snowpack, staying active through the cold season (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Phoenix reaching and working the frozen Martian arctic.
- **An avens hugs the cold, exposed arctic-alpine ground.** The Mountain Avens (*Dryas octopetala*), a low, mat-forming arctic-alpine evergreen subshrub native to the cold, rocky fellfields and tundra of the high Northern Hemisphere including the high North Cascades of Washington, is a classic plant of the frozen-ground arctic-alpine zone and an early colonizer of cold, disturbed ground (ecological description only) — a mirror of Phoenix making its stand on the cold arctic ground.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A sustain keeps the predecessor's axis vocabulary and replaces only the predecessor-specific content.** Phoenix shares the Mars-exploration vocabulary with the 2001 Mars Odyssey predecessor (Mars, the red planet, the surface, the subsurface, water ice, the soil, the atmosphere, dust, in situ, planetary exploration, the poles, the arctic) — that vocabulary is shared and correct. The rewrite removed only the Odyssey-specific content (the 2001 Mars Odyssey name, the Arthur C. Clarke "2001: A Space Odyssey" allusion, THEMIS, the GRS suite [GRS/HEND/NS], MARIE, the longest-operating-at-Mars framing, the aerobraking, the Sun-synchronous polar orbit, the relay-backbone role, the 7 April 2001 launch and 24 October 2001 orbit insertion, and the White Sturgeon + Port Orford Cedar pairing) and replaced it with Phoenix content. (William Boynton, an Odyssey-GRS PI, is also a Phoenix TEGA co-investigator — that overlap is fine.)
2. **A sustain has no axis rotation to frame — and 1.247 was itself a sustain, so there is no rotation framing to remove.** The Mars axis opened at 1.238 (Viking, substrate-axis rotation #36); 1.239 through 1.248 are all SUSTAINS, so the clone source carries no rotation narrative, and at most a single distant note records that the Mars axis itself opened at 1.238 by rotating from the X-ray-astronomy axis (rotation #35, held complete at obs#15).
3. **The water-ice first is the first to TOUCH and confirm IN SITU — Odyssey DETECTED it first from orbit.** Phoenix's robotic arm exposed bright sub-surface material, the chunks sublimated away, and TEGA confirmed water; the catalog scopes this as the first to physically TOUCH and directly confirm water ice on Mars in situ, frames it as present-day frozen water ice, notes that 2001 Mars Odyssey (obs#10) made the first global DETECTION from orbit in 2002 and helped target Phoenix's landing site, and never re-claims Odyssey's orbital detection or the Mars Exploration Rovers' (obs#4) ancient-mineral-water first.
4. **The arctic landing is "first landing in the Martian arctic / high northern latitudes" — not "first soft landing."** Phoenix landed at ~68.22°N, farther north than any prior lander; the catalog scopes this as the first landing in the Martian arctic / high northern latitudes, notes that Viking, Mars Pathfinder, and the rovers landed at lower latitudes and that Mars Polar Lander targeted the SOUTH pole but was lost on descent and never landed, and describes the propulsive landing without anchoring it (Viking landed propulsively in 1976).
5. **The perchlorate first is the first detection of perchlorate in Martian soil.** MECA's wet-chemistry laboratory detected perchlorate salts in the soil (Hecht et al. 2009); the catalog scopes this as the first detection of perchlorate in Martian soil, notes the oxidizer / brine / habitability implications, and holds the mildly alkaline soil, the calcium carbonate, and the lidar's snow from clouds as science results alongside the perchlorate anchor.
6. **Phoenix is a STATIONARY LANDER — never a rover or orbiter.** Phoenix worked from one place on the arctic plain; it did not rove or orbit. The catalog never describes Phoenix driving or orbiting and never re-claims any rover or orbiter first.
7. **The mission is framed positively and in the past tense — Phoenix achieved its goals and ended in November 2008.** Phoenix operated about five months and ended near 2 November 2008 as the arctic winter set in and the declining sunlight could no longer power the solar-powered lander; the catalog frames the mission positively as a successful mission that achieved its goals, in the past tense, with the ending stated once at low density.
