   Phoenix shares the Mars-exploration vocabulary with the 2001 Mars Odyssey predecessor (Mars, the red planet, the surface, the subsurface, water ice, the soil, the atmosphere, dust, in situ, planetary exploration, the poles, the arctic) — that vocabulary is shared and correct. 2. **A sustain has no axis rotation to frame — and 1.247 was itself a sustain, so there is no rotation framing to remove.**
   The Mars axis opened at 1.238 (Viking, substrate-axis rotation #36); 1.239 through 1.248 are all SUSTAINS, so the clone source carries no rotation narrative, and at most a single distant note records that the Mars axis itself opened at 1.238 by rotating from the X-ray-astronomy axis (rotation #35, held complete at obs#15).

3. **The water-ice first is the first to TOUCH and confirm IN SITU — Odyssey DETECTED it first from orbit.**
   Phoenix's robotic arm exposed bright sub-surface material, the chunks sublimated away, and TEGA confirmed water; the catalog scopes this as the first to physically TOUCH and directly confirm water ice on Mars in situ, frames it as present-day frozen water ice, notes that 2001 Mars Odyssey (obs#10) made the first global DETECTION from orbit in 2002 and helped target Phoenix's landing site, and never re-claims Odyssey's orbital detection or the Mars Exploration Rovers' (obs#4) ancient-mineral-water first.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12374_

4. **The arctic landing is "first landing in the Martian arctic / high northern latitudes" — not "first soft landing.**
   Phoenix landed at ~68.22°N, farther north than any prior lander; the catalog scopes this as the first landing in the Martian arctic / high northern latitudes, notes that Viking, Mars Pathfinder, and the rovers landed at lower latitudes and that Mars Polar Lander targeted the SOUTH pole but was lost on descent and never landed, and describes the propulsive landing without anchoring it (Viking landed propulsively in 1976).
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12375_

5. **The perchlorate first is the first detection of perchlorate in Martian soil.**
   MECA's wet-chemistry laboratory detected perchlorate salts in the soil (Hecht et al. 2009); the catalog scopes this as the first detection of perchlorate in Martian soil, notes the oxidizer / brine / habitability implications, and holds the mildly alkaline soil, the calcium carbonate, and the lidar's snow from clouds as science results alongside the perchlorate anchor.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12376_

6. **Phoenix is a STATIONARY LANDER — never a rover or orbiter.**
   Phoenix worked from one place on the arctic plain; it did not rove or orbit. The catalog never describes Phoenix driving or orbiting and never re-claims any rover or orbiter first.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12377_

7. **The mission is framed positively and in the past tense — Phoenix achieved its goals and ended in November 2008.**
   Phoenix operated about five months and ended near 2 November 2008 as the arctic winter set in and the declining sunlight could no longer power the solar-powered lander; the catalog frames the mission positively as a successful mission that achieved its goals, in the past tense, with the ending stated once at low density.

10. **The water-ice and arctic-landing scopes are the load-bearing guards of this sustain.**
