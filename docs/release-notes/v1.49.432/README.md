- ***Larus occidentalis* Taxonomy and the "Western Gull" Etymology.** The Western Gull is scientifically named *Larus occidentalis* — with *Larus* as the Latin-derived genus name for gulls (shared with *L. glaucescens*, *L. argentatus*, and other large gulls) and *occidentalis* as the species name meaning "of the west" or "western" (from the Latin *occidens*, "west," referring to the direction of the setting sun and to the Pacific coast range of the species). The species was described by **John Cassin** in 1840 based on specimens collected from the Pacific coast of North America, and the type locality is the California coast. The Western Gull is the Pacific coast's defining large breeding gull in the same sense that the **Herring Gull** (*Larus argentatus*) is the Atlantic coast's defining large gull and the **Glaucous-winged Gull** (*Larus glaucescens*) is the Pacific Northwest range-core gull. The three large-gull species' range cores are geographically distinct (Atlantic, Pacific Northwest, Pacific coast), and the engine's documentation preserves the range-core differentiation at first-class resolution through the three consecutive Laridae entries at degrees 289-291.

The species is primarily a year-round resident within its breeding range, with some post-breeding dispersal northward along the Pacific coast. The range overlaps with the **Glaucous-winged Gull** in Washington, Oregon, and northern California, and extensive hybridization between the two species produces a recognized hybrid zone that is taxonomically load-bearing. The Farallon colony is documented at first-class ornithological resolution through the **Point Blue Conservation Science** (formerly **PRBO Conservation Science**) long-term monitoring program, which has conducted continuous Farallon seabird research since 1968 and maintains one of the longest-running seabird colony datasets in North America. The hybridization between *L. occidentalis* and *L. glaucescens* is sufficiently extensive that individual gulls in the hybrid zone can display phenotypic characteristics (wingtip coloration, bill size and pattern, mantle coloration) that are intermediate between the two parent species and that resist definitive species-level assignment without extensive analysis.
