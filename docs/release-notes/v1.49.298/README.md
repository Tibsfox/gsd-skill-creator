## Taxonomic State

- **Family:** Phasianidae (6th and FINAL entry -- Wild Turkey 153, Blue Grouse 154, Ruffed Grouse 155, Sooty Grouse 156, Spruce Grouse 157, White-tailed Ptarmigan 158)
- **Order:** Galliformes (closing)
- **Class:** Aves
- **Species note:** The White-tailed Ptarmigan (*Lagopus leucura*) is the smallest grouse in North America and the only ptarmigan species endemic to the continent's alpine zones. In the Pacific Northwest, it inhabits the high alpine tundra of the Cascades, typically above 6,000 feet, where it lives year-round among rocks, snowfields, and low-growing alpine vegetation. The **cackling and clucks** are soft, intimate vocalizations -- quiet clucking contact calls between flock members and a distinctive cackling alarm call, all delivered at low volume suited to the open, windswept alpine environment where sound carries easily. E=3 reflects this restraint -- the ptarmigan has no need to project through dense forest canopy like the grouse below treeline. The **pure white winter plumage** is the species' most striking feature. Through a complete molt in autumn, every feather transforms from mottled brown, gray, and white (summer) to solid, brilliant white (winter). Only the dark eyes and bill remain visible against the snow. The feathered feet -- unique among the engine's species -- serve as snowshoes for walking on powder snow. This is one of only three North American bird species that turn fully white in winter (along with the Willow Ptarmigan and Rock Ptarmigan). As the **6th and FINAL Phasianidae**, the White-tailed Ptarmigan closes the longest single-family run in engine history. The six-degree sequence (153-158) demonstrated the full ecological range of the family: from lowland agricultural edges (Wild Turkey) through montane forests (Blue Grouse, Ruffed Grouse, Sooty Grouse), subalpine spruce (Spruce Grouse), to alpine tundra above treeline (White-tailed Ptarmigan). The acoustic energy descended in parallel with elevation gain -- from E=6 (Wild Turkey) to E=3 (ptarmigan), a pattern that reflects the thinning soundscape as habitat opens and vegetation recedes. Six species, one family, the full elevational gradient of the Pacific Northwest.
