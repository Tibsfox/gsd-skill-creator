COBE is a NASA Explorers Program mission (Explorer 66), managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center. The spacecraft reached space on 18 November 1989 aboard a Delta 5920-8 rocket from Vandenberg Air Force Base, into a Sun-synchronous near-polar orbit at about 900 kilometers, and returned data until it was deactivated on 23 December 1993 — roughly four years, well beyond its six-month planned life. Its COSPAR/NSSDC ID is 1989-089A. COBE carried three instruments cooled and shielded to read the faint sky: FIRAS, the Far Infrared Absolute Spectrophotometer; DMR, the Differential Microwave Radiometer; and DIRBE, the Diffuse Infrared Background Experiment. A quiet, highly successful observatory, COBE measured the relic radiation with a precision no instrument had reached before.

The signature of COBE at this degree is the opening of the COSMOLOGY / cosmic-microwave-background axis at obs#1. No prior space observatory had been built to measure the microwave background with precision; COBE read the oldest light in the universe and turned a descriptive cosmology into a measured science, opening the axis at obs#1.

The microwave background was measured as a near-perfect blackbody. FIRAS measured the spectrum of the cosmic microwave background and found it to match the curve of a blackbody at about 2.725 kelvin with extraordinary precision — the most perfect blackbody spectrum measured in nature, a direct confirmation that the radiation is the cooled relic of a hot early universe.

The faint variations across the background were detected for the first time. DMR mapped the cosmic microwave background across the whole sky and detected, for the first time, the tiny temperature fluctuations from place to place — variations of a few parts in a hundred thousand, announced on 23 April 1992 — the primordial seeds from which galaxies and the large-scale structure of the universe later grew.

The diffuse infrared glow of the sky was surveyed. DIRBE measured the diffuse infrared brightness of the whole sky across many wavelengths, separating the foreground light of dust in our own galaxy from the faint, uniform glow of the distant universe — an early measurement of the cosmic infrared background, the accumulated light of all the stars and galaxies that have ever formed.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis opens when a new way of seeing enters the catalog.** By turning the catalog's gaze from the bodies of the solar system to the relic radiation of the early universe, COBE opened a substrate-axis distinct from the planetary axis — the cosmology / cosmic-microwave-background axis — at obs#1, showing that the forward continuation rotates to a fresh way of seeing once a prior axis has earned its three observations and capped.
2. **A faint signal becomes a measured fact with the right cold instrument.** By cooling and shielding its instruments from the Earth, the Sun, and the Moon, COBE read a signal far too faint for ordinary observation and measured the spectrum of the microwave background as a near-perfect blackbody, showing that precision at the edge of the faint sky is an engineering achievement as much as a scientific one.
3. **The most precise blackbody in nature is the relic of a hot early universe.** By matching the measured spectrum to a blackbody curve at about 2.725 kelvin with extraordinary precision, FIRAS confirmed the background as the cooled afterglow of a hot early universe, showing that a single careful spectrum can turn a prediction into a measured fact.
4. **The seeds of all later structure are written in faint variations.** By detecting, for the first time, the tiny temperature fluctuations across the background — a few parts in a hundred thousand — DMR revealed the primordial seeds from which galaxies and the large-scale structure of the universe later grew, showing that the largest structures trace back to the smallest measured ripples.
5. **A diffuse glow carries the light of every star that has ever formed.** By separating the foreground dust of our own galaxy from the faint uniform glow of the distant universe, DIRBE measured the diffuse cosmic infrared background, showing that the accumulated light of all the stars and galaxies that have ever formed can be read as a single faint signal.
6. **A precise measurement turns a descriptive science into a measured one.** By measuring the blackbody spectrum and detecting the anisotropies, COBE turned cosmology from a largely descriptive science into a precise measurement of the early universe, showing that an observatory can open an entire era of precision — an achievement recognized by the 2006 Nobel Prize.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across an axis opening.** Because COBE reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.214 Dawn template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content from the Vesta-and-Ceres orbiter to the cosmic-microwave-background observatory while keeping the warm palette, making the axis-opener build a clean intra-template rewrite even as the substrate-axis rotates.

## Dedication

v1.215 COBE is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's first space observatory for the cosmic microwave background, the mission that read the oldest light in the universe and turned cosmology into a precise measurement: the COBE team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, who built and operated this Explorers Program observatory; the principal investigators John Mather and George Smoot, recognized by the 2006 Nobel Prize for the blackbody spectrum and the anisotropies; the FIRAS instrument team, who measured the spectrum of the relic radiation as a near-perfect blackbody at about 2.725 kelvin; the DMR instrument team, who detected for the first time the faint variations across the sky that are the seeds of cosmic structure; the DIRBE instrument team, who surveyed the diffuse infrared glow of the distant universe; the cryogenics and shielding engineering teams, whose cold, carefully shielded design let the faint signal be read against the deep cold of space; the Explorers Program; and the Deep Space Network, whose tracking carried every command and every measurement. COBE measured the afterglow of the early universe, and opened the era of precision cosmology from space.
