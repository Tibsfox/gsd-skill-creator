## Taxonomic State

- **Species note:** The Band-tailed Pigeon (*Patagioenas fasciata*) is the largest native pigeon in North America and the closest living relative of the extinct Passenger Pigeon. Its **deep, owl-like coo-coo** -- a low, resonant "whoo-whoo" repeated in slow cadence -- reverberates through old-growth conifer forests and is routinely mistaken for an owl by hikers unfamiliar with the species. The "band" in its name refers to the pale grey terminal band on the tail, visible in flight. Band-tailed Pigeons are the only North American pigeon that regularly visits **mineral springs**, where flocks congregate to drink sodium-rich water and ingest mineral-laden clay -- a behavior called geophagy that supplements their otherwise sodium-poor diet of acorns, berries, and seeds. These mineral sites have been used continuously for thousands of years; some are shared with other species, but Band-tails dominate. Historically overhunted in the early 20th century (their flocking behavior made them vulnerable to market hunting, echoing the fate of the Passenger Pigeon), populations have recovered under strict harvest management but remain below historical levels.
