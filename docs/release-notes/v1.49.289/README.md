**Part B: The Band-tailed Pigeon — First Columbidae, Native Wild Pigeon, Largest Pigeon in North America.** The **Band-tailed Pigeon** (*Patagioenas fasciata*) enters the engine at degree 149 as the **first Columbidae** and the native wild pigeon of western North America. This is the pigeon most people don't know exists because the ubiquitous **Rock Pigeon** (*Columba livia*) has colonized every sidewalk, every park, every bridge, every overpass, every grain storage facility, and every urban ledge. But the Band-tailed Pigeon is a fundamentally different bird: **length 33-40 cm** (versus 29-37 cm for the Rock Pigeon), **weight 300-450 grams** (versus 240-380 grams), and **far more colorful** with a purple-tinged breast, blue-gray back, yellow bill with dark tip, a diagnostic white crescent on the back of the neck, a gray terminal band on the tail (hence "band-tailed"), and an iridescent green-purple nape. The habitat is forest canopy — not cities, not parks, not sidewalks — with primary occupation of old-growth conifer, mixed conifer-deciduous, and montane forest. The range extends from southern British Columbia to Baja California along the Pacific coast with mountain populations from Colorado to Central America, and the Pacific Northwest is the core of the northern range. The Band-tailed Pigeon plays important **ecological roles** that distinguish it from its feral cousin: it is an important seed disperser for Pacific coast forests, transporting fruits and acorns over considerable distances with a large gape and digestive system that handles foods smaller birds cannot; it is one of the few PNW birds that specializes in acorns from **Garry oak** and **tanoak** and other oak species (making it a member of oak woodland ecology, which is itself a threatened habitat in the PNW); and it contributes to forest regeneration after fire, logging, or windthrow disturbance through its seed-dispersing behavior.

**The Mineral Spring Congregations — Geophagy, Traditional Sites, Flocks of Hundreds.** One of the most distinctive behaviors of the Band-tailed Pigeon is its **congregation at mineral springs**. Flocks of dozens to hundreds of pigeons gather at natural mineral springs, mineral licks, and areas of mineral-rich soil to drink sodium-rich water and consume mineral-laden soil and mud. This is **geophagy** — deliberate mineral consumption — and the reason is dietary: the pigeons' diet of fruits, nuts, and grain is low in sodium and other minerals, and the mineral springs provide essential sodium, calcium, and other mineral supplements. The same mineral spring sites are used year after year, generation after generation. The pigeons appear to learn the locations from their parents and from flock mates, and some mineral spring sites have been documented as pigeon gathering places for decades. Several mineral spring sites in Washington and Oregon are well-known Band-tailed Pigeon gathering points — typically in forested areas at low to mid elevation, often near streams or seeps. The mineral spring congregations also function as **community behavior** in an otherwise dispersed population. Flocks at mineral springs can number 50-200+ birds. The birds perch in surrounding trees, then descend to the spring in groups. Social interactions at the spring include pair bonding, dominance displays, and juvenile learning. The spring functions as a gathering point for an otherwise scattered population — the avian equivalent of a community center, a church, or an open mic. And the **vulnerability** that comes with these congregations is the same vulnerability that drove the Passenger Pigeon to extinction: when dozens of birds gather at a single predictable site, they become easy targets for hunters, predators, and disturbance. Historical market hunting at mineral springs contributed substantially to the Band-tailed Pigeon's population decline, and the 2-3% per year decline documented by the Breeding Bird Survey reflects the continued cost of concentration.

**The Passenger Pigeon Parallel — Extinction in 1914 and the Cost of Concentration.** The Band-tailed Pigeon's decline trajectory explicitly invokes the extinction of the **Passenger Pigeon** (*Ectopistes migratorius*), the closely related species that went from billions of individuals in the mid-1800s to zero with **Martha's** death at the **Cincinnati Zoo** on September 1, **1914**. The Passenger Pigeon was driven to extinction by market hunting that exploited the species' tendency to form enormous flocks and communal roosts. The Band-tailed Pigeon never reached Passenger Pigeon numbers, but the pattern of overhunting a concentrated flock-forming pigeon species is structurally identical. Historical abundance was substantial — early naturalists described flocks of thousands, and the species was one of the most common large birds in PNW forests — but commercial hunting in the late 1800s and early 1900s at mineral spring congregations reduced populations dramatically. Forest clearing, old-growth logging, and urban expansion have continued to erode habitat. Current populations have declined 2-3% per year in recent decades according to Breeding Bird Survey data. The Pacific coast population is estimated at 1.5-2 million birds — a fraction of historical numbers. The species is not listed as endangered but is classified as a species of conservation concern in multiple states. Hunting regulation is now strict (short seasons and low bag limits), but hunting pressure continues to contribute to population stress. The **Passenger Pigeon parallel** is one of the most important cautionary tales in North American ornithology, and the engine invokes it at degree 149 deliberately to foreground the structural lesson: a concentrated flock-forming pigeon species is especially vulnerable, and the Band-tailed Pigeon's current trajectory is a slower replay of the pattern that destroyed its closest relative.

**The Deep Owl-Like Coo and the Canopy Acoustic Register.** The Band-tailed Pigeon's acoustic contribution to the engine is the **deep background coo of the summer forest**. The individual call is a deep, resonant "coo-COO" or "whoo-OOO" — two notes, the second slightly higher and more emphatic, at a fundamental frequency of approximately **300-600 Hz**, surprisingly low for a pigeon and often mistaken for an owl. The call is repeated 3-6 times with 2-3 second pauses between repetitions, then silence. The sporadic pattern creates an intermittent forest soundscape rather than a sustained vocalization. The tonal quality is rich, resonant, with a breathy quality produced by the syrinx and amplified by the inflated crop. The resonance gives the call its owl-like depth. The call carries 100-200 meters through forest. Canopy foliage attenuates high frequencies, so the low-frequency fundamental carries farthest, and at distance the call is a diffuse atmospheric murmur rather than a sharp localized sound. Amplitude is moderate at the source but perceived as quiet from ground level due to canopy height (20-40 meters above the ground). The **E=3 rating** reflects the muted, ambient quality — present but not loud, pleasant but not dramatic, background texture rather than foreground performance. Calling peaks during breeding season (May-August in the Pacific Northwest). Males call from high perches to attract mates and advertise territory. Outside breeding season calling is infrequent. On takeoff, Band-tailed Pigeons produce a loud wing clap — not as dramatic as the Rock Pigeon's but noticeable — and this mechanical sound is a secondary acoustic element that foreshadows the Rock Pigeon wing-clap mechanical-sound feature at degree 152.

## Part B: Band-tailed Pigeon — First Columbidae, Native Wild Pigeon, Mineral Spring Congregations

- **First Columbidae (STRUCTURAL EVENT):** The Band-tailed Pigeon enters the engine at degree 149 as the **first Columbidae** — the pigeon and dove family — and opens a four-degree quartet (149-152) that will progress from native forest to native open to invasive to feral mechanical. This is the engine's **first sustained four-degree family sequence**, representing a deliberate architectural commitment to developing a family across consecutive degrees rather than treating each species as an isolated documentary entry.
- **Native Wild Pigeon of Western North America:** The Band-tailed Pigeon (*Patagioenas fasciata*) is the **native** wild pigeon of western North America — the species most people don't know exists because the ubiquitous Rock Pigeon has colonized every city, every park, every bridge. The Band-tailed Pigeon is a bird of the **deep forest** — old-growth conifer, mixed conifer-deciduous, montane forest. Its primary habitat is as far from a city pigeon coop as a forest can be, and its range extends along the Pacific coast from southern British Columbia to Baja California with mountain populations from Colorado to Central America.
- **Largest Pigeon in North America:** Length 33-40 cm, weight 300-450 grams — significantly larger than the Rock Pigeon (240-380 g average). The Band-tailed Pigeon is a substantial bird, the largest pigeon species on the continent. Plumage is far more colorful than the Rock Pigeon: purple-tinged breast, blue-gray back, yellow bill with dark tip, a **diagnostic white crescent** on the back of the neck (the field mark), gray tail band (hence "band-tailed"), iridescent green-purple nape. A genuinely beautiful bird that most people have never knowingly seen.
- **Deep Owl-Like Coo-Coo at 300-600 Hz:** The species' signature sound is a deep, resonant "coo-COO" or "whoo-OOO" — two notes, the second slightly higher and more emphatic. Fundamental frequency **approximately 300-600 Hz**, surprisingly low for a pigeon and often mistaken for an owl by hikers unfamiliar with the species. The call is repeated 3-6 times with 2-3 second pauses between repetitions, then silence. The tonal quality is rich, resonant, with a breathy quality produced by the syrinx and amplified by the inflated crop. The canopy height (20-40 meters above ground) combined with canopy foliage attenuation renders the call diffuse and atmospheric at ground level — present but muted, a background sound of the summer forest.
- **Mineral Spring Congregations and Geophagy:** One of the most distinctive behaviors of the species. Flocks of dozens to hundreds of Band-tailed Pigeons gather at natural **mineral springs**, mineral licks, and areas of mineral-rich soil to drink sodium-rich water and consume mineral-laden soil and mud. This is **geophagy** — deliberate mineral consumption — and supplements the otherwise sodium-poor diet of fruits, nuts, and grain. The same mineral spring sites are used year after year, generation after generation. The pigeons appear to learn the locations from parents and flock mates. Several sites in Washington and Oregon are well-known traditional gathering points used for decades.
- **Ecological Role as Seed Disperser and Acorn Specialist:** The Band-tailed Pigeon plays ecological roles that distinguish it from its feral cousin. It is an important **seed disperser** for Pacific coast forests, with a large gape and digestive system that handles fruits and acorns that smaller birds cannot. It is one of the few PNW birds that specializes in **acorns** — **Garry oak**, **tanoak**, and other oak species — making it a member of oak woodland ecology, which is itself a threatened habitat in the PNW. It contributes to forest regeneration after fire, logging, or windthrow disturbance through its seed-dispersing behavior.
- **Declining Populations — The 2-3% Annual Decline:** The Band-tailed Pigeon has experienced significant population decline. Historical abundance was substantial — early naturalists described flocks of thousands, and the species was one of the most common large birds in PNW forests. Market hunting in the late 1800s and early 1900s was devastating; mineral spring congregations made the species particularly vulnerable because hunters could kill dozens at a single predictable site. Forest clearing, old-growth logging, and urban expansion have continued to erode habitat. Breeding Bird Survey data documents a **2-3% per year decline** in recent decades. Pacific coast population is estimated at 1.5-2 million birds — a fraction of historical numbers. Hunting is now regulated with short seasons and low bag limits, but pressure continues.
- **The Passenger Pigeon Parallel:** The Band-tailed Pigeon's decline echoes the extinction of the **Passenger Pigeon** (*Ectopistes migratorius*), which went from billions of individuals to zero with **Martha's** death at the **Cincinnati Zoo** on September 1, **1914**. The Band-tailed Pigeon never reached Passenger Pigeon numbers, but the pattern of overhunting a concentrated flock-forming pigeon species is structurally identical. The Passenger Pigeon parallel is invoked deliberately at degree 149 as a cautionary tale about the vulnerability of concentrated flock-forming pigeons.
- **E=3 Family Baseline Established:** The Band-tailed Pigeon registers at **E=3**, notably lower than the recent Apodidae swift entries at E=4 and the sixteen-degree hip-hop E=5 plateau. The Columbidae begin at a lower energy register because pigeons and doves are not the showy performers of the bird world — they are the background texture, the ambient murmur, the heard-but-not-noticed layer of the soundscape. Three of the four Columbidae entries (149, 150, 151) will register at E=3, establishing the family's characteristic energy signature.

The native-to-non-native arc is visible from the first degree, and the quartet becomes the engine's first sustained four-degree family sequence.

- **Passenger Pigeon parallel is accurate and powerful.** Same pattern: overhunting of concentrated, flock-forming pigeons. The Band-tailed Pigeon never reached Passenger Pigeon numbers, but the vulnerability is the same and the parallel provides a cautionary lesson that resonates beyond the single species.

### What Could Be Better

- **J. Pinder's discography coverage is thin.** The research captures the tier and the philosophy but does not cite specific records, specific tracks, specific features, or specific collaborators in the detail that the established-indie entries (Common Market, Sol) offered. Future deep-underground documentation should push harder on specific releases to ground the tier-level claims in concrete artistic evidence.
- **Mineral spring site names are not enumerated.** The research mentions "several mineral spring sites in Washington and Oregon" but does not name specific traditional congregation points. The Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife has records of specific sites; future Band-tailed Pigeon documentation or the Columbidae quartet's continued development should name sites where possible to ground the geophagy behavior in verifiable geography.
- **The deep-underground-to-regional-indie tier boundary is under-theorized.** What distinguishes J. Pinder's tier from Blue Scholars' or Common Market's tier in structural terms beyond "national visibility"? The research sketches the distinction but does not develop the structural criteria that would allow future tier placement to be rigorous rather than intuitive. Subsequent deep-underground documentation should surface explicit structural criteria (distribution reach, label infrastructure, audience geography, economic model) and test them.

## Lessons Learned

1. **The first release sets the grain size, but the documentary register expands at structural firsts.** Degree 149 is the first Columbidae entry and the first deep-underground-tier entry — both firsts — and the paired firsts at a single degree demonstrate that the engine's documentary agenda expands on both axes simultaneously rather than one axis at a time. Future degree planning should look for paired firsts as high-value documentary events.
2. **Native-vs-feral is a structural story, not a side note.** The Columbidae quartet's native-to-non-native arc (149 native forest → 150 native open → 151 invasive → 152 feral mechanical) reads as a displacement story that reaches beyond ornithology. Similar native-vs-introduced contrasts in future family documentation should be foregrounded as structural rather than treated as incidental ecology.
3. **Invisible tiers and invisible species require active documentary work.** Commercial gatekeeping keeps the self-released tier out of the record. Ecological naïveté keeps the native pigeon out of the general public's awareness. Both require the engine's explicit documentary effort to make visible. The engine's documentary agenda expands toward the invisible because visibility systems systematically overlook the foundations.
4. **Energy plateaus are genre-level findings.** Sixteen consecutive E=5 entries across DJs, producers, collectives, scene-infrastructure figures, established artists, returns, and now deep underground is not coincidence — it is genre-level temperature. Future genre-era documentation should track plateau length as a first-class finding about the genre itself.
5. **Community-gathering-at-predictable-sites is a cross-domain pattern.** Open mics on the artist axis and mineral springs on the species axis both concentrate otherwise dispersed populations at predictable community points. The pattern explains both the social depth of the underlying populations (community life happens at these sites) and the vulnerability of concentration (the sites become attack surfaces for predation or policing or economic pressure).
6. **Extinction parallels are conservation tools.** The Passenger Pigeon's 1914 extinction provides the structural lesson for Band-tailed Pigeon conservation: concentrated flock-forming pigeons are vulnerable, and the current 2-3% annual decline is a slower replay of the pattern. Future species documentation with extinction-parallel potential should foreground the parallel explicitly as a cautionary framework.
7. **Boundary events like family pivots and tier entries deserve explicit framing.** Degree 149 is simultaneously a family-pivot (Apodidae at 148 closes, Columbidae at 149 opens), a tier-entry (deep underground opens), and a plateau-extension (E=5 reaches sixteen degrees). Future degree planning should identify boundary events in advance so the release framing can make the structural significance legible from the header.
8. **Four-degree family sequences create narrative arcs that single-degree entries cannot.** The Columbidae quartet will develop a native-to-non-native displacement story across 149-152 that no single-degree pigeon entry could carry. Future family documentation should consider multi-degree sequences where the family's internal structure can be developed as a narrative.
