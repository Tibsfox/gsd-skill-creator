## Mission Overview

Landsat 9 is the obs#18 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — the ninth satellite of the Landsat program, the joint NASA / USGS land-imaging mission extending the longest continuous record of Earth's land surface. ★ It is a NASA Earth-science mission: NASA builds and launches the satellite and performs on-orbit checkout, while the USGS operates the ground system and flight operations, archives the data, and freely distributes it at no cost through its EROS Center; the Landsat series began with Landsat 1 (ERTS-1) in 1972, the longest continuous space record of Earth's land surface, and Landsat 9 is the ninth in the line. Landsat 9 carries two instruments and images Earth's land from a sun-synchronous orbit: OLI-2 (the Operational Land Imager 2, a nine-band push-broom reflective imager spanning the visible, near-infrared, and shortwave-infrared plus a panchromatic band) images the land at about 30 m multispectral and 15 m panchromatic with 14-bit radiometry, up from OLI's 12-bit, for improved sensitivity over dark targets; and TIRS-2 (the Thermal Infrared Sensor 2) reads the land's thermal infrared in two bands, B10 and B11, at about 100 m resampled to 30 m. The mission launched 27 September 2021 (18:12 UTC) on an Atlas V 401 from Vandenberg, SLC-3E, California, and reached a sun-synchronous orbit at roughly 705 km altitude and ~98.2° inclination, descending across the equator at about 10:00 AM (the WRS-2 orbit inherited from Landsat 8), with a 99-minute period and a 16-day single-satellite repeat. Landsat 9 flies in the Landsat constellation, paired with Landsat 8 (coplanar, eight days out of phase), so the two together give an 8-day repeat of the global land surface — not the A-Train. The story is the Atlas V launch from Vandenberg, the climb to the sun-synchronous WRS-2 orbit, the two imagers turned on the daylit land, the changing land read from above as forests and farms and cities and water and snow and fire scars across the 30 m swath, and the long record of the land that began with Landsat 1 in 1972 and continues to be gathered today. ## Dedication

v1.298 Landsat 9 is dedicated to the people at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, at the U.S. Geological Survey and its EROS Center, and at Ball Aerospace who designed and flew the ninth satellite of the Landsat program. The teams who placed two instruments on one platform — OLI-2, reading the daylit land in nine reflective bands at thirty metres, and TIRS-2, reading the land's thermal infrared — so that the forests and the farms, the cities and the water, the snow and the fire scars could all be watched from above. The crews who carried the satellite aloft on an Atlas V from Vandenberg into its sun-synchronous orbit, paired with Landsat 8 so that every place on the land is revisited every eight days. The scientists who, since Landsat 1 in 1972, have built the longest continuous record of Earth's land surface, image after image, year after year, freely shared with the world at no cost. From 705 km the satellite still reads the changing land, and that long record of the open ground belongs to them.
