**The Steller Sea Lion and the Opening of the Otariidae Family.** The **Steller Sea Lion** (*Eumetopias jubatus*) enters the Sound of Puget Sound engine at degree 182 as the **FIRST OTARIIDAE** — the eared-seal family of pinnipeds — after the Harbor Seal (family Phocidae) opened the pinnipeds at degrees 180 and 181. The Otariidae-versus-Phocidae distinction is the primary morphological and behavioral divide within the living pinnipeds: Otariidae (eared seals, sea lions, fur seals) possess **external ear flaps** (pinnae), **rotating hind flippers** that allow four-limb terrestrial walking, and generally more social and noisy haul-out behavior; Phocidae (true or earless seals) lack external ear flaps, cannot rotate hind flippers forward (they drag on land), and generally haul out in smaller, quieter groups. The engine's pinniped vocabulary at degree 182 therefore now includes both major families in a three-degree window (180 Harbor Seal Phocidae, 181 Harbor Seal pup Phocidae, 182 Steller Sea Lion Otariidae), providing the taxonomic baseline for future pinniped entries including the **California Sea Lion** (*Zalophus californianus*, Otariidae), **Northern Fur Seal** (*Callorhinus ursinus*, Otariidae), and **Northern Elephant Seal** (*Mirounga angustirostris*, Phocidae). The Steller Sea Lion also anchors the engine's size-extremes vocabulary as the **largest sea lion species on Earth**, with adult bulls reaching approximately **1,000-1,100 kilograms** (2,200-2,500 pounds) and up to **3.3 meters** (11 feet) in length — substantially larger than the Harbor Seals at approximately 80-170 kilograms.

**The Eumetopias Genus and the Georg Wilhelm Steller Naming Lineage.** The Steller Sea Lion is the sole extant species in the monotypic genus **Eumetopias**, a Greek compound of *eu-* (well, good) + *metopion* (forehead) that references the broad, prominent forehead of adult bulls, and the species epithet *jubatus* (maned) refers to the thick mane of longer, coarser fur around the neck and shoulders of adult males. The naming honors the German-born naturalist **Georg Wilhelm Steller** (1709-1746), who observed the species during the 1741-1742 **Great Northern Expedition** of Russian-sponsored Pacific exploration under Captain **Vitus Bering**. The expedition reached the Aleutian archipelago, Kodiak Island, and Kamchatka before being wrecked on **Bering Island** in December 1741, where Steller survived for five months with the expedition's survivors and documented the region's fauna under conditions that produced the earliest Western scientific descriptions of six species now carrying his name: the Steller Sea Lion, the Steller Sea Eagle, the Steller's Jay, Steller's Eider, the Steller's Sea Cow (now extinct, hunted to extinction within 27 years of Steller's 1742 description), and Steller's fossil sea lion. The Steller lineage anchors the engine's taxonomic-history vocabulary as a mid-eighteenth-century foundation for Pacific-Rim natural history, a lineage that appears throughout Pacific Northwest species documentation and that the engine's first Otariidae entry makes explicit.

**Distinct Population Segment Listing Under the Endangered Species Act.** The Steller Sea Lion's conservation status introduces the **Distinct Population Segment (DPS)** regulatory concept under the **Endangered Species Act (ESA)** into the engine's vocabulary. The species is listed under two separate DPS categories: the **eastern DPS** (from southeast Alaska through British Columbia, Washington, Oregon, and California) is listed as **Threatened**, and the **western DPS** (from the Gulf of Alaska through the Aleutian Islands westward to the Kuril Islands of Russia) is listed as **Endangered** after an approximately **80% population decline** between the 1970s and 2000s. The causes of the western DPS decline remain not fully resolved, with leading hypotheses including prey-base shifts (reductions in high-quality fish prey like Atka mackerel and walleye pollock), fisheries interactions (bycatch, competition), predation by mammal-eating transient killer whales, and temperature-driven regime shifts in the Gulf of Alaska ecosystem. The DPS framework under the ESA — which allows population-level rather than species-level listing — is a regulatory mechanism essential for trans-boundary marine mammals with geographically distinct populations showing different population trajectories, and the engine's documentation at degree 182 preserves the DPS vocabulary as first-class conservation context alongside the **Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA)** framework that entered at degree 180.

**Puget Sound and Outer Washington Coast Haul-Out Sites.** Steller Sea Lions in Puget Sound and the outer Washington coast are predominantly animals of the **eastern DPS**, and the population in local waters is primarily composed of **adult males and subadults** — the female rookeries (the large breeding colonies where pupping occurs) are located further north in southeast Alaska (notably **Forrester Island** in the Alexander Archipelago) and in the Bering Sea (including **Marmot Island** in the Gulf of Alaska). Male Steller Sea Lions disperse widely from the breeding rookeries for much of the year, and many winter and forage in Washington, Oregon, and California waters, returning to rookeries during the May-July breeding season. Principal Washington haul-out sites include rocks in the **San Juan Islands**, the **Strait of Juan de Fuca** (particularly Race Rocks and Sail Rock), the **outer Washington coast** (including **Destruction Island**, **Split Rock**, and **Sea Lion Rock**), and the **Columbia River estuary** where Steller Sea Lions forage on migrating salmon. The Puget Sound haul-outs are smaller than the northern rookeries (typically tens rather than hundreds of animals) and fluctuate seasonally, but they are a reliable component of the Salish Sea marine-mammal community.

## Part B: Steller Sea Lion — The First Otariidae and the Opening of the Eared-Seal Family

- **First Otariidae (Eared Seals) in the Engine.** The Steller Sea Lion (*Eumetopias jubatus*) enters at degree 182 as the **FIRST OTARIIDAE** — the eared-seal family of pinnipeds distinct from the **family Phocidae** (true or earless seals) that the Harbor Seal opened at degree 180 and the Harbor Seal pup continued at degree 181; the family distinction is the primary morphological and behavioral divide within the living pinnipeds (Otariidae: external ear flaps, rotating hind flippers, larger more social haul-outs; Phocidae: no ear flaps, non-rotating hind flippers, smaller quieter haul-outs).
- **Largest Sea Lion Species on Earth.** *Eumetopias jubatus* is the **largest sea lion species on Earth** and the largest Otariidae; adult males reach approximately **1,000-1,100 kilograms** (2,200-2,500 pounds) and up to **3.3 meters** (11 feet) in length, substantially larger than the Harbor Seals at approximately 80-170 kilograms, and the species anchors the engine's size-extremes vocabulary with the largest pinniped entry to date.
- **Genus Eumetopias Opens.** The Steller Sea Lion is the sole extant species in the monotypic genus **Eumetopias**, a Greek compound *eu-* (well) + *metopion* (forehead) referring to the broad prominent forehead of adult bulls, with the species epithet *jubatus* (maned) referring to the thick mane of longer, coarser fur around the neck and shoulders of adult males; the genus opening complements the earlier polytypic **Phoca** opening at degree 180 (which contains the Harbor Seal and the spotted seal *Phoca largha* and fossil species) with a monotypic-genus template.
- **Deep Resonant Roar at E=6 Eared-Seal Acoustic Register.** The defining acoustic signature is a **deep resonant roar** produced primarily by adult bulls during haul-out territorial display, breeding-season competition, and social communication; the roar is acoustically distinct from the Harbor Seal's low breathy **hwooo** (louder, tonal, full-harmonic, continuous at rookeries) and calibrates at E=6 as a higher-intensity marine-mammal register than the E=4 Phocidae register of degrees 180-181.
- **Georg Wilhelm Steller and the 1741-1742 Great Northern Expedition.** The species name honors **Georg Wilhelm Steller** (1709-1746), the German-born naturalist whose observations during the Russian-sponsored **Great Northern Expedition** under Captain **Vitus Bering** produced the earliest Western scientific descriptions of the species; the expedition reached the Aleutian archipelago and Kamchatka before being wrecked on **Bering Island** in December 1741, where Steller documented the region's fauna for five months including six species now carrying his name.
- **Distinct Population Segment (DPS) Listing Under the ESA.** The species is listed under two separate **Distinct Population Segment (DPS)** categories under the **Endangered Species Act (ESA)**: the **eastern DPS** (southeast Alaska through British Columbia, Washington, Oregon, and California) is **Threatened**, and the **western DPS** (Gulf of Alaska through Aleutian Islands to the Kuril Islands of Russia) is **Endangered** following an approximately 80% population decline between the 1970s and 2000s; the DPS framework enters the engine's conservation vocabulary as the population-level rather than species-level listing mechanism.
- **Puget Sound and Outer Washington Coast Haul-Out Sites.** Principal Washington haul-out sites include rocks in the **San Juan Islands**, the **Strait of Juan de Fuca** (particularly **Race Rocks** and **Sail Rock**), the **outer Washington coast** (including **Destruction Island**, **Split Rock**, and **Sea Lion Rock**), and the **Columbia River estuary** where Steller Sea Lions forage on migrating salmon; Puget Sound animals are primarily adult males and subadults of the eastern DPS, with female rookeries located further north in southeast Alaska and the Gulf of Alaska.
- **Female Rookeries in Southeast Alaska and Gulf of Alaska.** The breeding rookeries are located outside Puget Sound — notably at **Forrester Island** in the Alexander Archipelago (southeast Alaska) and **Marmot Island** in the Gulf of Alaska — with the May-July breeding season producing pups that nurse for approximately **1-3 years** (substantially longer than the Harbor Seal's 4-6 weeks); Puget Sound serves as a foraging and wintering area rather than a breeding area for the species.
- **Western DPS Decline and Hypothesized Causes.** The western DPS declined by approximately 80% between the 1970s and 2000s, with causes still not fully resolved; leading hypotheses include prey-base shifts (reductions in high-quality fish prey like Atka mackerel and walleye pollock), fisheries interactions (bycatch, competition), predation by mammal-eating transient killer whales, and temperature-driven regime shifts in the Gulf of Alaska ecosystem; the eastern DPS has recovered since Threatened listing, while the western DPS remains Endangered.
- **NOAA Fisheries and NMFS Stock Assessment Framework.** Population monitoring is coordinated by **NOAA National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS)** through periodic stock assessment reports under the ESA and the **Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA)** of 1972; the **Alaska SeaLife Center** in Seward, Alaska rehabilitates stranded animals and conducts feeding ecology research, and the **Whale Museum** in Friday Harbor documents San Juan Islands haul-out observations through Soundwatch and stranding-network programs.
