## Surprises

- **A spacecraft deliberately changed the orbit of a celestial body for the first time.** DART reached the small moonlet Dimorphos of the binary near-Earth asteroid 65803 Didymos on 26 September 2022 and changed Dimorphos's orbital period around Didymos by ~33 minutes — the first time a spacecraft deliberately and measurably altered the orbit of a celestial body, and the first full-scale demonstration that a kinetic impactor can deflect an asteroid.
- **The change far exceeded the success criterion.** The ~33-minute change to Dimorphos's orbit (the refined peer-reviewed value, −33.0 ± 1.0 min) far exceeded the ~73-second minimum success criterion — a much larger deflection than the threshold the mission needed to clear, helped by the recoil of the rising plume of ejected material.
- **A small spacecraft found a small moonlet on its own.** Because the one-way light time made real-time piloting from Earth impossible, the SMART Nav autonomous optical-navigation system and the single DRACO camera found and steered DART onto the ~160 m moonlet Dimorphos in the final hours — distinguishing it from the ~780 m primary Didymos only in the last stretch of the approach.
- **A cubesat rode along to observe the redirect.** The Italian Space Agency cubesat LICIACube, deployed from DART days before the encounter, flew past on 26 September 2022 to image the brilliant flash and the rising plume of ejected material — and ESA's Hera mission, launched October 2024, will survey Didymos and Dimorphos to study the result in detail (forward context only — ESA, not NASA).
- **A small bird that drives off a far larger hawk or crow.** The Western Kingbird (*Tyrannus verticalis*), a tyrant flycatcher of the open country of the interior Pacific Northwest — sagebrush-juniper edges, grasslands, and the agricultural and riparian margins of eastern Washington and Oregon — boldly launches itself at and drives off birds many times its size, chasing the much larger intruder clear of its territory and off its flight path, a mirror of the small spacecraft that changed the course of a far larger body.
- **An early grass that sets the course of a recovering grassland.** Blue Wildrye (*Elymus glaucus*), a native cool-season perennial bunchgrass widespread across the Pacific Northwest, is one of the first grasses to establish after fire or disturbance, its quick roots holding the soil and setting the course of the recovering grassland that follows (ecological description only) — bound with DART's small, decisive force that changed the course of something far larger.
