## Mission Overview

DART is the obs#5 sustainer of the COMET-AND-ASTEROID-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's first planetary-defense mission, a small spacecraft whose precise, autonomous arrival changed the orbit of a far larger body, whose defining firsts are the first demonstration of asteroid deflection, the first deliberate change of a celestial body's orbit, and NASA's first planetary-defense mission. The spacecraft was built and managed by the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory (JHU APL, Laurel, MD) for NASA's Planetary Defense Coordination Office. DART launched 24 November 2021 on a SpaceX Falcon 9 from Vandenberg Space Force Base (SLC-4E) and cruised to the binary near-Earth asteroid 65803 Didymos — a primary about 780 m across and its small moonlet Dimorphos, about 160 m across. DART's target was the moonlet, small enough that a kinetic impactor could measurably change its orbit around the primary. The encounter was the mission: DART carried a single instrument, the DRACO camera, and used the SMART Nav autonomous optical-navigation system to find and steer itself onto the small moonlet in the final hours, because the one-way light time made real-time piloting from Earth impossible. On 26 September 2022 (~23:14 UTC) DART reached Dimorphos at ~6.1 km/s while the Italian Space Agency cubesat LICIACube, deployed days earlier, flew past to observe. The event produced a brilliant flash and a rising plume of ejected material — a kinetic-impactor demonstration of planetary defense. The story is the launch from Vandenberg, the cruise to the binary near-Earth asteroid, the autonomous approach to the tiny moonlet, the brilliant flash and the plume, and the changed course — the first time a spacecraft deliberately and measurably altered the orbit of a celestial body. The result is central: DART changed Dimorphos's orbital period around Didymos by ~33 minutes (the refined peer-reviewed value, −33.0 ± 1.0 min; NASA's initial October 2022 estimate was ~32 minutes), far exceeding the ~73-second minimum success criterion — measured from Earth and confirmed on 11 October 2022. This demonstrated that a kinetic impactor can measurably deflect an asteroid. ## Surprises

- **A spacecraft deliberately changed the orbit of a celestial body for the first time.** DART reached the small moonlet Dimorphos of the binary near-Earth asteroid 65803 Didymos on 26 September 2022 and changed Dimorphos's orbital period around Didymos by ~33 minutes — the first time a spacecraft deliberately and measurably altered the orbit of a celestial body, and the first full-scale demonstration that a kinetic impactor can deflect an asteroid.
- **The change far exceeded the success criterion.** The ~33-minute change to Dimorphos's orbit (the refined peer-reviewed value, −33.0 ± 1.0 min) far exceeded the ~73-second minimum success criterion — a much larger deflection than the threshold the mission needed to clear, helped by the recoil of the rising plume of ejected material.
- **A small spacecraft found a small moonlet on its own.** Because the one-way light time made real-time piloting from Earth impossible, the SMART Nav autonomous optical-navigation system and the single DRACO camera found and steered DART onto the ~160 m moonlet Dimorphos in the final hours — distinguishing it from the ~780 m primary Didymos only in the last stretch of the approach.
- **A cubesat rode along to observe the redirect.** The Italian Space Agency cubesat LICIACube, deployed from DART days before the encounter, flew past on 26 September 2022 to image the brilliant flash and the rising plume of ejected material — and ESA's Hera mission, launched October 2024, will survey Didymos and Dimorphos to study the result in detail (forward context only — ESA, not NASA).
- **A small bird that drives off a far larger hawk or crow.** The Western Kingbird (*Tyrannus verticalis*), a tyrant flycatcher of the open country of the interior Pacific Northwest — sagebrush-juniper edges, grasslands, and the agricultural and riparian margins of eastern Washington and Oregon — boldly launches itself at and drives off birds many times its size, chasing the much larger intruder clear of its territory and off its flight path, a mirror of the small spacecraft that changed the course of a far larger body.
- **An early grass that sets the course of a recovering grassland.** Blue Wildrye (*Elymus glaucus*), a native cool-season perennial bunchgrass widespread across the Pacific Northwest, is one of the first grasses to establish after fire or disturbance, its quick roots holding the soil and setting the course of the recovering grassland that follows (ecological description only) — bound with DART's small, decisive force that changed the course of something far larger.

## Dedication

v1.275 DART is dedicated to the people across NASA and the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory who built and flew the first mission to deliberately change the orbit of another world. The engineers in Laurel who built a small spacecraft and the autonomous navigation that let it find a tiny moonlet on its own, and the flight team who held the cruise and the final approach to the second across a distance no real-time command could cross. The teams behind the single DRACO camera and the SMART Nav system that distinguished Dimorphos from Didymos only in the last hours, and the navigators who steered toward the small body while the LICIACube cubesat flew past to watch the brilliant flash and the rising plume. The scientists who measured the changed course from Earth — a ~33-minute shortening of an orbit, far beyond what the test needed to prove — and the partners at the Italian Space Agency who sent a small companion to observe. They reached a far larger body with a small, precise force and nudged it onto a new course — the first demonstration of planetary defense.
