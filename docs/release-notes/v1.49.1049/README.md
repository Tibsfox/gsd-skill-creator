## Mission Overview

HEAO-1 (High Energy Astronomy Observatory 1) is the obs#10 continuation of the X-ray-astronomy axis and the adjacent-sustain of the survey/catalog form Uhuru originated — the first of NASA's three High Energy Astronomy Observatories, the dedicated X-ray sky-survey observatory that surveyed the whole sky about three times to about an order of magnitude deeper than Uhuru, the named survey-branch successor (Uhuru, then HEAO-1). Designated HEAO-A before launch and HEAO 1 / HEAO-1 on orbit, it lifted off 12 August 1977 (about 06:29 UTC) aboard an Atlas-Centaur (an Atlas SLV-3D first stage with a Centaur-D1AR upper stage) from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station, Launch Complex 36, Pad B (LC-36B), Florida, into a near-circular ~420–445-kilometer, ~22.7-degree low-Earth orbit (NSSDCA / COSPAR ID 1977-075A; SATCAT 10217; spacecraft mass about 2552 kg, of it about 1220 kg the scientific experiments; period about 93.5 minutes). It carried four large, complementary, non-imaging X-ray experiments designed to scan the sky as the spacecraft spun — collimated proportional counters, a wire-grid modulation collimator, and scintillation detectors, with no focusing optics of any kind and no image. A source's position on the sky was inferred from the rising and falling count rate as a collimator's field swept across it during the spin, together with the fringe pattern of the modulation collimator's two fine wire grids — never from a focused image. HEAO-1 was spin-stabilized: its spin (Z) axis was held within about half a degree of the Sun and stepped about half a degree twice a day to keep tracking it, while the spacecraft rotated about the Earth–Sun line at about 0.03 revolutions per minute (one turn per ~30 minutes), so its instruments swept a great circle on the sky 90 degrees from the Sun, that circle marching about one degree per day through the year, building a complete all-sky scan every about six months and surveying the whole sky about three times; HEAO-1 also performed pointed observations of about 3–6 hours. HEAO-1 was the first of NASA's three High Energy Astronomy Observatories, managed by NASA's George C. Marshall Space Flight Center with TRW as prime contractor; the program comprised HEAO-1 (HEAO-A, 1977, the X-ray survey mission), HEAO-2 (HEAO-B, 1978, the Einstein Observatory, the separate imaging telescope, obs#8), and HEAO-3 (HEAO-C, 1979, a cosmic-ray and gamma-ray mission). It operated from 12 August 1977 to 9 January 1979 (about seventeen months, ending when its hydrazine attitude-control gas was depleted) and reentered the atmosphere in March 1979, about two months later. It built the deep HEAO A-1 catalog of 842 sources, made the first precise broadband measurement of the diffuse cosmic X-ray background spectrum, located sources to arcsecond class for counterpart identification, and carried the survey into the hard X-ray and low-energy gamma-ray band, and SUSTAINS the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#10 (axis-rotation #35 sustains).

## Structural firsts

**HEAO-1-FIRST-INSTANCE:** HEAO-1 enters the catalog as the first of NASA's three High Energy Astronomy Observatories — the first instance, the obs#1 first — sustaining the X-ray-astronomy axis at obs#10. The dedicated X-ray sky-survey observatory, managed by Marshall Space Flight Center with TRW as prime contractor, it carried four large non-imaging instruments (A-1 the Large Area Sky Survey, A-2 the Cosmic X-ray Experiment, A-3 the Scanning Modulation Collimator, A-4 the Hard X-ray / Low-Energy Gamma-Ray experiment) that scanned the whole sky about three times over ~17 months and discovered on the order of 1,500 X-ray sources, building the deep HEAO A-1 catalog of 842 sources (0.25–25 keV) about an order of magnitude deeper than Uhuru's 4U. Three load-bearing qualifiers: HEAO-1 is a SUSTAIN, not the first all-sky X-ray survey or first X-ray catalog (those firsts belong to Uhuru, 1970, obs#9 — HEAO-1 is the deeper, broader-band successor); HEAO-1 is strictly NON-IMAGING (collimated counters, a wire-grid modulation collimator, and scintillators, no focusing optics — the imaging/focusing X-ray telescope of the same HEAO program is the separate HEAO-2 / Einstein, 1978, obs#8); and the deepest contribution is "of its era" (later all-sky surveys, ROSAT and eROSITA, went deeper).

**DIFFUSE-COSMIC-X-RAY-BACKGROUND-SPECTRUM-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The A-2 Cosmic X-ray Experiment (Elihu Boldt, NASA Goddard, with Gordon Garmire, Caltech/Penn State) made the first precise, definitive broadband (3–50 keV) measurement of the diffuse cosmic X-ray background spectrum — excellently fit by optically-thin thermal bremsstrahlung at kT = 40 ± 5 keV (the canonical "HEAO-1 A-2 CXB" spectrum, Marshall et al. 1980) — and definitively established its high degree of isotropy at high Galactic latitude (the dipole consistent with the Local Group's motion), the key evidence for an extragalactic origin (A-4 later extended the measured spectrum to ~180 keV). The qualifiers matter: this is a precise SPECTRUM / ISOTROPY measurement, not the DISCOVERY of the cosmic X-ray background (that was the 18–19 June 1962 AS&E Aerobee sounding ROCKET — Giacconi, Gursky, Paolini & Rossi; PRL 9, 439), and it is the measurement of the DIFFUSE spectrum, not the resolution of the background into discrete SOURCES (that came later: Einstein, then ROSAT and deep Chandra; Chandra owns COSMIC-X-RAY-BACKGROUND-SOURCE-RESOLUTION). "First" attaches to PRECISE / DEFINITIVE / BROADBAND — earlier 1960s–70s rocket/balloon experiments had rough spectra.

**SCANNING-MODULATION-COLLIMATOR-LOCALIZATION-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The A-3 Scanning Modulation Collimator (Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory and MIT; PIs Daniel Schwartz, SAO, and Hale Bradt, MIT; Herbert Gursky the original SAO PI and first author of the instrument paper), a satellite-borne pair of four-grid wire modulation collimators (transmission planes of 30 arcseconds and 2 arcminutes FWHM, a 4° × 4° field, 0.9–13.3 keV), delivered arcsecond-class X-ray source positions (~10–30 arcseconds for strong sources such as the LMC X-1/2/3 sources, projected ~2 arcminutes for the faintest) — precise enough to enable systematic optical and radio counterpart identification (SS Cygni, 4U1907+09, LMC X-1/2/3, Cygnus A as an extended source), a clear precise-localization advance beyond Uhuru's plain scanning slat collimator. The qualifier matters: these are precise POSITIONS inferred from grid modulation, not a focused IMAGE — HEAO-1 remains non-imaging; imaging belongs to Einstein (obs#8) and Chandra (obs#1).

**HARD-X-RAY-LOW-ENERGY-GAMMA-RAY-ALL-SKY-SURVEY-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The A-4 Hard X-ray / Low-Energy Gamma-Ray experiment (UC San Diego and MIT; PI Laurence Peterson, with James Matteson), seven actively-shielded NaI(Tl)/CsI(Na) scintillators, carried the all-sky survey up into the hard X-ray and low-energy gamma-ray band (~13 keV to 10 MeV), producing the first all-sky hard X-ray source catalog (the HEAO A-4 catalog, about 77 sources over 13–180 keV, Levine et al. 1984) and measuring the diffuse cosmic hard X-ray background spectrum (Gruber et al. 1999). The qualifiers matter: this is a non-imaging scanning SURVEY into the hard band — distinct from NuSTAR's hard-band FOCUSING telescope (obs#2) — and it is framed as a survey / band-extension, not a "broadest single-mission spectral span" record (OSO-7, 1971, already spanned ~1 keV–10 MeV).

**X-RAY-ASTRONOMY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION:** The X-ray-astronomy axis Chandra opened, NuSTAR extended, RXTE continued, IXPE continued, NICER continued, Swift continued, XRISM continued, Einstein continued as the focusing-imaging origin, and Uhuru continued as the survey/catalog origin sustains as an adjacent-sustain observation at obs#10 (axis-rotation #35 sustains) — HEAO-1 is the deeper, broader-band, second-generation all-sky survey, the named survey-branch successor to Uhuru. Continuation by observable form, not by chronology: HEAO-1 (1977) sustains the survey/catalog form Uhuru (1970) originated, an adjacent-sustain that deepened and broadened the all-sky X-ray catalog.

Because this sustains the same X-ray-astronomy axis, the shared X-ray-astronomy vocabulary (X-ray, neutron star, black hole, compact object, accretion, supernova remnant, galaxy cluster, active galactic nucleus, Seyfert, thermal bremsstrahlung, intracluster medium, proportional counter, scintillator, collimator, modulation collimator, field of view, effective area, energy band, keV, MeV, all-sky survey, source catalog, cosmic X-ray background, isotropy, Crab-unit / Uhuru-flux-unit sensitivity, the X-ray sky, Scorpius X-1, Giacconi, Rossi, the founding of X-ray astronomy) is correct and kept, and Chandra, NuSTAR, RXTE, IXPE, NICER, Swift, XRISM, Einstein, and Uhuru are referenced explicitly as the obs#1–obs#9 form complements / predecessor.
- **The non-imaging subtlety held: HEAO-1 is strictly NON-IMAGING.** "Imaging," "focusing," "Wolter," "mirror," "arcsecond image," and "grazing-incidence optics" belong to Einstein (obs#8) and Chandra (obs#1) and route there, never to HEAO-1 — HEAO-1 is a spinning collimated survey observatory (collimated proportional counters, a wire-grid modulation collimator, and scintillators) with no focusing optics and no image, and a source's position is inferred from the scan modulation and the modulation-collimator fringe pattern (arcsecond class for strong sources). The A-3 modulation collimator gives a position, not a focused image. The shared generic vocabulary is not over-scrubbed, but HEAO-1 is never described as imaging or focusing.
- **The "sustain, not origin" subtlety held as mandatory.** HEAO-1 did NOT conduct the first all-sky X-ray survey or build the first X-ray catalog — those firsts belong to Uhuru (1970, obs#9), so the bare claim "HEAO-1 conducted the first all-sky X-ray survey" is FALSE. HEAO-1 is the deeper, broader-band, second-generation SUSTAIN: the HEAO A-1 catalog = 842 sources vs Uhuru's 4U = 339, about an order of magnitude deeper, the deepest survey of its era.
- **The HEAO-2 = Einstein separation held.** HEAO-2 = Einstein is a SEPARATE mission in the same HEAO program (the imaging observatory, obs#8); HEAO-1 = HEAO-A = the survey mission. The shared "HEAO" program name was not allowed to blur the two — HEAO-1 is not Einstein and is not imaging, and Einstein's imaging-specific vocabulary (Wolter Type-I optics, nested mirror shells, the High Resolution Imager, focusing, arcsecond image) stays with Einstein.

HEAO-1 measured the DIFFUSE spectrum.

HEAO-1's genuine distinction is the deep survey, the soft-boundary extension, and the four firsts, not a span record.
- **The timing/spectroscopy subtlety held: HEAO-1 stamps NO timing or spectroscopy first.** HEAO-1's ~17-month scanning baseline enabled long-term variability monitoring (Hercules X-1's 35-day cycle) and complete AGN / cluster samples, but the dedicated timing-observatory form is RXTE (obs#3) and NICER (obs#5), and high-resolution spectroscopy is Chandra (gratings) and XRISM (microcalorimeter); HEAO-1 carried only broadband proportional counters and scintillators (coarse spectra), so it stamps no timing-observatory or spectroscopy first beyond the diffuse-cosmic-X-ray-background-spectrum measurement.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis can sustain by deepening and broadening an existing form, not only by adding a new observable or returning to a root.** The X-ray-astronomy axis sustains at obs#10 because HEAO-1 is the adjacent-sustain of the survey/catalog form Uhuru originated — the deeper, broader-band, second-generation all-sky survey, the named survey-branch successor (Uhuru, then HEAO-1). 2. **Continuation is by observable form, not by chronology.** HEAO-1 launched in 1977, seven years after Uhuru, and sits at obs#10 as the adjacent-sustain of the survey/catalog form Uhuru rooted at obs#9 — exactly as Einstein (1978) sits at obs#8 as the focusing-imaging origin even though it predates Chandra (1999). The axis records the survey form's root (Uhuru) and then its deeper second generation (HEAO-1).
3. **"Sustain, not origin" is the single most load-bearing HEAO-1 fact.** "HEAO-1 conducted the first all-sky X-ray survey / built the first X-ray catalog" is FALSE — those firsts belong to Uhuru (1970, obs#9). HEAO-1 is the deeper, broader-band, second-generation SUSTAIN: the HEAO A-1 catalog = 842 sources vs Uhuru's 4U = 339, about an order of magnitude deeper, the deepest survey of its era.

## Surprises

- **A satellite that just spun and swept the sky with collimators surveyed it an order of magnitude deeper.** HEAO-1 added no focusing optics and no image — it simply spun slowly about the Earth–Sun line and let four large non-imaging instruments sweep great circles across the sky. That capability deepened and broadened Uhuru's founding survey: it covered the whole sky about three times and built the HEAO A-1 catalog of 842 sources, about an order of magnitude deeper than Uhuru's 4U catalog of 339, discovering on the order of 1,500 X-ray sources in all.
- **The diffuse cosmic X-ray background turned out to have a clean ~40 keV thermal shape and a striking isotropy.** The A-2 Cosmic X-ray Experiment made the first precise, definitive broadband (3–50 keV) measurement of the diffuse cosmic X-ray background spectrum — excellently fit by optically-thin thermal bremsstrahlung at kT = 40 ± 5 keV — and established it as highly isotropic at high Galactic latitude (the dipole consistent with the Local Group's motion), the key evidence for an extragalactic origin; the background itself had been discovered on the 1962 rocket, and resolving it into discrete sources came later, but HEAO-1 read its diffuse spectrum precisely.
- **A pair of fine wire grids could pin an X-ray source to arcseconds without forming an image.** The A-3 Scanning Modulation Collimator delivered arcsecond-class positions (~10–30 arcseconds for strong sources, e.g. ~20 arcseconds for the LMC X-1/2/3 sources) by modulating each source into a fringe pattern as the spacecraft scanned, precise enough to enable systematic optical and radio counterpart identification (SS Cygni, 4U1907+09, LMC X-1/2/3, Cygnus A as an extended source) — a clear precise-localization advance beyond Uhuru's plain scanning slat collimator, yet a position from grid modulation, never a focused image.
- **The survey reached all the way up into the hard X-ray and low-energy gamma-ray band.** The A-4 experiment carried the all-sky survey up to ~10 MeV with seven actively-shielded scintillators, producing the first all-sky hard X-ray source catalog (~77 sources, 13–180 keV) and measuring the diffuse cosmic hard X-ray background — a non-imaging hard-band survey, distinct from NuSTAR's later hard-band focusing telescope, and a band-extension rather than a span record (OSO-7 had already spanned ~1 keV–10 MeV).
- **A harrier already surveys a whole field by systematic low sweeping, guided by sound.** The Northern harrier (*Circus hudsonius*) hunts by "quartering" — flying low and slow in systematic transects, covering an entire field or marsh section by section to inventory what is there — and uniquely among hawks it has an owl-like facial disc of stiff feathers that funnels sound to its ears, so it hunts substantially by ear, locating prey acoustically as it sweeps. The resonance with HEAO-1 is behavioral: the harrier's systematic area-survey of the whole ground mirrors HEAO-1's slowly spinning instruments scanning great circles across the whole sky (the harrier does not image its surroundings or "see X-rays," and it is a partial migrant).
- **A fungus surveys an entire forest floor as one connected network.** The honey fungus (*Armillaria ostoyae*) — a wood-decay fungus, not a plant — grows mostly as a vast underground/under-bark network of mycelium and dark cord-like rhizomorphs ("bootlaces") that spread through the soil and forest floor, mapping and exploiting the whole root system of a stand; a single genetic individual in the Malheur National Forest of eastern Oregon covers roughly 9–10 square kilometers and is by area among the largest single organisms on Earth — mirror to the survey side, as HEAO-1's deep, comprehensive all-sky survey went about an order of magnitude deeper than its predecessor (the "largest organism" claim attributed to that Oregon *Armillaria ostoyae* individual by area).

## Dedication

v1.232 HEAO-1 is dedicated to the people who built the observatory that surveyed the X-ray sky again, deeper and broader-band: the team at NASA's George C. Marshall Space Flight Center who managed the first of the High Energy Astronomy Observatories, and the engineers at TRW who built the largest scientific satellite of its day; Herbert Friedman of the Naval Research Laboratory, who led the A-1 Large Area Sky Survey that built the deep catalog of 842 sources; Elihu Boldt of NASA Goddard and Gordon Garmire, who led the A-2 Cosmic X-ray Experiment that read the diffuse cosmic X-ray background spectrum precisely; Daniel Schwartz, Hale Bradt, and Herbert Gursky of the Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory and MIT, who built the A-3 Scanning Modulation Collimator that pinned sources to arcseconds for counterpart identification; Laurence Peterson and James Matteson of UC San Diego and MIT, who carried the A-4 survey into the hard band; and Riccardo Giacconi and Bruno Rossi, who founded the field with the 1962 rocket. From a low orbit a slowly spinning collimator swept the X-ray sky once more — and the first map grew an order of magnitude deeper.
