## Summary

The forty-seventh paired release and the mission that went wrong at every turn -- and taught everything that needed to be taught before Apollo. Gemini 9A is the mission that should have been Gemini 9. The "A" suffix tells the story of a tragedy. On February 28, 1966, astronauts Elliot See and Charles Bassett -- the prime crew for Gemini 9 -- flew a T-38 Talon jet trainer from Ellington Air Force Base near Houston to the McDonnell Aircraft plant in St. Louis. The weather at Lambert Field was poor: low ceiling, reduced visibility, light rain, snow showers moving through the terminal area. See was flying the lead aircraft with Bassett in the back seat; the secondary T-38 carried Stafford and Cernan, who were the scheduled backup crew. See descended below the cloud deck on a visual approach and found himself too fast and too close to the runway. He attempted to go around. The T-38 did not have sufficient energy. The aircraft struck the roof of Building 101 at the McDonnell plant -- the very building where the Gemini 9 spacecraft was being assembled -- and crashed into a parking lot beyond. Both men died instantly. The spacecraft survived. The backup crew -- Tom Stafford and Gene Cernan -- became the prime crew. The mission was redesignated 9A. Both forms are named in the record: the scarlet letter of loss became a designation mark the engine now carries as a dedication at degree 46.

When Stafford rendezvoused with the replacement target vehicle -- the Augmented Target Docking Adapter, launched after the original Agena failed to reach orbit -- he saw the angry alligator. The ATDA's payload shroud had failed to separate. Two fiberglass clamshell halves gaped open at approximately 25 degrees on each side, the docking port visible inside the open jaws but physically inaccessible. "It looks like an angry alligator," Stafford radioed to Mission Control in Houston, and the phrase entered the Gemini vocabulary on the same channel that had carried "Jingle Bells" from Schirra six months earlier. The cause was a ground-handling error: a lacing wire and disconnect lanyard at the McDonnell plant that should have been removed before launch had not been properly configured. The explosive bolts fired but the wire held the shroud halves in place. The alligator's jaws opened but would not let go. Docking was impossible. Stafford and Cernan used the ATDA as a target for three different rendezvous techniques instead -- M=3 co-elliptic, equiperiod, and optical from above -- providing Apollo planners with contingency data for every possible lunar rendezvous scenario the Lunar Module might encounter. The same discipline Buzz Aldrin had validated mathematically for Gemini 6A at degree 42 was extended at degree 46 through three new profiles, each tested in live flight by a backup crew flying a backup target vehicle.

Then Cernan went outside. His EVA was planned as the most ambitious yet -- translate along the adapter section, don the Astronaut Maneuvering Unit jet backpack stowed at the rear of the Gemini equipment module, fly freely in space for the first time in history. Within minutes, everything collapsed. The G4C suit's rudimentary cooling system was overwhelmed by solar radiation and Cernan's metabolic heat output. The pressurized suit resisted every joint motion. There were no foot restraints at the work stations -- every push against a handrail sent his lower body swinging. Cernan was sweating heavily, the moisture saturating the suit's atmosphere, condensing on the coolest surface: the inside of his visor. Within twenty minutes, partially fogged. Within thirty minutes, nearly opaque. He was essentially blind on the outside of a spacecraft traveling at 7.8 km/s. His heart rate exceeded 180 BPM. He lost 4.5 kilograms of body weight from fluid loss. He never reached the AMU. Stafford ordered him back through the hatch. The EVA ran two hours and seven minutes. The EVA lessons went directly into Apollo: foot restraints at every work station, liquid cooling garments derived from the AiResearch prototypes already in test, visor anti-fog coatings formulated by Hamilton Standard, simplified task planning with built-in rest periods. Every failure taught. Each of the five redesign triggers -- foot restraints, liquid cooling, visor anti-fog, humidity management, task simplification -- entered the Apollo Extravehicular Mobility Unit specification directly traceable to Cernan's two hours in the sun.

## Part B: Spotted Towhee -- THE RASP

- **Second Passerellidae and the Consecutive Family Pair.** The Spotted Towhee is the second Passerellidae species in the engine after Song Sparrow at degree 45, forming the engine's first consecutive same-family pair among the New World sparrows. - **Ground-Scratching as Behavioral Signature.** The Spotted Towhee feeds by kicking backward through leaf litter with both feet simultaneously -- a vigorous double-footed hop-and-kick that throws debris behind the bird, exposing seeds and invertebrates beneath. The scratch is loud, energetic, and sustained, and in salal-dominated Pacific Northwest understory it is often the first sound an observer registers before any vocalization. The bird is heard twice: once from its voice (the chewink call and buzzy drink-your-tea trill), once from its feet (the continuous leaf-litter percussion). Cernan's EVA was the same kind of persistent, repetitive attack on an obstacle: both hands gripping smooth handrails with pressurized gloves that had the dexterity of oven mitts, body swinging with every push, scratching through the accumulated unknowns of EVA physics. Both acts expose what is underneath through disciplined physical effort.
- **The Hidden and the Visible Plumage Pattern.** The Spotted Towhee's plumage is among the most boldly patterned of any North American sparrow -- jet-black head, bright rufous flanks, white spots on black wings. Yet the bird spends most of its time invisible behind a curtain of salal and blackberry. Display is brief. Work is constant. Gemini 9A had the same dual character: the angry alligator photograph was the visible spectacle, one of the most recognizable images of the Gemini program. Behind it, the real work was invisible: three rendezvous profiles providing Apollo planners with contingency data, and the EVA crisis -- no live television coverage, communicated only through Cernan's strained voice on the UHF downlink. The world saw the alligator. The engineers used the data. The bold pattern and the hidden life.
- **Disturbance Creates the Habitat.** The Spotted Towhee thrives in disturbed habitats -- secondary growth that colonizes ground after fire, windthrow, or human clearing. The bird cannot live in mature, closed-canopy forest (too dark) or open grassland (too exposed). It needs the middle ground: the post-failure landscape where new growth is dense and productive. Gemini 9A was itself a product of disturbance: the death of See and Bassett destroyed the original plan, the backup crew flew instead. The mission thrived in the aftermath of tragedy, producing data that the original plan might not have generated. The Spotted Towhee is the disturbance-ecology bird. Gemini 9A was the disturbance-ecology mission.
- **The 1995 AOS Split and the Taxonomic Redesignation.** Until 1995, the Spotted Towhee and Eastern Towhee were classified as a single species: Rufous-sided Towhee (*Pipilo erythrophthalmus*). The American Ornithological Society's Classification Committee split them based on morphological, vocal, and behavioral differences that could no longer be accommodated within a single taxon. The 1995 split is a direct structural parallel to the 9/9A redesignation in 1966: one entity became two because distinguishing characteristics could no longer be lumped. The Spotted Towhee now carries its own species name and its own engine record, and the Eastern Towhee -- absent from Pacific Northwest -- is out of engine scope. The split registered in the engine's archaeology as an act of formal separation.
- **Vocal Palette and the Drink-Your-Tea Trill.** The Spotted Towhee's primary song is a buzzy trill often rendered phonetically as "drink-your-tea" with the first two syllables short and the trill sustained for 1.5 to 2 seconds. Regional variation is substantial -- PNW populations have simpler songs than interior-West populations -- and male song learning is a documented behavioral feature. The secondary vocalization is the sharp, descending "chewink" call used for alarm and contact. Together the two vocalizations and the leaf-scratch percussion constitute a three-channel acoustic signature that the engine registers as the Rasp element at degree 46.
- **The *Pipilo* Genus and the Largest Sparrow Complex.** *Pipilo* contains the Spotted, Eastern, Green-tailed, Canyon, Abert's, and Brown Towhees. The genus includes some of the largest sparrow species in North America -- Spotted Towhees are roughly 17-21 cm long, substantially larger than the Song Sparrow at degree 45. The size jump within the consecutive Passerellidae pair registers as one of the engine's scale gradients: degree 45's Song Sparrow at 14-18 cm, degree 46's Spotted Towhee at 17-21 cm.
- **Salal Understory and the Pacific Northwest Range.** Spotted Towhees are year-round residents throughout the lowland Pacific Northwest wherever dense understory exists. *Gaultheria shallon* -- salal -- is the dominant understory shrub in western Washington and Oregon coastal forests, and Spotted Towhees are reliably associated with the salal layer. The bird is one of the most consistently detected species in PNW Christmas Bird Counts, and the engine establishes the salal-understory habitat type as the signature Spotted Towhee environment.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **The backup-crew handoff preserved the mission.** When See and Bassett died on February 28, 1966, NASA already had Stafford and Cernan trained to within a month of being mission-ready. The Gemini backup-crew discipline -- every flight crew had a fully trained backup crew that could step into prime position within weeks -- worked as designed. Gemini 9A launched on schedule (June 3-6 was the original Gemini 9 window) because the program's crew-redundancy architecture had been built to survive exactly this kind of loss.
- **Three rendezvous profiles validated in a single mission.** With docking impossible, Stafford and Cernan executed the M=3 co-elliptic, the equiperiod, and the optical-from-above rendezvous as three independent technical demonstrations. All three entered the Apollo contingency playbook as tested, validated options. The mission turned its primary failure (no docking) into a secondary success (three rendezvous instead of one), which is the model for converting mission-plan failures into operational capability.
- **Schirra-style judgment under pressure extended to Stafford.** When the ATDA appeared with its shroud stuck half-open, Stafford did not abort. He recognized the angry alligator, reported it honestly ("It looks like an angry alligator"), and improvised the three-rendezvous alternate mission plan in real time with Mission Control. The same judgment-under-pressure discipline Schirra had demonstrated at Gemini 6A's pad abort three degrees earlier carried forward at Gemini 9A's in-orbit improvisation. Stafford flew as Schirra's pilot at 6A and commanded at 9A, and the judgment DNA is traceable across both missions.
- **Cernan's data closed the EVA research loop in a single flight.** Before Gemini 9A, the EVA research program was still largely theoretical -- based on neutral-buoyancy simulation and one prior EVA (White, Gemini 4). Cernan's two hours and seven minutes provided every critical data point the program needed to redesign the EMU: thermal load, joint resistance, humidity accumulation, visor anti-fog, foot-restraint necessity. Gemini 10, 11, and 12 progressively validated the redesign, and Apollo's lunar EVAs flew with the Cernan-informed architecture. The data cost was near-fatal but the curriculum it generated was dispositive.
- **Folk confirmed as sustained register at degree 46.** Degrees 45 and 46 (Jurado, Votolato) establish that folk is not a one-degree anomaly but a sustained register the engine will develop through Pass 2. Consecutive folk without genre-switching tempo pressure demonstrates that the engine can hold quiet registers across adjacent degrees without forcing contrast, and the decision to preserve the E=3 level across both sides of degree 46 reinforces the pattern.

### What Could Be Better

- **The McDonnell ground-handling error was a process failure that took four months to catch.** The ATDA's failed shroud separation was caused by a lacing wire and disconnect lanyard at the McDonnell Douglas plant that should have been removed before launch but was not. The quality-control process had approved the assembly. Post-mission reviews tightened inspection protocols across every Gemini separation mechanism, but the incident exposed that the ground-handling QC process was not comprehensive enough to catch a single-wire omission on a critical mechanism.
- **The EVA went ahead despite warning signs.** Cernan reported suit difficulties and elevated heart rate within the first twenty minutes of the EVA, and there was a reasonable decision window at that point to terminate and recover. The decision was made to continue toward the AMU task. The ultimate abort came closer to the end of the EVA than it could have. The postmortem lesson -- that EVA abort criteria should be biometric as well as observational -- was formalized in subsequent Gemini and Apollo EVA planning.
- **Votolato's commercial arc plateaued below sustained breakthrough.** The Barsuk/Death Cab production network, the *O.C.* placement, and the NPR designation produced high visibility without the sustained chart presence that would have validated full crossover. The engine documents the plateau as a structural observation: the independent folk circuit has a ceiling that even well-supported artists cannot systematically break through, and Votolato's catalog is a case study in that ceiling's consequences.
- **The Spotted Towhee's taxonomic status remains debated.** The 1995 AOS split separated Spotted and Eastern Towhees, but hybridization in the Great Plains contact zone continues, and some researchers have advocated for a reunified taxonomy or further sub-splits based on genomic evidence. The engine records the current status but cannot resolve the ongoing debate.

### Lessons Learned

1. **See and Bassett's death shadows the mission.** The original crew died in the building where their spacecraft was being assembled. The T-38 crash on February 28, 1966, was attributed to pilot error compounded by poor weather. The redesignation from Gemini 9 to 9A was NASA's scarlet letter of loss, and the engine carries both identities forward in the dedication.

2. **Three rendezvous techniques validated.** M=3 co-elliptic (standard), equiperiod (fuel-efficient alternative), and optical from above (contingency for off-nominal LM ascent). All three entered the Apollo contingency playbook as tested, validated options, extending the rendezvous capability first proven at Gemini 6A in the engine three degrees earlier.

3. **EVA lessons rewrite the curriculum.** Before Gemini 9A, the assumption was that zero-gravity EVA would be comparable to underwater training. Cernan's experience demolished this. The entire approach to extravehicular activity -- suit design, thermal management, task planning, foot restraints -- was rewritten, and the redesign propagated directly into Apollo's lunar surface EMU specification.

4. **The ATDA shroud was a process error, not a design error.** The separation system worked. The explosive bolts fired. The mechanical design was sound. Someone did not remove a wire, and inspection did not catch the omission. The lesson was direct: every separation mechanism must be inspected for ground-handling residue before launch, and the inspection protocol was updated across both Gemini and Apollo before the next flight.

5. **Consecutive folk established.** Degrees 45-46: Jurado then Votolato. The murmur and the confessional. Two modes of folk-rooted storytelling proving that folk is not a one-degree anomaly but a sustained register that the engine will continue to develop through Pass 2 and beyond.

6. **Punk-to-folk conversion as structural pattern.** Waxwing to acoustic guitar. The loud past giving way to the quiet confession. The emotional intensity retained, the instrumentation stripped. The pattern generalizes across multiple engine degrees and is a transferable structural template rather than a Votolato-specific biographical quirk.

7. **Buzz Aldrin's EVA on Gemini 12 benefited directly.** Foot restraints at every work station, simplified tasks, built-in rest periods, suit configuration informed by Cernan's data. Aldrin's Gemini 12 EVA in November 1966 was the first fully successful extended EVA of the Gemini program. It worked because Gemini 9A showed what does not work.

8. **Cernan's arc continues.** The man who could not see through his fogged visor on Gemini 9A walked on the Moon six years later (Apollo 17, December 1972). He became the last human being to stand on the Moon. The first EVA failure led to the last lunar footprint, and the six-and-a-half-year arc between the two endpoints is the longest individual-astronaut curriculum the engine documents.

9. **The 1995 species split mirrors the 1966 mission redesignation.** Rufous-sided Towhee became Spotted and Eastern because morphological, vocal, and behavioral differences could no longer be lumped. Gemini 9 became Gemini 9A because the prime-crew continuity could no longer be preserved. Both are formal acts of separation that the engine documents as a structural pair across thirty years of calendar time.

10. **Graceful partial failure as mission architecture.** Gemini 9A did not fail; it succeeded at a different mission than the one that was planned. Three rendezvous instead of one docking. Two hours and seven minutes of EVA data instead of AMU free flight. The graceful-partial-failure discipline that Lunar Orbiter 3 will exemplify at degree 55 begins its engine tenure at degree 46 with Gemini 9A.

*Degree 46: the angry alligator. They were not supposed to be the crew. Elliot See and Charles Bassett were supposed to fly Gemini 9 -- they had trained for it, earned it, prepared for it. On February 28, 1966, flying a T-38 to the McDonnell plant in St. Louis where their spacecraft was being assembled, they descended through a low cloud ceiling and crashed into the roof of the building that held their ship. Both died. The spacecraft survived. Tom Stafford and Gene Cernan flew instead. The target vehicle arrived in orbit with its shroud stuck half-open -- two fiberglass jaws gaping, the docking port visible but unreachable, the angry alligator in the dark above the Earth. No docking. Three rendezvous instead, each using a different technique, each providing data Apollo would need. Then Cernan went outside. His visor fogged. His suit overheated. His heart rate hit 180. He went blind hanging on the exterior of a spacecraft moving at 7.8 km/s, gripping handrails with pressurized gloves, his lower body swinging with every push because there were no foot restraints. Two hours and seven minutes. He never reached the jet backpack. Every failure taught: foot restraints, liquid cooling, visor anti-fog, simplified tasks. A folk singer from West Seattle drives from club to club, 250 shows a year, arriving at each venue to find the shroud half-open and the docking impossible. A towhee scratches through leaf litter in the undergrowth, bold plumage hidden behind salal, both feet kicking backward, exposing what lies beneath the surface. The alligator's jaws are open. The visor is fogged. The ground is obscured. Scratch through the debris. Find what is underneath. Everything that Cernan's failure taught went into Apollo's EVA design. The man who went blind on Gemini 9A walked on the Moon six years later -- the last human being to stand there.*

*"See and Bassett died in the building where their spacecraft was being assembled. The ship outlived the crew it was built for. The backup crew flew. The shroud would not open. The visor would not clear. Every failure taught. Every lesson landed in Apollo's design. The angry alligator bites, and the program learns to bite back."*
