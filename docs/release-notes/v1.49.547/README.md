## Summary

The forty-seventh paired release and the mission that went wrong at every turn -- and taught everything that needed to be taught before Apollo. Gemini 9A is the mission that should have been Gemini 9. The "A" suffix tells the story of a tragedy. On February 28, 1966, astronauts Elliot See and Charles Bassett -- the prime crew for Gemini 9 -- flew a T-38 Talon jet trainer from Houston to the McDonnell Aircraft plant in St. Louis. The weather was poor: low ceiling, reduced visibility, light rain. See descended below the cloud deck and found himself too fast and too close to the runway. He attempted to go around. The T-38 did not have sufficient energy. The aircraft struck the roof of Building 101 at the McDonnell plant -- the very building where the Gemini 9 spacecraft was being assembled -- and crashed into a parking lot beyond. Both men died instantly. The spacecraft survived. The backup crew -- Tom Stafford and Gene Cernan -- became the prime crew. The mission was redesignated 9A.

When Stafford rendezvoused with the replacement target vehicle -- the Augmented Target Docking Adapter, launched after the original Agena failed to reach orbit -- he saw the angry alligator. The ATDA's payload shroud had failed to separate. Two fiberglass clamshell halves gaped open at approximately 25 degrees on each side, the docking port visible inside the open jaws but physically inaccessible. "It looks like an angry alligator," Stafford radioed. The cause was a ground-handling error: a lacing wire and disconnect lanyard that should have been removed before launch had not been properly configured. The explosive bolts fired but the wire held the shroud halves in place. The alligator's jaws opened but would not let go. Docking was impossible. Stafford and Cernan used the ATDA as a target for three different rendezvous techniques instead -- M=3 co-elliptic, equiperiod, and optical from above -- providing Apollo planners with contingency data for every possible lunar rendezvous scenario.

Then Cernan went outside. His EVA was planned as the most ambitious yet -- translate along the adapter section, don the Astronaut Maneuvering Unit jet backpack, fly freely in space. Within minutes, everything collapsed. The G4C suit's rudimentary cooling system was overwhelmed by solar radiation and Cernan's metabolic heat output. The pressurized suit resisted every joint motion. There were no foot restraints -- every push against a handrail sent his lower body swinging. Cernan was sweating heavily, the moisture saturating the suit's atmosphere, condensing on the coolest surface: the inside of his visor. Within twenty minutes, partially fogged. Within thirty minutes, nearly opaque. He was essentially blind on the outside of a spacecraft traveling at 7.8 km/s. His heart rate exceeded 180 BPM. He lost 4.5 kilograms of body weight from fluid loss. He never reached the AMU. Stafford ordered him back. The EVA lessons went directly into Apollo: foot restraints at every work station, liquid cooling garments, visor anti-fog coatings, simplified task planning. Every failure taught.

The audience was there but never large enough. The reviews were strong but never loud enough. E=3: visible-but-not-mainstream. The angry alligator at every door.

The Spotted Towhee is the bird of the undergrowth -- boldly patterned in black, rufous, and white but nearly invisible in the dense cover where it spends 90% of its time. You hear the towhee constantly: the sharp descending chewink call, the buzzy drink-your-tea song, and the vigorous scratching of leaf litter as the bird feeds with its characteristic double-footed hop-and-kick. You see it rarely. The bird scratches through debris to expose what lies beneath -- the behavioral analog of what Gemini 9A accomplished: scratching through optimistic assumptions about EVA capability to expose the engineering problems that needed to be solved before Apollo.

## Part A: Rocky Votolato -- THE CONFESSIONAL

### Pass 2 Refinement Summary

Three structural alignments deepening the Gemini 9A connection:

**1. The Angry Alligator at Every Door.** Gemini 9A's ATDA arrived in orbit with its shroud stuck half-open -- visible but inaccessible, the docking port right there behind the fiberglass jaws. Votolato's career follows the same structure: the audience was visible but never large enough, the label interest was real but never sufficient, the breakthrough was right there behind a shroud that would not release. Barsuk Records put out his strongest work. Casey Foubert of Pedro the Lion produced four albums. Chris Walla of Death Cab for Cutie was in the orbit. A song on The OC. An NPR Song of the Day. Every signal said the docking should work. The shroud stayed half-open. The angry alligator at every door.

**2. Cernan's EVA as the Touring Grind.** Cernan's 2 hours and 7 minutes outside Gemini 9A were the hardest physical labor any astronaut had performed in space -- fogged visor, ballooned suit, near-total exhaustion, heart rate above 180 BPM, 4.5 kg of fluid lost. Votolato's 250-shows-per-year pace was the hardest physical labor in the engine's folk register. West Seattle to the road and back, over and over, the confessional voice deployed in every small room that would have him. The suit fought every motion. The road fought every mile. Both operated beyond the capacity of their equipment, producing something valuable precisely because the struggle was audible.

**3. Failure as Curriculum.** Gemini 9A did not fail -- it proved that some missions succeed by showing what does not work yet. The shroud error drove improvements in pre-launch inspection. The EVA crisis drove Apollo's entire suit redesign: foot restraints, liquid cooling garments, visor anti-fog, simplified tasks. Five failures, five lessons, each incorporated before the stakes included lives on the Moon. Votolato's catalog -- Makers, True Devotion, Hospital Handshakes, Wild Roots -- is the data from a career that showed what the independent folk circuit costs a body and a family. Each setback taught: punk energy became emotional intensity, major-label experience taught self-reliance, touring lessons taught how to connect with a small audience in a way a large audience never permits.

### Retrospective: Degree 46 Patterns Established

1. **See and Bassett's death shadows the mission.** The original crew died in the building where their spacecraft was being assembled. The T-38 crash on February 28, 1966, was attributed to pilot error compounded by poor weather. The redesignation from Gemini 9 to 9A was NASA's scarlet letter of loss.
2. **Three rendezvous techniques validated.** M=3 co-elliptic (standard), equiperiod (fuel-efficient alternative), and optical from above (contingency for off-nominal LM ascent). All three entered the Apollo contingency playbook as tested, validated options.
3. **EVA lessons rewrite the curriculum.** Before Gemini 9A, the assumption was that zero-gravity EVA would be comparable to underwater training. Cernan's experience demolished this. The entire approach to extravehicular activity -- suit design, thermal management, task planning, foot restraints -- was rewritten.
4. **The ATDA shroud was a process error, not a design error.** The separation system worked. The explosive bolts fired. The mechanical design was sound. Someone did not remove a wire, and inspection did not catch the omission. The lesson was direct: every separation mechanism must be inspected for ground-handling residue before launch.
5. **Consecutive folk established.** Degrees 45-46: Jurado then Votolato. The murmur and the confessional. Two modes of folk-rooted storytelling proving that folk is not a one-degree anomaly but a sustained register.
6. **Punk-to-folk conversion as structural pattern.** Waxwing to acoustic guitar. The loud past giving way to the quiet confession. The emotional intensity retained, the instrumentation stripped.
7. **Buzz Aldrin's EVA on Gemini 12 benefited directly.** Foot restraints at every work station, simplified tasks, built-in rest periods, suit configuration informed by Cernan's data. Aldrin's EVA was the first fully successful extended EVA of the Gemini program. It worked because Gemini 9A showed what does not work.
8. **Cernan's arc continues.** The man who could not see through his fogged visor on Gemini 9A walked on the Moon six years later (Apollo 17, December 1972). He became the last human being to stand on the Moon. The first EVA failure led to the last lunar footprint.

## Part B: Spotted Towhee -- THE RASP

### Pass 2 Refinement Summary

Three structural alignments deepening the organism-mission resonance:

**1. Scratching Through Debris.** The Spotted Towhee feeds by kicking backward through leaf litter with both feet simultaneously -- a vigorous double-footed hop-and-kick that throws debris behind the bird, exposing seeds and invertebrates beneath. The scratch is loud, energetic, and sustained. Cernan's EVA was the same kind of persistent, repetitive attack on an obstacle: both hands gripping smooth handrails with pressurized gloves that had the dexterity of oven mitts, body swinging with every push, scratching through the accumulated unknowns of EVA physics. The towhee exposes what is underneath the surface. Cernan exposed what was underneath the optimistic assumptions. Both are acts of revelation through physical effort, and both leave the surface disturbed but more honest.

**2. The Hidden and the Visible.** The Spotted Towhee's plumage is among the most boldly patterned of any North American sparrow -- jet-black head, bright rufous flanks, white spots on black wings. Yet the bird spends most of its time invisible behind a curtain of salal and blackberry. Display is brief. Work is constant. Gemini 9A had the same dual character: the angry alligator photograph was the visible spectacle, one of the most recognizable images of the Gemini program. Behind it, the real work was invisible: three rendezvous profiles providing Apollo planners with contingency data, and the EVA crisis -- no live television, communicated only through Cernan's strained voice. The world saw the alligator. The engineers used the data. The bold pattern and the hidden life.

**3. Disturbance Creates the Habitat.** The Spotted Towhee thrives in disturbed habitats -- secondary growth that colonizes ground after fire, windthrow, or human clearing. The bird cannot live in mature, closed-canopy forest (too dark) or open grassland (too exposed). It needs the middle ground: the post-failure landscape where new growth is dense and productive. Gemini 9A was itself a product of disturbance: the death of See and Bassett destroyed the original plan, the backup crew flew instead. The mission thrived in the aftermath of tragedy, producing data that the original plan might not have generated.

### Carry-Forward Items from Degree 46

- **Gemini EVA curriculum rewritten.** Foot restraints, liquid cooling, visor anti-fog, simplified tasks. Every lesson from Cernan's failure went into the next mission. Gemini 10, 11, and 12 all benefited. Aldrin's Gemini 12 EVA was the proof.
- **Three rendezvous profiles in Apollo's playbook.** Co-elliptic, equiperiod, from above. If the Lunar Module's ascent trajectory is off-nominal, alternatives are now tested and validated.
- **Pre-launch inspection procedures tightened.** The ATDA shroud failure -- a wire that should not have been there -- drove review of every separation mechanism, jettison system, and deployable structure across Gemini and Apollo.
- **Passerellidae pair established.** Song Sparrow (45) and Spotted Towhee (46). Wetland edge to forest shrub. Melodious to buzzy. The family's ground-level vocabulary expands.
- **The 1995 towhee split.** Rufous-sided Towhee became Spotted (western) and Eastern. The split mirrors the 9/9A redesignation: one entity became two because distinguishing characteristics could no longer be lumped.
- **Cernan's arc: from fogged visor to last footprint.** Gemini 9A (1966) to Apollo 17 (1972). The man who went blind on an EVA became the last human to stand on the Moon. Six years between the worst EVA in the program and the best lunar surface mission.
- **See and Bassett's arc ends.** Both men's first spaceflight would have been Gemini 9. Neither flew. The spacecraft they trained for -- in the building where they died -- was flown by their backup crew. The "A" suffix is a memorial.
- **Stafford's experience deepens.** Second Gemini flight (after 6A). He will command Apollo 10 -- the dress rehearsal, 15.6 km above the lunar surface -- and Apollo-Soyuz. Three rendezvous on 9A, three subsequent commands.
