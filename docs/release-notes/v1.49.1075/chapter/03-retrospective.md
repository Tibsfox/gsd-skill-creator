## Surprises

- **A great telescope was lifted above the air on a Space Shuttle.** Hubble launched 24 April 1990 aboard the Space Shuttle Discovery on mission STS-31 and was deployed by the crew into low Earth orbit the next day, carrying a 2.4-metre primary mirror above the bulk of the blurring, absorbing atmosphere — the first of NASA's four Great Observatories.
- **A mirror flaw found after launch was corrected by astronauts in orbit.** Shortly after deployment the primary mirror was found to have spherical aberration; on the first servicing mission, in December 1993, astronauts installed COSTAR and the Wide Field and Planetary Camera 2 and restored the observatory to its designed sharpness — an engineering triumph of on-orbit servicing, and the first time a telescope was serviced and upgraded by human hands in space.
- **A patient stare into a tiny dark patch of sky filled with thousands of distant galaxies.** The Hubble Deep Field (1995) and the Ultra Deep Field (2004) revealed thousands of faint, distant galaxies in slivers of seemingly empty sky, light from the deep past — the deep observation that the clear eye above the air makes possible.
- **A telescope read the air of a world around another star.** In 2001 Hubble made the first detection of an exoplanet's atmosphere, measuring sodium absorption in the transiting hot Jupiter HD 209458b (published in ApJ 2002) — the first time the composition of a planet's atmosphere around another star was measured.
- **A fleet shrub-steppe mammal has the keenest far sight of any North American land mammal.** The Pronghorn (*Antilocapra americana*), a fleet, open-country hoofed mammal of the interior Pacific Northwest's shrub-steppe and the sole living member of its family (Antilocapridae), has large protruding eyes giving a very wide field of view and the keenest eyesight of any North American land mammal — often likened to built-in binoculars (about 8x), sweeping the open distance (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Hubble, the clear eye sweeping the far distance.
- **A small woodland wildflower scatters its tiny white stars across the forest floor.** The Pacific Starflower (*Lysimachia latifolia*, formerly *Trientalis latifolia*), a small, delicate Pacific Northwest woodland wildflower of shady conifer forests, lifts tiny white-to-pink star-shaped flowers on slender threadlike stalks above a whorl of leaves — a scatter of stars across the forest floor (ecological description only) — a mirror of the deep field of stars and galaxies that Hubble gathers.
