1. **A rotation DROPS the predecessor's domain vocabulary and adopts a new one.**
   Unlike a sustain, this is a ROTATION (mode=rotation): Hubble and the New Horizons predecessor share almost no subject vocabulary, so the rewrite REPLACED nearly every content word — the deep-space probe, the flyby, the orbit insertion, the gravity assist, the giant planets, the Kuiper Belt, Pluto, Arrokoth, the radioisotope generator, the fastest-launch record, the Johns Hopkins APL, Alan Stern, the Atlas V 551, and the Silver-haired Bat + Tolmie's Saxifrage pairing all went OUT — and brought IN the new space-telescope vocabulary: the orbital observatory above the atmosphere, the 2.4-metre mirror, the science instruments, the astronaut servicing missions, the Great Observatories program, the deep fields, the first exoplanet-atmosphere detection, and the Pronghorn + Pacific Starflower…

2. **A rotation OPENS a new axis — the obs counter resets to obs#1 and there are no prior siblings on the new axis.**
   Hubble OPENS the SPACE-TELESCOPE-EXPLORATION axis at obs#1 (via substrate-axis rotation #38), so the SPACE-TELESCOPE-EXPLORATION-AXIS-OPEN counter starts at obs#1 and the substrate-axis-rotation discipline ADVANCES (obs#101 → obs#102) because a NEW rotation is observed. The only backward reference is the single deliberate nav-prev to the 1.257 New Horizons predecessor, noting that the now-complete outer-planets axis (obs#1–8) gives way to the new space-telescope axis.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12519_

3. **Hubble is a NASA + ESA mission, managed by Goddard with science operations at STScI — NOT JPL/APL/Ames.**
   It is a joint NASA + ESA mission (ESA joined 1975, about 15% of funding), managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center with science operations run by the Space Telescope Science Institute (operated by AURA) — a fresh management center for the new axis; the catalog states this precisely throughout.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12520_

4. **Hubble launched aboard the Space Shuttle Discovery (STS-31) on 24 April 1990 and was deployed into low Earth orbit the next day.**
   It launched 24 April 1990 (08:33 EDT) aboard the Space Shuttle Discovery on mission STS-31 and was deployed by the crew on 25 April 1990, carrying a 2.4-metre primary mirror above the bulk of the blurring atmosphere; the catalog states this precisely.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12521_

5. **The Great-Observatories achievement is the first of NASA's Great Observatories, not "the first space telescope.**
   Hubble is the first of NASA's four Great Observatories (and the first launched of the four); the catalog scopes this strictly to "first of NASA's GREAT OBSERVATORIES" and explicitly does NOT call it the first space telescope — earlier orbiting observatories existed (OAO-2 1968, IUE 1978).
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12522_

6. **The servicing achievement is the first/only telescope serviced and upgraded by astronauts in orbit; the optics fix is an engineering triumph.**
   Hubble was serviced five times by Space Shuttle crews (1993–2009); the catalog scopes this as the first and only telescope ever serviced and upgraded by astronauts in space, and frames the 1990 spherical-aberration flaw and the 1993 COSTAR/WFPC2 correction positively as an engineering triumph of on-orbit servicing, not a failure.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12523_

7. **The exoplanet achievement is the first detection of an exoplanet's atmosphere (HD 209458b, 2001/2002).**
   In 2001 Hubble detected sodium absorption in the transiting hot Jupiter HD 209458b (Charbonneau et al., published in ApJ 2002); the catalog scopes this as the first detection of an exoplanet's atmosphere and notes that NASA lists it among Hubble's science firsts.
   _⚙ Status: `investigate` · lesson #12524_

Hubble's firsts are being the first of NASA's Great Observatories, the first telescope serviced and upgraded by astronauts in orbit, and the first detection of an exoplanet's atmosphere, yielding 0 re-stamps.
