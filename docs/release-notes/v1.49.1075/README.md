## Mission Overview

The Hubble Space Telescope is the obs#1 opener of the SPACE-TELESCOPE-EXPLORATION axis — a large optical/ultraviolet space observatory whose defining achievements are being the first of NASA's four Great Observatories, the first (and only) telescope serviced and upgraded by astronauts in orbit, and the first to detect the atmosphere of a planet around another star. It is a joint NASA + ESA mission, managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center with science operations run by the Space Telescope Science Institute (STScI, operated by AURA — NOT JPL/APL/Ames). A school-bus-sized observatory carrying a 2.4-metre primary mirror, it launched aboard the Space Shuttle Discovery on mission STS-31 on 24 April 1990 and was deployed by the crew into low Earth orbit the next day, observing in optical, ultraviolet, and near-infrared light from a roughly 95-minute orbit above the bulk of the blurring, absorbing atmosphere. Hubble carried a suite of science instruments that has been renewed across five astronaut servicing missions; the instruments flown over its life include the Wide Field and Planetary Camera (WFPC and later WFPC2), the Wide Field Camera 3 (WFC3), the Faint Object Camera (FOC) and Faint Object Spectrograph (FOS), the Goddard High Resolution Spectrograph (GHRS), the Space Telescope Imaging Spectrograph (STIS), the Near Infrared Camera and Multi-Object Spectrometer (NICMOS), the Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS), the Cosmic Origins Spectrograph (COS), the Fine Guidance Sensors that hold the telescope steady and serve as astrometric instruments, and the Corrective Optics Space Telescope Axial Replacement (COSTAR) that corrected the early mirror flaw. The story is the launch above the air, the on-orbit repair, the long deep watch, and the science it returns. Shortly after deployment the primary mirror was found to have spherical aberration; on the first servicing mission, in December 1993, astronauts installed COSTAR and the Wide Field and Planetary Camera 2 to correct the figure — an engineering triumph that restored the observatory to its designed sharpness. Across five Space Shuttle servicing missions (December 1993, February 1997, December 1999, March 2002, and May 2009) astronaut crews repaired and upgraded the telescope in orbit — the first and only telescope ever serviced and upgraded by human hands in space. In 2001 Hubble made the first detection of an exoplanet's atmosphere, measuring sodium absorption in the transiting hot Jupiter HD 209458b (Charbonneau, Brown, Noyes, and Gilliland; published in ApJ 2002) by transmission spectroscopy across four transits — the first time the composition of a planet's atmosphere around another star was measured. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: it is the first of NASA's Great Observatories (NOT "the first space telescope" — earlier orbiting observatories existed, among them OAO-2 in 1968 and IUE in 1978); it is the first/only telescope serviced and upgraded by astronauts in orbit (the optics fix is an engineering triumph, framed positively); and it made the first detection of an exoplanet's atmosphere. Hubble continues to operate in 2026, in its 36th year, in single-gyro mode since 2024, with no further servicing missions scheduled. Hubble OPENS the SPACE-TELESCOPE-EXPLORATION substrate axis at obs#1 (substrate-axis rotation #38). NSSDCA/COSPAR 1990-037B.

### What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived Hubble imagery.** The Hubble shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual mission data or Hubble images. A future revision could render a real Hubble view — a deep field, the Pillars of Creation, or the telescope itself above the blue limb of Earth (publicly available from STScI and the National Space Science Data Center) — for a higher-fidelity view.
- **The Hubble diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the Hubble Space Telescope with its primary-mirror assembly, solar arrays, and aperture door.
- **The launch, repair, and deep-field modes are depicted rather than driven by real data.** The deployment, the servicing repair, and the deep-field modes are depicted but not rendered from archived STS-31 imagery, servicing-mission footage, or the real Hubble Deep Field exposures; a future ship could drive the modes from the actual image sets for a quantitatively faithful reading.
- **The long-watch depiction is depicted rather than computed.** The long deep watch and the slow orbital decay are depicted rather than rendered from the real orbital ephemeris and the measured altitude decline; a future ship could drive a long-watch sub-mode from the actual two-line-element history and the published orbit-decay figures for a quantitatively faithful view.

v1.258 Hubble Space Telescope is dedicated to the people across NASA, the European Space Agency, the Goddard Space Flight Center, the Space Telescope Science Institute, and the mission's many partners who built, launched, repaired, and have kept watch over the clear eye above the air for more than thirty-five years.
