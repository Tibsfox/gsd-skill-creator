### NORTH AMERICA'S SMALLEST WOODPECKER

Downy Woodpecker (*Dryobates pubescens*). 14-17 cm body length. Wingspan 25-30 cm. Weight 21-28 g. The smallest woodpecker in North America. Black-and-white plumage with white back stripe, black wings with white spots, males with small red nape patch. Short, stubby bill -- distinctly shorter than head depth. *Dryobates* from Greek *drys* ('tree') + *bates* ('walker'). *pubescens* from Latin 'downy' -- referring to the soft feathers on the lower back.

### THE SOUND: PIK + SOFT RATTLE DRUM -- THE QUIET PERCUSSION

Pik: a soft, flat contact call. Much quieter than the Hairy Woodpecker's sharp peek. The rattle drum: a rapid descending series of notes, softer and higher-pitched than the Hairy's drum. E=4 -- present but not dominant, audible but not commanding. The smallest woodpecker makes the smallest sound. The pik is diagnostic: flat where the Hairy's peek is sharp. The drum is diagnostic: soft where the Hairy's is loud. Everything about the Downy is a quieter, gentler version of the Hairy.

The classic field-identification challenge in North American birding. Hairy Woodpecker (110, sharp peek + drum, E=5, 23-26 cm, 40-95 g) then Downy Woodpecker (111, pik + soft rattle, E=4, 14-17 cm, 21-28 g). Consecutive degrees. The engine places them side by side. Key differences:

The Downy is not a smaller Hairy -- they are ecologically distinct. The Downy forages on smaller branches and twigs that cannot support the Hairy's weight. Size partitioning allows coexistence. The comparison pair at consecutive degrees is deliberate: the engine teaches by juxtaposition.
