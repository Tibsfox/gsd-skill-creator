**The Jaw-Pop as Mechanical Percussive Vocalization.** The **jaw-pop** is acoustically distinct from vocal-fold-produced vocalizations in ways that the E=6 rating captures but does not fully explain. The sound is mechanical rather than vocal: produced by rapidly opening and snapping shut the jaws, causing the upper and lower teeth to clack together and be amplified by the resonant mouth cavity. This produces a broadband percussive transient covering 100-5,000 Hz with peak energy in the 200-2,000 Hz range, individual pops lasting 0.05-0.15 seconds, typically repeated at 1-3 pops per second in sequences of 3-5 pops when agitation escalates. The broadband frequency content penetrates dense Pacific Northwest forest vegetation more effectively than narrower-frequency vocal-fold vocalizations because the transient's broad spectral content ensures that at least some frequency components will traverse the vegetation's acoustic filtering, carrying the jaw-pop 50-100 meters in closed-canopy conditions. The sound's sharpness — its rise-time and spectral content — is designed for the startle response; the Black Bear jaw-pop does not merely communicate information, it triggers involuntary stress response in the listener, converting the encounter from passive-observation to active-alert within fractions of a second. The E=6 rating captures this startle-designed percussive intensity, placing the jaw-pop at the upper end of the species' defensive-vocalization amplitude range short of actual aggression.

**The Defensive Escalation Ladder: Huff, Jaw-Pop, Bluff Charge.** The Black Bear's defensive-communication repertoire operates as a graduated escalation ladder designed to resolve encounters without physical conflict. The **huff** (degree 200, E=5) is the initial awareness-signaling vocalization — a rapid, forceful exhalation produced through the nose and mouth that communicates "I notice you" without demanding behavioral response. The **jaw-pop** (degree 201, E=6) escalates from awareness to defensive-stress distance-demand — communicating "back off now" and triggering the startle response that the broadband percussive transient is designed to produce. The **bluff charge** (behavioral display beyond the vocal-and-percussive repertoire) represents the final pre-contact display, where the bear approaches rapidly without actual physical contact, usually veering off at the last moment. This produces a broadband percussive transient covering 100-5,000 Hz with peak energy in the 200-2,000 Hz range, individual pops lasting 0.05-0.15 seconds, typically repeated at 1-3 pops per second in sequences of 3-5 pops when agitation escalates. The jaw-pop carries 50-100 meters in closed-canopy conditions, which is a significant acoustic range given that Pacific Northwest old-growth and mature second-growth forest typically attenuates narrower-frequency sounds much more aggressively. The forest-acoustics register — how different sound-production mechanisms interact with dense-vegetation acoustic filtering — enters the engine's acoustic-ecology vocabulary through the jaw-pop's specific spectral-vegetation-interaction signature.

The broadband percussive rise-time — the rate at which the sound rises from silence to peak amplitude — is calibrated to activate the listener's acoustic-startle reflex, converting the encounter from passive-observation to active-alert within fractions of a second. The Black Bear jaw-pop does not merely communicate information; it physiologically alters the listener's state. This startle-design register enters the engine's acoustic-function vocabulary as distinct from information-communication registers (bugle, cow call) and from generic alarm-signaling registers (huff).
