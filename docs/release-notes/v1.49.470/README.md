The mass emergence of Plecoptera (stoneflies) and Trichoptera (caddisflies) from Pacific Northwest mountain streams is one of the most acoustically significant events in the region's riparian ecosystem, and one of the most delayed arrivals in the engine's acoustic inventory. The simultaneous emergence of thousands of adult insects from their aquatic larval form generates a multi-component acoustic event: the collective wing-beat hum of mass emergence (thousands of newly-winged adults simultaneously taking first flight), the water-surface disturbance of larvae breaking the meniscus at emergence, the acoustic feeding frenzy of fish (cutthroat trout, rainbow trout, Dolly Varden char) responding to surface emergence, and the arrival and wing-beat signatures of riparian insectivores (dippers, swallows, flycatchers) responding to the hatch.

E=9 for the stonefly and caddisfly mass emergence is the correct assignment for an event that is both ecologically foundational and acoustically unprecedented in the SPS track. Stoneflies (*Plecoptera*) are among the most ancient insect orders -- their lineage traces to the Permian, 300 million years ago -- and their presence in a mountain stream is the gold standard indicator of water quality: no stoneflies means water quality failure. Caddisflies (*Trichoptera*) are the Pacific Northwest's most speciose aquatic insect group, with over 1,200 North American species and dozens of species present in any given healthy mountain stream. Their mass emergence, coordinated by photoperiod and water temperature, is a synchronized event that represents millions of years of evolved timing precision.
