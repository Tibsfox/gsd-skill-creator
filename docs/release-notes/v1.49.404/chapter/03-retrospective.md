Future same-band documentation should enumerate the full release chronology so that the catalog is anchored to specific release dates rather than to the bracketing full-lengths alone.
- **The Cascadia Subduction Zone documentation at degree 263 identified the January 26, 1700 full-rupture event and the modeled M9.0 scenario but did not enumerate the previous pre-1700 full-rupture events documented in the Brian Atwater tidal-marsh subsidence record.** The degree 263 documentation notes that the Brian Atwater and collaborators' coastal paleoseismology record documents multiple previous subsidence events beneath the January 1700 layer, but does not enumerate the specific previous events (their approximate dates, their estimated magnitudes, the recurrence-interval distribution). Future Cascadia-adjacent documentation should enumerate the full paleoseismology record so that the January 1700 event is contextualized within the multi-event recurrence cycle rather than treated as a standalone event.
- **The P-wave physics documentation at degree 263 identified the 5-7 km/s crustal velocity and the compressional-wave longitudinal mode but did not include the distance-amplitude decay curve that would translate the source amplitude to typical-observer amplitudes at typical Puget Sound distances from a Cascadia source.** The P-wave propagation speed is specific and physically anchored; the typical-observer experience depends on the distance-amplitude decay curve, which follows geometric spreading plus anelastic attenuation in crustal rock. Future P-wave-adjacent documentation should include the distance-decay curve so that the source amplitude is contextualized by the typical-observer amplitude at representative Pacific Northwest population-center distances from the fault.
- **The Doug Martsch developmental-arc documentation at degree 263 referenced the future Built to Spill engine entry but did not specify the anticipated engine position or energy profile of that entry.** The degree 263 documentation notes that **Built to Spill** will arrive at a different engine position at a different energy profile, but does not commit to the specific position or amplitude. Future developmental-arc documentation should either commit to the anticipated future position (allowing cross-reference from the earlier-band entry) or explicitly defer the commitment (with a designated review point at which the commitment will be made). The present documentation is in the unresolved middle, which is less structurally useful than either of the bracketing alternatives.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Multi-band developmental arcs should be documented across multiple engine positions rather than collapsed into a single entry.** The Treepeople-to-Built to Spill arc documents **Doug Martsch**'s developmental trajectory across two structurally distinct bands (post-hardcore versus melodic alternative rock), and the engine's decision to place Treepeople at degree 263 and Built to Spill at a later engine position preserves the distinct stylistic centers of the two bands without collapsing either into the other. Future multi-band developmental arcs (Mark Arm across Green River and Mudhoney, Kurt Bloch across multiple bands, and others) should follow the same multi-position template rather than single-position summary.
2. **Regional music-infrastructure registers should explicitly acknowledge geographic breadth beyond administrative boundaries when the documented activity operated at the broader scope.** The Treepeople entry at degree 263 explicitly extends the Pacific Northwest alternative-rock regional network beyond Seattle, Olympia, Bellingham, and Portland to include Boise, Idaho via the I-5 corridor and the Cascade-crossing routes. Future regional-infrastructure documentation should acknowledge similar geographic breadth when the documented activity genuinely operated at the broader scope, because collapsing the broader scope to narrower administrative boundaries would misrepresent the actual infrastructure that supported the activity.
3. **First-arrival acoustic signals should be documented at amplitudes that reflect their first-arrival character rather than at the peak amplitudes of the larger structures they precede.** The P-wave at E=5 and Treepeople at E=7 both reflect first-arrival amplitudes appropriate to the first-arrival character of each signal. The P-wave is not the peak amplitude of a Cascadia earthquake sequence; Treepeople is not the peak amplitude of Doug Martsch's developmental arc. Future first-arrival documentation should register amplitudes that match the first-arrival character rather than projecting the eventual peak amplitude backward onto the first-arrival entry.
4. **Paleoseismology documentation benefits from citation of multiple independent evidence lines when they independently converge on the same conclusion.** The January 26, 1700 Cascadia full-rupture date is confirmed by two independent evidence lines: **Japanese Hōei-era** tsunami records from across the Pacific and **Brian Atwater** and collaborators' coastal paleoseismology record from the regional coast. Future paleoseismology-adjacent documentation should explicitly cite the multiple independent evidence lines when they exist, because the convergence of independent lines is what distinguishes rigorous paleoseismology from speculative date-assignment.
5. **Geophysical-hazard documentation should anchor acoustic claims in specific physics parameters (propagation speed, wave mode, frequency range) rather than in generic low-frequency description.** The degree 263 P-wave entry grounds the subsonic-rumble acoustic register in the 5-7 km/s propagation speed, the below-20-Hz frequency range, the compressional-wave longitudinal mode, and the arrival-time relationship to the S-wave and surface waves. Future geophysical-hazard documentation should anchor acoustic claims in specific measurable physics parameters rather than in impressionistic description, because the physics-grounding register is what distinguishes measurable geophysical documentation from impressionistic weather-style description.
6. **Cross-sync registers that operate across radically different temporal scales (years-scale band catalog versus seconds-scale wave arrival) benefit from explicit temporal-scale bridging in the cross-sync paragraphs.** The degree 263 cross-sync paragraphs explicitly bridge the Treepeople catalog (4-year active span) and the P-wave first-arrival (seconds-scale arrival before S-wave) by identifying the shared structural principle (first arrival of something larger to come) at the level of abstraction that holds across both temporal scales. Future cross-sync documentation of radically-different-temporal-scale pair-halves should operate at this level of temporal-scale-bridging abstraction.
7. **Geophysics arcs benefit from explicit documentation of the position-within-arc (e.g., "position 3 of 7") so that readers can locate the current entry within the arc's full trajectory.** The degree 263 entry explicitly designates GEOPHYSICS ARC DEGREE 3 OF 7, locating the current entry at the arc's approximate midpoint on the upslope. Future multi-degree-arc documentation should include the position-within-arc designation so that readers can orient the current entry to the arc's opening, midpoint, and closing positions, because the arc-position register is a first-class structural element of multi-degree-arc navigation.
8. **Subduction-zone structure documentation should cite the fault length, plate convergence rate, and plate geometry rather than treating the fault as a generic earthquake-source region.** The Cascadia Subduction Zone entry at degree 263 cites the 620-mile fault length, the approximately 40 mm/year Juan de Fuca Plate convergence rate, the plate geometry (Juan de Fuca subducting beneath North American), and the locked-portion depth range (approximately 20-30 kilometers). Future subduction-zone or fault-zone documentation should cite the specific tectonic parameters rather than treating the fault as a generic seismic-hazard region, because the parameter-grounding register is what distinguishes specific tectonics documentation from generic earthquake description.
