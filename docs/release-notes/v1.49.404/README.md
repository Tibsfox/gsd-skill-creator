## Summary

**Treepeople** arrive at E=7 -- the post-hardcore and alternative rock band from Boise, Idaho, operating in the Pacific Northwest orbit through their years of touring and recording from 1988 to 1994, and documented here as the pre-Built to Spill vessel for Doug Martsch's development as a musician and songwriter. Treepeople formed in Boise around Doug Martsch (guitar, vocals), Pat Brown (bass), and a series of drummers -- including Scott Schmaljohn and Wayne Flower -- and released records on C/Z Records and Toxic Shock before Martsch dissolved the project and formed Built to Spill in 1992. The Treepeople catalog -- *Time Whore* (1990), *Guilt, Regret & Embarrassment* (1992), *Something Vicious for Tomorrow* (1993) -- documents a band moving rapidly through the stages of post-hardcore toward the melodic rock architecture that Martsch would fully develop in Built to Spill. The songs are faster, less polished, more aggressive than what came after, but the harmonic intelligence that would define *There's Nothing Wrong with Love* (1994) and *Perfect from Now On* (1997) is already present in compressed form. E=7 for Treepeople reflects the energy of a band playing post-hardcore at a level of technical ambition above the average for the format -- fast, rhythmically precise, melodically inventive under the distortion.

The Treepeople entry at degree 263 is a document of what the Pacific Northwest rock scene looked like in the late 1980s and early 1990s outside Seattle proper: Boise, Idaho is not Seattle, but it was networked into the same touring circuit, the same label infrastructure, and the same alternative rock conversation that was happening in Seattle, Olympia, Bellingham, and Portland. The I-5 corridor and the connecting routes through the Cascades created a regional music network that extended further east than the Puget Sound basin, and Boise was part of it. Treepeople toured the Pacific Northwest extensively during their active years, and Doug Martsch moved to Seattle in the early 1990s -- the Seattle 360 engine documents both the origin and the transit.

The Treepeople entry is also a Cascadia Subduction Zone document. The CSZ is the geological structure that underlies all of the Pacific Northwest's seismic hazard planning, and it appears in the engine here for the first time in the SPS track, through the earthquake P-wave that is the acoustic signature of a Cascadia event.

On the soundscape side, **Earthquake P-Wave Cascadia** arrives at E=5 -- the subsonic rumble of a P-wave (primary wave, compressional wave) propagating through the crust from a Cascadia Subduction Zone source. The P-wave is the first arrival in an earthquake sequence: it travels faster than the S-wave (shear wave) and the surface waves that follow, and it arrives as a deep subsonic rumble, a low-frequency vibration below the range of clear human hearing, seconds before the more violent S-wave and surface wave ground motion. In a large Cascadia event -- the modeled M9.0 scenario that has been the centerpiece of Pacific Northwest earthquake preparedness since the 1990s -- the P-wave would propagate through the Pacific Northwest at velocities of 5-7 km/s. The acoustic signature of this propagation, heard as infrasound or felt as physical vibration in the seconds before stronger shaking begins, is what the engine documents at E=5. E=5 is accurate: the P-wave is not the most energetic part of the earthquake sequence, but it is real, it is infrasonic, and it is the first signal that a major seismic event has occurred.

The pairing at degree 263 -- post-hardcore's compressed, fast, technically ambitious energy alongside the first acoustic signal of the largest seismic threat in the Pacific Northwest -- is the geophysics arc's most architecturally precise degree. Treepeople as the pre-Built to Spill iteration of Doug Martsch is itself a kind of P-wave: the first arrival of something larger that is coming.
