**NBHF Echolocation and the Ultrasonic Communication Above the Human Ceiling.** The Harbor Porpoise's **Narrow-Band High-Frequency (NBHF)** echolocation is among the highest-frequency echolocation signals produced by any cetacean. The clicks concentrate energy in a narrow bandwidth around **125-140 kHz**, durations of approximately 100 microseconds, and extremely high directionality — the beam is narrow and the off-axis amplitude drops steeply. The narrow bandwidth and high frequency combine to produce a signal that is **inaudible to humans**, **inaudible to most cetaceans** (including the primary predator, the transient orca, whose hearing peaks in the 15-30 kHz range), and highly informative for prey detection against cluttered coastal backgrounds. The **Au et al. 1999** study in the *Journal of the Acoustical Society of America* was the canonical characterization of free-ranging harbor porpoise clicks; the **Villadsgaard et al. 2007** follow-up in *Journal of Experimental Biology* confirmed the parameters in wild populations. The engine's acoustic vocabulary at degree 187 preserves NBHF as the **ultrasonic echolocation register** distinct from the broadband echolocation of dolphins and the lower-frequency tonal calls of baleen whales.

**Whistle Loss and the Evolutionary Signature of Anti-Predator Stealth.** One of the Harbor Porpoise's most remarkable adaptations is what is **absent** rather than present: the species has undergone **evolutionary whistle loss**. Unlike most dolphins and many other odontocetes which produce tonal whistles for social communication, the Harbor Porpoise produces **no whistles at all**. The **Morisaka & Connor 2007** paper in *Journal of Evolutionary Biology* documented the co-evolution of whistle loss and NBHF clicks as a response to **killer whale predation** pressure. The evolutionary logic: any broadband or tonal sound below 100 kHz is audible to orcas and thus reveals the porpoise's location to its primary predator. The Harbor Porpoise has eliminated **all** broadband sound production and concentrated all signaling above the orca hearing range, making the animal effectively **acoustically invisible** to its primary predator. **Silence is not absence — it is strategy.** The engine's evolutionary vocabulary at degree 187 adds **whistle loss** as a first-class anti-predator adaptation register, joining **crypsis**, **camouflage**, **mimicry**, **nocturnal activity**, and other stealth strategies across the engine's prior species documentation.

## Part B: Harbor Porpoise (*Phocoena phocoena*) — The New Absolute Engine Minimum and the Stealth Strategy of Phocoenidae

- **Second Phocoenidae in the Engine and the Close of the Family Pair.** The Harbor Porpoise (*Phocoena phocoena*) enters at degree 187 as the second Phocoenidae in the engine after the **Dall's Porpoise** at degree 186, closing the family pair in two consecutive degrees; the pair documents **opposite survival strategies** within the same family — the Dall's Porpoise at E=5 with rooster-tail spray and bow-riding representing the **speed strategy**, and the Harbor Porpoise at E=1 with acoustic and behavioral silence representing the **stealth strategy** — a within-family strategy-binary register that enters the engine's taxonomy vocabulary at degree 187 as a first-class structural event.
- **E=1 New Absolute Engine Minimum and the Inaudibility Floor.** The Harbor Porpoise establishes E=1 as the new absolute engine minimum, below the prior E=2 floor held jointly by **Hollis Resnik** (degree 119) and the **Flammulated Owl** (degree 144); E=1 does not represent absence of sound production (echolocation is active and functional) but rather complete inaudibility to human ears — the signal exists and the failure is in reception rather than transmission; the engine's energy-calibration vocabulary at degree 187 adds E=1 as the inaudibility floor distinct from a hypothetical E=0 (which would represent absence of sound production altogether).
- **NBHF Echolocation at 120-150 kHz Six to Seven Times Above Human Hearing.** The Harbor Porpoise's communication is exclusively through **Narrow-Band High-Frequency (NBHF)** echolocation clicks concentrated at **120-150 kHz** — six to seven times above the human hearing ceiling of approximately 20 kHz; the clicks are extremely directional with narrow beams, durations of approximately 100 microseconds, and concentrate energy in a narrow bandwidth; the signal is among the highest-frequency echolocation produced by any cetacean, and **Au et al. 1999** (*J. Acoust. Soc. Am.*) and **Villadsgaard et al. 2007** (*J. Exp. Biol.*) provide the canonical and field-study characterizations.
- **Whistle Loss as Anti-Predator Adaptation Against Bigg's Transient Orcas.** The Harbor Porpoise has undergone evolutionary **whistle loss** — the elimination of tonal broadband social vocalizations — as anti-predator adaptation against **Bigg's transient orca** predation; the **Morisaka & Connor 2007** paper in *Journal of Evolutionary Biology* documents the co-evolution of whistle loss and NBHF clicks under orca predation pressure; the evolutionary logic is that any broadband sound below 100 kHz is audible to orcas (whose hearing peaks 15-30 kHz) and thus reveals the porpoise's location, so the species eliminated all such sound and concentrated signaling above the predator's hearing range.
- **Behavioral Crypsis: Small Body, Brief Surfacing, No Breaching, Boat-Avoidance.** The Harbor Porpoise exhibits behavioral crypsis paired with acoustic silence: the small body (1.4-1.9 meters length, 55-75 kilograms weight), brief surfacing (a small dorsal fin and a rolling breath lasting under a second), absence of breaching, absence of water-slapping, absence of bow-riding (in contrast to the Dall's Porpoise which bow-rides explosively), and active avoidance of boats and humans combine to minimize visual and kinetic detectability; sound silence + behavioral silence = near-invisibility.
- **Salish Sea Resident Population of 10,000-15,000 and Recolonization Since 2000s.** An estimated 10,000-15,000 Harbor Porpoises inhabit the inland waters of Washington State (**Puget Sound**, **Strait of Juan de Fuca**, **San Juan Islands**, outer coast); the species largely disappeared from southern Puget Sound during the 1970s-1990s possibly due to pollution, vessel traffic, and prey declines, and has been recolonizing southern Puget Sound since the 2000s in a quiet recovery — a counter-narrative to the usual marine mammal decline stories; the species is a year-round resident present in all twelve months without migratory movement.
- **Diet and Foraging in Shallow Coastal Waters.** The Harbor Porpoise forages in shallow coastal waters on small schooling fish including **herring**, **anchovy**, **sand lance**, and **smelt**; dives are typically 30-90 seconds in duration and 50-100 meters in depth; the NBHF echolocation is optimally suited to prey detection against cluttered coastal backgrounds, and the shallow-water habitat is where the narrow-beam high-frequency signal provides maximum advantage.
- **Name Etymology and the Habitat-Not-Behavior Convention.** The English name "Harbor Porpoise" reflects its frequent presence in harbors, bays, and sheltered coastal waters — the habitat-describing convention rather than a behavior-describing convention; the genus name *Phocoena* derives from the Greek *phokaina* (porpoise); the scientific binomial *Phocoena phocoena* is the type species of the genus and family, making the Harbor Porpoise the canonical Phocoenidae representative against which other species in the family are compared.
- **Conservation Status and Ongoing Recovery Narrative.** The Harbor Porpoise is not currently listed under the Endangered Species Act but the Pacific population is monitored by the **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW)** through cetacean surveys; the Salish Sea recolonization since the 2000s constitutes a conservation success story in the context of marine mammal declines elsewhere, and the engine's conservation vocabulary at degree 187 preserves this recovery narrative as first-class context distinct from the decline-and-recovery framework documented at prior pinniped entries (degree 180 Harbor Seal MMPA recovery, degree 184 Northern Fur Seal 1911 Convention recovery).
- **Foundational Scientific References and the NBHF Literature.** The Harbor Porpoise's NBHF echolocation is documented in a well-developed scientific literature: **Au et al. 1999** (*J. Acoust. Soc. Am.*) characterized the free-ranging clicks; **Villadsgaard et al. 2007** (*J. Exp. Biol.*) confirmed parameters in wild populations; **Morisaka & Connor 2007** (*J. Evol.
