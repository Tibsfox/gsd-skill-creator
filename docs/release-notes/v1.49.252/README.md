### 764-HERO -- ELECTRONIC PIONEERS (1993-2003)

764-HERO. Named after the phone number on a flyer. John Atkins (guitar, vocals, electronics) and Polly Johnson (drums). Capitol Hill. 1993-2003. Atkins incorporated drum machines, samplers, and electronic textures into indie rock starting in 1993. Six years before The Postal Service made electronic-indie fusion a mainstream concept (2003, *Give Up*). 764-HERO's approach was lo-fi, handmade, exploratory -- not polished electronic pop but rough-edged indie rock with electronic elements woven in. Albums: *Salt Sinks & Sugar Floats* (1996), *Get Here and Stay* (1997), *Weekends of Sound* (2001). Weekends of Sound in particular pushed the electronic integration further, layering loops and samples into sprawling, atmospheric compositions.

### THE FLYCATCHING WOODPECKER -- A WOODPECKER THAT DOESN'T DRILL

Lewis's Woodpecker (*Melanerpes lewis*). 26-28 cm body length. Wingspan 49-52 cm. Weight 88-138 g. Dark iridescent green-black upperparts with a pink belly, gray collar, and dark red face. One of the most strikingly colored woodpeckers in North America -- and one of the most behaviorally unusual. Lewis's Woodpecker **does not drill for food**. Instead, it perches on exposed snags and sallies out to catch insects on the wing -- flycatcher-style. Broad wings, slow wingbeats, buoyant flight. The aerial foraging strategy is unique among North American woodpeckers. In autumn and winter, it switches to acorns and other mast, storing them in crevices (like the Acorn Woodpecker at degree 107, but without the granary-tree specialization).

### THE SOUND: CHURRING CALL -- THE QUIET VOICE

Churring: a soft, rolling, low-pitched call. Chrrrr. Unobtrusive. Not the sharp peek of the Hairy (110) or the soft pik of the Downy (111) or the raucous waka-waka of the Acorn (107). The churring is barely a woodpecker sound at all -- more like a quiet thrush or a distant frog. E=4. The quietest woodpecker voice in the Picidae streak. ### CONSERVATION CONCERN -- 50-70% DECLINE

Lewis's Woodpecker populations have declined an estimated 50-70% since the mid-20th century. Primary causes: loss of open ponderosa pine habitat, fire suppression (Lewis's WP depends on burned snag habitat for nesting and perching), and competition for nest cavities from European Starlings. Listed as a species of conservation concern in multiple states and provinces. The flycatching woodpecker is losing its perches. The burned snags it depends on are being suppressed out of existence.
