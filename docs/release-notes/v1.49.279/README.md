**Part B: The Long-eared Owl and the Asio Genus Arrival.** The **Long-eared Owl** (*Asio otus*) is a medium-sized owl of the Strigidae family whose range spans most of temperate North America, Europe, and Asia. It is the most secretive owl in the Pacific Northwest — strictly nocturnal, roosting communally in dense conifer stands during daylight, hunting small mammals across open grasslands and field edges at night, and vocalizing primarily during breeding season with the deep rhythmic *whooo* pumping call that characterizes the species. The arrival of the Long-eared Owl at degree 139 introduces the **Asio** genus to the engine's species axis. Prior to this degree, Strigidae documentation had passed through *Bubo* (Great Horned Owl at degree 135 and pair duet at degree 136) and *Strix* (Barred Owl at degree 137 and caterwaul at degree 138). Degree 139 extends the genus catalog to three distinct Strigidae genera across five consecutive owl entries. The Asio genus is named for the prominent ear tufts that define the genus morphologically — tufts that are not actually ears (the real ears are asymmetric openings in the skull hidden by feathers) but feather displays that function in camouflage, species recognition, and emotional signaling. The Long-eared Owl's ear tufts are among the most prominent of any North American owl species, and the Short-eared Owl (*Asio flammeus*, the other Asio species that inhabits the Pacific Northwest) takes its common name from the comparatively shorter tufts. Both Asio species are present in the PNW — the Long-eared Owl in forest-interior and forest-edge habitats, the Short-eared Owl in open grasslands and marsh edges — and the engine's degree 139 entry documents the forest-interior species first.

**The Deep *Whooo* Pumping Call and Low-Frequency Carrying Quality.** The Long-eared Owl's vocalization is the **deep rhythmic *whooo*** — a low-frequency pumping call that males produce in series of 10-200+ individual hoots at roughly 2-3 second intervals. The call frequency concentrates in the 400-500 Hz range, placing it between the Great Horned Owl's five-note pattern (200-500 Hz with energy 250-400 Hz) and the Barred Owl's *who-cooks-for-you* (higher pitched, around 500-700 Hz). The Long-eared Owl's call is moderate in volume — nowhere near the ground-shaking authority of a Great Horned Owl hoot or the emphatic punctuation of a Barred Owl territorial call — but it carries remarkably well through forest interior because of the low-frequency acoustic diffraction around tree trunks and branches. The rhythmic spacing at 2-3 second intervals distinguishes the call from other owl vocalizations: where the Great Horned Owl's pattern is five discrete notes delivered in rapid sequence, and the Barred Owl's pattern is either a compact *who-cooks-for-you* or a chaotic caterwauling outburst, the Long-eared Owl's pattern is an extended metronomic series of spaced single hoots. Males can call for 20-40 minutes continuously during breeding season, producing 200-400 hoots in a single bout, and the cumulative effect across forest interior is an unmistakable but easy-to-miss rhythmic pulse that fills the acoustic space without dominating it. The E=4 energy rating captures this balance — moderate acoustic presence with long carrying quality, not overwhelming but unmistakably diagnostic when recognized.

**Communal Winter Roosts and Dense Conifer Daytime Behavior.** The Long-eared Owl's daytime behavior is **communal roosting in dense conifer stands**. Unlike the Great Horned Owl (solitary or paired) and the Barred Owl (paired, occasionally with offspring), the Long-eared Owl forms communal winter roosts that can hold from a handful to dozens of individuals in a single conifer stand. The communal roosts are one of the most characteristic behaviors of the species, and they explain why the owl is so rarely seen despite being moderately common across its range. A roost of thirty Long-eared Owls in a dense spruce or fir grove remains essentially invisible from below — the birds tuck tight against trunks with eyes closed, sway minimally with wind, and rely on cryptic plumage and stillness to avoid detection. Winter bird surveys occasionally discover such roosts and document twenty to forty birds in a single stand, but most PNW residents walk past Long-eared Owl roosts without ever noticing the birds above them. The communal roosting is structurally significant for engine documentation because it positions the species as the engine's first communal-winter-roost owl, a behavioral register distinct from the solitary territoriality of the Great Horned Owl (135) and the pair-bonded Barred Owl (137-138).

## Part B: Long-eared Owl — The Asio Genus Enters and the Most Secretive PNW Owl

The Asio genus includes both the Long-eared Owl (*Asio otus*, forest-interior habitat) and the Short-eared Owl (*Asio flammeus*, open-grassland habitat), both present in the Pacific Northwest but in different habitat contexts. The communal winter roosting is one of the most characteristic behaviors of the species — Winter Bird Counts occasionally discover roosts of 20-40 Long-eared Owls in single conifer groves — but most PNW residents walk past Long-eared Owl habitat without ever knowingly encountering one. Frequency concentration at 400-500 Hz, moderate volume, long carrying quality through forest interior via low-frequency diffraction around tree trunks and branches. The rhythmic spacing distinguishes the call from other owl vocalizations (Great Horned five-note pattern, Barred territorial *who-cooks-for-you*, Barred caterwaul cacophony), and the extended metronomic series fills forest acoustic space across breeding season without dominating it. The habitat requirement distinguishes the Long-eared Owl from the Great Horned Owl (which roosts in diverse forest types) and the Barred Owl (which prefers mature mixed forest with open understory for hunting), and it makes the Long-eared Owl a diagnostic species for dense-conifer interior habitat.
- **Open-country edges as nocturnal hunting habitat.** The **Long-eared Owl**'s nocturnal hunting occurs in **open-country edges** — grasslands, agricultural fields, marsh edges, and clear-cuts adjacent to forest where small-mammal prey is abundant. The habitat contrast between daytime roosting (dense conifer interior) and nocturnal hunting (open-country edges) is a defining feature of Long-eared Owl ecology, and it structures the species' movement across the landscape. The owl roosts deep in interior conifer during daylight, emerges at dusk, flies to open-country edges to hunt, returns to the roost before dawn. This bimodal habitat use is structurally distinct from the Great Horned Owl's more habitat-unified pattern (forest hunting throughout range) and the Barred Owl's mature-mixed-forest focus.

The real ears of the Long-eared Owl are asymmetric openings in the skull hidden beneath facial-disc feathers, with the left and right openings at different vertical heights to enable triangulation of prey sound. The prominent tufts at the top of the head function in cryptic camouflage (breaking up the silhouette against branches), species recognition (signaling Long-eared Owl identity to other individuals), and possibly emotional signaling (raised tufts during alarm, lowered tufts during relaxed roosting). The morphological feature that names the species is not what it appears to be — a behind-the-scenes anatomical misnomer that fits the species' broader behind-the-scenes ecology.
- **Small-mammal predation as nocturnal trophic role.** The **Long-eared Owl**'s diet consists primarily of **small mammals** — voles, mice, deer mice, shrews, and occasional small birds caught at roost. The species' trophic role is nocturnal predation on small-mammal populations in open-country edges adjacent to its daytime conifer roost. The dietary register is moderate — the Long-eared Owl is neither an apex predator at the Great Horned Owl's scale (300 psi talons, raptor-on-raptor predation) nor a highly specialized hunter at the Barred Owl's amphibian / fish / reptile breadth. The Long-eared Owl's small-mammal focus makes it a population regulator for vole and mouse numbers in its hunting range, and the species' moderate commonness across the Pacific Northwest reflects the moderate abundance of its preferred prey.
