## Mission Architecture: SDO

SDO is the NASA Living With a Star flagship heliophysics observatory that launched on 2010-02-11 at 15:23:00 UTC from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Space Launch Complex 41 (SLC-41) on an Atlas V 401 configuration (Atlas V launch vehicle, 4-meter fairing, zero solid-rocket strap-on boosters). The spacecraft prime was NASA Goddard Space Flight Center (GSFC), Greenbelt, Maryland — in-house spacecraft prime substrate-anchor. Mission management was provided by the NASA GSFC Living With a Star Program Office. Mission operations and science operations are integrated at NASA GSFC, Greenbelt, Maryland; three instrument science operations centers operate at the PI institutions (LMSAL + Stanford-Lockheed Institute Palo Alto, Stanford University, LASP Boulder).

Total spacecraft mass at launch was ~3,000 kg (~1,300 kg dry + ~1,400 kg hydrazine propellant for trans-orbit injection, apogee-raising burn sequence, and multi-decade station-keeping). Two deployable solar array wings provide ~1,500 W at 1 AU. Communications use Ka-band high-rate downlink (~150 Mbit/s) via continuous Ka-band downlink to the dedicated SDO Mission Operations Center ground station at White Sands Complex, New Mexico; S-band telemetry and telecommand via NASA GSFC ground stations. The spacecraft is three-axis-stabilized with a sun-pointed instrument deck carrying AIA, HMI, and EVE.

The Atlas V 401 launch vehicle performed a two-stage ascent. The Atlas V Common Core Booster, with its single RD-180 main engine (LOX/RP-1, ~3,830 kN sea-level thrust), provided first-stage thrust. The Centaur III upper stage, with a single RL10 engine (LH2/LOX, ~99 kN thrust, restart-capable), performed parking-orbit insertion followed by trans-orbit injection. The spacecraft hydrazine propulsion system then executed a multi-burn apogee-raising sequence over approximately 69 days. Inclined geosynchronous orbit insertion completed 2010-04-21 at approximately 102° W longitude (Earth-fixed sub-satellite reference) with 28.5° orbital inclination.

## 3-Instrument Science Suite

SDO carries three scientific instruments characterizing solar atmospheric dynamics and internal structure across multiple wavelengths and measurement domains. Three primary science domains: solar atmospheric dynamics via 10-channel EUV imaging (AIA); photospheric helioseismology + magnetogram via Michelson-tunable-filter interferometry (HMI); EUV spectral irradiance + variability via 1-105 nm spectroscopy (EVE).

| Instrument | Full name | PI institute | Science substrate |
|---|---|---|---|
| **AIA** | Atmospheric Imaging Assembly | LMSAL + Stanford-Lockheed Institute (USA) | 10-channel EUV imaging (94, 131, 171, 193, 211, 304, 335 Å + 1600, 1700, 4500 Å) at 4096 × 4096 / ~12-s cadence |
| **HMI** | Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager | Stanford University + Stanford-Lockheed Institute (USA) | Full-disk Doppler + LOS + vector magnetogram via Fe I 6173 Å; 45-s cadence; HMI-SOHO-MDI lineage |
| **EVE** | Extreme Ultraviolet Variability Experiment | LASP, University of Colorado Boulder (USA) | 1-105 nm spectral irradiance at 0.1 nm resolution; MEGS-A + MEGS-B + MEGS-P + ESP channels |

AIA's 10-channel EUV imaging at 4096 × 4096 / ~12-second cadence is the substrate-anchor for solar atmospheric dynamics characterization across the transition region, chromosphere, and corona (EUV-DYNAMICS-SUBSTRATE-ANCHOR obs#1 NEW LOCKED at v715). HMI is the direct architectural and scientific successor to SOHO MDI (Michelson Doppler Imager) — both instruments use Michelson-tunable-filter interferometers; both measure photospheric Doppler velocity and magnetogram via spectral-line scanning; both serve as the primary helioseismology and magnetogram substrate for their missions (HMI-SOHO-MDI-LINEAGE-CONTINUATION-SUBSTRATE-CUMULATIVE obs#1 NEW LOCKED at v715 — the first measurement-architecture-continuation thread within the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY axis substrate). EVE measures solar EUV spectral irradiance 1-105 nm at 0.1 nm spectral resolution via four instrument channels, supporting upper-atmosphere thermospheric modeling, ionospheric variability characterization, and satellite drag modeling for low-Earth orbit operations (EVE-SPECTRAL-IRRADIANCE-SUBSTRATE-ANCHOR obs#1 NEW LOCKED at v715).

## Mission Profile

SDO's mission profile unfolds across four canonical phases. The launch phase began 2010-02-11 15:23:00 UTC with Atlas V 401 ascent from CCAFS SLC-41. The Atlas V Common Core Booster with its RD-180 main engine provided first-stage thrust; the Centaur III upper stage performed parking-orbit insertion followed by trans-orbit injection. The trans-orbit cruise phase lasted approximately 69 days, with the spacecraft hydrazine propulsion system executing a multi-burn apogee-raising sequence. Inclined geosynchronous orbit insertion completed 2010-04-21, placing SDO in a circular geosynchronous orbit at approximately 102° W longitude (Earth-fixed sub-satellite reference) with 28.5° orbital inclination — producing a daily figure-8 ground track over the Western Hemisphere and continuous-view geometry over the dedicated White Sands Complex ground station throughout the 24-hour orbital period.

The nominal mission phase was designed for five years; the extended baseline mission added five additional years for a total designed lifetime of ten years. SDO completed nominal + extended baseline objectives during 2010-2020. Mission extensions have been approved continuously since, and operational lifetime has now reached approximately 16 years as of 2026-05-21. AIA and HMI produce ongoing science data across full original capability. EVE MEGS-A concluded operations 2014 per its designed CCD operational baseline completion (the MEGS-A substrate is retained in the mission archive, and the substantial 2010-2014 MEGS-A baseline supports the continuing MEGS-B + MEGS-P + ESP measurements with cross-calibration). Mission extensions are approved through 2030+ with hydrazine propellant reserves sufficient for continued station-keeping through approximately 2032+.

Across that operational lifetime, SDO has observed Solar Cycle 24 (~2008-2019) and Solar Cycle 25 (~2019-present, with the activity peak occurring in the 2025-2026 timeframe). The mission profile sustained across multiple solar cycles is the substrate-cumulative observation with v713 SOHO (~30-year) and v714 ACE (~28-year) for **LONG-DURATION-OPERATIONAL-SUBSTRATE-ANCHOR obs#3 cumulative at v715** (designed-shorter sustained-longer substrate pattern).

The defining operational substrate of SDO is the continuous high-bandwidth downlink. From inclined geosynchronous orbit with continuous-view geometry over the dedicated White Sands ground station, SDO sustains a ~150 Mbit/s Ka-band downlink throughout the 24-hour orbital period, delivering ~1.5 TB/day science data — the highest sustained science data rate of any NASA solar physics mission to date. This continuous-downlink architecture is the enabling substrate for AIA's full-cadence 12-second 10-channel imaging plus HMI's continuous magnetograms plus EVE's continuous spectral measurements simultaneously. This is the **CONTINUOUS-WHITE-SANDS-SCIENCE-DOWNLINK-SUBSTRATE-ANCHOR obs#1 NEW LOCKED at v715** — substrate-form-distinct operational paradigm absent from v712 + v713 + v714 baselines (all three use distributed Deep Space Network downlink).
