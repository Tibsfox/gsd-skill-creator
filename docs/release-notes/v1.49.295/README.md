## Taxonomic State

- **Family:** Phasianidae (3rd entry -- grouse trio continues)
- **Order:** Galliformes
- **Class:** Aves
- **Species note:** The Ruffed Grouse (*Bonasa umbellus*) is the most widely distributed grouse in North America, found across the boreal and mixed forests from Alaska to the Appalachians. In the Pacific Northwest, Ruffed Grouse inhabit deciduous and mixed forest edges, particularly where alder, birch, and aspen provide both food and cover. The **drumming display** is one of the most extraordinary sounds in North American ornithology. The male selects a fallen log -- the "drumming log" -- and stands atop it, then begins beating his wings rapidly against the air (not against his body or the log). Each wing stroke compresses a pocket of air, creating a miniature sonic boom. The sequence starts slowly -- a deep, resonant *thump... thump... thump* -- then accelerates smoothly into a rapid blur that sounds like a distant engine turning over: *thump-thump-thump-th-th-th-rrrrrrr*. The entire display lasts 8-10 seconds and carries several hundred yards through dense forest. The acceleration pattern is remarkably consistent -- a logarithmic curve from roughly 1 beat per second to 20 beats per second. This is a **purely mechanical sound** -- no vocalization is involved. The air compression between the wing and the body creates each percussive pulse. Males return to the same drumming log for years, and choice logs are contested territory. The sound is often mistaken for a distant chainsaw or motorcycle engine starting, which speaks to both its mechanical quality and its carrying power. As the engine's **4th mechanical sound**, the Ruffed Grouse drum represents the most complex mechanical display yet -- not a single strike (Rock Pigeon wing clap) or a steady tone (Mourning Dove wing whistle) but a structured, accelerating rhythmic pattern.
- **The Physics arc:** The Physics are a Seattle hip-hop group known for their energetic live performances and full-band instrumentation, bringing a dynamic, high-energy approach that distinguishes them from the solo-MC format that has dominated recent degrees. - **Mechanical sound catalog:** 1. Common Nighthawk wing boom (146, E=5) -- air through primaries in power dive. 2. Mourning Dove wing whistle (150, E=3) -- air through modified feathers on takeoff. 3. Rock Pigeon wing clap (152, E=4) -- feathers striking together above body. 4. Ruffed Grouse wing-drum (155, E=5) -- air compression between wing and body, accelerating pattern. Four distinct mechanisms, four distinct acoustic signatures.
