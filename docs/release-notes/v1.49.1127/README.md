## Mission Overview

TOPEX/Poseidon is the obs#30 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — the joint NASA–CNES free-flying ocean-altimetry satellite (the Ocean Topography Experiment + Poseidon) that measured the height and shape of the global ocean surface to a few centimetres, from which oceanographers read the ocean's circulation, its currents and eddies, El Niño and La Niña, the upper-ocean heat, the tides, and the slow rise of the global sea. ★ TOPEX/Poseidon was a partnership of NASA (which provided the spacecraft bus, the TOPEX dual-frequency radar altimeter, the TOPEX Microwave Radiometer, and the GPS demonstration receiver) and CNES (which provided the Poseidon-1 single-frequency solid-state altimeter, the DORIS precision-orbit tracking system, and the Ariane launch). ★★ Its instruments were the TOPEX Radar Altimeter (TOPEX / ALT) — a dual-frequency Ku-band (13.6 GHz) + C-band (5.3 GHz) nadir radar altimeter, the two frequencies correcting the ionospheric path delay for centimetre range accuracy, built by NASA; Poseidon-1 (CNES) — an experimental single-frequency Ku-band (13.65 GHz) solid-state altimeter sharing the same antenna (a lighter, lower-power proof-of-concept flown part of the time); TMR (the TOPEX Microwave Radiometer) — a three-frequency (18 / 21 / 37 GHz) radiometer measuring water vapour for the wet-troposphere path delay; DORIS (CNES) — precise orbit determination from a global network of ground beacons; GPSDR — an experimental GPS receiver; and LRA — a laser retroreflector array for laser-ranging orbit calibration. ★ The TOPEX altimeter was the first spaceborne dual-frequency altimeter, and the Poseidon-1 solid-state altimeter proved a lighter, lower-power design later carried into the Jason series — TOPEX/Poseidon carrying the founding altimeter payload of the modern reference record. ★ It measured global ocean-surface topography (sea-surface height) to a few centimetres, from which oceanographers derived the ocean's circulation and geostrophic currents, the mesoscale eddies, El Niño / La Niña and the heat stored in the upper ocean, the ocean tides, and the beginning of the continuous climate-quality global mean-sea-level record; the data are open. TOPEX/Poseidon read the height and the shape of the world's ocean — the currents and the eddies, the warm-water signal of El Niño, the reach of the tides, and the slow rise of the sea — the reading of the height and reach of the world's ocean. The mission entered the story on 10 August 1992 on an Ariane 42P from the Guiana Space Centre, Kourou, French Guiana, and rose into a free-flying non-sun-synchronous ~1336 km orbit. The story is the Ariane launch from Kourou, the free-flying ~1336 km reference orbit chosen high to reduce drag and gravity-field error, the dual-frequency Ku/C-band radar pulse ranging down to the sea surface and back to a few centimetres, the TMR reading the water vapour along the path and DORIS fixing the orbit from the ground beacons, and the ~10-day repeat ground track building the global map of sea-surface height cycle after cycle — the height of the sea read to a few centimetres, the ocean's circulation drawn from space. The science is central: from the reference orbit TOPEX/Poseidon measured the range to the sea surface and, with the orbit fixed to a few centimetres and the atmospheric corrections applied, turned it into the ocean-surface topography from which the circulation, the eddies, the tides, and the rise of the sea are read. ★★★ TOPEX/Poseidon is RETIRED, so the catalog frames the mission's OPERATIONS in the PAST (TOPEX/Poseidon measured the height of the sea, mapped the ocean's circulation, read the tides, and carried the dual-frequency altimeter); it launched 10 August 1992 and operated until January 2006 — the launch and the retirement the past sub-events. ★ EXCEPTION: the continuous sea-surface-height / sea-level reference record TOPEX/Poseidon began is still extended today by Jason-1/2/3 and Sentinel-6 — that record and its reference series are written in the PRESENT. TOPEX/Poseidon's firsts are scoped carefully: it was the first satellite mission dedicated to centimetre-class ocean-surface-topography altimetry, it founded the reference orbit and continuous record the Jason series and Sentinel-6 carry forward, and the dual-frequency Ku/C-band ionosphere correction gave the centimetre range accuracy. ★ The scope is load-bearing: earlier, coarser radar altimeters preceded it (Skylab's S-193, GEOS-3, Seasat in 1978, Geosat in 1985), so TOPEX/Poseidon is NOT "the first satellite altimeter"; its distinction is the first *dedicated, centimetre-precision* ocean-topography mission and the reference standard the Jason series and Sentinel-6 continue. ## Surprises

- **A satellite that read the height of the sea to a few centimetres.** Where the ocean surface rises and falls by metres with the tides and the currents, the TOPEX dual-frequency radar altimeter ranged from ~1336 km down to the sea and back and, with the ionospheric and wet-troposphere corrections and the DORIS orbit, resolved the height of that surface to a few centimetres — precise enough to draw the ocean's circulation, its eddies, and the slow rise of the global sea from space.
- **Two frequencies that cancel the ionosphere.** The TOPEX altimeter transmitted at both Ku-band (13.6 GHz) and C-band (5.3 GHz); because the ionosphere delays the two frequencies by different amounts, comparing the two returns measures and removes the ionospheric path delay — the first spaceborne dual-frequency altimeter, sharpening the range to the centimetre.
- **A high orbit chosen to make the measurement cleaner.** TOPEX/Poseidon flew at ~1336 km — much higher than most Earth observers — precisely because the thin air and the smoother gravity field at that altitude reduce the atmospheric drag and the orbit-determination error, so that the sea-surface height could be recovered to a few centimetres over the ~10-day exact-repeat ground track.
- **A mission that founded a record still growing today.** TOPEX/Poseidon operated from 1992 to 2006, but the reference orbit and the continuous sea-surface-height record it began did not end with it: Jason-1 (2001), Jason-2/OSTM (2008), Jason-3 (2016), and Sentinel-6 (2020) fly the same reference ground track and extend the same climate-quality global-mean-sea-level record — one founding mission joined to a chain of successors.
- **A diving sea duck of the Pacific Northwest nearshore.** The Surf Scoter (*Melanitta perspicillata*, family Anatidae), a stocky black sea duck that winters along the marine nearshore of the Pacific Northwest, rides the swells of the open water and dives through the surface to feed on mussels and other shellfish over the nearshore beds (behavioral / ecological description only) — riding the rise and fall of the same sea surface whose height TOPEX/Poseidon measured from above.
- **A tide-line legume of the ocean beach.** The Beach Pea (*Lathyrus japonicus*, family Fabaceae), a sprawling perennial legume of the sand and gravel of Pacific Northwest ocean beaches, roots along the upper strand at the line the highest tides reach, its buoyant salt-tolerant seeds carried and sorted by the sea — a plant of the exact line the sea reaches on the shore, bound with the throughline of the rise and reach of the sea.

## Dedication

v1.310 TOPEX/Poseidon is dedicated to the people at NASA and CNES, and across the mission's instrument teams, who built and flew the satellite that read the height and shape of the world's ocean to a few centimetres and founded the precision sea-surface-height record. To the engineers who shaped the TOPEX dual-frequency radar altimeter and the TOPEX Microwave Radiometer, and to the teams at CNES who built the Poseidon-1 solid-state altimeter and the DORIS orbit-tracking beacons and who carried the satellite aloft on an Ariane from Kourou in August 1992. To the crews who kept the satellite pointed and the orbit fixed across thirteen years of measurement, and to the teams who joined its record to Jason-1, Jason-2, Jason-3, and Sentinel-6 so the reading of the sea would continue unbroken. To all who keep the data open and free. The height and reach of the world's ocean, read from a free-flying orbit and handed forward to the missions that still measure it, is theirs to keep.
