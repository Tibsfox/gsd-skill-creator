On the soundscape side, **Thunderstorm Cascades** arrives at E=8 -- the fourth consecutive maximum SPS entry, the fourth degree of the storm arc, and the acoustic event that brings the storm out of the Olympic Mountains and into the Cascade Range. A Cascade thunderstorm is a different acoustic event than an Olympic wind storm: where the wind storm is sustained, continuous, and broadband, the thunderstorm is episodic, punctuated, and dramatic. The thunder crack of a close lightning strike -- within a half-mile -- produces a sound level of approximately 120 decibels at the point of origin, decaying rapidly with distance. The rumble that follows is the acoustic signature of the lightning channel reverberated through the Cascade valleys. The rain associated with a Cascade thunderstorm, at high intensity, adds a broadband noise floor beneath the thunder punctuation. Together, the thunder crack and the rain roar constitute an acoustic environment that the engine rates at E=8: maximum sustained energy at the highest episodic peak.

Four consecutive E=8 SPS entries -- degrees 256 through 259 -- document a storm system that has moved from the Pacific coast to the Cascade Range in four degrees. The storm arc closes at degree 260. One degree remains, and the arc's maximum has not yet been reached.
