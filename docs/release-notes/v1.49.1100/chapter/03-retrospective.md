## Surprises

- **The radiometer reads the moisture of the soil from the natural microwave emission of the surface.** SMAP's L-band radiometer reads the natural ~1.41 GHz microwave emission of the land surface; because the emission depends on the strong dielectric contrast between dry soil and water, the radiometer recovers the water content of the top ~5 cm of soil, gridded at ~36 km — NASA's first mission dedicated to mapping global soil moisture, where the moisture of the soil is read from the soil's own faint glow at radio wavelengths.
- **A great mesh antenna spins at ~14.6 rpm to sweep a wide swath of the land.** The ~6 m deployable mesh reflector — the "spinning lasso" — rotates at ~14.6 rpm so that the radiometer (and, in its first months, the radar) sweeps a wide conical swath across the surface, building a 2–3 day global revisit from an ~8-day exact repeat in the dawn-dusk sun-synchronous orbit, where a fixed staring instrument would see only a narrow strip.
- **The passive radiometer carried the mission after the radar's first months.** The L-band radar (active, ~1.26 GHz) was designed to merge with the radiometer into a ~9 km soil-moisture product, and it operated for its first months (~April–July 2015); when the radar halted transmissions on 7 July 2015 (a low-voltage power supply for the high-power amplifier), the L-band radiometer (passive, ~1.41 GHz, ~36 km) carried the mission forward — and it has returned the global soil-moisture and freeze/thaw record continuously ever since, more than ten years deep.
- **A small endemic mole that lives in the moist surface soil and litter.** The American Shrew-Mole (*Neurotrichus gibbsii*), the smallest North American mole, tunnels just beneath the surface through the damp leaf litter and the moist top layer of soil of moist Pacific Northwest forest floors, ravines, and streambanks, active day and night and intolerant of dry ground: a mirror of SMAP reading the moisture of the world's surface soil from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only; a small semi-fossorial mole, distinct from the larger Townsend's mole *Scapanus townsendii* and the coast mole *Scapanus orarius*).
- **A moist-forest forb that roots in consistently damp ground.** The Foamflower (*Tiarella trifoliata*), a forb of moist, shaded PNW forest floors, streambanks, and seeps, lifts airy sprays of tiny white flowers above heart-shaped, lobed basal leaves from a base rooted in consistently damp ground (ecological description only; a moist-forest forb, distinct from the fringecups *Tellima grandiflora* and the youth-on-age / piggyback plant *Tolmiea menziesii* it shares moist forests with) — bound with the throughline of the moisture of the living soil, the same damp forest-floor ground whose moisture SMAP measures from orbit.
