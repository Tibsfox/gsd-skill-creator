## Mission Overview

SMAP is the obs#3 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — the NASA Earth-observing mission that circles Earth in a dawn-dusk sun-synchronous orbit and reads the water held in the top layer of the world's soil, measuring the soil moisture of the continents and the seasonal freeze and thaw of the boreal land, behind a great deployable mesh antenna that spins as it sweeps a wide swath. ★ It was built by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, with the L-band radiometer provided by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center. The observer launched ~31 January 2015 on a Delta II from Vandenberg (SLC-2W) and settled into a sun-synchronous, near-polar orbit at roughly 685 km: an advanced soil-moisture Earth observer. The story is the launch from Vandenberg, the climb into the dawn-dusk ~685 km orbit, the deployment and spin-up of the ~6 m mesh reflector, the radiometer reading the water in the soil across a wide swath, the soil moisture of the continents and the freeze/thaw front of the boreal land resolved, and the long ongoing watch that continues today. The science is central: the L-band radiometer provides NASA's first mission dedicated to mapping global soil moisture — the water content of the top ~5 cm of soil, gridded at ~36 km (native footprint ~40 km) — and the global mapping of the landscape freeze/thaw state across the boreal and seasonally-frozen regions; and the combined L-band radar (active) + radiometer (passive) design was built to merge the radar's resolution with the radiometer's accuracy into a ~9 km soil-moisture product (also resolved to ~3 km), and a ~3 km freeze/thaw product. ★ The soil-moisture result is load-bearing: it is NASA's first mission DEDICATED to mapping global soil moisture (the water content of the top layer of the world's soil) — also defensible as NASA's first Earth Systematic Mission from the Earth Science Decadal Survey — NOT "first to measure soil moisture from space," since ESA's SMOS (2009) is a prior L-band soil-moisture radiometer. ★ The freeze/thaw result is equally load-bearing: it is the global mapping of the landscape freeze/thaw state (the boreal / seasonally-frozen regions), a second core product of the radiometer. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: NASA's first mission dedicated to mapping global soil moisture (the radiometer, the water in the top ~5 cm of soil) — not "first to measure soil moisture"; the global mapping of the landscape freeze/thaw state (the radiometer, the boreal land) — a second core product; and the first combined L-band active-and-passive soil-moisture measurement (the radar + radiometer design for the ~9 km product) — credited as a design first WITHOUT implying it is ongoing, since the radar operated only ~April–July 2015 and the L2_SM_AP combined product is not ongoing, while the radiometer-only ~36 km product continues. ★★ The L-band radiometer is STILL OPERATING (since 2015), so the catalog frames its launch and completed milestones in the PAST (SMAP launched ~31 January 2015, reached its orbit, deployed and spun up its mesh antenna, completed its commissioning phase, and began its global soil-moisture and freeze/thaw record) while its ongoing role is framed in the PRESENT (SMAP orbits Earth, continues to map the moisture of the world's soil, has returned a long record more than ten years deep, and remains operational). The L-band radar halted transmissions on 7 July 2015 (~2.5 months of combined operation) and was formally declared lost on 2 September 2015 (a low-voltage power supply for the high-power amplifier) — stated factually and positively: the passive radiometer carries the mission, and the soil-moisture and freeze/thaw record continues unbroken. ## Surprises

- **The radiometer reads the moisture of the soil from the natural microwave emission of the surface.** SMAP's L-band radiometer reads the natural ~1.41 GHz microwave emission of the land surface; because the emission depends on the strong dielectric contrast between dry soil and water, the radiometer recovers the water content of the top ~5 cm of soil, gridded at ~36 km — NASA's first mission dedicated to mapping global soil moisture, where the moisture of the soil is read from the soil's own faint glow at radio wavelengths.
- **A great mesh antenna spins at ~14.6 rpm to sweep a wide swath of the land.** The ~6 m deployable mesh reflector — the "spinning lasso" — rotates at ~14.6 rpm so that the radiometer (and, in its first months, the radar) sweeps a wide conical swath across the surface, building a 2–3 day global revisit from an ~8-day exact repeat in the dawn-dusk sun-synchronous orbit, where a fixed staring instrument would see only a narrow strip.
- **The passive radiometer carried the mission after the radar's first months.** The L-band radar (active, ~1.26 GHz) was designed to merge with the radiometer into a ~9 km soil-moisture product, and it operated for its first months (~April–July 2015); when the radar halted transmissions on 7 July 2015 (a low-voltage power supply for the high-power amplifier), the L-band radiometer (passive, ~1.41 GHz, ~36 km) carried the mission forward — and it has returned the global soil-moisture and freeze/thaw record continuously ever since, more than ten years deep.
- **A small endemic mole that lives in the moist surface soil and litter.** The American Shrew-Mole (*Neurotrichus gibbsii*), the smallest North American mole, tunnels just beneath the surface through the damp leaf litter and the moist top layer of soil of moist Pacific Northwest forest floors, ravines, and streambanks, active day and night and intolerant of dry ground: a mirror of SMAP reading the moisture of the world's surface soil from orbit (behavioral / ecological description only; a small semi-fossorial mole, distinct from the larger Townsend's mole *Scapanus townsendii* and the coast mole *Scapanus orarius*).
- **A moist-forest forb that roots in consistently damp ground.** The Foamflower (*Tiarella trifoliata*), a forb of moist, shaded PNW forest floors, streambanks, and seeps, lifts airy sprays of tiny white flowers above heart-shaped, lobed basal leaves from a base rooted in consistently damp ground (ecological description only; a moist-forest forb, distinct from the fringecups *Tellima grandiflora* and the youth-on-age / piggyback plant *Tolmiea menziesii* it shares moist forests with) — bound with the throughline of the moisture of the living soil, the same damp forest-floor ground whose moisture SMAP measures from orbit.

A future revision could render real SMAP data — the measured launch trajectory, the dawn-dusk orbit tracks, the radiometer soil-moisture fields, the freeze/thaw maps, or the spinning-antenna swath geometry — for a higher-fidelity view.
- **The SMAP diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the SMAP spacecraft (with the deployed ~6 m mesh reflector) and the Delta II, and a sectioned view of Earth with the dawn-dusk ~685 km orbit and the spinning antenna sweeping a wide swath of the land.
- **The launch, spinning-antenna, soil-moisture, and freeze/thaw modes are depicted rather than driven by published data.** The four shader modes are depicted but not rendered from the published SMAP data (the launch ephemeris, the orbit tracks, the radiometer soil-moisture products, the freeze/thaw fields, or the antenna swath geometry); a future ship could drive the modes from those data sets for a quantitatively faithful reading.
- **The moisture-of-the-soil mode is depicted rather than tied to the actual soil-moisture and freeze/thaw measurements.** The soil-moisture mode depicts the radiometer reading the water in the top layer of the soil at a representative level rather than tying it to the published measurements (the SMAP L2/L3 soil-moisture products, the freeze/thaw maps, the long radiometer record); a future ship could update the mode from the archived data for a faithful rendering of the moisture of the world's soil.

## Dedication

v1.283 SMAP is dedicated to the people across NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory and Goddard Space Flight Center who built and still fly the observer that reads the moisture of the world's soil. The teams who lifted it from Vandenberg on a Delta II and turned it into a dawn-dusk orbit, and the engineers behind the great mesh reflector — six metres of woven wire that unfolds and spins like a lasso — so the radiometer can sweep a wide swath and read the faint glow of water held in the top layer of soil across whole continents, and watch the boreal land freeze and thaw with the seasons. The teams behind the radar, who in its first months merged an active and a passive reading into a finer grain, and who carried the science forward on the radiometer alone when the radar fell quiet, so the long record never broke. Together they read a hidden thing — the water in the ground beneath our feet — from far above, returning a record that grows by the day. They measure a world by the moisture of its living soil, and the watch they keep goes on.
