## Summary

v1.49.1046 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.228 to 1.229, adding XRISM (the X-Ray Imaging and Spectroscopy Mission) and SUSTAINING the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY axis at obs#7 — a SUBSTRATE-FORM-DISTINCT sustain by detector class rather than a new rotation or a new pointing strategy. The axis opened at v1040 with the Chandra X-ray Observatory (substrate-axis rotation #35), the grazing-incidence telescope that brought soft and medium-band cosmic X-rays to a half-arcsecond focus, continued at v1041 with NuSTAR, the first telescope to *focus* the hard X-ray band on a deployable mast, continued at v1042 with RXTE, the first satellite dedicated to high-time-resolution X-ray *timing*, continued at v1043 with IXPE, the first *imaging* X-ray *polarimeter*, continued at v1044 with NICER, the soft-band timing instrument that turned precision pulse timing into a neutron-star ruler and a navigation clock, and continued at v1045 with the Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory, the rapid-response, autonomous, multi-wavelength observatory that catches a fleeting transient as it happens. Chandra and NuSTAR read the focused image of the high-energy sky; RXTE and NICER read its variability in time; IXPE read the polarization of its light; Swift caught the fleeting transient. XRISM reaches a seventh observation that is distinct in *kind*: not a new pointing strategy but a new detector class — it is the first observatory to make a high-resolution X-ray spectrum by *weighing the energy of each photon as heat*. Its Resolve instrument is an X-ray microcalorimeter — an array of tiny detectors held at fifty thousandths of a degree above absolute zero, each of which absorbs a single X-ray photon and reports that photon's energy from the minute temperature pulse the photon deposits. Because the measurement does not disperse the light through a grating, it delivers about five electron-volts of spectral resolution *on extended, diffuse sources* — the hot gas of a galaxy cluster, the expanding shell of a supernova remnant — that a grating spectrometer cannot cleanly resolve. The way of seeing extends from the sharp soft-band image (Chandra), to the focused hard-band image (NuSTAR), to the rapid light curve (RXTE), to the polarization vector field (IXPE), to the precision soft-band pulse that becomes a ruler and a clock (NICER), to the autonomous multi-wavelength capture of a transient (Swift), to the *high-resolution, non-dispersive spectrum that resolves the motion of cosmic plasma* (XRISM). The distinction across this seventh observation is the energy-resolving detector class — not a new pointing strategy: XRISM completes the X-ray observable set (image, hard band, timing, polarimetry, transient, spectroscopy) on the axis. The axis sustains at obs#7 (axis-rotation #35 sustains at obs#7) rather than capping, and the forward cadence can continue to obs#8 — a next-generation large-area or wide-field microcalorimeter or grating spectrometer — or rotate onward per the substrate-axis-rotation discipline.

Its near-circular low-Earth orbit sits at about 550 kilometers altitude at a 31-degree inclination, with a period near 96 minutes — a low orbit, not a high or Sun-synchronous one. The signature of this degree is the high-resolution non-dispersive spectroscopy form. The six X-ray observatories before it each read a different facet of the high-energy sky, but none could make a high-resolution spectrum of an extended source. High-resolution X-ray spectroscopy already existed — Chandra's and XMM-Newton's transmission and reflection gratings resolve fine spectral detail — but *dispersively*: a grating spreads the light by wavelength, which works only for a point source and smears when the source is extended. XRISM's Resolve instrument resolves the spectrum *non-dispersively*, by measuring each photon's energy directly, so the resolution is independent of how large the source is on the sky. That is the defining first: not high-resolution spectroscopy itself (the gratings did that), nor the microcalorimeter as a device (Suzaku's XRS reached orbit in 2005; Hitomi's SXS returned the landmark Perseus measurement in 2016) — it is the operational, sustained, non-dispersive microcalorimeter observatory, the first time the microcalorimeter became a working observatory open to the community, returning resolved spectra of clusters, remnants, and active nuclei across a multi-year program. That capability opened the resolved-velocity era of X-ray astronomy: for the first time the bulk motions and turbulence of hot plasma could be read directly from the shapes and shifts of its emission lines. ## Mission Overview

XRISM is the obs#7 continuation of the X-ray-astronomy axis and the substrate-form-distinct sustain by detector class — a high-resolution non-dispersive imaging-spectroscopy observatory that reads the energy of each X-ray photon as the tiny heat it deposits in a cryogenic detector, resolving the emission lines and Doppler velocities of hot cosmic plasma even on extended sources. A JAXA (ISAS)-led mission built with NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center and ESA, the explicit recovery of ASTRO-H / Hitomi developed under the working name XARM, it launched 6 September 2023 (23:42:11 UTC) aboard an H-IIA (flight No. 47, F47) from the Yoshinobu Launch Complex at the Tanegashima Space Center, co-manifested with the SLIM lunar lander, into a near-circular ~550-kilometer, 31-degree low-Earth orbit (international designator 2023-137A; launch mass ~2,300 kg). It carries two co-aligned instruments, each fed by one of two identical NASA-Goddard X-ray Mirror Assemblies: Resolve, the soft X-ray microcalorimeter spectrometer — a 6 × 6 = 36-pixel array (35 sky-active plus one corner calibration pixel, about three arcminutes square on the sky), each pixel a mercury-telluride absorber on an ion-implanted silicon thermistor whose heat pulse from an absorbed photon is proportional to that photon's energy, delivering about 5 eV resolution near 6 keV (requirement 7 eV) over a design band of 0.3–12 keV but an as-flown operational band of 1.7–12 keV, held at 50 millikelvin by a multi-stage cooling chain (mechanical Stirling coolers, a Joule-Thomson stage, a ~30-litre superfluid-helium dewar, and a three-stage adiabatic demagnetization refrigerator); and Xtend, the Soft X-ray Imager — a back-illuminated CCD camera (four CCDs in a 2 × 2 array) over 0.4–13 keV with a ~38 × 38-arcminute field of view, one of the largest of any flown X-ray imaging satellite, that gives the wide-field context around Resolve's narrow spectroscopic field. The two X-ray Mirror Assemblies are identical grazing-incidence Wolter Type-I optics (about 203 nested gold-coated foil shells each, 5.6-metre focal length), with arcminute-class angular resolution (~1.3′ for the Resolve mirror, ~1.5′ for the Xtend mirror) — modest by design, because XRISM's strength is spectral, not spatial. The mission Principal Investigator is Makoto S. Tashiro (ISAS/JAXA); the U.S. PI is Richard L. Kelley (NASA Goddard); the project scientist is Brian J. Williams (NASA Goddard); Resolve's co-PIs are Yoshitaka Ishisaki (Tokyo Metropolitan University) and Kelley, with Caroline Kilbourne (Goddard) a key detector scientist; Xtend's PI is Koji Mori (University of Miyazaki). Resolve is a near-identical reflight of the Hitomi SXS. As of 2025–2026 XRISM is operating and conducting its science program — first light came in early 2024 (N132D), Guest Observer Cycle 2 began about 1 November 2025, and Cycle 3 about mid-2026 — and it SUSTAINS the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#7 (axis-rotation #35 sustains). XRISM operates as the recovery that finally made the microcalorimeter an operational, sustained observatory open to the community.

## Structural firsts

**XRISM-FIRST-INSTANCE:** XRISM enters the catalog as the high-resolution non-dispersive imaging-spectroscopy observatory — the first instance, the obs#1 first — sustaining the X-ray-astronomy axis at obs#7. The load-bearing qualifier is the conjunction *operational + sustained + non-dispersive microcalorimeter*: not "first X-ray microcalorimeter flown" (Suzaku's XRS reached orbit in 2005 but lost its helium before any celestial science; XQC sounding rockets flew suborbitally in the 1990s), not "first microcalorimeter science result" (Hitomi's SXS returned the landmark Perseus turbulence measurement in its ~37-day life in 2016), and not "first high-resolution X-ray spectroscopy" (Chandra HETG/LETG and XMM-Newton RGS did that years earlier, dispersively, on point sources).

**GUEST-OBSERVER-MICROCALORIMETER-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The first X-ray microcalorimeter detector made available to guest observers through a competed, open observing program — Guest Observer Cycle 1 opened in 2024. The qualifier matters: neither Suzaku's XRS nor Hitomi's SXS survived to run a guest-observer program, so this first is intrinsic to XRISM and uncontested. It is NOT weakened to "first microcalorimeter" unqualified (that would leak to Suzaku's XRS).

**SMBH-CLUMPY-ULTRAFAST-WIND-STRUCTURE-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The first to resolve the complex, clumpy (bullet-like) velocity structure of an ultra-fast wind from a supermassive black hole — PDS 456, where Resolve separated five or more distinct gas components moving at 20–30% of the speed of light, with a mass outflow of order tens to hundreds of solar masses per year. The qualifier matters: ultra-fast outflows themselves were detected before XRISM (by Chandra, XMM-Newton, and Suzaku) — XRISM resolved their internal velocity structure, enabled by the ~5 eV non-dispersive resolution, not their existence.

**EXTENDED-SOURCE-DOPPLER-VELOCITY-FIELD-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The first non-dispersive, Doppler-resolved plasma velocity field of an extended, diffuse X-ray source — the supernova remnant N132D in the Large Magellanic Cloud at first light, where Resolve resolved iron-line broadening and bulk motion that revised the remnant's geometry, later extended to the remnant W49B. The qualifier matters: dispersive gratings (Chandra HETG, XMM-Newton RGS) measure line velocities only for point-like sources and smear on extended ones — this is NOT credited as "first supernova-remnant velocity measurement" unqualified.

**X-RAY-ASTRONOMY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION:** The X-ray-astronomy axis Chandra opened, NuSTAR extended, RXTE continued, IXPE continued, NICER continued, and Swift continued sustains as a substrate-form-distinct observation at obs#7 (axis-rotation #35 sustains) — XRISM is the high-resolution non-dispersive spectroscopy form, distinguished by an energy-resolving detector class (the cryogenic microcalorimeter) rather than a new pointing strategy, completing the X-ray observable set (image, hard band, timing, polarimetry, transient, spectroscopy) on the axis.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis can sustain on a new detector class, not just a new pointing strategy.** The X-ray-astronomy axis sustains at obs#7 because XRISM reads the high-energy sky in a way the six focusing, timing, polarimetry, precision-timing, and rapid-response observatories could not — by weighing each photon's energy as the tiny heat it deposits in a detector held at 50 mK, resolving emission-line shapes and Doppler velocities even on extended sources. The distinction is the energy-resolving detector class, not a new way of pointing, so the cadence continues the axis as a substrate-form-distinct sustain rather than rotating away.
2. **Non-dispersive spectroscopy is distinct from dispersive spectroscopy on an axis that already has both.** Chandra's HETG/LETG and XMM-Newton's RGS did high-resolution X-ray spectroscopy first, but dispersively, on point sources — a grating smears on an extended source. XRISM's first is the operational, sustained, non-dispersive microcalorimeter form, whose ~5 eV resolution is independent of source size, so it works on the diffuse hot gas of clusters and remnants. The qualifier keeps XRISM's spectroscopy first distinct from Chandra's grating-spectroscopy first.
3. **A first can hinge on the conjunction of its qualifiers.** XRISM's defining first is not the microcalorimeter device (Suzaku flew it first) nor the first microcalorimeter result (Hitomi returned the Perseus measurement) — it is the operational + sustained + non-dispersive microcalorimeter observatory, the conjunction of all three properties. The guest-observer, ultra-fast-wind-structure, and extended-source-velocity-field firsts each carry their own qualifiers (open competed program; resolve the clumpy structure, not detect the wind; extended / diffuse source, not point source).
4. **A focusing telescope on an axis of focusing telescopes still needs the resolution stated.** The X-ray Mirror Assemblies genuinely focus X-rays, but at arcminute-class half-power diameter (~1.3′ for the Resolve mirror, ~1.5′ for the Xtend mirror) — roughly 150–180× coarser than Chandra's ~0.5″. Stating the modest resolution keeps "focusing," "Wolter-I," "mirror," and "imaging" correct XRISM content while never claiming Chandra's first-focusing or sub-arcsecond imaging.
5. **The microcalorimeter capability opened a resolved-velocity era of science.** Because Resolve measures each photon's energy directly at ~5 eV, the bulk motions and turbulence of hot plasma can be read straight from emission-line shapes and shifts — the first non-dispersive velocity field of an extended source (N132D), the resolved Fe-Kα structure of an active nucleus (NGC 4151), the quiescent core of a galaxy cluster (Centaurus), and the resolved clumpy ultra-fast wind of a quasar (PDS 456), each a consequence of energy-resolving spectroscopy on extended and point sources alike.
6. **A continuation keeps the predecessors' vocabulary and references them by name.** Because XRISM sustains the X-ray-astronomy axis, the shared X-ray vocabulary is correct and kept, and Chandra (obs#1, soft/medium focusing), NuSTAR (obs#2, hard-band focusing), RXTE (obs#3, timing), IXPE (obs#4, polarimetry), NICER (obs#5, soft-band timing), and Swift (obs#6, rapid-response transient) are referenced as the form complements — but XRISM's own facts (the two co-aligned instruments, the single-photon heat-pulse measurement, the cold chain, the as-flown 1.7–12 keV band, the resolved velocity fields) and only its own five anchors are stamped; the predecessors' anchors and predecessor-specific hardware (Swift's BAT / coded mask / XRT / UVOT / autonomous slew; NICER's X-ray Timing Instrument / concentrators / SEXTANT; IXPE's Gas Pixel Detectors; RXTE's PCA/HEXTE; NuSTAR's deployable mast; Chandra's sub-arcsecond optics) stay with their missions.
7. **Accuracy guardrails guard against the design-vs-operational and fabricated-citation classes of error.** Distinguishing the Resolve design band (0.3–12 keV) from the as-flown operational band (1.7–12 keV, the gate valve never opened — the single most error-prone XRISM fact); attributing the Perseus 164 km/s measurement to Hitomi (2016), not XRISM; keeping the Centaurus bulk-streaming (~130–310 km/s) and turbulent-dispersion (≤~120 km/s) figures distinct; stating XRISM resolved the PDS 456 wind's structure rather than discovering ultra-fast outflows; framing it as a JAXA-led mission with NASA and ESA (the recovery of Hitomi, formerly XARM); using the 6 September 2023 H-IIA launch from Tanegashima alongside SLIM and the ~550 km / 31° orbit; and using only the verified references — keeps the facts load-bearing and correct.
8. **The status is a body of work, not a single live state.** XRISM is operating and conducting its science program (first light early 2024, Guest Observer Cycle 2 from ~1 November 2025, Cycle 3 ~mid-2026), with the Resolve gate valve closed (September 2025 reopening attempts unsuccessful) — described as the body of work and the honest degradation rather than asserted as a single present-tense status.

## Surprises

- **A new detector class, not a new way of pointing, opens a whole era of science.** XRISM adds no sharper image and no new pointing strategy; by weighing each photon's energy as the tiny heat it deposits in a detector held at 50 mK, it resolves the emission lines of hot plasma at ~5 eV — independent of how large the source is — and that single capability read the bulk motion of a supernova remnant, the structure of an active nucleus, the quiescent core of a galaxy cluster, and the clumpy bullet-like wind of a quasar.
- **A galaxy cluster's core gas was remarkably quiescent.** In the Centaurus cluster, Resolve measured a core turbulent velocity dispersion of order ≤~120 km/s — small — with a larger ~130–310 km/s bulk line-of-sight streaming, showing that the energy released by the central black hole is coupled gently into the surrounding intracluster gas rather than stirring it violently.
- **A quasar's ultra-fast wind is made of distinct bullets.** Toward PDS 456, Resolve separated five or more distinct gas components of an ultra-fast wind from a supermassive black hole moving at 20–30% of the speed of light — the first time the internal velocity structure of such a wind was resolved, with a mass outflow of order tens to hundreds of solar masses per year, a structured rather than smooth outflow.
- **A fire-chaser beetle already senses heat the way Resolve does.** *Melanophila acuminata* finds fire by its heat: its paired mesothoracic infrared pit organs each carry fifty to a hundred dome-shaped sensilla, microscopic fluid-filled spheres that absorb mid-infrared radiation and expand by about a hundred nanometres, firing a mechanoreceptor — a genuine photomechanic thermal detector — so the beetle can sense a distant forest fire and fly to the freshly burned wood; a living microcalorimeter, exactly Resolve sensing a faint distant source by the tiny heat a single photon deposits on a cold, quiet absorber.
- **A morel rises only where the fire's heat has already passed.** The Burn morel (*Morchella tomentosa*), a fire-obligate fungus, fruits almost exclusively in high-elevation conifer forest the season after a fire has passed through, reading the thermal aftermath of the burn from the soil — mirror to the heat-aftermath side of the pairing, as XRISM reads the energy a photon has already deposited: the beetle senses the fire's heat, the morel rises where that heat has passed, and Resolve reads energy as heat.

## Dedication

v1.229 XRISM is dedicated to the people who built the observatory that reads the energy of a single X-ray photon as the tiny heat it deposits, and to the lineage they recovered: Makoto S. Tashiro, the mission Principal Investigator at ISAS/JAXA; Richard L. Kelley, the U.S. Principal Investigator and a Resolve co-lead at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center; Brian J. Williams, the project scientist at Goddard; Yoshitaka Ishisaki of Tokyo Metropolitan University, the other Resolve co-lead, and Caroline Kilbourne of Goddard, a key detector scientist; Koji Mori of the University of Miyazaki, who led the Xtend wide-field imager; the teams across JAXA, NASA, ESA, and Canada — more than seventy institutions — who flew the recovery of ASTRO-H / Hitomi; and Riccardo Giacconi and Bruno Rossi, who founded X-ray astronomy with the 1962 rocket flight that discovered Scorpius X-1. From a low orbit, an array of detectors held a few thousandths of a degree above absolute zero waited in the dark, and each time one cosmic X-ray arrived it warmed a single pixel by a measurable trace — and from that trace the temperature, the makeup, and the motion of distant hot gas could be read.
