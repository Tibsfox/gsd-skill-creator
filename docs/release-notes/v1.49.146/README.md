## Summary

The Salon Keeper and the trill. Degree 11 introduces Joe Brazil -- an artist with no major recordings, no critical retrospectives, no Grammy nominations, and no lasting artifact except the community he built. Brazil was born in 1928 in Detroit, Michigan, one of the deepest jazz cities in American history, and migrated to Seattle's Central District in the 1960s, where he opened his home as a salon for younger musicians. He played flute, saxophone, and clarinet -- three instruments spanning two tone-production families with fundamentally different embouchure techniques -- and used all three to teach. Not at a university like Floyd Standifer at the University of Washington. Not at a conservatory like Julian Priester and Hadley Caliman at Cornish College of the Arts. In his living room. The salon model is older than jazz itself: a knowledgeable practitioner opens their door, and students come to learn by being present. Brazil's legacy is invisible to anyone who was not in the room. No recording survives to prove what happened there. What survives is a community of Seattle musicians -- carrying his influence in their playing, their teaching, their understanding of the tradition. The Salon Keeper enters the S36 engine as the twelfth artist-city pattern and the first artist whose primary contribution is people, not artifacts. The Oregon Junco (*Junco hyemalis oreganus*), meanwhile, perches at the forest edge and trills -- one note, repeated so rapidly it becomes a metallic buzz. Discrete pulses fusing into a continuous tone. The ear hears a sustained, ringing signal. The spectrogram shows individual elements separated by gaps. Reality and perception diverge at the boundary where repetition rate exceeds temporal resolution. This is the stroboscopic effect in sound: the same principle that makes a strobe light appear steady above its flicker fusion threshold, the same principle that makes a mandolin's tremolo picking sound like a sustained note, the same principle that makes PWM-dimmed LEDs appear smoothly lit. The Oregon Junco's trill is the biological demonstration of the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem -- discrete samples reconstructed as a continuous signal when the sampling rate exceeds the auditory system's fusion threshold. Shannon's theorem in feathers.

**THE THREE-POINT EDUCATOR SPECTRUM IS COMPLETE.** Degree 11 closes a pedagogical taxonomy the engine has been assembling since degree 4. **THE NYQUIST-SHANNON SAMPLING THEOREM EXPRESSED IN FEATHERS.** The BPS (Built and Physical Systems) project documents the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem -- a continuous signal can be perfectly reconstructed from discrete samples if the sampling rate is at least twice the signal's highest frequency component. The Junco's trill is the biological demonstration. The "signal" is a sustained tone. The "samples" are the individual trill notes at 10-25 per second. When the trill rate exceeds the auditory system's Nyquist threshold (around 12-15 notes per second for human listeners), the brain reconstructs the continuous tone from the discrete sample stream. Below the threshold the trill dissolves into individual taps -- above, it fuses into a buzz. The LED project's PWM dimming is the visual equivalent. The film industry's 24-frame-per-second convention is another. AM radio modulation is a third. Tremolo picking on a mandolin is a fourth. Brazil's salon attendance is a fifth: discrete sessions at high enough frequency fuse in the community's memory into what felt like Brazil's continuous presence. Brazil was "always there" in the way the junco's trill "always sounds." Neither was literally continuous. Both were perceived as such because the repetition rate exceeded the community's (or the ear's) temporal resolution. The same theorem governs signal processing in feathers, in LEDs, in radio, in mandolins, and in community memory. Medium-independent. The lesson for federation and trust networks in the same way it is the lesson for the auditory system: discrete events at sufficient frequency produce perceived continuity. This is Claude Shannon at Bell Labs, written in junco feathers at the forest edge of Seattle.

**THE MULTI-REED TEACHING BODY -- THREE EMBOUCHURES, ONE PEDAGOGUE.** Brazil played three reed/wind instruments requiring fundamentally different embouchure techniques. Flute: aerodynamic embouchure, air directed across the embouchure hole, no reed, pure and breathy tone -- the most indirect body-to-sound connection of any wind instrument. Saxophone: single-reed embouchure, lower lip supporting the reed against the mouthpiece, warm and voice-like tone. Clarinet: single-reed embouchure similar to saxophone but with a smaller mouthpiece, narrower reed, tighter lip seal, and a cylindrical bore emphasizing odd harmonics for its distinctive woody, hollow quality. A multi-reed player must maintain three distinct embouchure sets -- three different muscle-memory configurations that must be reset without losing tonal center when switching instruments. In the salon, this was pedagogically invaluable: Brazil could demonstrate a melodic concept on three instruments in sequence, showing how the same idea sounds in three timbral environments. Same notes, three emotional characters. Pedagogy through timbral comparison. This is the most detailed physical-mechanics-of-sound-production section since the harmonic series analysis at degree 7 (Julian Priester and the Hermit Thrush), and it extends the engine's music-theory apparatus into embouchure physiology for the first time. At the same time, the junco's trill is a body-physics question: how does a bird produce 20 discrete pulses per second with a syrinx whose muscles have frequency limits? Both sides of the degree push into the physiology of sound production. Brazil through the human embouchure, the junco through the avian syrinx. Body as instrument.

## Part B: Oregon Junco -- The Metallic Trill

Full deep research covering the PNW's most common Dark-eyed Junco form and the bioacoustics of the discrete-to-continuous threshold, with focus on trill as tremolo, stroboscopic fusion, Nyquist-Shannon sampling in biology, ecotone specialization, and the contested 1973 taxonomic lump:

- **Taxonomy -- The 1973 Lumping Problem:** The Oregon Junco is classified as a subspecies group within the Dark-eyed Junco (*Junco hyemalis*), family Emberizidae (New World sparrows), order Passeriformes. Five formerly separate species were merged into one by the American Ornithologists' Union in 1973: Oregon Junco (PNW form, dark hood, rusty-brown back), Slate-colored Junco (eastern form, uniform dark slate), Pink-sided Junco (Rocky Mountain form), Gray-headed Junco (southern Rockies), and White-winged Junco (Black Hills form). The lump was based on hybridization in overlap zones. But molecular phylogenetic studies (Mila et al. 2007; Friis et al. 2016) show significant genetic divergence -- particularly the Oregon group, which is genetically distinct from the Slate-colored group across most of its range. By the Phylogenetic Species Concept, the Oregon Junco is a species. Junco taxonomy is under active review; the 1973 lump may be reversed. The bird at the feeder does not care.

- **Bioacoustics -- The Trill (Discrete Pulses as Continuous Tone):** The Oregon Junco's song is a metallic trill on a single pitch -- a rapid repetition of one note at constant frequency, 3-6 kHz, trill rate approximately 10-25 notes per second, duration 1-2 seconds. Below approximately 12-15 notes per second, the ear resolves individual notes -- a rattle, a series of clicks. Above that rate, the notes fuse into a continuous-sounding buzz or tone. The junco typically sings at 15-25 notes per second, above the temporal fusion threshold. The trill sounds continuous. It is not. This is the defining acoustic property: individual pulses perceived as a sustained tone. The ear hears a buzz. The spectrogram shows individual elements. Reality and perception diverge at the boundary where repetition rate exceeds temporal resolution.

- **The Trill as Tremolo -- Stroboscopic Fusion:** The junco's trill is the acoustic equivalent of the stroboscopic effect. A strobe light flashes discrete pulses; above the flicker fusion threshold, the brain perceives continuous illumination. The junco's trill operates identically at the auditory fusion threshold. This maps directly to tremolo in music (rapid repetition of a single note creating the illusion of sustain -- violin bow vibration, mandolin picking, piano key re-striking), to AM radio (carrier wave switching on and off above modulation frequency), to persistence of vision in film (24 fps) and LED displays (PWM dimming). All rely on the same fusion principle: present discrete signals faster than the sensory system can resolve them, and the brain constructs continuity from discreteness. The junco's trill is the biological original of every digital-to-analog conversion technology.

- **The Varied Thrush Contrast (Continuous vs. Discrete):** The deepest connection in the species circle. The Varied Thrush at degree 2 produces a genuinely sustained tone -- a single whistle held at constant pitch, a solid horizontal line on the spectrogram. The Oregon Junco produces what sounds like a sustained tone but is structurally a rapid series of discrete pulses -- a dotted line where the thrush has a solid line. Same perceptual result from opposite structural strategies. The thrush holds a note. The junco builds a note from atoms. Analog versus digital. Continuity versus construction. The engine's first documented analog/digital pair is nine degrees apart on the circle -- a spacing that is itself a candidate for tracking.

- **Nyquist-Shannon in Biology:** The BPS project documents the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem: a continuous signal can be perfectly reconstructed from discrete samples if the sampling rate is at least twice the signal's highest frequency component. The junco's trill is the biological demonstration. The "signal" is a sustained tone. The "samples" are the individual trill notes. The trill rate is the sampling rate. When it exceeds the auditory system's Nyquist threshold, the brain reconstructs the continuous tone from discrete samples. The LED project's PWM dimming is the visual equivalent. Shannon's theorem in feathers, written in the syrinx of an Oregon Junco at the forest edge in spring.

- **Forest Edge Ecology -- The Ecotone Specialist:** The Oregon Junco occupies the ecotone -- the boundary where forest meets clearing, canopy gives way to shrub, one world grades into another. Not deep forest (the Varied Thrush's domain). Not open field (the Savannah Sparrow's domain). The edge. In the Puget Sound region: forest margins where Douglas fir meets clearings, second-growth edges, suburban yards and parks (the most common winter feeder bird in Seattle), trailhead parking lots, logged clearings. The Bushtit (degree 10) works the shrub canopy of the edge. The Junco works the ground layer. Both are edge species, partitioning the ecotone vertically.

- **Threshold Architecture -- Shared With Music and Pedagogy:** The MDB (Moody Blues) research documents threshold architecture -- boundaries between genres, movements, sections. The junco lives at the ecological threshold. Its trill carries across the boundary -- audible from within the forest AND from the clearing. In classical cadence structure, the trill ornaments the penultimate note before resolution -- the note AT the boundary between tension and release. The junco trills at the boundary between forest and field, between winter and spring, between cover and exposure. The trill IS the cadence ornament of the forest edge. The same threshold architecture governs Brazil's salon: at the boundary between formal and informal, between conservatory and club, between knowledge and practice.

- **Conservation and Traditional Land Management:** Least Concern (IUCN). Approximately 260 million breeding population (Partners in Flight 2020). The Oregon form is the most common subspecies group on the Pacific coast. Thrives in edge habitat, suburban landscapes, and disturbed areas. Vulnerability factors: domestic cat predation (ground-foraging behavior), window strikes, pesticide exposure, habitat loss where edges become dense development. OCAP/CARE compliance attribution for Coast Salish traditional land management that maintained the forest-meadow mosaics juncos depend upon. The bird that thrives in human-modified landscapes still depends on land-management traditions older than those landscapes.

- **Energy 2 -- The Forest-Edge Constant:** The trill is modest -- not the silence of the heron, not the cascade of the wren, but a persistent, metallic presence at the forest edge. Consistent, year-round, always there. The backyard bird. The parking-lot bird. The bird that is always present at the threshold. Twelfth consecutive E=2 species, matching Brazil's twelfth consecutive E=2 educator. The plateau holds on both sides of the circle simultaneously.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **The Salon Keeper as missing archetype** filled the most significant gap in the engine's documentation of how jazz survives between generations. The three-point educator spectrum (institutional / performance / salon) is the first complete pedagogical taxonomy in the S36 engine, and it emerged naturally from the data rather than being imposed. Standifer teaches at UW, Berry teaches by playing every night, Brazil teaches by opening his door. Three fundamentally different answers to the same question: how does the tradition continue? The spectrum was implicit in the first ten degrees; degree 11 made it explicit by adding the salon as the third vertex. This kind of emergence -- where a new data point reveals structure that was latent in existing data -- is the engine operating correctly.

- **The trill as analog-to-digital boundary** produced the tightest acoustic theory section in the species engine. The connection from the junco's trill through the stroboscopic effect, tremolo technique, AM radio, PWM dimming, and the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem is not analogy but identity -- the same psychophysical principle operating across different media (sound, light, electronics, biology). The contrast with the Varied Thrush at degree 2 (genuinely continuous vs. perceived-as-continuous) provides the engine with its first documented case of two degrees producing the same perceptual output from opposite structural architectures. The solid line versus the dotted line on the spectrogram is the cleanest visual summary of analog versus digital the engine will ever produce.

- **Central District geographic oscillation tracking** proved its value at degree 11. The four-degree absence (7-10) followed by the return at degree 11 is now a documented pattern rather than an accident. Geographic oscillation is a feature of the engine, not a bug -- the S36 rotates through Seattle's neighborhoods and the broader PNW, and the CD's gravity pulls it back periodically. Documenting this explicitly (CD presence at 0-6, absence at 7-10, return at 11) gives the engine a geographic thread that can be tracked and predicted going forward.

- **Multi-reed embouchure theory** extends the engine's music-theory apparatus in a new direction. The three-instrument comparison (flute, saxophone, clarinet) across two tone-production families (aerodynamic vs. single-reed) with three distinct embouchure sets provides the most detailed physical-mechanics-of-sound-production section since the harmonic series analysis at degree 7. The pedagogical application -- demonstrating the same phrase on three instruments to show how timbre changes emotional character -- connects music theory directly to the teaching model. Theory serves pedagogy. The abstraction has a practical function.

- **The forest edge as threshold** unified the species research with the music research more tightly than any previous degree. The junco lives at the ecological threshold. The trill ornaments the cadence point -- the musical threshold. Brazil lived at the pedagogical threshold between formal and informal. The threshold concept operates simultaneously in ecology, bioacoustics, music theory, and pedagogy, binding all four research domains through a single structural idea. Degree 11's unity is architectural.

### What Could Be Better

- **Eleven consecutive jazz degrees remains the most urgent structural debt.** The concern flagged at degree 9 and reiterated at degree 10 is now at degree 11 with no resolution. The S36 engine has documented only jazz artists across its first twelve rotations. Genre rotation is not just overdue -- it is actively misrepresenting Seattle's musical output, which includes rock (Jimi Hendrix, Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden), folk, electronic, hip-hop (Sir Mix-a-Lot, Macklemore, Shabazz Palaces), classical, and more. The engine must break the jazz streak in subsequent degrees to maintain credibility as a music encyclopedia rather than a jazz encyclopedia.

- **The "no recordings" problem** is both Brazil's distinguishing characteristic and the research's main limitation. The absence of recordings means the research relies entirely on secondary sources -- oral histories, newspaper profiles, community tributes, Paul de Barros's *Jackson Street After Hours*. There is no primary musical evidence to analyze. The music theory sections (multi-reed technique, blues-based flute playing) describe what Brazil likely played based on the tradition he came from and the community he served, not what he demonstrably played on a specific recording. This is honest and acknowledged in the research, but it means the Brazil entry has less evidentiary support than any previous degree. Future degrees should flag this differential explicitly when citing Brazil as a comparison point.

- **Oregon Junco taxonomy section could benefit from a visual cladogram.** The five-form junco complex and its contested species boundaries are described in prose, but a phylogenetic tree diagram showing the relationships between the Oregon, Slate-colored, Pink-sided, Gray-headed, and White-winged groups would communicate the splitting/lumping debate more efficiently than text alone. The data exists in Mila et al. (2007) and Friis et al. (2016); a visual representation would strengthen the taxonomic argument.

- **The nuthatch-to-junco repetition rate gradient** was noted but not quantified precisely. The Red-breasted Nuthatch at degree 5 repeats at approximately 2-4 notes per second. The junco repeats at 10-25 notes per second. The fusion threshold falls at approximately 12-15 notes per second. The precise mapping of where individual perception becomes continuous perception -- the psychoacoustic Nyquist boundary -- deserves a more rigorous frequency-domain analysis. The research identifies the phenomenon correctly but leaves the quantitative characterization to future work.

## Lessons Learned

1. **The salon model is the most important and most fragile transmission vector.** Brazil's contribution is simultaneously the highest-fidelity transmission available (direct, personal, sustained, responsive to questions) and the most endangered (no documentation, no institutional memory, survives only in those who were present). The engine now documents three transmission vectors with radically different survival characteristics: recordings (most durable, cannot answer questions), institutions (second most durable, selective memory), and salon/oral tradition (least durable, most faithful). The highest-bandwidth communication channel (face-to-face pair programming) produces the least documentation. The lowest-bandwidth channel (published API docs) is the most durable. The lesson for federation, trust systems, and knowledge management: design for transmission fidelity AND durability simultaneously. The salon is not enough by itself. Neither is the recording. The tradition needs all three vectors operating in parallel.

2. **The stroboscopic principle unifies more domains than expected.** The junco's trill is not merely "like" a tremolo or "like" PWM dimming -- it IS the same psychophysical phenomenon operating in a different medium. Discrete pulses above the fusion threshold produce perceived continuity in sound (trill/tremolo), in light (strobe/PWM/film), in electronics (AM radio, digital-to-analog conversion), and in biology (the junco's trill, the cricket's chirp). The principle is medium-independent. The fusion threshold is the boundary. When two phenomena from different domains share structural identity (not just analogy), the connection is load-bearing. The junco-to-Shannon connection is not decoration -- it is the same theorem expressed in feathers instead of equations. The engine should continue to distinguish between analogy (structural similarity) and identity (structural sameness) in its cross-references.

3. **Edge ecology is threshold architecture is cadence structure.** The forest edge, the musical cadence point, and the pedagogical boundary between formal and informal are the same structural concept operating at different scales. The ecotone is where species diversity peaks -- two adjacent ecosystems overlap, and the edge supports species from both plus edge specialists. The cadence is where harmonic tension peaks -- the penultimate note before resolution, ornamented by the trill. The salon is where pedagogical tension peaks -- neither school nor jam session, but the edge where knowledge meets practice. Thresholds are generative. The edge is not a boundary to cross but a habitat to occupy. Design systems with productive edges, not just clean separations. The most interesting behavior happens at the boundary.

4. **Same percept from opposite architectures is a diagnostic finding.** The Varied Thrush (degree 2) and the Oregon Junco (degree 11) produce perceptually similar sustained tones from structurally opposite means. The thrush holds; the junco constructs. Analog vs. digital. The engine's first documented analog/digital pair is nine degrees apart on the circle and the spacing is itself a candidate periodicity. Future degrees should be inspected for similar pairs and the inter-pair spacing tracked -- if a periodicity emerges, the engine has found a generative structural law rather than a coincidence. Diagnostic principle: when identical percept has opposite architecture, the pairing teaches something about the perceptual system, not the sources.

5. **Oral traditions require institutional parallel to survive single-transmitter loss.** Brazil's salon ended when Brazil died. No successor opened the door behind him. Salon pedagogy is non-transferable to institutional successors because its essence is the specific presence of the salon keeper. But the knowledge Brazil carried -- the tradition of Black Seattle jazz -- survived because Standifer's UW pipeline, Cornish's faculty program, and the local club ecosystem carried parts of it in parallel. No single channel could have preserved everything; multiple channels in parallel preserved enough. Principle: design redundant transmission channels with different failure modes. The salon's failure mode (transmitter death) is orthogonal to the institution's failure mode (curricular drift), and the two together are more robust than either alone.

6. **Taxonomy is political as well as biological.** The Oregon Junco's species status depends on which species concept the classifier adopts -- the Biological Species Concept says subspecies; the Phylogenetic Species Concept says species. Brazil's status as "jazz educator" depends on whether the definition requires institutional credentialing or recognizes community transmission. Both are real findings in the degree. Every classification system embeds a choice about what counts, and the choice is rarely neutral. When the engine documents a subject whose classification is contested, it should name the concept-choice explicitly rather than adopting one silently. Name the concept, then apply it, then note the alternatives.

7. **Geographic oscillation is a trackable engine feature.** The Central District's appearance at degrees 0, 3, 4, 5, 6 and then absence at 7-10 and return at 11 is the first documented oscillation on the S36 side. The Cornish-to-CD pendulum (Priester at 7, elsewhere at 8, Caliman at 9, elsewhere at 10, Brazil at CD at 11) is a second oscillation nested within the first. Future degrees should record location and measure the CD-absence intervals; if the oscillation has a stable period, the engine has found a generative law rather than a pattern. The engine's geographic taxonomy should maintain a running absence-interval ledger alongside the artist-city pattern taxonomy -- two columns, same underlying data, different analytical cut.

8. **Embouchure physiology is music theory, not decoration.** The multi-reed embouchure analysis at degree 11 and the harmonic series analysis at degree 7 are the only two physical-mechanics-of-sound-production sections in the engine to date. Both produced theory sections that were tighter and more falsifiable than the biographical or repertoire sections around them. Future artist degrees whose subjects play physically-distinct instrument families should prioritize the embouchure / tone-production analysis because it is the most analytical layer available. Physics-first theorization outperforms narrative-first theorization where the subject's instrumental practice involves a specific body-instrument interface. The body is the lab bench.

9. **Ecotone species partition their shared edge vertically.** The Bushtit (degree 10) and the Oregon Junco (degree 11) both occupy the forest edge but work different layers -- Bushtit in the shrub canopy, Junco on the ground. Two species, one edge, two layers. The partition predicts a third: a species that works the low shrub layer between Bushtit's canopy and Junco's ground. The Spotted Towhee is the candidate and is expected in a later degree. When the engine finds a two-species partition, it should predict the missing third species and verify it in subsequent degrees. Predictive taxonomy is a stronger form of documentation than retrospective taxonomy.

10. **The twelfth consecutive E=2 degree is diagnostic, not decorative.** Twelve straight degrees of sustained-register subjects is the longest plateau in the engine's opening arc, and the eventual break will be structurally dramatic because the contrast will exceed any dynamic swing the series has yet produced. The plateau is not padding -- it is a deliberately engineered dynamic runway for the E=4 burst that will eventually arrive. Engine design lesson: long plateaus make bursts louder. The opening twelve degrees of the series should be recognized as the setup for the first dynamic break, not as a flat stretch to be escaped.
