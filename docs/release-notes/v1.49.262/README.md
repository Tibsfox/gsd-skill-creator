## Bufflehead -- The Smallest Diver

The Bufflehead (*Bucephala albeola*) is the smallest diving duck in North America. A compact, energetic bird that looks almost toy-like on the water -- oversized head relative to body, striking black-and-white plumage on the male, muted browns on the female.

The **squeaky peeping** is exactly what it sounds like -- thin, high-pitched, squeaky vocalizations that seem undersized for even this small duck. E=3. The quietest entry in the Anatidae streak so far.

The **Flicker connection**: Buffleheads are obligate cavity nesters, and they are so small that they can nest in cavities excavated by Northern Flickers -- one of the few ducks small enough to use flicker holes. This creates a direct ecological link between the woodpecker sequence (degrees 107-115) and the current Anatidae sequence. The woodpeckers built the infrastructure; the Bufflehead inherits it. Habitat as cross-reference.

Diving behavior: Buffleheads are diving ducks that forage by diving underwater to catch aquatic invertebrates and small fish. They dive with a characteristic forward leap, disappearing completely, then popping back up like a cork. Rapid, energetic diving -- more active than the mergansers (119-120).

Lake habitat. Buffleheads winter on sheltered bays and lakes throughout the Pacific Northwest. Common on Lake Washington, Lake Union, Puget Sound coves.
