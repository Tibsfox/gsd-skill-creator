## Summary

v1.49.1018 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.208 to 1.209, adding Landsat — the first program of systematic, repeated multispectral imaging of Earth's whole land surface from orbit — as the obs#1 first INSTANCE that OPENS the EARTH-OBSERVATION substrate-axis (axis-rotation #30 OPENED). The exoplanet-detection axis sustained two observations (v1016 Kepler's transit-photometry survey of a single deep field, v1017 TESS's all-sky transit survey of the nearest bright stars) and reached both the deep-field and the all-sky transit forms; the forward cadence now rotates to a new substrate-axis on a different way of seeing — not outward to the distant sky, but back to our own planet, the systematic imaging of Earth's land surface from space. Where the exoplanet surveys watched distant stars for new worlds, Landsat turns the satellite gaze back to the land beneath.

Landsat is a joint program of NASA, which develops, builds, and launches the satellites, and the United States Geological Survey, which operates them and archives and distributes the data. The first satellite, Landsat 1, reached its Sun-synchronous near-polar orbit on 23 July 1972, launched as the Earth Resources Technology Satellite (ERTS-1) and renamed Landsat in 1975, carried aloft on a Delta rocket from Vandenberg Air Force Base, California. The program has continued through a succession of satellites, Landsat 1 to 9, with Landsat 8 and 9 operating today, the longest continuous record of Earth's land surface from space.

The signature of the program is the systematic multispectral land imaging. Landsat records each scene of the surface in several bands of visible and infrared light at once, so vegetation, soil, water, rock, and built surfaces can be told apart by how they reflect across the spectrum, turning an image of the land into a measurement of what covers it.

The continuous record carries the survey across the decades. Landsat has imaged the same ground in repeated passes for decades, the longest unbroken record of the surface from space, each satellite handing the record to the next, the whole archive held together so a scene can be compared with images of the same ground made years or decades earlier.

The civilian land program broadened the use of the record. Landsat was the first satellite program built to image the land for civilian use — agriculture, forestry, geology, water resources, and land-use planning — and the imagery, made free and openly available by the USGS in 2008, put a consistent, shared view of the surface in the hands of scientists, farmers, and planners worldwide.

The land-change monitoring is the measurement the program made possible. By comparing images of the same ground across the years, Landsat lets scientists measure how the land changes — the clearing and regrowth of forests, the spread of cities, the retreat of glaciers, and the shifting of coastlines and rivers — turning single images into a record of a changing planet.

The Sun-synchronous repeat coverage makes the long record consistent. Landsat's near-polar orbit brings each satellite back over the same ground every 18 days for Landsat 1 to 3 and every 16 days from Landsat 4 onward, crossing each part of the land at about the same local solar time so the surface is imaged again and again under comparable lighting.

The axis-relevant substrate of Landsat at this degree is its OPENING of the EARTH-OBSERVATION substrate-axis at obs#1, the systematic multispectral imaging of Earth's land surface. Landsat sustains the EARTH-OBSERVATION-AXIS thread at obs#1 NEW LOCKED and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#81 cumulative (axis-rotation #30 OPENED). The forward continuation may favor a later Earth-observing mission that sustains the axis at obs#2 — a flagship multi-instrument Earth-observing platform, an ice-and-elevation mission, or a gravity / soil-moisture mission — or a substrate-axis rotation #31 to an under-served axis.

Landsat opens the earth-observation way of seeing on the catalog, and the mission records the originators of the Earth Resources Technology Satellite, the Landsat team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, the multispectral-imager engineering teams, and the operators and archivists of the United States Geological Survey. ## Lessons Learned

1. **A new substrate-axis opens by turning the gaze a different way.** By turning the satellite gaze back from the distant sky to our own planet, Landsat opened the earth-observation axis at obs#1 distinct from the exoplanet axis the cadence rotated away from, showing that a rotation opens a new way of seeing rather than extending an old one.
2. **Recording several bands at once turns a picture into a measurement.** By imaging each scene in several bands of visible and infrared light, Landsat let one kind of cover be told from another by how it reflects across the spectrum, showing that the multispectral record measures what covers the land, not only depicts it.
3. **A long record is what measures change.** By imaging the same ground again and again under comparable lighting for decades, Landsat made it possible to measure how the land changes from one year to the next, showing that a single image shows the land at a moment while a continuous record shows how it changes.
4. **Comparable lighting is what makes a record consistent.** By flying a Sun-synchronous orbit that crosses each part of the land at about the same local solar time, Landsat kept the lighting comparable across the years, showing that a difference between two images is a difference in the land only when the conditions of measurement are held fixed.
5. **Opening an archive multiplies a record's reach.** By making the imagery free and openly available, the USGS broadened Landsat's use across many fields and many countries, showing that a record's value grows when it is handed freely to all who would read it.
6. **A record consistent across instruments needs careful calibration.** Because each satellite hands the record to the next, the imagery from one sensor must be made consistent with the next through radiometric calibration, showing that a continuous record across the decades rests on keeping the measurement comparable as the instruments change.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across an axis rotation.** Because Landsat reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.208 TESS template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the land survey while keeping the warm palette, making the axis-opening a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The first land-imaging satellite program was civilian and open.** Landsat was built to image the land for civilian use across agriculture, forestry, water, and planning, and its archive was eventually made free and openly available, so the longest record of the surface became a shared resource rather than a closed one.
- **A long-lived eagle mirrors the decades-long survey of the same ground.** The Bald Eagle, the raptor that returns to the same territory across a long life and surveys the broad country from above, enters as the return-to-the-same-ground-and-survey-it-across-the-years mirror to Landsat returning over the same land again and again from orbit.
- **A great tree's rings mirror the continuous land record.** Douglas-fir, the long-lived tree that lays down one growth ring each year, enters as the continuous-yearly-record-of-the-land mirror, so the catalog's continuous Earth-surface record is paired with a tree whose rings make an unbroken yearly record of the land.

A future revision could load encoded Landsat scenes for a higher-fidelity rendering keyed to real land cover and real land change.

- **The land-change measurement is described rather than computed.** The vegetation index and the land-change difference are described but not run against the archived imagery; a future ship could query two real Landsat scenes of the same ground from years apart and compute a vegetation-index change directly.

## Dedication

v1.209 Landsat is dedicated to the people who built and have kept the longest continuous record of Earth's land surface from space: the originators of the Earth Resources Technology Satellite, who first turned the satellite gaze back to our own planet to image the land systematically; the Landsat team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, who develop, build, and launch the satellites; the engineering teams that designed the multispectral imagers, from the Multispectral Scanner through the Operational Land Imager, that record each scene in several bands of visible and infrared light; and the operators and archivists of the United States Geological Survey, who fly the satellites, keep the record consistent across the decades, and made the imagery free and openly available to scientists, farmers, and planners worldwide. Landsat images the whole land surface again and again under comparable lighting, building a record from which we read how the land changes across the years, and the program continues today through Landsat 8 and 9.
