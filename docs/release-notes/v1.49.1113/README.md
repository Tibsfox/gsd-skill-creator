## Mission Overview

Terra is the obs#16 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's first Earth Observing System flagship, the satellite that opened the modern era of coordinated, multi-instrument Earth observation. ★ It is a NASA Earth-science mission managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center (GSFC); originally designated EOS AM-1 (the "AM" for its morning equator crossing) and renamed Terra, it is the first of the EOS flagship trio (Terra, Aqua, Aura) and the morning-orbit anchor of the EOS series. Terra carries five instruments at once and reads the whole Earth from a sun-synchronous morning orbit: MODIS (the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer, 36 bands at 250 m–1 km) images the land, ocean, and atmosphere daily and near-globally, reading vegetation, fires, snow, and clouds; ASTER (the Advanced Spaceborne Thermal Emission and Reflection Radiometer, a Japan METI/ERSDAC instrument with VNIR/SWIR/TIR channels at ~15–90 m) returns digital elevation models and surface temperature and emissivity; CERES (Clouds and the Earth's Radiant Energy System) measures the planet's radiation budget; MISR (the Multi-angle Imaging SpectroRadiometer) reads aerosols, cloud heights, and surface bidirectional reflectance with nine cameras at different view angles; and MOPITT (Measurements of Pollution in the Troposphere, a Canada/CSA instrument) mapped tropospheric carbon monoxide from 2000 until its deactivation in 2025. The mission launched 18 December 1999 (10:57 PST) on an Atlas IIAS (vehicle AC-141, the first Atlas Centaur from Vandenberg) from Vandenberg AFB, SLC-3E, California, and reached a sun-synchronous orbit at roughly 705 km altitude and ~98.2° inclination, descending across the equator at about 10:30 AM (the morning orbit), with data collection beginning 24 February 2000. Terra led the Morning (AM) Constellation (with Landsat-7, EO-1, and SAC-C), not the A-Train. The story is the Atlas IIAS launch from Vandenberg, the climb to the sun-synchronous morning orbit, the five instruments turned at once on the daylit globe, the green Earth read from above as land cover and vegetation and fires and snow, and the long daily near-global imaging record that continues to be gathered today. The science is central: from the morning orbit Terra reads land, atmosphere, and the radiation budget at once — MODIS imaging the daylit globe, ASTER returning fine surface detail, CERES weighing the radiation budget, MISR reading nine angles, and MOPITT having mapped the troposphere's carbon monoxide — a five-instrument synergy. ★★★ Terra is OPERATING, so the catalog frames its whole story in the PRESENT (Terra orbits its sun-synchronous morning orbit, carries its five instruments, observes and images the land and atmosphere, and continues the long daily near-global imaging record); individual past events stay in the past — ASTER's SWIR subsystem failed in 2008 (VNIR and TIR continue), Terra exited the Morning Constellation in October 2022, and MOPITT was deactivated on 9 April 2025. Terra's firsts are scoped carefully: it is the first EOS flagship, opening the coordinated multi-instrument EOS era; the five-instrument synergy reads land, atmosphere, and the radiation budget at once from the morning orbit; and MODIS, which first flew on Terra, began the long daily near-global land+atmosphere imaging record. ★ The scope is load-bearing: the Earth had been imaged from space before Terra (Landsat from 1972, AVHRR, and others), so Terra was NOT "the first Earth-observing satellite"; its advance is the EOS-flagship multi-instrument synergy and the long MODIS/MISR records (Terra-MODIS the morning complement to Aqua-MODIS). ## Dedication

v1.296 Terra is dedicated to the people at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, and at the international partners — Japan's METI and ERSDAC, who built ASTER, and Canada's CSA, who built MOPITT — who designed and flew the first flagship of the Earth Observing System. The scientists who built the long daily record of the whole green Earth seen from above — its vegetation, its fires, its snow, its clouds — image after image, day after day. From 705 km the satellite still reads the morning side of the planet, and that long record of the living Earth belongs to them.
