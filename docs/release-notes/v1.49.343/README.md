**Why Surf Rock in Seattle: The Pacific Northwest Cold-Water Idiom.** Seattle is not traditionally understood as a surf-rock city. The iconic surf-rock geography of Southern California — Huntington Beach, Manhattan Beach, the Santa Monica Pier, Dick Dale's Newport Beach origins, the Ventures' surf-instrumental hits of the early 1960s — is built on warm water, bikini-beach culture, and sun-and-sand aesthetic coding that the Pacific Northwest coastline does not share. The Washington and Oregon coasts are cold (water temperatures 50-55°F year-round), rugged, frequently foggy, and home to a genuine surf culture but one practiced in wetsuits rather than bikinis at towns like **Ocean Shores**, **Westport**, and **Long Beach, Washington**, and down the Oregon coast at Cannon Beach, Pacific City, and Seaside. La Luz's sound is not a stylistic relocation of California surf rock to the Pacific Northwest; it is a genuine Pacific Northwest idiom built on the actual acoustic conditions of the regional coastline. The reverb evokes fog rather than sunshine. The tremolo suggests cold swells rather than warm breaks. The minor-key compositional tendencies and darker tonal palette reflect a regional sensibility where the ocean feels gray and cold and the weather is persistently overcast rather than bright. Shana Cleveland's guitar work specifically references Dick Dale's tremolo picking, Link Wray's sustained-distortion vocabulary, and The Ventures' instrumental-surf tradition, but filters these influences through indie-rock sensibility and Pacific Northwest melancholy rather than reproducing their California origins. The result is a coherent regional idiom — surf rock for people who surf in wetsuits, not bikinis.

**The Mechanism and Function of the Cub Distress Call.** The cub distress call is a high-pitched, thin, sustained mewling — pitched at 300-800 Hz, higher than any adult bear vocalization — produced by cubs separated from their mother or experiencing fear. The pitch is generated by the cub's small vocal cords and small body cavity, and as the cub grows through summer the pitch gradually drops. Repetition rates scale with distress level: a slightly worried cub mews occasionally (every 5-10 seconds), a moderately distressed cub mews at 2-5 second intervals, and a panicked cub mews continuously without pause. The call serves one primary function: to bring the sow back. It operates simultaneously as a contact call, a location beacon, and a plea for help, and the sow's response to the distress call is consistently urgent — she will often crash through brush at high speed to reach the cub, prioritizing the cub's welfare above her own safety. This response pattern makes the sound of a mewing bear cub on a trail the highest-risk bear encounter a hiker can have in the Pacific Northwest lowlands: the sow is already coming, and her defensive aggression toward any perceived threat (including nearby humans) is significantly elevated when a cub is in distress. **WDFW** (Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife) bear safety protocols explicitly document this risk and recommend immediate retreat from any encounter with a mewing or audibly distressed bear cub. The distress call also appears in tree-climbing contexts: when a sow perceives danger, she sends cubs up a tree by huffing or woofing, and cubs who are too young or too frightened to climb may mew in distress from the base of the tree as the sow positions herself between the threat and the cub.

**Cub Winter-Den Development and First-Summer Learning.** Black bear cubs enter the world in darkness. Cubs are born in the winter den (typically January-February in the Pacific Northwest) while the sow is still in hibernation, with typical litter sizes of 2-3 cubs. Birth weight is approximately 200-400 grams (less than 1 pound), and newborns are tiny, blind, and nearly hairless. Cubs nurse in the den for 2-3 months, growing rapidly on the sow's rich milk (20-25% fat content, comparable to marine mammal milk), and by the time the family emerges in April-May the cubs weigh 2-4 kg (5-10 lbs). The first summer is the learning period: cubs follow the sow constantly, learning what to eat (salmonberries, huckleberries, salmon runs, carrion, insects, occasional small mammals), where to forage, how to climb trees as the primary escape behavior, and when to be afraid. The cub distress call is the vocal tether that keeps the family together during this critical period, activating whenever the cub loses sight of the sow or feels threatened. Cubs typically nurse through the first summer and are weaned by fall, then den with the sow for their second winter and separate in their second spring at approximately 16 months of age. The complete developmental arc from 200-gram newborn through first-summer learning to independent subadult at 16 months requires the distress-call communication channel to function reliably, since the cub cannot survive without the sow during this window.

## Part B: Black Bear Cub Distress Call — The Closing of the Bear Trio and the Maternal-Response Register

- **Species Baseline and Pacific Northwest Distribution:** **Black Bear** (*Ursus americanus*) is a member of **Order Carnivora / Family Ursidae / Genus Ursus / species americanus**, the only Ursidae present in Pacific Northwest lowland habitat since grizzlies are restricted to remote areas of the North Cascades, the Selkirk Mountains, and outlying populations. Black bear distribution covers forest and meadow habitat across the **Olympic Peninsula**, the **Cascade Range** (both western and eastern slopes), Oregon coastal forests, northern California redwoods, and the continental distribution extending eastward across North America. Adult females (sows) range 100-250 pounds; adult males (boars) range 150-350 pounds. The species is omnivorous, adapted to a wide range of forest-and-meadow habitat types, and has recovered from late-nineteenth-century and early-twentieth-century hunting pressure to become the most numerous large carnivore in the Pacific Northwest lowlands. Black bear encounters with hikers, campers, and residents in bear habitat are common but typically non-aggressive under normal conditions.
- **The Bear Trio Across Degrees 200-202 and the 5-6-4 Amplitude Arc:** The engine's documentation of the **BEAR TRIO** across degrees 200-202 covers three vocalizations — the **huff** at degree 200 (E=5, mild agitation warning), the **jaw-pop** at degree 201 (E=6, defensive stress escalation), and the **cub distress call** at degree 202 (E=4, maternal-response vulnerability) — forming a complete acoustic arc of the species' emotional communication repertoire. The **5-6-4 amplitude arc** descends from adult warning through adult escalation to infant vulnerability, capturing the species' full emotional range across three consecutive degrees rather than concentrating on any single amplitude register. The arc is structurally distinctive because it concludes with a *lower*-amplitude vulnerability register rather than a higher-amplitude combat or challenge register, which is the pattern the Roosevelt Elk trio's 7-5-7 arc at degrees 197-199 followed. This contrast establishes that three-degree species arcs can resolve in either high-amplitude or low-amplitude directions depending on the species' full communication repertoire, and for the black bear the cub distress register is essential to complete documentation.
- **The Cub Distress Mechanism and 300-800 Hz High Pitch:** The cub distress call is a high-pitched, thin, sustained mewling — "meeew, meeew" — pitched at 300-800 Hz, higher than any adult bear vocalization. The pitch is produced by the cub's small vocal cords and small body cavity, which physically constrain the available frequency range to the high end of the bear-species vocalization spectrum. As the cub grows through summer and the vocal cords lengthen, the pitch gradually drops, so that cubs in their second summer produce distress calls at lower frequencies than cubs in their first summer. The call's timbre is thin rather than full because of the cub's small resonating cavity, and the sustained duration (0.5-2.0 seconds per mew, repeated every 2-5 seconds at moderate distress) combined with the high frequency makes the call acoustically distinctive and immediately identifiable in forest environments.
- **Function and Maternal-Response Pattern:** The cub distress call serves one primary function: to bring the sow back. The call operates simultaneously as a contact call, a location beacon, and a plea for help, and the sow's response to the distress call is consistently urgent. Sows respond to the cub's distress call by crashing through brush at high speed to reach the cub, prioritizing the cub's welfare above their own safety. This response pattern is genetically hardwired and essentially universal across black bear populations, making it one of the most reliable maternal-response behaviors in North American mammals. The response is also defensive: the sow's approach to the cub is accompanied by elevated defensive aggression toward any perceived threat, including nearby humans, and this defensive aggression makes the sow-cub interaction window the highest-risk bear encounter in Pacific Northwest hiking habitat.
- **WDFW Bear Safety Protocols and Hiker Risk Assessment:** **WDFW** (Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife) bear safety protocols explicitly document the sow-cub encounter as the highest-risk bear interaction a hiker can have in Pacific Northwest lowland habitat. The protocol recommendation for any encounter with a mewing or audibly distressed bear cub is immediate retreat: the sow is already responding, she is approaching the cub at high speed, and her defensive aggression toward any nearby human is significantly elevated. The protocol specifically warns against approaching the cub to photograph, identify, or assist, since even well-intentioned approach is interpreted by the sow as threat. **Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife** and **California Department of Fish and Wildlife** maintain analogous protocols for their respective jurisdictions, and the consistent cross-jurisdiction recommendation reflects the genetic universality of the sow-response pattern across black bear populations.
- **Tree-Climbing Context and Escape Behavior:** Black bear cubs' primary escape behavior is tree-climbing, which they learn during first-summer following of the sow. When a sow perceives danger, she sends cubs up a tree by huffing or woofing (the vocalizations documented at degree 200's huff entry and adjacent), and the cubs climb quickly to sufficient height. Cubs that are too young or too frightened to climb may instead produce the distress call from the base of the tree as the sow positions herself between the threat and the cub. This tree-climbing-plus-distress-call behavioral complex is the core escape strategy for first-summer cubs and provides the cub-survival mechanism during the vulnerable first-year window before independent predator-avoidance capability develops. The strategy's reliability depends on both the cub's climbing capability and the distress call's maternal-response activation, creating a two-part survival system that functions redundantly.
- **Cub Development from Winter-Den Birth to 16-Month Independence:** Black bear cubs are born in the winter den (typically January-February in the Pacific Northwest) during the sow's hibernation, with typical litter sizes of 2-3 cubs and birth weight of 200-400 grams (less than 1 pound). Newborns are tiny, blind, and nearly hairless. Cubs nurse in the den for 2-3 months on the sow's rich milk (20-25% fat content, comparable to marine mammal milk), growing rapidly so that by April-May den emergence they weigh 2-4 kg. The first summer is the learning period for food identification, foraging locations, tree-climbing escape behavior, and predator-avoidance timing. Weaning occurs through the first fall. Cubs den with the sow for their second winter and separate in their second spring at approximately 16 months. The complete developmental arc requires the distress-call communication channel to function reliably during the first-summer and second-summer windows when the cub cannot yet survive independently.
- **Ursidae Register Closes at Degree 202:** The black bear cub distress call at degree 202 closes the engine's Ursidae family register for Pacific Northwest species documentation since grizzlies are restricted to remote areas and the black bear is the only lowland bear in the region. The bear trio's three entries at degrees 200-202 (huff, jaw-pop, cub distress) constitute the engine's complete Ursidae documentation, and future engine degrees will not add additional bear entries unless the scope expands to include remote-area grizzly or other non-regional Ursidae species. This family-level closure is structurally parallel to other single-species-dominant family closures that may follow, and establishes degree 202 as a boundary event where the engine's family-level vocabulary consolidates around the three-entry closure pattern.
- **Comparison to Fawn Bleat and Infant Contact Call Register:** The cub distress call (202, E=4) and the fawn bleat (documented at degree 195 with Black-tailed Deer maa bleat, E=3) are both infant contact calls but differ in amplitude and character. The cub is slightly louder and more insistent than the fawn — E=4 vs. E=3 — reflecting both the larger body size of a first-summer black bear cub compared to a first-summer black-tailed deer fawn and the more developed maternal-response urgency in the black bear sow compared to the black-tailed deer doe. Bear cubs are more vocal than deer fawns, the cub squalls while the fawn mews more quietly, and the maternal response in bears is more aggressive than in deer. This comparison across the two infant-contact-call documentations establishes that the infant-calls-parent register operates across different species with species-specific amplitude and character variation, opening future documentation space for additional infant-contact-call entries.
- **Acoustic Properties and Forest Carry Distance:** The cub distress call carries 50-150 meters through Pacific Northwest forest habitat — sufficient for maternal response within typical sow-cub separation distances but not designed for long-range communication. The carry distance is shorter than adult bear vocalizations (the huff at degree 200 carries 100-300 meters, the jaw-pop at degree 201 is audible at 200+ meters as an impulse) because the cub's small body produces less acoustic energy and the high-frequency content attenuates more quickly in forest environments than lower-frequency content. The carry distance is calibrated by evolutionary pressure to match typical sow-cub separation during normal foraging and movement activity, so that cubs who stray 50-150 meters from the sow can reliably signal their location and need, but cubs who stray further than this distance are already at high survival risk regardless of whether they call.
