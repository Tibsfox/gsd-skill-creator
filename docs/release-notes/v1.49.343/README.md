## Summary

**La Luz** arrives at E=5 -- and with them, **FIRST SURF ROCK enters the Seattle 360 engine as Genre 106**. La Luz is Shana Cleveland's reverb-drenched, melody-forward project that proved surf rock could be a Pacific Northwest idiom: not the sun-bleached California variety, but something darker, wetter, filtered through overcast skies and cold saltwater. Albums like *It's Alive* (2013) and *Floating Features* (2018) on Hardly Art built a sound that is simultaneously retro and distinctly regional -- Dick Dale's tremolo picking reimagined for a coastline where the water is 50 degrees and the fog never fully lifts. At E=5, La Luz brings warmth to a stretch that has been running cool since degree 196. On the species side, the **Black Bear cub distress** call closes the bear trio at E=4 -- the lowest energy in the sequence, and deliberately so. The cub distress call is a high-pitched, crying vocalization produced by cubs separated from their mother or experiencing fear. It is the acoustic opposite of the jaw-pop: where the adult bear's warning is designed to repel, the cub's cry is designed to attract. The bear trio's energy arc (5-6-4) moves from warning to escalation to vulnerability -- the full emotional range of a species in three consecutive degrees.

- Cub distress acoustics -- Black Bear cubs produce a high-frequency, repetitive crying sound when separated from their mother; the call is piercing and urgent, designed to trigger the mother's protective response; it is one of the few bear vocalizations that reliably brings an adult bear toward the sound source rather than away from it
- Hardly Art streak continues -- La Luz is the second of four consecutive Hardly Art entries (201-204); the label's roster is revealing itself as a coherent aesthetic: deadpan humor (Chastity Belt), reverb-soaked melody (La Luz), with Tacocat and Childbirth still to come
- Pacific Northwest surf -- La Luz's reimagining of surf rock for the PNW is not just aesthetic relocation; the band's minor-key tendencies and darker tonal palette reflect a genuine regional sensibility; surf rock in Seattle sounds different because the ocean feels different here
