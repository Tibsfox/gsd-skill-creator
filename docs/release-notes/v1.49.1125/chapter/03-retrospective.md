## Surprises

- **A land imager modernized to the push-broom.** Landsat 1 through 7 swept the land with a whisk-broom scan mirror rocking side to side. Landsat 8 replaces that moving mirror with the OLI's long, fixed detector arrays — over 7,000 detectors per band that stare across the ~185 km swath while the satellite flies forward, a push-broom that dwells longer on each patch of ground and gathers a cleaner, more sensitive signal: the first push-broom Operational Land Imager and Thermal Infrared Sensor in the Landsat series.
- **Two new bands for the edges of the land record.** Beyond continuing the familiar reflective bands, Landsat 8's OLI adds a coastal/aerosol band (band 1) tuned for shallow coastal water and fine aerosol, and a cirrus band (band 9) tuned to catch thin high cloud that would otherwise contaminate a scene — two new windows opened on the changing land and the water at its edge.
- **A single satellite that carries a record kept since 1972.** Landsat 8 does not begin the land record; it continues it. The Landsat archive reaches back to Landsat 1 in 1972, the longest continuous space-based record of Earth's land surface, and Landsat 8 extends that comparable, calibrated series forward at 30 m — the free and open USGS archive of the changing land, decade upon decade.
- **A pair of satellites that halve the wait.** Each Landsat images the whole land on a 16-day repeat cycle. Flown with Landsat 9 (launched 2021) 8 days out of phase, the two together bring the global land revisit down to 8 days — a tandem that images the changing land twice as often, the introducer Landsat 8 and its near-twin successor sharing the sky.
- **A ground-nesting songbird of the open grassland.** The Western Meadowlark (*Sturnella neglecta*, family Icteridae), a ground-nesting songbird of the open grasslands, prairies, and pastures of the Pacific Northwest, sings its flute-like song from fenceposts and shrubs over the open land Landsat images from above (behavioral / ecological description only; distinct from the Eastern Meadowlark *Sturnella magna*).
- **A prairie perennial with roots gathered from the camas prairies.** The Yampah (*Perideridia gairdneri*, Gairdner's Yampah, family Apiaceae), a slender perennial of the open prairies, meadows, and grasslands of the Pacific Northwest, in the carrot family, roots in the prairie meadow, its edible roots long gathered from the camas prairies — a plant of the open living land, bound with the throughline of the open living land.
