## Mission Overview

Landsat 8 is the obs#28 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — the NASA–USGS free-flying land-imaging satellite, originally the Landsat Data Continuity Mission (LDCM), that images Earth's entire land surface with the Operational Land Imager and the Thermal Infrared Sensor and continues the longest continuous space-based record of the changing land. ★ Landsat 8 is a partnership of NASA (which built and launched the satellite) and the U.S. Geological Survey (which operates the mission and archives and distributes the data); it was renamed Landsat 8 on 30 May 2013 when operations passed to the USGS. ★★ Its instruments are the OLI (Operational Land Imager) — a push-broom sensor with nine spectral bands in the visible, near-infrared, and shortwave-infrared, including the new coastal/aerosol band (band 1) and the cirrus band (band 9), at 30 m (15 m panchromatic), with over 7,000 detectors per band across a four-mirror telescope and 12-bit quantisation, built by Ball Aerospace; and the TIRS (Thermal Infrared Sensor) — two thermal-infrared bands (band 10 ~10.9 µm, band 11 ~12 µm), a push-broom QWIP instrument at 100 m, built at NASA Goddard. ★ OLI and TIRS are the first push-broom imagers in the Landsat series (Landsat 1–7 used whisk-broom scan-mirror instruments) over a ~185 km swath. ★ It images the global land surface for land-cover and land-use change, agriculture, forestry, water use, glaciers and ice, urban growth, wildfire, and coastal and inland water — the longest continuous space record of Earth's land (the Landsat archive since Landsat 1 in 1972); the data are free and open from the USGS. Landsat 8 images the whole land surface across a 16-day repeat cycle, reading the mosaic of fields and forests, cities and rivers, ice and prairie — the long record of the living land. The mission launches into the story on 11 February 2013 (10:02 a.m. PST) on a United Launch Alliance Atlas V 401 (extended payload fairing) from Vandenberg Air Force Base (SLC-3E), and operations pass to the USGS on 30 May 2013, when the satellite is renamed Landsat 8. The science is central: from the sun-synchronous orbit Landsat 8 records the reflected sunlight and the emitted heat of the land and turns them, by the radiometric and geometric calibration and the surface-reflectance and land-cover processing, into the long, comparable archive of the changing land surface. ★★★ Landsat 8 is OPERATING, so the catalog frames the whole MISSION in the PRESENT (Landsat 8 images the land surface, maps the land cover, records the long land archive, and observes the changing land); it launched 11 February 2013, was renamed Landsat 8 on 30 May 2013, and operates today — the launch and the handover being the only past events. ## Surprises

- **A land imager modernized to the push-broom.** Landsat 1 through 7 swept the land with a whisk-broom scan mirror rocking side to side. Landsat 8 replaces that moving mirror with the OLI's long, fixed detector arrays — over 7,000 detectors per band that stare across the ~185 km swath while the satellite flies forward, a push-broom that dwells longer on each patch of ground and gathers a cleaner, more sensitive signal: the first push-broom Operational Land Imager and Thermal Infrared Sensor in the Landsat series.
- **Two new bands for the edges of the land record.** Beyond continuing the familiar reflective bands, Landsat 8's OLI adds a coastal/aerosol band (band 1) tuned for shallow coastal water and fine aerosol, and a cirrus band (band 9) tuned to catch thin high cloud that would otherwise contaminate a scene — two new windows opened on the changing land and the water at its edge.
- **A single satellite that carries a record kept since 1972.** Landsat 8 does not begin the land record; it continues it. The Landsat archive reaches back to Landsat 1 in 1972, the longest continuous space-based record of Earth's land surface, and Landsat 8 extends that comparable, calibrated series forward at 30 m — the free and open USGS archive of the changing land, decade upon decade.
- **A pair of satellites that halve the wait.** Each Landsat images the whole land on a 16-day repeat cycle. Flown with Landsat 9 (launched 2021) 8 days out of phase, the two together bring the global land revisit down to 8 days — a tandem that images the changing land twice as often, the introducer Landsat 8 and its near-twin successor sharing the sky.

## Dedication

v1.308 Landsat 8 is dedicated to the people at NASA and the U.S. Geological Survey, and across the Landsat program and its instrument teams, who built and operate the satellite that images the whole living land from orbit and carries forward the longest continuous record of Earth's changing surface. To the engineers at Ball Aerospace who shaped the Operational Land Imager, its four-mirror telescope and its long push-broom arrays of more than seven thousand detectors a band, and to the teams at NASA Goddard who built the Thermal Infrared Sensor and its cooled thermal detectors. To the crews who carried the satellite aloft on an Atlas V from Vandenberg in February 2013, and to the operators who took the controls when the mission passed to the Survey that May. To the scientists and archivists who keep the land record open and free, comparable decade upon decade, and who joined the satellite with its near-twin to halve the wait between looks. The long record of the living land, imaged and remembered at thirty metres, prairie and forest and city and ice, is theirs to keep.
