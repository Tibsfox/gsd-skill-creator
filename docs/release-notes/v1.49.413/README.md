## Summary

**The U-Men** arrive at E=7 -- the Seattle post-punk band formed in 1980, one of the most distinctive and difficult-to-classify acts in the Pacific Northwest underground, and an important presence in the transition between the hardcore scene documented at degrees 270-271 and the wider alternative underground that would follow. The U-Men -- Tom Price (guitar, vocals), John Bigley (vocals), Charlie Ryan (bass), and Jim Tillman (drums) in their most documented configuration -- operated from 1980 to 1989, releasing work on their own label and on Sub Pop's earliest releases. Their sound is not straightforwardly hardcore: it draws on post-punk, blues, no wave, and the Chicago electric blues tradition in ways that set them apart from the bands around them. The U-Men are not a genre band. They are a Seattle band that sounds specifically like Seattle in the early 1980s -- which means they sound like no other scene and no other city.

Tom Price is the central figure. His guitar work on U-Men recordings is angular, blues-derived, rhythmically complex, and committed to a form of controlled ugliness that was more self-aware than most of their contemporaries in the hardcore scene. Price would later join Gas Huffer, another engine entry, and his career traces the through-line of Seattle's underground from hardcore's origin through the grunge era. The U-Men are his primary document.

E=7 for The U-Men reflects the post-punk complexity of their sound. They are not as purely fast or as purely loud as The Fartz or Solger. They are harder to categorize, more textured, more willing to use space and structure in ways that hardcore does not. E=7 is correct: high energy, but with the variation and craft that separates them from the E=8 pure hardcore entries.

**Transient Orca Hunt** arrives at E=8 on the SPS track -- the acoustic event produced by Bigg's Killer Whales (*Orcinus orca*, transient ecotype) during the cooperative hunting of marine mammal prey in Puget Sound. Bigg's Killer Whales are a different ecotype from the Southern Residents: where SRKW are fish specialists, salmon-dependent, acoustically rich, and highly social, Bigg's Killer Whales are mammal specialists, stealth hunters, and acoustically tactical. They hunt by silence.

The acoustic signature of a Bigg's orca hunt is not the pod calls and whistles of the SRKW -- it is the absence of sound: a period of acoustic suppression in which the whales coordinate their approach to a prey animal using only the minimum of echolocation, then close the distance in near-silence before the final rush. The transient hunt sequence is defined by its tactical quiet: the explosive breach that terminates the hunt, the thrashing of prey at the surface, the post-hunt vocalizations of the group -- these are bookended by stretches of deliberate acoustic restraint that are unlike anything in the SRKW behavioral repertoire.

E=8 for the Transient Orca Hunt reflects the energy of the hunt sequence -- the final rush, the prey capture, the surface activity -- rather than the tactical silence that precedes it. The silence is the tactic; the hunt is the event. Both are documented here. The Bigg's ecotype's acoustic profile -- minimal, deliberate, explosive at the moment of contact -- is one of the most dramatic sound events the Salish Sea produces, and it documents a fundamentally different ecological strategy from the fish-hunting, acoustically rich, salmon-dependent SRKW.

The Orca Arc contains both major ecotypes of killer whale present in the Pacific Northwest. The Southern Residents are the salmon-dependent family pod. The Bigg's whales are the mammal-hunting stealth hunters. Together they constitute the full acoustic range of *Orcinus orca* in Puget Sound.
