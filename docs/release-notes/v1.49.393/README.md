**The Cobble Beach Physics — Uprush, Maximum Uprush, Backwash, Settlement.** The signature sound of the Pacific Northwest cobble beach is not a single acoustic event but a four-phase sequence that repeats with the tidal pulse. **Wave approach (uprush):** as the wave advances up the beach, it picks up stones and pushes them forward. The initial uprush is relatively smooth — a sudden surge of water over already-wet stones. The sound is a rushing, hissing, waterfall-like sound. **Maximum uprush:** at the moment of maximum wave height on the beach, the stones are in turbulent suspension — moving in all directions, colliding randomly. The sound is a high-amplitude, complex, broadband percussion. **Backwash:** as the wave retreats down the slope, it drags cobbles with it. The retreating water pulls stones downslope while gravity and the next incoming wave resist. This creates the most characteristic cobble beach sound: a sustained, rhythmic clatter as thousands of stones slide over each other in the retreating water. The backwash clatter is the signature cobble-beach sound — the sound that identifies the beach as PNW rather than Atlantic, the sound that appears in acoustic recordings as the cobble-beach identifier. **The pause between waves (settlement):** between waves, the beach returns to relative stillness. The stones come to rest against each other. In this pause, the sounds of individual stone-to-stone contacts as the beach settles are audible. The four-phase sequence repeats with each wave, establishing the rhythmic pulse at 5-15 seconds per cycle that is the tidal beat of the PNW coast. **In parallel**, No-Anchor's noise rock work operates through specific multi-phase acoustic mechanisms: the pick attack produces the initial impulse, the string vibration establishes the note envelope, the distortion pedal shapes the harmonic content through saturation, the amplifier adds further harmonic distortion at the preamp and power amp stages, the speaker cabinet resonates at its characteristic modes, and the feedback between speaker and pickup produces the sustained oscillation that is a primary noise rock texture. Like the cobble beach's uprush-maximum-backwash-settlement sequence, the noise rock sound is a multi-stage process rather than a single event. **The same** multi-phase-acoustic-sequence structural principle organizes both halves of degree 252. **Each side** demonstrates that characteristic sounds emerge from multi-stage processes rather than from single-event impulses, and the **paired coordinate** converts the individual multi-phase observations into a multiplicative statement about how the engine's acoustic vocabulary tracks process structure as a first-class documentation element.

## Part B: Tidal Surge on Cobble Beach — The First Lithophonic Sound, the Abiotic Arc Closure, and the Coast Salish Cultural Landscape

- **The Four-Phase Acoustic Sequence — Uprush, Maximum, Backwash, Settlement:** The signature cobble beach sound is not a single acoustic event but a four-phase sequence repeating with each tidal wave cycle. **Wave approach (uprush):** the wave advances up the beach picking up stones and pushing them forward; the sound is a rushing, hissing, waterfall-like sound as water floods already-wet stones. **Maximum uprush:** at the moment of peak wave height on the beach, stones are in turbulent suspension colliding in all directions; the sound is high-amplitude, complex, broadband percussion. **Backwash:** as the wave retreats, it drags cobbles downslope while gravity and the next incoming wave resist; the retreating-water clatter is the signature cobble-beach sound — a sustained rhythmic clatter as thousands of stones slide over each other, the identifier that marks the beach as PNW rather than Atlantic. **Inter-wave settlement:** between waves, the beach returns to relative stillness as stones come to rest against each other; individual stone-to-stone contact sounds are audible in this pause. The four-phase sequence repeats at 5-15 seconds per cycle, establishing the rhythmic pulse of the PNW coast.
- **Deception Pass — 4 Million Visitors and 8-9 Knot Tidal Currents:** **Deception Pass State Park**, straddling the narrow strait between **Whidbey Island** and **Fidalgo Island** in **Skagit County**, Washington, is the most-visited Washington state park at approximately 4 million visitors per year. The narrow strait opening creates dramatic tidal currents: at maximum ebb or flood, the current in the pass reaches 8-9 knots (approximately 10 mph) — among the strongest tidal currents in inner Puget Sound. The state park has cobble beaches on both the Puget Sound side and the Rosario Strait side. The tidal energy at the passage drives the cobble beach acoustic sequence to its peak amplitude during major tidal exchanges, and the park's accessibility means the signature sound is heard by more humans per year at Deception Pass than at any other PNW cobble beach location.
- **The San Juan Islands — 172-Island Cobble Beach Archipelago:** The **San Juan Islands** archipelago of 172 named islands sits at the confluence of **Puget Sound**, the **Strait of Georgia**, and **Rosario Strait**. The complex tidal flows through the islands create energetic tidal conditions at many island shorelines, and the islands have extensive cobble and gravel beaches shaped by the same tidal forces documented at Deception Pass. **San Juan Island**, **Orcas Island**, and **Lopez Island** all have accessible cobble beach locations with the characteristic sound. The archipelago represents the primary type locality for the PNW cobble beach soundscape and anchors the documentation in specific named places rather than in generic regional description.
- **Why PNW Beaches Are Stone, Not Sand — Geological Foundation:** The Pacific Northwest has very few sandy beaches compared to the East Coast or the South. This is a geological fact rather than a recreational oddity: sandy beaches require a large supply of fine-grained quartz sand derived from distant river systems and reworked by long-shore drift, a wave climate that sorts the sediment without removing it, and a specific beach geometry. The PNW has none of these conditions in most locations. The rocks that make up PNW cobble beaches are primarily volcanic (basalt, andesite) and metamorphic (greenstone, quartzite, schist) derived from the Cascade Range and the Olympic Mountains. These rock types produce angular to sub-rounded cobbles when broken down by wave action — not the rounded quartz spheres of a tropical beach but flatter, harder, more angular stones that produce a distinctly different sound. The absence of sandy beaches is not a scenic deficiency but a geological signature: the stone percussion exists because the geology produces cobble rather than sand.
- **Cobble Size Classification — 64 to 256 Millimeters:** Geologically, **cobble** refers to particles between 64 and 256 millimeters (approximately 2.5 to 10 inches). **Gravel** is smaller (2-64 mm). PNW beaches typically have a mix of gravel and cobble, with specific size distributions varying by beach exposure, wave energy, and source rock. High-energy beaches on the outer coast tend to have larger cobbles; protected embayments tend to have finer gravel. The size distribution determines the acoustic signature: larger stones at higher velocity produce lower-frequency higher-amplitude impacts; smaller stones at lower velocity produce higher-frequency lower-amplitude clicks. The collective sound is broadband precisely because the stone size distribution is broad.
- **Puget Sound Tidal System — Mixed Semidiurnal, 10-14 Foot Range:** **Puget Sound** has a **mixed semidiurnal tidal pattern**: two high tides and two low tides per day, with the two highs and two lows of unequal height. The tidal range at Seattle is 10-14 feet (3-4 meters) — substantial. On the outer coast, the tidal range is comparable. This large tidal range means the Sound experiences significant water movement twice daily, and the beaches are exposed and inundated on a 6-hour cycle. The rhythmic quality of cobble beach sound comes from the tidal cycle and from the wave periods (5-15 seconds) within each tidal exchange — the tidal beat of the PNW coast, as characteristic as a 4/4 rhythm is to rock music but rooted in physics rather than convention.
- **The Lithophonic Landscape — Stone Percussion as Oldest Instrument:** Sound produced by striking rocks — **lithophonic sound** — is among the oldest categories of human music. Lithophones (stone percussion instruments) predate metal instruments by tens of thousands of years in the archaeological record. The cobble beach is a natural lithophone where the wave energy is the player, the cobbles are the instrument, and the beach geometry determines the rhythm. The Pacific Northwest coast is a natural lithophonic landscape due to its volcanic and metamorphic bedrock source and the glaciation history that broke the rock into the size ranges that produce audible percussion when moved by wave action. The engine's documentation at degree 252 opens the lithophonic sound category as a first-class acoustic register.
- **Coast Salish, Lummi, Samish — Millennia of Cobble Beach Cultural Use:** The cobble beaches of the San Juan Islands and the northern Puget Sound have been used by **Coast Salish** peoples, the **Lummi Nation**, the **Samish Indian Nation**, and other Puget Sound first peoples for millennia. The gravel and cobble substrate is the specific environment in which **butter clam**, **littleneck clam**, and **geoduck** live, and the tidal cycle exposes the substrate twice daily for harvest. **Clamshell middens** — archaeological mounds of discarded shellfish shells and other cultural material — are found throughout the cobble beach zone with some middens thousands of years old, demonstrating continuous human use of cobble beach environments. The acoustic experience of cobble beaches is embedded in millennia of human activity in this place rather than being a novel recreational experience. For Coast Salish peoples, the cobble beach soundscape is the soundscape of the land they have always inhabited.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Round-number engine completion milestones carry documentary weight when they coincide with structural arc closures.** Degree 252's 70.0% engine completion milestone lands at the exact moment three multi-degree arcs close simultaneously. Future engine sequencing should target round-number completion points as arc-closure moments when possible, because the combination of round-number milestone + arc-closure is interpretively stronger than either phenomenon in isolation. The 70.0% threshold as a batch-closing, streak-closing, arc-closing moment is documented at higher structural resolution than a single-degree milestone would be.
2. **Commercial-institutional spectrum documentation requires all positions to be named, not only the commercially visible ones.** The 249-252 batch documents Sub Pop, Hollywood Records, Sub Pop, and self-released in sequence. Future batch-level documentation should explicitly name the institutional position of each entry so that the commercial spectrum can be read as a documentary element rather than being absent from the record. The engine's documentary function requires surfacing peripheral positions (self-released, underground) at documentary grade rather than treating them as lesser forms of the commercially visible positions.
3. **Sustained single-energy streaks document generational consistency as meaningful data.** The 249-252 E=7 streak documents the current Seattle rock generation's consistent energy across four genres and four institutional positions. Future sustained-energy streaks should be surfaced explicitly as documentary evidence of generational consistency rather than treated as coincidental sequences, because the streak register carries interpretive weight about scene-wide amplitude norms that single-entry energy placement does not capture.
4. **Multi-phase acoustic sequences require explicit phase-by-phase decomposition rather than single-event description.** The cobble beach sound is a four-phase sequence (uprush → maximum uprush → backwash → settlement); noise rock sound is a multi-stage process (pick attack → string vibration → distortion saturation → amplifier stages → speaker resonance → feedback oscillation). Future acoustic documentation of multi-phase sound sources should decompose the phase sequence explicitly rather than treating the source as a single-event impulse, because the multi-phase structure is constitutive of the characteristic sound rather than a minor elaboration on it.
5. **Backwash-as-signature identification requires understanding which phase of a multi-phase sequence is the identifier.** The signature cobble beach sound is the backwash clatter, not the uprush hiss or the maximum-uprush broadband percussion. Future multi-phase acoustic documentation should identify the specific phase that serves as the recognizable identifier, because the identifier phase is what makes the sound distinguishable in recordings and in memory rather than merely present.
6. **Natural lithophones are a first-class acoustic category parallel to biological vocalizations and hydrodynamic sounds.** Stone percussion under wave action is as constitutive of the PNW acoustic landscape as bird vocalizations or waterfall roar. Future SPS documentation should treat lithophonic landscapes as first-class categories rather than as subcategories of water or beach sound, because the acoustic mechanism (stone-on-stone impact under applied force) is distinct from either aeolian vibration or hydrodynamic flow.
7. **Geological explanations for landscape character are acoustically constitutive, not incidentally contextual.** The absence of sandy beaches in the PNW is geologically explained (volcanic/metamorphic bedrock, lack of quartz-sand supply) and acoustically consequential (stone percussion where other coasts have silent sand absorption). Future SPS documentation should surface geological explanations explicitly when they are acoustically constitutive, because the geology is not a backdrop to the sound — it is the reason the sound exists.
8. **Self-release as a philosophical position warrants documentation equal to major-label positioning.** The engine's documentation of No-Anchor at degree 252 treats self-release as a philosophical commitment with specific implications (ownership, artistic freedom, audience directness) rather than as a lesser institutional status. Future self-released-artist documentation should carry the same analytical depth as major-label-artist documentation, because the institutional position is a documentary element rather than a default condition, and the engine's documentary function requires treating philosophical choices at documentary grade rather than as absences of institutional support.
9. **Arc closures at threshold moments create structural resonance that arc closures at mid-sequence points do not.** The three arc closures at degree 252 (E=7 streak, meteorological/hydrological arc, abiotic soundscape arc) coincide with the 70.0% engine completion milestone. Future arc sequencing should consider threshold alignment when possible, because the closure-at-threshold structural resonance is interpretively stronger than closure-at-mid-sequence, and the engine's documentation can surface this resonance as documentary evidence of deliberate sequencing rather than coincidental alignment.
10. **Indigenous cultural landscapes embedded in millennia of use are constitutive contexts for abiotic acoustic phenomena.** The Coast Salish, Lummi, and Samish use of the San Juan Islands and northern Puget Sound cobble beach zone for butter clam and littleneck clam harvest across millennia (documented by clamshell middens) makes the cobble beach soundscape a cultural landscape rather than an unpeopled natural feature. Future abiotic-soundscape documentation should surface indigenous cultural landscapes explicitly when the acoustic phenomena occur within tribal homelands with archaeologically documented continuous use, because the cultural-landscape register documents a relational element that generic geographic documentation does not capture.
