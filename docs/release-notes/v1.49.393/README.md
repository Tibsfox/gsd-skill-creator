No-Anchor plays in the noise rock tradition: high-volume, dissonant, technically demanding, not commercially oriented. On the soundscape side, **Tidal Surge Cobble** closes the abiotic arc with **stone percussion** -- the sound produced when a tidal surge moves over a cobble beach, lifting and rolling the stones against each other and against the substrate. A cobble beach is a beach composed of rounded stones roughly 2-8 inches in diameter, formed by wave action over long periods. When a tidal surge reaches a cobble beach, the wave lifts the upper layer of stones, carries them forward, and as the wave retreats, the stones fall back against each other, producing a sustained clatter-and-rattle that is distinctly rhythmic but not metrically regular. Each stone has a slightly different mass and produces a slightly different pitch; the collective sound is a broadband percussion event.

The E=4 rating reflects the moderate energy of this sound -- it is louder than wind through spruce (E=4) but quieter than a roaring waterfall (E=7); it is rhythmic in the way that natural percussion systems are rhythmic, which is to say: regular enough to be musical, irregular enough to be geological. Puget Sound has extensive cobble beaches on its eastern and western shores, many of them accessible from parks and waterfront trails. The tidal surge cobble sound is one of the defining acoustic signatures of the Sound at low water -- the moment when the tide turns and the surge runs up the beach.
