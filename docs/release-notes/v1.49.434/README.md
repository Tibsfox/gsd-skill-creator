## Part B: Caspian Tern — Laridae Return at E=6, *Hydroprogne caspia*, World's Largest Tern, Estuary and Large Bay Specialist, East Sand Island Colonial Nesting, Raspy Descending *ka-arr* at 800-2,000 Hz Carrying 300-500 Meters

- ***Hydroprogne caspia* as Monotypic Genus for the World's Largest Tern.** The Caspian Tern is scientifically named *Hydroprogne caspia* — with *Hydroprogne* as the monotypic genus that distinguishes the species from all other terns (recent taxonomy sometimes merges it back into *Sterna* while the outlier-scale designation captures the bird's ecological position). The species holds a specific acoustic position in the Laridae family: the world's largest tern at any latitude, with wingspan 50-57 inches and body length 19-24 inches, exceeding the wingspan of many gulls. For scale: a Caspian Tern's wingspan exceeds that of a **Herring Gull** (**290** degree entry). The species was once classified in its own monotypic genus to distinguish it from all other terns, and the genus designation captures the bird's outlier size and outlier ecological role.
- **The Blood-Red Bill as Functional Tool for Aggressive Predation.** The Caspian Tern's most visually striking feature is the massive bright blood-red to orange-red bill with a dark subterminal spot near the tip. The bill is not merely colored for identification; it is a functional tool for the species' aggressive predation. Caspian Terns plunge-dive for fish from heights with greater impact force than smaller terns due to their size, and they pursue prey with force. The large bill enables capture of fish too large for smaller tern species and supports the species' kleptoparasitic feeding strategy: Caspian Terns take fish that smaller terns — including **Forster's Tern**, **Common Tern**, and other Laridae sharing the same estuarine habitat — have already caught. The kleptoparasitism places the Caspian Tern in direct competition with the smaller terns and shapes the acoustic environment at shared estuarine sites.
- **Black Cap and Gull-Like Presence in Mixed-Species Flocks.** The Caspian Tern in breeding plumage carries a full black cap extending from bill to nape, retained through much of the year rather than developing the streaked or mottled winter pattern of many smaller tern species. The solid black cap + blood-red bill field mark combination makes a perched or flying Caspian Tern unmistakable at any distance. Because of its size, the Caspian Tern is often associated with gulls in mixed-species flocks at estuary and coastal sites, and it flies with heavier more powerful wingbeats than the buoyant swallow-like flight of smaller terns. A Caspian Tern in flight reads as something between a gull and a tern, with the evolutionary logic of scale made visible in the wingbeat pattern and overall aerial presence.
- **The *ka-arr* Call: Lower Pitch, Longer Duration, Carrying Distance.** The Caspian Tern's vocalization is among the most recognizable in the Laridae family. The *ka-arr* — rendered in various field guides as "ka-arr," "kaar," "kraa," or "kraah" — is a loud low-pitched raspy emphatic single call approximately one second in duration with a characteristic descending rasp that begins at a higher frequency and drops in pitch over the syllable's duration, creating the raspy quality. The fundamental frequency is approximately 800-2,000 Hz, significantly lower than the Common Tern's *keerr* at 2,000-4,000 Hz, and the lower pitch combined with the larger body produces the powerful projecting quality that distinguishes the Caspian from other terns. The call carries 300-500 meters in calm conditions — significantly farther than smaller tern species — because the combination of large body, powerful syrinx, and lower-frequency vocalization produces a call that cuts through wind and ambient noise at estuarine sites.
- **East Sand Island Colony and PNW Colonial Nesting Geography.** The **East Sand Island** colony at the **Columbia River mouth** is the most significant PNW Caspian Tern nesting site and has been the subject of substantial ornithological study and conservation management due to the colony's impacts on juvenile salmonid survival (the Caspian Terns at East Sand Island forage on juvenile salmon migrating from the Columbia River to the Pacific). Additional PNW foraging sites include the **Nisqually River** delta, **Commencement Bay**, and the **Duwamish** mouth, where the combination of fish availability and open water suits the species' plunge-diving strategy. The Puget Sound presence is concentrated at estuary and large bay sites during summer breeding and peak foraging June-August, with presence on the PNW coast spring through fall and a small overwintering population along the Oregon-California coast.
- **Colony Vocal Behavior and Acoustic Density.** At colonial nesting sites Caspian Terns call continuously during active colony periods: partner recognition calls during incubation exchange, food-begging calls from chicks (high-pitched insistent), alarm calls during predator approach, and the steady ambient *ka-arr* of a colony going about its business. The overall acoustic character is lower-pitched and more raucous than Common Tern colonies at the same sites, and the Caspian colony acoustic register is structurally distinct from the Common Tern colony at degree 292 that closed the 289-292 Laridae arc at E=7. The engine's E=6 placement for the Caspian Tern registers the individual call rather than the full colony-event register, and the amplitude calibration preserves the distinction between single-call and colony-event modes at first-class resolution.
