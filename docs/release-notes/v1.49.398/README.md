On the soundscape side, **Juan de Fuca Surf** arrives at E=8 -- the second consecutive maximum SPS entry, and the second degree of the storm arc. The Strait of Juan de Fuca is the 96-mile waterway that connects the Pacific Ocean to the inland waters of Puget Sound and the Gulf of Georgia. Its western entrance, between Cape Flattery on the Olympic Peninsula and Vancouver Island's southern tip, faces the open North Pacific without obstruction. A storm system tracking into the coast generates swells that enter the strait and produce surf conditions along its southern shore -- the Clallam Bay to Port Angeles stretch -- that are among the most powerful in the Pacific Northwest's inland waters. The roar of Juan de Fuca surf under storm conditions is a broadband sound that covers the full audible spectrum: low-frequency concussion from the wave impact, mid-frequency turbulence in the run-up, high-frequency spray and foam at the wave lip.
