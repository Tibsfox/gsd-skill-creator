The biography of Jimi Hendrix at the Central District scale is the biography of the engine's most important geographic node. Johnny Allen Hendrix, later renamed James Marshall by his father Al, was born to a seventeen-year-old Seattle schoolgirl mother Lucille Jeter while his father was stationed at Fort Rucker, Alabama. Al's parents, Zenora and Bertram Philander Ross Hendrix, were traveling vaudeville performers whose troupe disbanded in Seattle in 1911 -- three decades of Hendrix roots in the city before Jimi was born. The Jackson Street corridor that had incubated Ray Charles from 1947 to 1950 and produced Quincy Jones in the same era was still the living artery of Black Seattle when young Jimmy was absorbing R&B and blues in the 1950s. The Black and Tan Club, the Rocking Chair, the Washington Social and Educational Club -- still operating. The same neighborhood that had already exported two of the most important musicians in American history was in parallel forming a third. The overlap is one of the engine's strongest findings. Quincy Jones at Garfield High School graduating approximately 1950. Jimi Hendrix at Garfield High School from 1959 to October 1960 (expelled for non-attendance; the only failing grades were in music). Luther Rabb at Garfield High School in the early 1960s. Hendrix and Rabb were born in the same year (1942), grew up in the same neighborhood, attended the same school, and played in their first band together: the Velvetones, with Hendrix on guitar, Rabb on tenor and baritone saxophone, Pernell Alexander on guitar, Walter Jones on drums, Robert Green on piano, performing at teen dances in recreation halls at area housing projects including Yesler Terrace because they were too young for Jackson Street nightclubs. Three engine degrees share Garfield High School (0, 32, 36). Three engine degrees share direct Hendrix-era Central District formation within the same decade. The most important educational institution in the engine is a single public school in the CD that produced, in parallel across two decades, the jazz-orchestral export, the bass-driven funk-rock foundation, and the rock-guitar supernova.

The blues education was not a curriculum. It was an environment. At age fifteen in mid-1958, Hendrix acquired his first acoustic guitar for five dollars; before that, he air-guitared on a broom. No formal instruction. Left-handed but playing right-handed guitars flipped upside down and restrung, a physical adaptation he would carry into every Fender Stratocaster for the rest of his life. His father Al's records were his teachers: B.B. King's butterfly vibrato, Louis Jordan's R&B swing, Muddy Waters' amplified Delta blues -- "I heard one of his old records when I was a little boy and it scared me to death." Also Howlin' Wolf, Lightnin' Hopkins, Albert King, Slim Harpo, Robert Johnson, Leadbelly, Elmore James. Listening before playing. Absorbing before imitating. When he joined the Rocking Kings in 1959-1961 and played the Spanish Castle on the highway between Seattle and Tacoma (later immortalized as "Spanish Castle Magic" on *Axis: Bold as Love* in 1967), the Birdland club, PTA dances, sock hops, after-football-game events, the Garfield Funfest, a photograph from Washington Hall on February 20, 1960, shows Hendrix in a powder-blue sports jacket holding his white Supro Ozark guitar, learning the trade the same way every blues musician before him had learned it: by watching, listening, and playing whenever anyone would let him. Then the U.S. Army, the 101st Airborne Division at Fort Campbell Kentucky in 1961 (caught riding in stolen cars, offered the military as alternative), discharge in 1962 after a reportedly broken ankle during a parachute jump, and the chitlin' circuit. The chitlin' circuit was the network of venues, clubs, and roadhouses across the American South and urban North that served Black audiences during the era of segregation -- a trade school and a hazing ritual and a conservatory all at once. Three sets a night, six nights a week. Backing Little Richard with the Upsetters (fired by Richard's brother for tardiness and stage antics). Recording "Testify" with the Isley Brothers in 1964. Playing behind Gorgeous George Odell, Curtis Knight and the Squires in New York, King Curtis session work. Every one of those gigs was rooted in the blues: Little Richard's R&B foundation was blues; the Isley Brothers' soul guitar parts were blues vocabulary; the circuit was, at its core, a blues institution. Cray had Tacoma's bar circuit; Hendrix had the entire Jim Crow South. Two PNW blues educations, two circuits, one shared vocabulary.

Albert King was the left-handed mirror -- the Memphis bluesman who played a right-handed Gibson Flying V flipped upside down and did NOT restring, so his bass strings ran nearest the floor and his gravity-assisted bends couldn't be replicated by any right-handed player. Hendrix called King his idol. Cited "Crosscut Saw" as a favorite. Listen to "Red House" -- one of the most sophisticated blues recordings in history, recorded during the *Are You Experienced* sessions in 1966-1967, slow blues in the key of B played as blues in A with the guitar tuned down a half step, standard twelve-bar progression, standard lyrics (man returns home, woman has left, key doesn't fit the lock) -- and you hear Albert King in the DNA: the scale of the bends, the deliberate placement of notes, the silence between phrases carrying as much meaning as the notes themselves. "Red House" was included on the UK release of *Are You Experienced* on Track Records but omitted from the initial US release on Reprise Records in 1967, because Reprise positioned Hendrix as a rock act and a slow blues didn't fit the "Purple Haze" and "Foxey Lady" marketing strategy. The blues was the part of Hendrix that his American label thought wouldn't sell. It took the posthumous compilation *Blues* (1994, MCA) to place "Red House" in its proper context. The same logic applied at higher volumes: feedback as the electronic equivalent of a field holler (a note that sustains indefinitely the way a blues singer holds a note beyond the instrument's limits); distortion as the "cry" -- the roughness in the voice of a Howlin' Wolf applied to the guitar signal; wah-wah as the most literal guitar-as-voice technique, directly emulating human vowel sounds. As Rolling Stone wrote: "Hendrix pioneered the use of the instrument as an electronic sound source. Players before him had experimented with feedback and distortion, but Hendrix turned those effects and others into a controlled, fluid vocabulary every bit as personal as the blues with which he began." Marshall Super Lead 100-watt heads connected to stacks of 4x12 cabinets, typically three stacks per side, cranked to maximum, frying vacuum tubes, cracking speakers. The loudest sound possible with late-1960s technology. Through the blues lens, this is not a departure from the tradition. It is the blues' final amplification: the technology finally catching up to the emotional scale of the music.

The paired side walks a parallel biography across the species boundary. *Calypte* from Ancient Greek *kaluptre* meaning "woman's veil" or "headdress," from *kalupto* meaning "to cover." John Gould erected the genus in 1856 for hummingbirds whose males have "not only the throat, but the entire head as glitteringly resplendent as if they had been dipped in molten metal." The iridescent crown and gorget that cover the male's head like a jeweled veil -- a headdress of structural color shifting from brilliant magenta-rose to dull black with viewing angle, the brilliance not pigment but physics: thin-film interference from melanosomes in feather barbules. *Anna* honors Anna de Belle Massena, Duchess of Rivoli, the aristocratic patron of French ornithology. Compare with Rufous at degree 35: *Selasphorus rufus* = "red flame-bearer," pure description. *Calypte anna* = "Anna's veiled one," person plus physics. The flame-bearer is named for what it looks like. The veiled one is named for who named it and for the physics of its plumage. One name is description. The other is social history -- colonial-era European naturalists naming New World birds after aristocratic patrons. Monotypic. No subspecies recognized. Unlike the Rufous, whose range is ancient (Alaska to Mexico, millions of years), Anna's current range is largely a 20th-century expansion from California and Baja California, beginning 1960s, first Seattle records approximately 1964, established resident in western Washington by the 1970s-1980s, current range year-round from Baja to British Columbia with regular occurrence in southeastern Alaska. Approximately 900-mile northward expansion in 60 years. The hummingbird did not evolve in Seattle. It chose to come.

The courtship dive is the species' signature acoustic performance. The male hovers 2-4 meters in front of a display object (female, person, hawk, or other hummingbird), climbs in a wavering ascent to approximately 40 meters (131 feet, higher than a thirteen-story building), folds his wings, plummets near-vertically at maximum speed 27.3 meters per second, and at the nadir spreads his tail feathers for approximately 60 milliseconds, producing an explosive chirp. He pulls approximately 9 g of centripetal acceleration at pullout. Entire dive: approximately 12 seconds. 385 body lengths per second is the highest known length-specific velocity attained by any vertebrate. Nearly twice the peregrine falcon's stoop relative to body length. About ten times faster than a fighter jet relative to fuselage length. Christopher Clark's 2008 research in *Proceedings of the Royal Society B* definitively demonstrated the sound mechanism: outermost tail feathers (R5) are modified in males -- narrower and stiffer than in females. R5 vibrates like a reed in a clarinet when exposed to airflow at dive speeds. Brief tail spread at maximum speed produces a loud, sharp chirp at approximately 4 kHz fundamental. Louder than the bird's vocal song. Likely too loud to be produced vocally by the small hummingbird syrinx. Mechanical sound amplified by dive speed -- faster dive, louder chirp. Compared to the Rufous J-dive at degree 35: Anna's climbs higher (40 m vs. 10-30 m), dives faster (385 BL/s vs. ~25 m/s), documented G-force (~9 g), specific feather mechanism (R5 reed vibration), specific frequency (4 kHz). The Rufous introduced the dive-display sonation. Anna's perfects it. The two hummingbirds, paired in parallel across consecutive degrees, demonstrate aeromechanical sound at ambient and at extreme scales.

Anna's learned song is a separate revolution hidden inside the same species. After thirty-five degrees in which vocal learning appeared only in oscine passerines (the "true songbirds" with enlarged higher vocal center brain structures), degree 36 presents the first non-passerine vocal learner. Anna's Hummingbird sings a thin, squeaky, buzzy series of high-pitched phrases lasting over ten seconds, interspersed with buzzes, chips, and scratchy warbling. Males learn songs by imitation. They practice subsong at 4-7 months. Plastic song follows. The song crystallizes by the onset of the next breeding season around November. Geographic dialects: neighbors share song types; populations separated by kilometers differ in syllable structure. Cultural transmission, not genetics. Males possess an enlarged higher vocal center developing in response to testosterone; females and non-singing hummingbirds (including the Rufous at degree 35) lack this brain structure. Among birds, vocal learning is documented in three groups: oscine passerines (~4,000 species), parrots (~400 species), and hummingbirds (~375 species). These three lineages evolved vocal learning independently -- convergent evolution of a complex neural and behavioral trait. Anna's is the best-studied hummingbird vocal learner. The engine's first non-passerine vocal learner: proof that song learning is not exclusive to songbirds, that the neural circuitry for vocal imitation can evolve independently in an entirely different avian lineage. Within the same family Trochilidae, the engine encounters both absence (Rufous, no complex song, innate chitter only) and presence (Anna's, complex learned song with dialects) of vocal learning. This parallels the oscine/suboscine split in passerines, but occurs between genera rather than suborders. Maximum contrast within minimum taxonomic distance -- the hummingbird pair's defining feature.

The conservation contrast between the two hummingbirds is the sharpest in the engine. Rufous Hummingbird: IUCN Near Threatened, approximately 65% decline since 1970s, rates accelerating nearly twofold from 2009 to 2019, State of the Birds 2022 "Tipping Point" species, closest to potential Endangered listing if current trends continue. Anna's Hummingbird: IUCN Least Concern, population increasing approximately 2% per year since 1966, population approximately 9.6 million and growing, range expanding northward. Two closely related species, same family, opposite population trajectories. Rufous declining because natural habitat (wildflower meadows) is degrading through fire suppression and climate-driven phenological mismatch. Anna's increasing because adopted habitat (urban gardens, feeders) is expanding through ornamental horticulture and milder winters. The migrant is losing the flowers it follows. The resident is gaining the feeders that sustain it. Not a simple success story -- Anna's dependence on human-provided resources creates fragility: a population crash could follow if feeder culture declines, if ornamental palettes shift, or if a severe winter exceeds the torpor threshold. The bird has bet its range on humans continuing to plant flowers and hang feeders. So far, the bet is paying off. The engine's most instructive species pair makes an ecological argument without editorial comment: specialists depending on nature are losing in parallel to generalists depending on humans, and the difference is measured in millions of birds per percent per year.

- **The first guitar and the blues education -- five dollars, a broom, Muddy Waters on the turntable.** In mid-1958, at age fifteen, Hendrix acquired his first acoustic guitar for five dollars. Before that: air guitar on a broom. No formal instruction. Left-handed, he played right-handed guitars flipped upside down and restrung. His father Al had records by B.B. King, Louis Jordan, Muddy Waters. The first guitarist Hendrix was aware of was Muddy Waters: "I heard one of his old records when I was a little boy and it scared me to death." Also Howlin' Wolf, Lightnin' Hopkins, Albert King, Slim Harpo, Robert Johnson, Leadbelly, Elmore James.

- **Leaving Seattle in 1961 -- Army enlistment, 101st Airborne, the Export at maximum velocity.** Enlisted in the U.S. Army, assigned to the 101st Airborne Division at Fort Campbell, Kentucky. Partly pragmatic -- caught twice riding in stolen cars, authorities offered the military as alternative. Discharged 1962, reportedly after breaking his ankle during a parachute jump. Never returned to Seattle to live. The city that formed him became the city he left behind. The engine's most dramatic instance of the Export pattern (degree 0, Quincy Jones). Jones left for a national career. Charles left for Los Angeles. Hendrix left for the Army, then Nashville, then the chitlin' circuit, then New York, then London, then the world. Died in London on September 18, 1970, age twenty-seven. Buried in Renton, Washington.

- **The chitlin' circuit 1962-1966 -- blues education that formal training could not provide, Little Richard, Isley Brothers, Curtis Knight, King Curtis.** The network of venues, clubs, and roadhouses across the American South and urban North that served Black audiences during the era of segregation. A trade school, a hazing ritual, and a conservatory all at once. Three sets a night, six nights a week. Hendrix backed Little Richard (the Upsetters, fired by Richard's brother), the Isley Brothers (recorded "Testify" 1964), Gorgeous George Odell, Curtis Knight and the Squires (New York), King Curtis (session work). Every one of these gigs was rooted in the blues. What Hendrix learned was not just repertoire but discipline: read a room, support a vocalist, play rhythm guitar with precision, absorb the entire vocabulary of the blues guitar tradition.

- **Albert King as left-handed mirror -- Crosscut Saw, the DNA of Red House.** Of all the blues musicians Hendrix encountered on the circuit, Albert King made the deepest impression. King was a left-handed guitarist who played a right-handed Gibson Flying V flipped upside down but DID NOT restring -- strings in reverse order, bass strings nearest the floor, gravity-assisted bends no right-handed player could replicate. Hendrix called Albert King his idol. Cited "Crosscut Saw" as a favorite. The connection runs deeper than admiration: both left-handed guitarists who turned the physical act of playing into something visually and sonically distinct.

- **"Red House" as pure blues -- the skeleton key to Hendrix as a blues musician, omitted from the US Reprise release because a slow blues didn't fit the rock marketing.** Recorded during the *Are You Experienced* sessions (1966-1967). Slow blues in the key of B (played as blues in A with guitar tuned down a half step). Standard twelve-bar progression. The only track on *Are You Experienced* that a Delta blues musician transported forward in time would recognize completely. Standard blues lyrics. Guitar work: pure Albert King, B.B. King, Muddy Waters filtered through Hendrix's hands. Included on the UK release (Track Records, 1967) but OMITTED from the initial US release (Reprise Records, 1967). The blues was the part of Hendrix that his American label thought wouldn't sell.

- **"Voodoo Chile" as electrified Delta blues -- fifteen minutes of Muddy Waters' "Rollin' Stone" in the psychedelic era.** The fifteen-minute jam (not "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)"). Recorded May 2, 1968, for *Electric Ladyland* (Reprise, 1968). Based on Muddy Waters' "Rollin' Stone" (1950) -- the same record that named the Rolling Stones, the same record that terrified the young Jimi on his father's turntable. Slow blues in E. Steve Winwood on organ, Jack Casady (Jefferson Airplane) on bass. Fifteen-minute improvisation in the Delta tradition.

- **"Machine Gun" as blues-protest -- Band of Gypsys at the Fillmore East January 1 1970, feedback as gunfire, wah-wah as air-raid sirens.** The apex of Hendrix's blues-as-protest work. Nearly twelve minutes. Opening dedication: "I'd like to dedicate this to all the soldiers fighting in Chicago, Milwaukee, and New York... oh yes, and all the soldiers fighting in Vietnam." Feedback simulates gunfire. Wah-wah creates wailing air-raid sirens. Volume reproduces the overwhelming sensory assault of combat. The underlying structure: blues. Bent notes, call-and-response, emotional arc from tension to release to devastation.

- **Band of Gypsys as blues-funk culmination -- Billy Cox, Buddy Miles, Fillmore East, the direction Hendrix was heading before London took him.** Hendrix on guitar, Billy Cox on bass, Buddy Miles on drums. Four concerts at the Fillmore East (December 31, 1969 and January 1, 1970). The *Band of Gypsys* album (Capitol Records, 1970 -- on Capitol due to contractual obligation, not Reprise). A blues-funk synthesis integrating James Brown's rhythmic complexity with Delta harmonic language and Experience-era textural experimentation. Scalding cover of Elmore James' "Bleeding Heart." Albert King's "Born Under a Bad Sign" as instrumental jam.

- **Stratocaster as blues instrument -- left-handed restrung, thumb fretting from Albert King, tremolo bar as blues expression, "Little Wing".** First Fender Stratocaster acquired 1966, a used ~1964 model. The left-handed restrung configuration gave the lowest string brighter sound than intended, highest string darker. Rejected standard barre chords in favor of fretting low sixth-string roots with his thumb -- a technique from the blues tradition (Albert King, Otis Rush). Opening bars of "Little Wing" are the most famous demonstration. The tremolo bar became a blues-expression device.

- **Feedback, distortion, and wah-wah as blues expression -- extensions of the guitar-as-voice project from Robert Johnson to B.B. King to Hendrix.** Through the blues lens, Hendrix's innovations are not rock innovations but extensions of the blues guitarist's eternal project of making the guitar sound like a human voice. Feedback as the electronic equivalent of a field holler. Distortion as the "cry" -- roughness in the voice of a Howlin' Wolf applied to the guitar signal. Wah-wah as the most literal guitar-as-voice technique.

- **Marshall stacks -- volume as blues amplification, the blues' final amplification, technology catching up to emotional scale.** Marshall Super Lead 100-watt heads connected to stacks of speaker cabinets, typically three stacks per side, each two 4x12 cabinets. The loudest sound possible with late-1960s technology. Cranked to maximum, frying vacuum tubes, cracking speakers. Through the blues lens, volume is the amplification of emotion.

- **E=6 first time above five -- the ceiling rises, transformative tidal, energy distribution now 2:13:6:11:4:1.** The engine has operated with an energy ceiling of E=5 for thirty-five degrees. At degree 36, E=6. First time. Not gradual. The scale expanded in a single step. E=5 was defined through four modes: personal tidal (32, Rabb), scene-cumulative tidal (33, English), collective tidal (34, The Tempos), breakthrough tidal (35, Cray). E=6 exceeds all four simultaneously. Hendrix was not merely a musician with a long career, or a product of a high-energy scene, or a member of a powerful group, or an artist who broke through from local to global. He was all of these AND he transformed the physical possibilities of the instrument, the amplifier, the stage show, and the audience's sensory experience.

- **Three studio albums on Reprise Records -- Frank Sinatra's label, the American major-label machinery, increasing blues depth.** *Are You Experienced* (1967, Reprise US / Track UK), *Axis: Bold as Love* (1967, Reprise), *Electric Ladyland* (1968, Reprise). Arc of increasing blues depth: *Experienced* most pop-oriented; *Axis* weaves blues into psychedelic textures; *Electric Ladyland* is fundamentally a blues record dressed in studio experimentation. *Band of Gypsys* (1970) released on Capitol due to contractual obligation. Reprise enters the engine for the first time. Founded by Sinatra 1960, acquired by Warner Bros. 1963.

- **Export pattern amplified -- Seattle claims him anyway, MoPOP, the statue, Jimi Hendrix Park, the gravesite.** Despite Hendrix's absence from Seattle during his entire professional career, the city claimed him comprehensively. Museum of Pop Culture (MoPOP) founded by Paul Allen in 2000 as Experience Music Project, began as a Hendrix museum -- 6,000+ artifacts, central Sky Church named in homage. Bronze statue on Capitol Hill (Broadway and Pine, 1997). Jimi Hendrix Park in the Central District. Northwest African American Museum exhibit: "Bold As Love: Jimi Hendrix at Home." Gravesite at Greenwood Memorial Park in Renton.

- **The Supernova -- thirty-seventh artist-city pattern, intense formation, total departure, compressed career, premature death, posthumous reclamation.** The pattern describes an artist whose formation in a city is intense but brief (CD years, 1942-1961), whose departure is total (never returns), whose career burns at maximum intensity for a compressed period (1966-1970, four years of global fame), and whose death is premature and final (age 27, London). The city claims the artist posthumously -- museum, park, statue, tourism. Differs from The Export (0, Jones: long sixty-year career), The Incubation (30, Charles: transit point), The Deep Root (35, Cray: never leaves the PNW).

- **The controlled scream -- thirty-seventh acoustic element, consecutive Stratocaster pairing with Cray's stinging clean, same instrument opposite philosophy.** The sound of a Fender Stratocaster through Marshall amplifiers at maximum volume, with feedback, distortion, and wah-wah as deliberate timbral choices. Same instrument as Robert Cray's stinging clean at degree 35. Opposite approach. The stinging clean says the blues can be powerful without being loud. The controlled scream says the blues WAS ALWAYS THIS LOUD.

- **The screaming bend -- thirty-sixth blue note deployment, blue note as physical force, feedback-sustained, wah-wah modulated.** The blue note played through overdriven amplification at maximum volume, where the pitch bend is not merely heard but physically felt through speaker vibration, room resonance, and electromagnetic interaction between pickups and amplifier speakers. Extends from the articulate sting (degree 35, Cray) into a hurricane.

- **Death September 18 1970 London age 27 -- the Samarkand Hotel, asphyxia after aspiration, nine Vesparax tablets.** Open verdict. Death by asphyxia after aspiration of vomit while intoxicated with barbiturates. Nine Vesparax sleeping tablets, eighteen times the recommended dosage. Whether intentional or accidental has been debated for over fifty years. The Central District's most famous son never came home. Eight years from the chitlin' circuit to the grave.

## Part B: Anna's Hummingbird -- The Sonic Boom

Full deep research covering Anna's Hummingbird as the second consecutive Trochilidae, completing the hummingbird pair (Rufous migrant/declining vs. Anna's resident/expanding), introducing the sonic boom as the thirty-seventh acoustic element, documenting the first non-passerine vocal learner in the engine, the first explicitly urban ecosystem, the fastest vertebrate by body-length metric, the R5 tail-feather sonation mechanism, learned song with geographic dialects, Least Concern conservation status as mirror opposite of Rufous's Near Threatened, and year-round residency as the PNW's only winter hummingbird.

- **Calypte anna "Anna's veiled one," Lesson 1829, Gould 1856, named for physics and patronage not description.** *Calypte* from Ancient Greek *kaluptre* ("woman's veil" or "headdress"), from *kalupto* ("to cover"). John Gould erected the genus in 1856 for hummingbirds whose males have "not only the throat, but the entire head as glitteringly resplendent as if they had been dipped in molten metal." *Anna* honors Anna de Belle Massena, Duchess of Rivoli. Compare with Rufous: *Selasphorus rufus* = "red flame-bearer" (pure description). *Calypte anna* = "Anna's veiled one" (person + physics).

- **Monotypic, Calypte genus -- two species (C. anna and C. costae), both relatively sedentary, both with elaborate iridescent crowns, both sing learned songs.** No subspecies recognized. Unlike the Rufous whose range is ancient (Alaska to Mexico, millions of years), Anna's current range is largely a 20th-century expansion. *Calypte* contrasts with *Selasphorus*: flame-bearers are migrant, veiled ones are resident. *Calypte* males sing complex, learned songs -- unusual for hummingbirds.

- **The hummingbird pair -- fourth species pair in the engine, Rufous (35) vs. Anna's (36), maximum contrast within minimum taxonomic distance.** Same family (Trochilidae), different genus (*Selasphorus* vs. *Calypte*). The hummingbird pair explores temporal strategy: Rufous arrives for the summer, defends flower meadows, and leaves. Anna's never leaves. It is the only hummingbird that stays through the PNW winter. The Rufous follows the bloom. Anna's waits for the bloom to return.

- **The metallic chip -- vocal call, sharp tsik, 6-8 kHz, universal contact note.** The most frequently heard vocalization. A single hard, high-pitched metallic *chip* given during foraging, perching, chasing intruders, and as a contact call. More versatile than the Rufous's buzzy chitter. Both sexes give it. Where the Rufous gives rapid series ("ch-ch-ch-ch-ch"), Anna's gives isolated crisp chips.

- **The learned song -- first non-passerine vocal learner in the engine, convergent evolution of vocal learning with songbirds and parrots.** Anna's Hummingbird sings a thin, squeaky, buzzy series of high-pitched phrases lasting over ten seconds. Males learn songs by imitation, practicing subsong at 4-7 months, crystallizing by onset of next breeding season (~November). Geographic dialects: neighbors share song types, populations separated by kilometers differ in syllable structure. Males possess an enlarged higher vocal center (HVC). Among birds, vocal learning is documented in three groups: oscine passerines, parrots, hummingbirds -- three lineages evolved it independently.

- **The dive display buzz as sonic boom -- 40-meter plunge, 385 body lengths per second, R5 tail-feather reed vibration, loudest sound relative to body size.** The male performs a courtship display: hovers 2-4 m in front of display object, climbs in wavering ascent to ~40 m, folds wings, plummets near-vertically at 27.3 m/s = 385 body lengths per second. At the nadir, spreads tail feathers for ~60 ms, producing an explosive chirp at ~4 kHz. Pulls ~9 g of centripetal acceleration at pullout. 385 body lengths per second is the highest known length-specific velocity of any vertebrate -- nearly twice the peregrine falcon's stoop, about ten times a fighter jet relative to fuselage length. Clark 2008 demonstrated R5 feather as the sound mechanism.

- **Urban/coastal -- engine's first explicitly urban ecosystem, human-defined habitat, bird that colonized cities within living memory.** The first explicitly urban ecosystem in 36 degrees of old-growth, mountain conifer, riparian, mixed woodland, sky, cone crop, flower meadow. Anna's is an urban adapter: residential neighborhoods, urban parks, garden landscapes, ornamental plantings. Nests on porch lights, wind chimes, hanging baskets, Christmas wreaths, electrical wires. Food: nectar from ornamental flowers (winter-blooming *Mahonia*, rosemary, *Grevillea*, winter jasmine), sugar-water feeders, tree sap, small insects. The ecosystem exists because humans built it.

- **The year-round resident -- only hummingbird staying through the PNW winter, northernmost year-round resident hummingbird in the world.** Every other PNW hummingbird migrates: Rufous departs July-September for Mexico, Calliope August-September, Black-chinned east of Cascades. Anna's is the ONLY year-round resident. Northernmost year-round hummingbird in the world. Remarkable for a 4-gram bird facing the highest metabolic rate by mass of any bird.

- **Torpor -- nightly survival, body temperature 40C to 8.8C, heart rate 1,200 to 50 bpm, metabolic rate drops 95%, the price of year-round residence.** Nightly during winter, routine not occasional. Body temperature drops from ~40C to as low as 8.8C. Heart rate from ~1,200 bpm to ~50 bpm. Breathing from 245/min to fewer than 10. Metabolic rate drops by 95%. The Rufous enters torpor on cool summer nights as routine metabolic strategy. Anna's enters torpor on freezing winter nights as survival imperative.

- **Range expansion -- 900 miles northward in 60 years, 20th-century phenomenon enabled by human landscaping and feeders.** Historical range: California and Baja California. Expansion: beginning 1960s, northward into Oregon, Washington, British Columbia. First Seattle records approximately 1964. By 1970s-1980s, established resident in western Washington. Drivers: ornamental plantings (winter-blooming species), hummingbird feeders (sugar-water maintained year-round), climate moderation, urban heat islands.

- **Conservation -- Least Concern, population increasing ~2%/year, ~9.6 million, mirror opposite of Rufous.** IUCN Least Concern. Population trend increasing approximately 2% per year since 1966. Population approximately 9.6 million and growing. The sharpest conservation contrast in the engine: two closely related species, same family, opposite population trajectories. Rufous declining because natural habitat (wildflower meadows) is degrading. Anna's increasing because adopted habitat (urban gardens, feeders) is expanding.

- **The sonic boom -- thirty-seventh acoustic element, brief explosive mechanically generated sound at extreme airflow speed, loudest sound relative to body size.** After the thrum (degree 35), Anna's introduces the sonic boom: a brief, explosive, mechanically generated sound produced by tail feathers vibrating at extreme airflow speed during a courtship dive, the loudest sound relative to body size in the vertebrate world, a single chirp concentrating all the energy of a 40-meter plunge into 60 milliseconds. The thrum is continuous. The sonic boom is instantaneous.

- **The R5 tail-feather mechanism connected the thrum and the sonic boom as two scales of the same phenomenon.** Clark 2008 in *Proceedings of the Royal Society B* supplied the aeromechanical sound mechanism with explicit feather physics (outermost rectrix, narrower and stiffer in males, vibrates like a clarinet reed at dive-speed airflow). The Rufous wingbeat thrum and the Anna's dive chirp are both aeromechanical sound but at opposite scales. The category is no longer a single phenomenon. It has sub-structure.

### What Could Be Better

- **The Hendrix death passage risked sensationalism even through the blues lens.** The Samarkand Hotel, the nine Vesparax tablets, the open verdict -- these facts are documented but carry a narrative pull toward the sensational. The research leaned on factual reporting and cultural context (posthumous reclamation, Renton gravesite, MoPOP) to counterbalance, but future degrees covering premature deaths should codify the balance explicitly.

- **The transition from the blues-rock genre mapping to the rock-era psychedelic legacy was compressed.** Blues Rock as Genre Stage 18 is a clean mapping, but *Axis: Bold as Love* and *Electric Ladyland* contain blues, rock, R&B, and psychedelia in variable proportions that the single-label mapping flattens. - **Anna's range-expansion documentation is northward-focused; the southern and inland range history is thinner.** The 900-mile northward expansion is well documented. The inland expansion across Arizona, New Mexico, and Texas from the 1980s onward is less well covered in the research. Future hummingbird range studies should trace all expansion vectors.

- **The 385 body lengths per second metric is technically correct but rhetorically dominant.** The metric deserves inclusion but risks overwhelming the aeromechanical sound mechanism, which is the more important engine contribution. Future peak-velocity claims should be paired with mechanism claims to keep the reader's attention on the engine's acoustic taxonomy rather than on the headline number.

- **The CD concentration pattern (nine degrees of 37) raises a geographic-balance question the engine has not yet addressed.** Nine CD degrees suggest the engine's geographic vocabulary remains weighted toward a single neighborhood. The Tacoma expansion at 35 and future PNW cities (Olympia, Bellingham, Everett) will rebalance, but the pattern should be tracked explicitly, not buried in the cumulative statistics.

## Lessons Learned

- **Editorial lenses in the CSV are analytical constraints, not editorial suggestions.** The "(blues context)" specification produced methodology, not merely a framing. Degree 36 research followed the lens rigorously, and the connections to prior degrees (0, 30, 32, 35) surfaced organically from the constraint. Future degrees with CSV editorial lenses should treat them as first-class inputs, not as optional flavor.

- **The energy scale expands at landmark degrees, not at drift.** E=1 through E=5 covered thirty-five degrees of musical energy. E=6 arrives at the degree that also breaks three other ceilings simultaneously (genre fusion, urban ecosystem, non-passerine vocal learning). The engine's dynamic range grows at concurrence points, not at incremental milestones. When multiple firsts co-occur, expect the energy scale to expand with them.

- **Fusion genres arrive after roots, not before them.** Blues entered at 35 (Genre Stage 17). Blues Rock enters at 36 (Genre Stage 18). The derivative mapping preceded the root for thirty-four degrees (R&B, Soul, Neo-Soul); the root arrived once; then the fusion arrived the very next degree. Future fusion genres should be watched for the degree after their root genre arrives, not long after.

- **Urban ecosystems are first-class even when they arrive late.** Thirty-five degrees of natural ecosystems preceded the urban/coastal arrival. Once urban/coastal enters, it cannot be retrofitted to earlier degrees. The engine should code ecosystem type as "natural" versus "human-modified" from degree 0, even when all early entries are natural, so later additions slot cleanly into the schema rather than creating a retroactive binary.

- **Convergent evolution of vocal learning is a structural finding the engine should track as an axis.** Oscine passerines, parrots, and hummingbirds independently evolved the same neural and behavioral trait. Anna's Hummingbird is the first non-passerine vocal learner in the engine. If a third non-oscine vocal learner enters in a future degree (e.g., a parrot in a migration context, though none occur in the PNW), the engine will have documented all three lineages. Track the HVC (higher vocal center) brain structure presence or absence as a taxonomic dimension.

- **The strongest direct artist-to-artist link (Hendrix-Rabb Velvetones) should not be the accidental byproduct of biographical research.** The engine should maintain an explicit "direct collaboration" index alongside the cross-references table. Degrees 32 and 36 share more than a neighborhood and a school: they share a literal band. Future degree-to-degree artist links should be surfaced as the same kind of first-class relationship as the S36/SPS cross-reference lines.

- **The hummingbird pair demonstrates that species pairs are most instructive when they maximize axis count, not depth per axis.** The engine's richest pair is not the one with the deepest single-axis contrast but the one with the broadest multi-axis contrast. Future species pair design should prioritize breadth (eight axes at moderate depth) over depth (one axis at extreme depth). The sum of contrasts is the engine's signal.

- **The combined-degree energy metric (E-sum across Part A and Part B) is worth tracking as a continuous variable.** Degree 36's combined energy of 11 (E=6 + E=5) exceeds the previous maximum of 10 (dual E=5 at degrees 32 and 35). The metric captures degree-level intensity across both sides of the paired architecture and should be ledgered alongside the individual artist-side and species-side distributions.

- **The Supernova pattern fills the vacancy between Export and Deep Root along the duration axis.** The Export (Jones, 60-year career) and the Deep Root (Cray, 50-year career) are long. The Incubation (Charles, transit point) is brief on the PNW side but followed by a 40-plus-year career. The Supernova (Hendrix, 8-year total professional career) is short on both sides. The taxonomy now has a compressed-career archetype explicit in its vocabulary, not implicit.

- **Precision-as-power is a cross-degree thread that should be named.** Cray's articulate sting at 35 and Hendrix's screaming bend at 36 occupy opposite ends of the same dynamic-precision axis (clean-tone micro-tonal exposure versus overdriven-tone physical-force). Both are precision. Both are power. The engine should name the thread explicitly rather than observing it ad hoc at each Stratocaster degree.
