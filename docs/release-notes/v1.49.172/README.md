### Part A: Jimi Hendrix (Blues Context) -- THE MOST FAMOUS MUSICIAN FROM SEATTLE (E=6 First Above Five, Blues Rock Genre Stage 18, The Supernova, The Controlled Scream, The Screaming Bend, Reprise Records New Label, Central District Returns, Garfield HS Thread x3, Velvetones-Rabb Direct Link)

Full deep research covering Jimi Hendrix through the blues lens specified by the CSV, documenting the blues foundation beneath the most recognized name in rock guitar history, the E=6 energy assignment that expands the engine's dynamic range for the first time, Blues Rock as the first fusion genre, the Supernova as the thirty-seventh artist-city pattern, the controlled scream as the thirty-seventh acoustic element, the screaming bend as the thirty-sixth blue note deployment, and the Central District's return as the engine's most important geographic node:

- **JIMI HENDRIX -- BORN NOVEMBER 27, 1942, KING COUNTY HOSPITAL, SEATTLE, THIRD-GENERATION SEATTLEITE, Central District Childhood, Broken Home, Music as Survival:** Johnny Allen Hendrix, later renamed James Marshall Hendrix by his father Al Hendrix. Mother Lucille Jeter was seventeen at his birth -- a Seattle schoolgirl. Father Al was stationed at Fort Rucker, Alabama, and did not meet his son until 1945. Al's parents, Zenora and Bertram Philander Ross Hendrix, were traveling vaudeville performers whose troupe disbanded in Seattle in 1911. The Hendrix lineage in Seattle stretches to the early twentieth century. Jimi was not a transplant. He was a third-generation Seattleite. Childhood defined by poverty, instability, and the Central District: constant moves through cheap hotels, boardinghouses, apartments. Rotating caregivers -- grandmother Clarice Jeter, Aunt Delores Hall, family friend Dorothy Harding. Parents divorced 1950. Mother Lucille died February 2, 1958, of a ruptured spleen caused by cirrhosis -- she was thirty-three. Jimi was fifteen. The Central District held all of it: the loss and the music. The Jackson Street corridor that had incubated Ray Charles (1947-1950) and produced Quincy Jones was still the living artery of Black Seattle when young Jimmy Hendrix was absorbing R&B and blues in the 1950s. The Black and Tan, the Rocking Chair, the Washington Social and Educational Club -- still operating. The same neighborhood that had already exported two of the most important musicians in American history was forming a third.

- **THE SAME NEIGHBORHOOD, THE SAME SCHOOLS -- Geographic Density of Central District Musical Production, Garfield High School Thread (0, 32, 36):** The overlap is one of the engine's most important findings. Quincy Jones at Garfield HS, graduated ~1950. Jimi Hendrix at Garfield HS, 1959-1960 (expelled October 1960 for non-attendance; only failing grades were in music). Luther Rabb at Garfield HS, early 1960s. Hendrix and Rabb were born in the same year (1942), grew up in the same neighborhood, attended the same school, and played in their first band together (the Velvetones). Three engine degrees share Garfield High School: 0, 32, 36. The most important educational institution in the engine -- a single public school in the Central District that produced musicians who reshaped jazz (Jones), bass-driven funk-rock (Rabb), and rock guitar (Hendrix). The school is a node. The neighborhood is a factory.

- **THE VELVETONES (c. 1958-1959) -- First Band, With Luther Rabb (Degree 32), Central District Teenagers, Yesler Terrace Housing Project Dances:** Hendrix on guitar, Luther Rabb on tenor and baritone saxophone, Pernell Alexander on guitar, Walter Jones on drums, Robert Green on piano. Too young for Jackson Street nightclubs, the Velvetones performed at teen dances in recreation halls at area housing projects, including Yesler Terrace. The Hendrix-Rabb connection is a direct cross-reference between degree 36 and degree 32 -- the strongest direct link in the engine. These were neighborhood kids growing up on the same blocks. Rabb would go on to found Ballin' Jack (signed to Columbia Records with Hendrix's help), join War, and tour with Santana. The Velvetones were the seed.

- **THE ROCKING KINGS (c. 1959-1961) AND THE SPANISH CASTLE -- Professional Gigs, Birdland Club, Spanish Castle Magic:** Hendrix joined the Rocking Kings, a more established outfit that played professionally at the Birdland club in Seattle and the Spanish Castle, a legendary roadhouse on the highway between Seattle and Tacoma (later immortalized in "Spanish Castle Magic" on *Axis: Bold as Love*, 1967). A photograph from Washington Hall, February 20, 1960, shows the Rocking Kings in performance: Hendrix wearing a white shirt, dark tie, peg-style pants, powder-blue sports jacket, holding his white Supro Ozark guitar. The Rocking Kings played PTA dances, sock hops, after-football-game events, the Garfield Funfest, and the club circuit. Between 1957 and 1961, Hendrix earned a local reputation for showing up at gigs and asking to sit in. Learning the trade the same way every blues musician before him had learned it: by watching, listening, and playing whenever anyone would let him.

- **THE FIRST GUITAR AND THE BLUES EDUCATION -- Five Dollars, A Broom, Muddy Waters on the Turntable:** In mid-1958, at age fifteen, Hendrix acquired his first acoustic guitar for five dollars. Before that: air guitar on a broom. No formal instruction. Learned by listening to his father's records, watching musicians at Jackson Street clubs, playing along with the radio. Left-handed, he played right-handed guitars flipped upside down and restrung. His father Al had records by B.B. King, Louis Jordan, Muddy Waters. The first guitarist Hendrix was aware of was Muddy Waters -- "I heard one of his old records when I was a little boy and it scared me to death." Also listened to Howlin' Wolf, Lightnin' Hopkins, Albert King, Slim Harpo, Robert Johnson, Leadbelly, Elmore James. These were his teachers before he had a guitar. They remained his touchstones after he became the most famous guitarist on the planet.

- **LEAVING SEATTLE (1961) -- Army Enlistment, 101st Airborne, The Export at Maximum Velocity:** Enlisted in the U.S. Army, assigned to the 101st Airborne Division at Fort Campbell, Kentucky. Partly pragmatic -- caught twice riding in stolen cars, authorities offered the military as alternative. Discharged 1962, reportedly after breaking his ankle during a parachute jump. Never returned to Seattle to live. The city that formed him became the city he left behind. This is the engine's most dramatic instance of the Export pattern (degree 0, Quincy Jones). Jones left for a national career. Charles left for Los Angeles. Hendrix left for the Army, then Nashville, then the chitlin' circuit, then New York, then London, then the world. Died in London on September 18, 1970, age twenty-seven. Buried in Renton, Washington -- in the Puget Sound region, but not in Seattle, not in the Central District. The Export was complete.

- **THE CHITLIN' CIRCUIT (1962-1966) -- The Blues Education That Formal Music Training Could Not Provide, Little Richard, Isley Brothers, Curtis Knight, King Curtis:** The chitlin' circuit was the network of venues, clubs, and roadhouses across the American South and urban North that served Black audiences during the era of segregation. A trade school, a hazing ritual, and a conservatory all at once. Three sets a night, six nights a week. Hendrix backed Little Richard (The Upsetters, fired by Richard's brother over tardiness and stage antics), the Isley Brothers (recorded "Testify" 1964, given freedom to stretch), Gorgeous George Odell, Curtis Knight and the Squires (New York), King Curtis (session work). Every one of these gigs was rooted in the blues. Little Richard's R&B foundation was blues. The Isley Brothers' soul guitar parts were blues vocabulary. The circuit was, at its core, a blues institution. What Hendrix learned was not just repertoire but discipline: read a room, support a vocalist, play rhythm guitar with precision, absorb through repetition the entire vocabulary of the blues guitar tradition -- bends, vibrato, call-and-response, turnarounds, twelve-bar structure, the pentatonic scale in all its variations. Then: Jimmy James and the Blue Flames in Greenwich Village. Discovered by Chas Chandler. London. The world.

- **THE ENCOUNTER WITH ALBERT KING -- Left-Handed Mirror, "Crosscut Saw," The DNA of "Red House":** Of all the blues musicians Hendrix encountered on the circuit, Albert King made the deepest impression. King was a left-handed guitarist who played a right-handed Gibson Flying V flipped upside down but DID NOT restring -- strings in reverse order, bass strings nearest the floor, giving him unique gravity-assisted bends that no right-handed player could replicate. Hendrix called Albert King his idol. Cited "Crosscut Saw" as a favorite. The connection runs deeper than admiration: both left-handed guitarists who turned the physical act of playing into something visually and sonically distinct. Listen to "Red House" -- one of the most sophisticated blues recordings in history -- and you hear Albert King in the DNA: the scale of the bends, the deliberate placement of notes, the way silence between phrases carries as much meaning as the notes themselves.

- **"RED HOUSE" -- PURE BLUES, The Skeleton Key to Understanding Hendrix as a Blues Musician:** Recorded during the *Are You Experienced* sessions (1966-1967). Slow blues in the key of B (played as blues in A with guitar tuned down a half step). Standard twelve-bar progression. The only track on *Are You Experienced* that a Delta blues musician transported forward in time would recognize completely. Standard blues lyrics: man returns home, woman has left, key doesn't fit the lock. Guitar work: pure Albert King, B.B. King, Muddy Waters filtered through Hendrix's hands. Included on the UK release (Track Records, 1967) but OMITTED from the initial US release (Reprise Records, 1967). Reprise positioned Hendrix as a rock act; a slow blues didn't fit the "Purple Haze" / "Foxey Lady" image. The blues was the part of Hendrix that his American label thought wouldn't sell. It took the posthumous compilation *Blues* (1994, MCA) to place "Red House" in its proper context.

- **"VOODOO CHILE" -- ELECTRIFIED DELTA BLUES, Fifteen Minutes of Muddy Waters in the Psychedelic Era:** The fifteen-minute jam (not "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)"). Recorded May 2, 1968, for *Electric Ladyland* (Reprise, 1968). Based on Muddy Waters' "Rollin' Stone" (1950) -- the same record that named the Rolling Stones, the same record that terrified the young Jimi on his father's turntable. Slow blues in E. Steve Winwood on organ, Jack Casady (Jefferson Airplane) on bass. Fifteen-minute improvisation in the Delta tradition -- long, hypnotic, repetitive, trance-inducing. Guitar overdriven but controlled, each note placed with surgical precision and emotional weight. This is the blues at its most expansive: the twelve-bar structure stretched across a quarter of an hour, with feedback, wah-wah, and volume as expressive tools WITHIN the blues vocabulary, not departures from it. "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)," recorded the next day, is the compressed four-minute detonation -- became No. 1 UK after his death.

- **"MACHINE GUN" -- THE BLUES AS POLITICAL STATEMENT, Band of Gypsys at the Fillmore East, January 1, 1970:** The apex of Hendrix's blues-as-protest work. Nearly twelve minutes. Opening dedication: "I'd like to dedicate this to all the soldiers fighting in Chicago, Milwaukee, and New York... oh yes, and all the soldiers fighting in Vietnam." Feedback simulates gunfire. Wah-wah creates wailing air-raid sirens. Volume reproduces the overwhelming sensory assault of combat. The underlying structure: blues. Bent notes, call-and-response, emotional arc from tension to release to devastation. "Machine Gun" is what happens when a blues guitarist with military experience turns the blues into a war narrative. Generally regarded as the *Band of Gypsys* album's highlight and one of Hendrix's greatest achievements.

- **"HEAR MY TRAIN A COMIN'" -- THE DELTA STANDARD, Acoustic Performance on Dick Cavett, The Blues Guitarist Beneath the Rock Star:** Hendrix's most direct engagement with the acoustic Delta tradition. The acoustic version (Dick Cavett Show, 1969) is critical: twelve-string acoustic guitar in open tuning, slide-like technique without an actual slide. No Marshall stacks, no wah-wah, no feedback. The performance strips away every electronic innovation and reveals the Delta blues guitarist underneath the rock star. Pure Robert Johnson, Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf imagery: waiting at the train station, going away, coming back. The music he learned from his father's records, performed with the raw emotional directness of the earliest Chess recordings.

- **BAND OF GYPSYS -- THE BLUES-FUNK STATEMENT, Hendrix's Most Explicitly Black Musical Project, Where He Was Heading Before London Took Him:** Hendrix on guitar, Billy Cox on bass, Buddy Miles on drums. Four concerts at the Fillmore East (December 31, 1969 and January 1, 1970). The *Band of Gypsys* album (Capitol Records, 1970 -- on Capitol due to contractual obligation, not Reprise). The clearest recorded evidence of where Hendrix was heading before his death: a blues-funk synthesis integrating James Brown's rhythmic complexity with Delta harmonic language and Experience-era textural experimentation. Scalding cover of Elmore James' "Bleeding Heart." Albert King's "Born Under a Bad Sign" as instrumental jam. Set lists heavy with blues material -- a deliberate artistic statement about roots and direction. Band of Gypsys was not a side project. It was a declaration of intent. Hendrix was going deeper into the blues, not further from it.

- **THE STRATOCASTER AS BLUES INSTRUMENT -- Left-Handed Restrung, Thumb Fretting from Albert King, Tremolo Bar as Blues Expression, "Little Wing":** First Fender Stratocaster acquired 1966, a used ~1964 model. The left-handed restrung configuration gave the lowest string brighter sound than intended, highest string darker. Rejected standard barre chords in favor of fretting low sixth-string roots with his thumb -- a technique from the blues tradition (Albert King, Otis Rush) that allowed sustaining bass notes while playing melody on treble strings. Opening bars of "Little Wing" are the most famous demonstration. The tremolo bar became a blues-expression device: deep pitch dives emulating a blues singer's cry. His vibrato was not decorative -- it was the blues expression of emotional pain applied to electric guitar at volume and intensity no acoustic blues instrument could achieve.

- **FEEDBACK, DISTORTION, AND WAH-WAH AS BLUES EXPRESSION -- Extensions of the Guitar-as-Voice Project That Runs from Robert Johnson to B.B. King to Hendrix:** Through the blues lens, Hendrix's innovations are not rock innovations but extensions of the blues guitarist's eternal project of making the guitar sound like a human voice. Johnson used slide. B.B. King used butterfly vibrato. Albert King used enormous bends. Hendrix added twentieth-century electronics: FEEDBACK as the electronic equivalent of a field holler (a note that sustains indefinitely, the way a blues singer holds a note beyond the instrument's limits). DISTORTION as the "cry" -- roughness in the voice of a Howlin' Wolf applied to the guitar signal. WAH-WAH as the most literal guitar-as-voice technique, directly emulating human vowel sounds. As Rolling Stone wrote: "Hendrix pioneered the use of the instrument as an electronic sound source. Players before him had experimented with feedback and distortion, but Hendrix turned those effects and others into a controlled, fluid vocabulary every bit as personal as the blues with which he began."

- **MARSHALL STACKS -- VOLUME AS BLUES AMPLIFICATION, The Blues' Final Amplification, Technology Catching Up to the Emotional Scale of the Music:** Marshall Super Lead 100-watt heads connected to stacks of speaker cabinets, typically three stacks per side, each two 4x12 cabinets. The loudest sound possible with late-1960s technology. Cranked to maximum, frying vacuum tubes, cracking speakers. Through the blues lens, volume is the amplification of emotion. Delta blues was quiet: acoustic instruments in small rooms. Chicago blues (Muddy Waters) was louder: amplified, in bars. Hendrix took the trajectory to its conclusion: the blues at the volume of a jet engine, Robert Johnson's emotional content delivered at physical intensity felt in your chest, your bones, your teeth. The Marshall stack was not a departure from the blues. It was the blues' final amplification -- the technology catching up to the emotional scale of the music.

Consecutive Stratocaster degrees: precision then detonation.

- **THE SCREAMING BEND -- THIRTY-SIXTH BLUE NOTE DEPLOYMENT, The Blue Note as Physical Force, Feedback-Sustained, Wah-Wah Modulated, Full-Body Sensory Experience:** The blue note played through overdriven amplification at maximum volume, where the pitch bend is not merely heard but physically felt through speaker vibration, room resonance, and electromagnetic interaction between pickups and amplifier speakers. Extends the thread from the articulate sting (degree 35, Cray) -- where every micro-tonal increment is audible in clean-tone clarity -- to the opposite extreme, where the bend is embedded in a wall of harmonic overtones, feedback, and distortion. The screaming bend follows the articulate sting. Where the articulate sting (degree 35, Cray) was the blue note under a magnifying glass -- every micro-tonal increment audible in clean-tone clarity, the bend exposed in surgical precision -- the screaming bend is the blue note in a hurricane. The pitch bend played through overdriven amplification at maximum volume, where every micro-tonal increment is still present but embedded in a wall of harmonic overtones, feedback, distortion, and wah-wah. The bend is not merely heard. It is physically felt -- through speaker vibration, room resonance, electromagnetic interaction between guitar pickups and amplifier speakers. Hendrix's bends on "Machine Gun," "Voodoo Chile," and "Red House" are canonical: begin with a note in the blues scale, pass through the blue-note zone, arrive at the target -- but saturated, sustained, modulated, delivered at a volume where the listener's bones resonate. The progression from the articulate sting to the screaming bend is the progression from microscope to earthquake. Same phenomenon. Same pitch-bend physics. Same micro-tonal blue-note zone. But the articulate sting lets you see every grain of the bend in clean-tone transparency. The screaming bend lets you FEEL every grain of the bend in your chest cavity. The ghost bend (33) was absent -- the blue note never recorded. The unison bend (34) was collective -- multiple players bending together. The articulate sting (35) was solitary and precise -- one clean guitar, maximum visibility. The screaming bend (36) is solitary and overwhelming -- one overdriven guitar, maximum force. Four consecutive deployments spanning the full spectrum of blue-note presence: invisible, collective, exposed, and detonated. Thirty-six deployments across thirty-seven degrees.
