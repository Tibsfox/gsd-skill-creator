## Mission Overview

Mars Global Surveyor is the obs#9 sustain of the MARS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA orbiter whose defining achievements are the first precise global topographic map of Mars, the discovery of an ancient crustal magnetic field and an extinct core dynamo, and the first global thermal-infrared survey of the planet's surface mineralogy. It was managed by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory and built by Lockheed Martin, and it was the first fully successful U.S./NASA Mars orbiter since the Viking orbiters — launched to recover the science lost when Mars Observer failed in 1993, carrying rebuilt copies of several Mars Observer instruments (the Soviet Phobos 2 reached Mars orbit in 1989 before failing, and Mars Observer never reached orbit, so MGS is scoped as the first fully successful U.S./NASA orbiter since Viking, not the first to orbit Mars — Mariner 9 owns first-to-orbit). An ORBITER — not a lander; it did not land or traverse — MGS carried MOC (the Mars Orbiter Camera, high-resolution and wide-angle, principal investigator M. Malin), MOLA (the Mars Orbiter Laser Altimeter, D. Smith), TES (the Thermal Emission Spectrometer, P. Christensen), and MAG/ER (the Magnetometer/Electron Reflectometer, M. Acuña), plus a radio-science investigation, a Mars Relay, and an accelerometer used during aerobraking. It launched on a Delta II 7925 from Cape Canaveral SLC-17A on 7 November 1996 (17:00 UTC) and entered Mars orbit on 12 September 1997 (the insertion burn began 01:17 UT on 12 September, 9:17 p.m. EDT on 11 September — the source of occasional "11 September" citations), then used aerobraking — repeatedly dipping into the upper atmosphere and using drag to shed energy — to shrink and circularize its orbit into a near-circular, Sun-synchronous mapping orbit (~370–450 km); a solar panel had bent backward early in aerobraking, forcing a gentler, longer plan, so systematic mapping began about 1999. The science is the shape and record of the whole planet: MOLA built the first precise global topographic map by laser ranging — quantifying the hemispheric dichotomy (smooth low northern plains versus cratered high southern highlands), the great depth of the Hellas basin (floor ~8 km below datum), and the heights of Olympus Mons (~22 km) and the Tharsis rise (MOLA reached roughly meter-to-decimeter radial precision; this is the first global TOPOGRAPHIC / elevation map, distinct from Mariner 9's first global IMAGE map). MAG/ER discovered strong bands of remanent ("fossil") crustal magnetism locked into the most ancient southern-highland crust — the record of a global magnetic field, generated by an early core dynamo, that switched off billions of years ago, leaving its imprint frozen in the oldest rocks (Mars has no global field today; the banding is sometimes read as a hint of early plate-tectonic-like crustal spreading, an interpretation). TES produced the first global thermal-infrared survey of the surface mineralogy and thermophysical properties and identified gray crystalline hematite at Meridiani Planum — the deposit that later guided a rover landing-site choice (a forward cross-link: MGS/TES 1999 preceded the Mars Exploration Rover Opportunity in 2004, not a re-claim of the rovers' in-situ mineral-water first). MOC imaged gullies on crater walls and slopes that suggested geologically recent liquid-water seepage and layered sedimentary rocks, and monitored the weather and dust storms over years. MGS operated for about ten years — the longest-serving Mars spacecraft of its era at the time — until contact was lost in November 2006; NASA ended recovery efforts in January 2007. ## Surprises

- **A laser altimeter measured the precise relief of a whole planet for the first time.** MGS's MOLA fired laser pulses at the surface and timed their return to measure elevations across all of Mars — the first precise global topographic map, quantifying the stark hemispheric dichotomy (smooth low northern plains versus cratered high southern highlands), the great depth of the Hellas basin, and the towering heights of Olympus Mons and the Tharsis rise, and giving the elevation reference that later missions use.
- **An ancient global magnetic field left its record frozen in the oldest crust.** MGS's MAG/ER found strong bands of remanent magnetism locked into the most ancient crust of the southern highlands — and because Mars has no global field today, those stripes are the record of a global magnetic field that once existed, generated by an early core dynamo that switched off billions of years ago, frozen into the oldest rocks.
- **A thermal spectrometer mapped the planet's minerals from orbit and pointed the way to a rover.** MGS's TES produced the first global thermal-IR survey of Mars's surface mineralogy and identified gray crystalline hematite at Meridiani Planum — the signpost that later guided the choice of Opportunity's landing site, a forward cross-link rather than a re-claim of the rovers' own in-situ water finding.
- **Gullies on the slopes suggested geologically recent water.** MGS's camera (MOC) imaged gullies on crater walls and slopes that were interpreted as evidence of geologically recent seepage of liquid water, alongside layered sedimentary rocks, while the orbiter also monitored the weather and dust storms of Mars over years.
- **A hawk reads the lay of the open land from high soaring flight.** The Ferruginous Hawk (*Buteo regalis*), the largest North American *Buteo*, a pale, broad-winged raptor of the open shrub-steppe and high desert of the interior West, hunts over wide open country from high soaring flight and from elevated perches, reading the lay of the open land below (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of MGS mapping the whole planet's surface from orbit.
- **A cushion plant marks the precise line of high elevation.** The Moss Campion (*Silene acaulis*), a low, dense alpine cushion plant of the highest, most exposed rocky ground across the Northern Hemisphere (including the high Cascades and Olympics of the Pacific Northwest), forms tight compact mats precisely zoned to the harsh high-elevation band where little else survives (ecological description only) — a mirror of MGS measuring the planet's precise relief and contours.

## Dedication

v1.246 Mars Global Surveyor is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's mapping orbiter and gave Mars its first precise relief. The Jet Propulsion Laboratory teams who managed the mission; the Lockheed Martin engineers who built the orbiter from a recovered design and steered it to Mars; the instrument teams who rebuilt the camera, the laser altimeter, the thermal spectrometer, and the magnetometer after the 1993 Mars Observer loss; the navigators who fired the engines into Mars orbit on a September night in 1997; and the aerobraking team who dipped the craft into the top of the atmosphere, pass after pass, to circle it into a Sun-synchronous mapping orbit. Year after year, a single craft circled high above the planet and read the shape of the whole world — the smooth low plains and the cratered highlands, the deep bowl of Hellas, the towering Olympus Mons — while the magnetometer found the frozen record of a global field switched off long ago and the spectrometer picked out the minerals below. It imaged gullies and layered rock and relayed data, until its long watch was done — a patient surveyor that mapped Mars in detail for the first time.
