## What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived mission imagery.** The MGS shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual mission data or MOLA topography. A future revision could render a real MOLA global elevation grid or a TES mineral map (publicly available from the Planetary Data System) for a higher-fidelity view.
- **The MGS diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the orbiter with its solar panels, its high-gain antenna, and the nadir-pointing instrument deck.
- **The relief-mapping mode is depicted rather than driven by real altimetry.** The topographic relief in the mapping mode is depicted but not rendered from the archived MOLA elevation grid; a future ship could drive the mode from the real gridded topography for a quantitatively faithful reading of the hemispheric dichotomy, Hellas, and Olympus Mons.
- **The fossil-magnetism mode is depicted rather than computed.** The remanent magnetic bands in the third mode are depicted rather than rendered from the published MAG/ER crustal-field maps; a future ship could drive the mode from the real measured magnetic-anomaly maps for a quantitatively faithful view.

## Surprises

- **A laser altimeter measured the precise relief of a whole planet for the first time.** MGS's MOLA fired laser pulses at the surface and timed their return to measure elevations across all of Mars — the first precise global topographic map, quantifying the stark hemispheric dichotomy (smooth low northern plains versus cratered high southern highlands), the great depth of the Hellas basin, and the towering heights of Olympus Mons and the Tharsis rise, and giving the elevation reference that later missions use.
- **An ancient global magnetic field left its record frozen in the oldest crust.** MGS's MAG/ER found strong bands of remanent magnetism locked into the most ancient crust of the southern highlands — and because Mars has no global field today, those stripes are the record of a global magnetic field that once existed, generated by an early core dynamo that switched off billions of years ago, frozen into the oldest rocks.
- **A thermal spectrometer mapped the planet's minerals from orbit and pointed the way to a rover.** MGS's TES produced the first global thermal-IR survey of Mars's surface mineralogy and identified gray crystalline hematite at Meridiani Planum — the signpost that later guided the choice of Opportunity's landing site, a forward cross-link rather than a re-claim of the rovers' own in-situ water finding.
- **Gullies on the slopes suggested geologically recent water.** MGS's camera (MOC) imaged gullies on crater walls and slopes that were interpreted as evidence of geologically recent seepage of liquid water, alongside layered sedimentary rocks, while the orbiter also monitored the weather and dust storms of Mars over years.
- **A hawk reads the lay of the open land from high soaring flight.** The Ferruginous Hawk (*Buteo regalis*), the largest North American *Buteo*, a pale, broad-winged raptor of the open shrub-steppe and high desert of the interior West, hunts over wide open country from high soaring flight and from elevated perches, reading the lay of the open land below (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of MGS mapping the whole planet's surface from orbit.
- **A cushion plant marks the precise line of high elevation.** The Moss Campion (*Silene acaulis*), a low, dense alpine cushion plant of the highest, most exposed rocky ground across the Northern Hemisphere (including the high Cascades and Olympics of the Pacific Northwest), forms tight compact mats precisely zoned to the harsh high-elevation band where little else survives (ecological description only) — a mirror of MGS measuring the planet's precise relief and contours.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A sustain keeps the predecessor's axis vocabulary and replaces only the predecessor-specific content.** MGS shares the Mars-exploration vocabulary with the MAVEN predecessor (Mars, the red planet, the surface, the atmosphere, dust, in situ, planetary exploration, crust, topography, mineralogy) — that vocabulary is shared and correct. The rewrite removed only the MAVEN-specific content (the MAVEN name, the Mars Scout Program, Bruce Jakosky, LASP, the upper-atmosphere / ionosphere / atmospheric-escape anchors, the solar-wind stripping, the persistent metal-ion layers, the aurora, SWIA/SWEA/STATIC/SEP/LPW/IUVS/NGIMS, Deep Dip, the Atlas V 401, the 2013/2014 dates, and the Violet-green Swallow + Showy Milkweed pairing) and replaced it with MGS content.
2. **A sustain has no axis rotation to frame — and 1.245 was itself a sustain, so there is no rotation framing to remove.** The Mars axis opened at 1.238 (Viking, substrate-axis rotation #36); 1.239 through 1.246 are all SUSTAINS, so the clone source carries no rotation narrative, and at most a single distant note records that the Mars axis itself opened at 1.238 by rotating from the X-ray-astronomy axis (rotation #35, held complete at obs#15).
3. **MGS is the first FULLY SUCCESSFUL U.S./NASA Mars orbiter since Viking — never "the first Mars orbiter."** Mariner 9 was the first spacecraft to orbit another planet, the Soviet Phobos 2 orbited Mars in 1989 before failing, and Mars Observer never reached orbit; the catalog scopes MGS as the first fully successful U.S./NASA orbiter since Viking (recovering the 1993 Mars Observer loss) and never re-claims Mariner 9's first-to-orbit.
4. **The topography first is the first global TOPOGRAPHIC / elevation map — distinct from Mariner 9's IMAGE map.** MOLA measured precise elevation and relief by laser ranging, quantifying the hemispheric dichotomy, the depth of Hellas, and the heights of Olympus Mons and Tharsis; the catalog keeps "topographic / elevation" and never writes "first global map of Mars," which belongs to Mariner 9's first global image map.
5. **The magnetism result is the discovery of an ancient, now-extinct global field — not a present-day field.** MAG/ER found remanent fossil banding in the ancient crust; the catalog scopes this as evidence of a global field once generated by an early core dynamo, now extinct, notes that Mars has no global field today, and presents the early-plate-tectonic-like reading as an interpretation where mentioned.
6. **The TES Meridiani-hematite note is a forward cross-link that GUIDED a rover landing site — not a re-stamp.** TES produced the first global thermal-IR mineral survey and found the Meridiani hematite; the catalog presents the hematite as the deposit that later guided the choice of Opportunity's landing site (MGS/TES 1999 preceded the Mars Exploration Rover Opportunity in 2004) and never re-claims the Mars Exploration Rovers' (obs#4) in-situ mineral-water-evidence first.
7. **Aerobraking is MGS's orbit-attainment technique, never an anchor.** MGS used aerobraking to shrink and circularize its orbit into the mapping orbit; the catalog mentions it only as the technique and never anchors "first aerobraking," because Magellan demonstrated aerobraking at Venus earlier.
8. **The mission's end is framed positively and mentioned once at low density.** MGS operated for about ten years — the longest-serving Mars spacecraft of its era at the time; contact was lost in November 2006 and NASA ended recovery efforts in January 2007. The catalog states this once and frames the mission positively as a long, successful mapping mission, with no failure / loss dwelling.
9. **A sustain references its predecessor exactly once, deliberately.** The only correct predecessor reference is a single nav-prev lineage note to v1.245 MAVEN (Mars obs#8), plus lineage references to InSight (1.244, obs#7), Perseverance (1.243, obs#6), Curiosity (1.242, obs#5), the Mars Exploration Rovers (1.241, obs#4), Mars Pathfinder (1.240, obs#3), Mariner 9 (1.239, obs#2), Viking (1.238, obs#1), and Mariner 4 (1.36, the first Mars flyby). The predecessors' anchors stay with them — 0 re-stamps.
10. **Clone source is MAVEN (a NASA upper-atmosphere orbiter, Mars Scout, 2013–2025); MGS is a NASA mapping orbiter (JPL / Lockheed Martin, 1996–2006).** The most structural replacement in the rewrite is removing ALL MAVEN-specific content — "MAVEN," "Mars Scout Program," "Bruce Jakosky," "LASP," the upper-atmosphere / ionosphere / atmospheric-escape anchors, the solar-wind stripping, the persistent metal-ion layers, the aurora, "SWIA/SWEA/STATIC/SEP/LPW/IUVS/NGIMS," "Deep Dip," "Atlas V 401," the 2013/2014 dates, and the Violet-green Swallow + Showy Milkweed pairing — and replacing it with Mars Global Surveyor, the recovery of the 1993 Mars Observer loss, the Delta II 7925 launch from Cape Canaveral SLC-17A (7 November 1996), the 12 September 1997 Mars orbit insertion, the aerobraking into the Sun-synchronous mapping orbit, the instruments MOC, MOLA, TES, and MAG/ER, the first precise global topographic map, the ancient crustal magnetism and extinct dynamo, the first global thermal-IR mineral survey and the Meridiani hematite, the gullies and layered rocks, the ~ten-year mission ending in November 2006, NSSDCA/COSPAR 1996-062A, and the Ferruginous Hawk + Moss Campion pairing.
