## Summary

**Heart** arrives at E=6 -- and with it, the **FIRST BELLEVUE BAND** in the Seattle 360 engine, the **FIRST CLASSIC ROCK entry** in the catalog, and the beginning of a two-entry Heart sequence that will run through degree 235. Ann and Nancy Wilson founded Heart in Vancouver, British Columbia, and relocated to the Seattle area, eventually becoming associated with Bellevue -- the east-side city across Lake Washington from Seattle, connected by the I-90 and SR-520 floating bridges, and as different from Capitol Hill as any two places in the same metropolitan area can be. Bellevue was the suburb, the money, the planned shopping centers and corporate campuses and school districts that Seattle families moved to when they wanted lawns; Capitol Hill was the renters, the artists, the bars open until 2 AM and the record stores on every corner. Heart is the **first Eastside band beyond Issaquah** in the engine (Modest Mouse entered at degree 199), and the gap -- 35 degrees -- documents how long the engine has been working through a Seattle-centric lens before the east side appears in its full commercial force. The **35 million records** figure is the engine's largest commercial footprint for a band to date: Dreamboat Annie (1975), Little Queen (1977), Dog & Butterfly (1978), Bebe le Strange (1980), the massive mid-1980s comeback with "What About Love," "Never," and "Alone." Ann Wilson's voice is one of the most technically accomplished in rock -- four-octave range, trained for both rock and ballad, capable of the full-throated power of "Barracuda" and the intimate restraint of "Crazy on You" -- and Nancy Wilson's acoustic and electric guitar work has always been underestimated by critics who kept reaching for the voice as the story. At E=6, this is the classic-era Heart entry: albums, arena tours, the catalog that sold 35 million copies before the 1985 comeback even happened. The E=6 reading captures Heart at their most balanced: powerful, accomplished, melodic, not straining for the maximum register that will characterize degree 235's hard rock entry. On the species side, the **American Kestrel** arrives at E=5 and closes the **FALCONIDAE ARC** at degree 234. *Falco sparverius* is North America's smallest falcon -- sparrow-sized, brilliantly colored in rufous and slate-blue, a bird that hunts from utility wires and fence posts with a hovering technique that is unlike any other North American falcon. Its *killy-killy-killy* call is rapid, repetitive, and high-pitched, a sound that carries across open fields and country roads. At E=5, the Kestrel's call is gentler than the Peregrine's E=7 staccato and the Prairie Falcon's E=6 alarm -- appropriate for the smallest member of the family. The Falconidae arc closes: four consecutive degrees (231-234), four species (Peregrine, Prairie, Merlin, Kestrel), four distinct sizes, four different habitats, four different acoustic signatures. The largest falcon closed at E=7. The smallest closes at E=5. The family has been fully documented.
