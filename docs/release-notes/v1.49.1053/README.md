## Mission Overview

ASCA (Advanced Satellite for Cosmology and Astrophysics) is the obs#14 continuation of the X-ray-astronomy axis and the CCD-imaging-spectroscopy adjacent-sustain of the focusing-imaging branch — the ISAS/Japan X-ray observatory (pre-launch ASTRO-D, in-orbit Asuka), Japan's 4th cosmic X-ray astronomy satellite, the first observatory to combine imaging optics with X-ray CCDs, giving the first true broadband X-ray imaging spectroscopy (a focused image plus a CCD-grade spectrum of the same source together), with four co-aligned thin-foil X-Ray Telescopes feeding two SIS X-ray CCD cameras and two GIS gas imaging spectrometers. Launched 20 February 1993 on a Mu-3SII (M-3SII, flight M-3SII-7 — the last successful M-3SII) from Kagoshima Space Center / Uchinoura, Japan, into a low Earth orbit (perigee ~525 km, apogee ~615 km, inclination ~31°, period ~96 min; NSSDCA / COSPAR ID 1993-011A; SATCAT 22521; ~420 kg). The four co-aligned XRT telescopes use a conical-approximation (segmented conical) Wolter-I design with ~120 nested gold-coated aluminum foils each (0.125 mm aluminum + acrylic lacquer + ~60 nm gold), 3.5 m focal length, GSFC-led (GSFC + Nagoya University + ISAS) and feed the two SIS X-ray CCD cameras (SIS0, SIS1; each four MIT Lincoln Laboratory frontside-illuminated frame-transfer CCD chips, 420×422 px → 840×848 focal plane). The XRT band is ~0.5–12 keV with large effective area sustained to ~10 keV (no hard cutoff), ~1,300 cm² at 1 keV summed and ~600 cm² at 7 keV; the angular resolution is ~2.9 arcmin HPD (coarse, energy-independent). The SIS band is 0.4–10 keV with ~120 eV FWHM (~2% at 5.9 keV) MODERATE spectral resolution; team MIT + Osaka University + ISAS. The two GIS gas imaging spectrometers (GIS2, GIS3; Xe cell + imaging photomultiplier; University of Tokyo + ISAS) cover ~0.7–10 keV with a ~50 arcmin diameter field (usable central region ~40–44 arcmin) at ~8% FWHM at 6 keV, winning on ~4× the SIS field of view, hard-band efficiency above ~5 keV, and timing to ~61 µs. Attitude / Sun-pointing control was lost 14 July 2000 during a geomagnetic storm; reentry followed 2 March 2001 after 7+ productive science years. ## Structural firsts

**ASCA-FIRST-INSTANCE:** ASCA (Advanced Satellite for Cosmology and Astrophysics, pre-launch name ASTRO-D, in-orbit nickname Asuka "flying bird") enters the catalog as Japan's 4th cosmic X-ray astronomy satellite (after Hakucho, Tenma, Ginga), built and led by ISAS (Institute of Space and Astronautical Science, Japan; spacecraft by NEC) — the first instance, the obs#1 first — sustaining the X-ray-astronomy axis at obs#14. COSPAR 1993-011A; SATCAT 22521. Launched 20 February 1993 on a Mu-3SII (M-3SII, flight M-3SII-7 — the last SUCCESSFUL M-3SII; the final flight M-3SII-8 in January 1995 failed) from Kagoshima Space Center / Uchinoura, Japan — a Japanese site, NOT a US site — into a low Earth orbit (perigee ~525 km, apogee ~615 km, inclination ~31°, period ~96 min); ~420 kg. Payload: four co-aligned thin-foil X-Ray Telescopes (XRT) feeding two SIS (Solid-state Imaging Spectrometer, X-ray CCD cameras) and two GIS (Gas Imaging Spectrometer). ASCA is NOT a NASA mission — ISAS led the mission, bus, launch, GIS, and operations; the NASA/US contribution is the four XRT foil telescopes (NASA/GSFC-LED, Peter Serlemitsos thin-foil conical design; GSFC + Nagoya University + ISAS) and the SIS CCDs (US-led MIT + Osaka University + ISAS, CCD chips from MIT Lincoln Laboratory) plus a US Guest Observer Facility at GSFC — "GSFC designed and led the foil optics," never "NASA built the mirrors," and never "GSFC built the SIS." **X-RAY-CCD-IMAGING-SPECTROSCOPY-FIRST-INSTANCE:** the two SIS were the first CCDs flown on an X-ray observatory in orbit (photon-counting), delivering the first broadband X-ray imaging spectroscopy with CCDs — a focused image plus a ~120 eV-resolution (~2% at 5.9 keV) spectrum of the same source at once over 0.4–10 keV (Tanaka, Inoue & Holt 1994, PASJ 46, L37). The qualifiers are mandatory: "in orbit / on an X-ray observatory" (CCDs were used in X-ray lab + sounding-rocket contexts and in optical-space astronomy before 1993) and "WITH CCDs" (BBXRT did broadband focused Si(Li) spectroscopy in 1990). It is NEVER "first high-resolution X-ray spectroscopy" — Chandra gratings (obs#1) and XRISM microcalorimeter (obs#7) own high-res, and SIS resolution is MODERATE (~120 eV). It is NEVER "first X-ray CCD survey" — eROSITA (obs#12) owns the CCD all-sky survey, and ASCA was a POINTED observatory. Chandra ACIS (1999) and XMM EPIC (1999) postdate ASCA by ~6 years; each SIS camera carried four MIT Lincoln Laboratory CCD chips (MIT + Osaka University + ISAS — not GSFC).

**THIN-FOIL-CONICAL-X-RAY-TELESCOPE-SATELLITE-FIRST-INSTANCE:** the four GSFC-led thin-foil conical-approximation Wolter-I telescopes (~120 nested gold-coated aluminum foils each, 3.5 m focal length, ~2.9 arcmin HPD) were the first lightweight nested-foil X-ray mirrors flown on a free-flying satellite, extending focused imaging across ~0.5–12 keV (large effective area sustained to ~10 keV; ~1,300 cm² at 1 keV summed) at a fraction of the mass of figured-glass optics; the Serlemitsos foil lineage continued onto ASTRO-E/Suzaku (Serlemitsos et al. 1995, PASJ 47, 105). The claim keeps "on a free-flying satellite": BBXRT flew the same Serlemitsos foil concept on Space Shuttle ASTRO-1 (2–11 December 1990, with Si(Li) detectors, NOT CCDs) and a 1988 sounding rocket flew foil optics earlier — ASCA is NOT first-ever foil mirrors. These mirrors are COARSE (~2.9 arcmin) — NOT sub-arcsecond (Chandra, ~0.5″ HEW) and NOT the focusing-imaging origin (Einstein, obs#8, the first imaging X-ray telescope). The thin-foil optics traded angular resolution for low mass and broadband coverage, the lightweight architecture that put focused imaging across the soft-and-medium band on a small satellite.

2. **The Chandra/Einstein/XMM partition must be stated in every imaging or spectroscopy claim.** "Sub-arcsecond imaging" and "first high-resolution X-ray spectroscopy" belong to Chandra (obs#1); "first imaging X-ray telescope / focusing-imaging origin" belongs to Einstein (obs#8); "largest pointed effective area," "first reflection gratings," and "first co-aligned optical/UV monitor" belong to XMM-Newton (obs#13). 3. **ISAS-mission framing is an accuracy requirement, not a qualifier.** ASCA is an ISAS mission of Japan (Japan's 4th X-ray satellite, NEC bus, Mu-3SII launch from Uchinoura), not a NASA mission; the NASA/US contribution is confined to the GSFC-LED XRT foil optics (GSFC + Nagoya + ISAS, "designed and led" not "built"), the MIT-led SIS CCDs (MIT + Osaka + ISAS, not GSFC), and the GOF at GSFC. ## Surprises

- **An ISAS mission of Japan — not a NASA mission — was the first observatory to unite imaging optics with X-ray CCDs.** ASCA was built and operated by the Institute of Space and Astronautical Science (Japan's 4th X-ray satellite, spacecraft by NEC) and launched on a Japanese Mu-3SII, with the NASA/US contribution confined to the GSFC-led XRT foil optics and the MIT-led SIS CCDs plus the GOF at GSFC; the two SIS were the first CCDs flown on an X-ray observatory in orbit, delivering the first broadband X-ray imaging spectroscopy with CCDs ~6 years before Chandra ACIS and XMM EPIC.
- **A lightweight thin-foil mirror put focused imaging on a small satellite.** Each XRT stacked ~120 nested gold-coated aluminum foils in a conical-approximation Wolter-I geometry, gathering ~0.5–12 keV at a fraction of the mass of figured-glass optics, the first lightweight nested-foil X-ray mirrors on a free-flying satellite (BBXRT flew the same Serlemitsos concept on Space Shuttle ASTRO-1 in December 1990); the ~2.9 arcmin resolution is coarse, the deliberate trade for low mass and broadband coverage.
- **The signature of matter near a black hole was resolved in a single iron line.** ASCA's long July 1994 observation of MCG−6−30−15 gave the first clear, spectrally-resolved relativistically broadened, skewed iron K-alpha line profile (Tanaka, Nandra, Fabian et al. 1995) — a red wing stretched by gravitational redshift and a blue peak shaped by Doppler beaming, the first observational confirmation of the inner-accretion-disk / strong-gravity signature near a supermassive black hole, confirming the profile Fabian et al. predicted in 1989.
- **A supernova-remnant rim glowed with synchrotron X-rays from ~100 TeV electrons.** ASCA gave evidence for X-ray synchrotron emission from shock-accelerated ~100 TeV electrons at the rims of the supernova remnant SN 1006 (Koyama, Petre, Gotthelf et al. 1995, Nature 378, 255), the first clear X-ray synchrotron signature of cosmic-ray-energy electron acceleration in a supernova-remnant shock.
- **A falcon reads an image and an extra colour channel of the same ground at once.** The American Kestrel (*Falco sparverius*), North America's smallest and most widespread falcon and a year-round Pacific Northwest resident, reads UV cues in the field (vole runways marked by scent that reflect ultraviolet) while forming a high-acuity spatial image of the same ground from a hover — gathering at once a focused image AND an extra spectral channel, a behavioral analogue of ASCA's first true X-ray imaging spectroscopy (the SIS recording a focused image AND a resolved spectrum of the same source).
- **A fern frond is an ordered array of repeated collecting elements.** The Western Sword Fern (*Polystichum munitum*), an evergreen, long-lived fern of moist Pacific Northwest conifer-forest understory, carries dozens of paired pinnae in precise opposed rows along a single rachis — a regular, repeated detector-array geometry that evokes ASCA's two SIS X-ray CCDs (arrays of identical pixels each reading photon energy) and the ~120 nested thin-foil shells stacked in each XRT telescope.

## Dedication

v1.236 ASCA is dedicated to the people who built and flew the Advanced Satellite for Cosmology and Astrophysics — the ISAS observatory that first united a focused X-ray image with a CCD-resolved spectrum of the same source: the Institute of Space and Astronautical Science teams in Japan who built, launched, and operated it as Asuka, the nation's 4th cosmic X-ray satellite, and NEC, who built the spacecraft; Peter Serlemitsos and the Goddard team who designed and led the thin-foil conical X-Ray Telescopes, with Nagoya University; the MIT and Osaka University teams who built the SIS X-ray CCD cameras, with chips from MIT Lincoln Laboratory; the University of Tokyo team who built the GIS spectrometers; Yasuo Tanaka, Kazuo Makishima, and Hajime Inoue, who led it; Tanaka, Nandra, and Fabian, whose long look at MCG−6−30−15 resolved the relativistic iron line near a black hole; Koyama, Petre, and Gotthelf, who found the synchrotron rim of SN 1006; the Goddard Guest Observer Facility, who served the US community; and Riccardo Giacconi and Bruno Rossi, who founded the field with the 1962 rocket. From a low Earth orbit, a focused image and a resolved spectrum were read together for the first time, in focused light.
