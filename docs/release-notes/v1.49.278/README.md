**Part B: Barred Owl Caterwauling and the Wild Ascending Scream.** The **Barred Owl** (*Strix varia*) returns at degree 138 with its second vocalization — the caterwauling, wild ascending screaming sequences that constitute one of the most alarming regular nocturnal animal sounds in Pacific Northwest forests. The caterwauling is not random noise — it serves specific behavioral functions. The most common context is interaction between mated pairs during pair bonding and courtship, where male and female engage in escalating vocal exchanges that build from hooting into full caterwauling. This is analogous to the **Great Horned Owl** pair duet at degree 136 but taken to an extreme: the GHO duet is measured and coordinated, operating within a bounded pitch range and rhythmic structure, while the Barred Owl caterwaul is wild and escalating, chaotic and overlapping, sometimes going on for minutes at a time. Caterwauling also appears in territorial disputes between neighboring Barred Owl pairs, in response to intruders or predators, during nest defense, and during any source of high behavioral arousal. The acoustic anatomy is unmistakable: ascending screams ("AAAH-aaah-AAAH-aaah-AAAAH!" with each successive note climbing in pitch and intensity), sharp percussive barks ("WAH! WAH! WAH!" punctuating the sequence), rapid-fire manic cackling ("ah-ah-ah-ah-ah" that sounds like deranged laughter), drawn-out bass howls ("HOOOOO-AWWW" providing a low-frequency foundation), and overlapping multi-voice chaos when pairs engage. The frequency range spans 300-2000+ Hz (compared to the hooting's narrower 400-550 Hz), and the amplitude is significantly louder — audible 500+ meters through dense forest.

**The +2 Energy Step and the Fourth Double-Entry Species.** The Barred Owl's **+2 energy step** from E=4 hooting at degree 137 to E=6 caterwauling at degree 138 is the most dramatic jump any double-entry species has produced in the engine's 138-degree history. The three previous double-entry species all showed **+1 energy steps**: Pileated Woodpecker at 108 (laughing call, E=4) and 109 (territorial drumming, E=5), American Dipper at 116 (song, E=3) and 117 (dunking splash, E=4), Great Horned Owl at 135 (solo hoot, E=4) and 136 (pair duet, E=5). These sound like two completely different species. The hooting is speech-like, rhythmic, and in a narrow frequency band. The caterwauling is wild, chaotic, and spans multiple octaves. The hooting establishes territory through measured announcement. The caterwauling negotiates pair bonding and territorial defense through high-arousal escalation. Same species, radically different acoustic outputs. The double-entry pattern is now established at four species, and the +2 outlier establishes that energy steps within double entries can exceed +1 when the acoustic contrast justifies it.

**The Human Alarm Response as Acoustic Document.** The Barred Owl caterwauling is the single most commonly misidentified owl sound in the Pacific Northwest. Police departments in forested suburban areas — Bothell, Edmonds, Kirkland, Bellevue, Shoreline, Redmond, North Seattle — report regular calls from residents convinced that someone is being attacked in the woods. The ascending screams genuinely sound like a human in distress. The raspy quality and the chaotic overlapping structure heighten the effect. The night amplifies it. Uninitiated listeners at 2 AM in a dark forested environment have no cognitive frame for the sound and default to the most alarming explanation available to them. Descriptions by first-time listeners fall into consistent categories: "someone is being murdered in the woods" (the most common), "monkeys in the forest" (for the cackling component that has a primate-like quality), "demonic" (for the combination of ascending screams and raspy quality), "a woman screaming" (for the high-pitched ascending sequences), "something supernatural" (for the combination that has no recognizable animal counterpart in North American forest soundscapes). The human alarm response is itself an acoustic document — a record of what the caterwauling sounds like to a listener without species-identification training, and the consistency of the misidentification across decades and across geographic regions validates the E=6 calibration at the psychological-impact level beyond acoustic-volume measurement alone.

## Part B: Barred Owl Caterwauling — The Wild Ascending Scream and the Most Dramatic +2 Energy Step

- **Fourth species with double entries and the most dramatic +2 energy step.** The **Barred Owl**'s double entry at degrees 137-138 establishes it as the **fourth species with double entries** in the engine, joining **Pileated Woodpecker** (108 laughing call E=4 + 109 territorial drumming E=5), **American Dipper** (116 song E=3 + 117 dunking splash E=4), and **Great Horned Owl** (135 solo hoot E=4 + 136 pair duet E=5). - **Fourth consecutive Strigidae and the longest same-family streak.** Degree 138 extends the **Strigidae** streak to **four consecutive entries**: 135 Great Horned Owl solo hoot, 136 Great Horned Owl pair duet, 137 Barred Owl measured hooting, 138 Barred Owl caterwauling. The Anatidae streak at seventeen consecutive (118-134) achieved its record by individual species each at a single entry; the Strigidae quartet achieves its record by two species each at two entries. The streak type is structurally different: Anatidae was single-species-per-degree streaking, Strigidae is double-species-per-pair streaking, and both record types now coexist in the engine's record book.

The V-shape dips at degree 137 before surging to maximum at degree 138, and the dip-and-surge pattern is structurally distinct from simple monotonic progressions. The arc signals that the owl family contains both measured and wild vocal registers, both coordinated and chaotic behavioral modes, and both species-specific intensification patterns (GHO goes from E=4 to E=5 through pair coordination; Barred Owl goes from E=4 to E=6 through caterwaul escalation). The V-shape closure at maximum energy at degree 138 is the owl family's peak acoustic contribution to the engine.
- **Second E=6 species-axis entry after Trumpeter Swan at degree 128.** The Barred Owl caterwauling is the **second E=6 species-axis entry** in the engine, ten degrees after **Trumpeter Swan** bugling at degree 128 (within the Anatidae streak). The E=6 ceiling on the species axis has now been reached twice: once by the largest native North American waterfowl in a wetland acoustic environment, and once by a forest owl's chaotic multi-voice vocalization in a forest acoustic environment. Both E=6 entries involve loud, far-carrying sounds that dominate their acoustic environments at the fundamental level of human perception. The ten-degree interval between E=6 entries is not arbitrary — E=6 is reserved for genuine ceiling-register vocalizations, and the engine's restraint in assigning E=6 validates the calibration when it does occur.
- **The wild ascending scream as acoustic anatomy.** The **caterwauling** is composed of multiple acoustic components layered chaotically: **ascending screams** ("AAAH-aaah-AAAH-aaah-AAAAH!" with each successive note climbing in pitch and intensity, screaming with a raspy throat-tearing quality), **sharp percussive barks** ("WAH! WAH! WAH!" punctuating the sequence with aggressive provocation-response quality), **rapid-fire manic cackling** ("ah-ah-ah-ah-ah" that sounds like deranged laughter and contributes to the demonic-psychoacoustic effect), and **drawn-out bass howls** ("HOOOOO-AWWW" providing a low-frequency foundation and the clearest connection to the measured hooting at degree 137 but distorted and extended into something wilder). When multiple Barred Owls caterwaul simultaneously, these components overlap and interleave unpredictably, creating dense layered soundscape of screaming, barking, cackling, and howling that resembles a group of creatures in violent conflict or ecstatic frenzy.
- **Frequency range spans multiple octaves.** The caterwauling frequency range is **300-2000+ Hz** — dramatically wider than the hooting's narrower 400-550 Hz band. The caterwauling spans multiple octaves, from bass howls in the 300-600 Hz range through barks and low cackling in the 500-1000 Hz range through screams in the 1000-1500+ Hz range. The wide frequency range is part of what makes the caterwauling so acoustically alarming — it occupies a huge portion of the audible spectrum simultaneously, and no single frequency band is free from the sound. This acoustic breadth contrasts with the measured hooting's tight frequency concentration in a single band, and the breadth-versus-concentration contrast is the spectral signature of the +2 energy step from degree 137 to degree 138.
- **Primal human alarm response as acoustic document.** The Barred Owl caterwauling is the single most **commonly misidentified** owl sound in the Pacific Northwest. Police departments in forested suburban areas (Bothell, Edmonds, Kirkland, Bellevue, Shoreline, Redmond, North Seattle) report regular calls from residents convinced that someone is being attacked in the woods. The consistent misidentification patterns — "someone is being murdered," "monkeys in the forest," "demonic," "a woman screaming," "something supernatural" — validate the E=6 calibration at the psychological-impact level. The ascending pitch sequences triggered instinctive alarm response because rising pitch is universally associated with threat and distress, the raspy quality suggests anguish or injury, the cackling has a primate-like quality that is deeply incongruous in a coniferous forest, and the chaotic overlapping multi-voice structure exceeds what any single animal could produce, heightening the sense that something extraordinary is happening.
- **Behavioral context and seasonal peaks.** The caterwauling serves specific behavioral functions. The most common context is **interaction between mated pairs** during pair bonding and courtship, where male and female engage in escalating vocal exchanges that build from hooting into full caterwauling. **Territorial disputes** between neighboring Barred Owl pairs produce boundary caterwauling — screaming at each other across the territorial boundary, sometimes for extended periods as each pair tries to out-vocalize the other. **Agitation and excitement** contexts include the presence of an intruder (owl or other predator), disturbance near the nest, or post-hunt arousal. **Nest defense** can trigger caterwauling toward specific threats. Seasonal peaks coincide with breeding season (February-April in the Pacific Northwest) when territorial boundaries are being established and pair bonds reinforced, but caterwauling can occur year-round. Time-of-day is primarily nocturnal with dusk and pre-dawn peaks, though in the Pacific Northwest's overcast climate daytime caterwauling is not uncommon.
- **Range expansion and citizen-science documentation.** The **Barred Owl**'s range expansion into the Pacific Northwest over the past century has brought the caterwauling into regular contact with PNW residents for the first time. Before the expansion, the caterwauling was less familiar in the region and even more alarming when encountered. Citizen-science data from Seattle Audubon, eBird, and regional birding forums document thousands of caterwauling reports from PNW forests and suburban areas, and the consistency of observer-reported acoustic characteristics (ascending screams, barking, cackling, howling, overlapping multi-voice) across decades and across geographic regions validates the E=6 calibration across independent observation conditions. The range expansion also carries conservation implications: the Barred Owl's expansion into Pacific Northwest forests has displaced the **Spotted Owl** (Strigidae degree 4, earlier in the engine) through competitive and hybridization pressure, and the caterwauling's audibility serves as a partial census indicator for Barred Owl presence in forests where Spotted Owl conservation remains a concern.
- **Same species, two radically different acoustic outputs.** The Barred Owl's double entry at 137-138 demonstrates that **same species can contain radically different acoustic signatures** at the maximum divergence scale. The measured hooting at degree 137 (E=4, narrow frequency band 400-550 Hz, rhythmic speech-like "Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?" structure, solo territorial announcement) and the chaotic caterwauling at degree 138 (E=6, wide frequency range 300-2000+ Hz, chaotic overlapping multi-voice ascending-screaming-barking-cackling-howling structure, pair-bonding/territorial-dispute/high-arousal function) sound like two completely different species. An uninitiated listener encountering both vocalizations separately would have no reason to identify them as the same animal. The double-entry treatment at adjacent degrees 137-138 is therefore essential to capturing the full acoustic complexity of the species — single-entry treatment would have missed the caterwauling entirely and reduced the Barred Owl to its measured hooting alone.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Energy steps within double entries can exceed +1 when acoustic contrast is extreme.** The Barred Owl's +2 energy step (E=4 hooting to E=6 caterwauling) is unique among the engine's four double-entry species. The +2 reflects the genuinely extreme acoustic contrast between the two vocalizations — measured narrow-band rhythmic hooting versus chaotic multi-octave ascending screaming. Previous assumptions that double entries would always show +1 steps were based on the first three species (Pileated Woodpecker, American Dipper, Great Horned Owl); the Barred Owl demonstrates that the calibration must scale with the acoustic contrast rather than default to +1. Future double-entry species should be calibrated based on acoustic contrast magnitude, not based on +1 precedent.
2. **Same-family streaks with double-entry species structurally differ from single-entry streaks.** The Strigidae quartet at 135-138 achieves the longest same-family streak in the engine through two species each at two entries — structurally different from the Anatidae seventeen-species streak at 118-134 that achieved its record through many species each at single entries. Recognizing that the streak-type categories are distinct (single-species-per-degree versus double-species-per-pair) enriches the engine's record structure and accommodates future streaks of either type without conflating them.
3. **V-shaped energy arcs across multi-degree family streaks are structurally meaningful.** The Strigidae quartet's energy arc (4, 5, 4, 6) traces a V-shape that dips and then surges to maximum at closure. V-shapes differ from monotonic progressions and should be named as first-class energy-arc patterns alongside monotonic-up, monotonic-down, and flat patterns. Future same-family streaks should be examined for V-shapes, inverted-V-shapes (surge-and-dip), and more complex patterns that reveal the family's internal energy structure.
4. **E=6 species-axis ceiling is reserved for sounds that dominate acoustic environments at the fundamental level of human perception.** The two E=6 species-axis entries in engine history (Trumpeter Swan bugling at 128, Barred Owl caterwauling at 138) both involve loud, far-carrying vocalizations that fundamentally alter their respective ecosystem soundscapes. The engine's restraint in assigning E=6 — only two entries in 138 degrees — validates the calibration when it does occur. Future E=6 candidates should be evaluated against the Trumpeter Swan and Barred Owl caterwauling benchmarks for both acoustic volume and psychological-impact criteria.
5. **Human alarm response is a valid dimension of acoustic-energy calibration.** The Barred Owl caterwauling's E=6 calibration is partly justified by the primal human alarm response it triggers — the consistent police-report misidentification patterns, the universal "someone being murdered" interpretation, the demonic-supernatural associations. Acoustic energy is not purely a physical measurement; psychological impact on human perceivers is part of what makes a sound occupy an E=6 register.
