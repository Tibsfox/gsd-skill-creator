# Retrospective — v1.49.43

## What Worked

1. **The PNW connection is the strongest yet.** WYR doesn't just reference the ecology cluster — it IS the ecology cluster viewed through an industrial lens. Every forest documented in COL/CAS/ECO was also a Weyerhaeuser timberland. Every salmon run in SAL was affected by logging. The cross-reference count (24 projects) proves the integration is genuine, not forced.

2. **Module 03 "What Was Lost" and Module 07 "Whose Forest Is This?" carry the weight.** These are the modules that make WYR more than a corporate profile. The Timber Wars, spotted owl crisis, 15M→5M acre loss, Stevens treaties, Boldt Decision, environmental justice — this is where the research becomes honest about cost. The "working forest" concept in Module 04 only means something after you've read what Module 03 documents.

3. **The "Evergreen" name lands.** Washington IS the Evergreen State. Weyerhaeuser IS what made it evergreen (through replanting) and what made it not-evergreen (through clear-cutting). The name holds the tension that the project examines.

## Lessons Learned

1. **The most connected projects are the ones closest to home.** WYR's 24 cross-references happened naturally — the connections didn't need to be forced because Weyerhaeuser's timberlands ARE the PNW bioregion. The ecology cluster projects (COL, CAS, ECO) map species; WYR maps the industry that shaped those species' habitat. Same place, different layer. This is the Research series working as designed.

2. **Corporate history and environmental critique can coexist in one project.** WYR documents Weyerhaeuser's founding, growth, and innovations (Module 01, 02, 04, 06) alongside the destruction those activities caused (Module 03, 05, 07). Neither is suppressed. The "working forest" concept (Module 04) is presented as both a genuine improvement and an incomplete answer. Research that's honest about complexity is more useful than research that picks a side.
