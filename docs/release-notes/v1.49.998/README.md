## Summary

v1.49.998 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.188 to 1.189, adding Polar — the Global Geospace Science Polar spacecraft — as the obs#4 INTRA-AXIS continuation of the reopened near-Earth magnetosphere substrate-axis. The magnetosphere axis reopened at v995 FAST (obs#1, the in-situ high-cadence auroral-acceleration-region snapshot science), was carried at v996 DE-1 (obs#2, the first global imaging of the complete auroral oval) and at v997 IMAGE (obs#3, the first dedicated global imaging of the entire inner magnetosphere), and at v998 Polar continues the axis at obs#4 with simultaneous multi-wavelength auroral imaging joined to combined polar-cusp in-situ sampling. Where IMAGE rendered the ring current and the plasmasphere as whole structures, Polar imaged the same aurora in the visible, ultraviolet, and X-ray bands at once while sampling the polar cusp, the polar cap, and the polar wind from within, adding the polar-cap vantage and the X-ray imaging band to the global-imaging foundations the axis already holds.

Polar was a NASA Global Geospace Science mission within the International Solar-Terrestrial Physics program, managed by NASA Goddard Space Flight Center, and a member of the coordinated GGS fleet alongside Wind and Geotail. The mission idea brought two methods to one region: a set of imagers tuned to the visible, ultraviolet, and X-ray bands would render the aurora as a global picture, and a suite of in-situ particle, field, and wave instruments would measure the particles and fields directly as the spacecraft flew through them. Polar launched 1996-02-24 at 11:24 UTC on a Delta II 7925 vehicle from Vandenberg Air Force Base into a highly elliptical polar orbit with apogee positioned over the northern polar region, the high vantage from which the imagers could view the auroral zone whole and the trajectory through the polar cusp and polar cap for the in-situ instruments.

The signature of the mission is the first global X-ray imaging of the aurora. When energetic electrons precipitate into the upper atmosphere they emit bremsstrahlung X-rays, and the Polar Ionospheric X-ray Imaging Experiment mapped that emission across the auroral zone, producing the first global map of where the most energetic electrons were coming down. The X-ray band added a direct global picture of the most energetic precipitation that the visible and ultraviolet auroral views could not provide directly, the deepest-reaching part of the precipitation rendered as a whole-oval image.

The Visible Imaging System and the Ultraviolet Imager observed the same aurora in the visible and ultraviolet bands while PIXIE observed it in X-rays, giving a three-band view of the same auroral display at once. The three imagers together separated the optical glow, the ultraviolet emission, and the energetic-electron precipitation, each band carrying a different aspect of one aurora, so comparing the bands across the same oval revealed how the energy of the precipitation was distributed. Alongside the imagers, the thermal-ion instruments measured the cold ionospheric ions flowing outward from the polar cap as the polar wind, quantifying the ionosphere as a direct supplier of magnetospheric plasma, and the particle, field, and wave instruments sampled the polar cusp and the polar cap as the spacecraft passed through them.

The science return joined a three-band view of the aurora to direct measurements of the polar magnetosphere and the polar wind. For the first time the aurora could be seen in X-rays as well as in the visible and ultraviolet, and the polar wind could be measured as a quantified source of magnetospheric plasma. Polar operated from 1996 through 2008 as part of the Global Geospace Science fleet, observing the polar magnetosphere while Wind monitored the solar wind upstream and Geotail observed the magnetotail, contributing the polar vantage to a coordinated multi-point view of the solar-terrestrial system.

The axis-relevant substrate of Polar at this degree is its continuation of the reopened near-Earth magnetosphere axis at obs#4 with the multi-wavelength polar imaging and polar-cusp in-situ sampling. Polar sustains the MAGNETOSPHERE-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#4 cumulative (v995 FAST + v996 DE-1 + v997 IMAGE + v998 Polar), it extends the GLOBAL-MAGNETOSPHERE-IMAGING-LINEAGE thread at obs#4 cumulative (v996 DE-1 + v997 IMAGE + v998 Polar, Polar adding the polar-cap vantage and the X-ray band to the imaging lineage), and it sustains the ISTP-GGS-FLEET-COORDINATION thread at obs#3 cumulative (v1.174 Geotail + v1.179 Wind + v998 Polar). As the obs#4 entry on the reopened axis, Polar adds the polar-cap vantage and the X-ray imaging band, and it sets the multi-wavelength polar-observation anchor from which the reopened magnetosphere axis will grow. The forward continuation favors further inner-magnetosphere, ring-current, and ionosphere-coupling missions that build on the in-situ and global-imaging foundations now established.

Polar is the first Global Geospace Science fleet member in the catalog, and the GGS-fleet membership records the coordinated multi-point observation alongside Wind and Geotail. ## Lessons Learned

1. **A new imaging band can be added to an existing axis without changing the imaging idea.** DE-1, IMAGE, and Polar all image the magnetosphere from space, but Polar added the X-ray band to the visible and ultraviolet auroral imaging, showing that an axis can deepen by adding a new sensing band to the same whole-system imaging substrate, an obs#4 continuation that extends the imaging lineage in a fresh band.
2. **Imaging from above and sampling from within can ride the same orbit.** Polar's highly elliptical polar orbit carried the imagers to high apogee over the pole and carried the in-situ instruments through the polar cusp and cap on each pass, showing that a single orbit can serve both a remote imaging vantage and a direct in-situ sampling pass, the combined architecture that distinguishes Polar from the imaging-only and in-situ-only entries.
3. **The most energetic precipitation is best seen in X-rays.** PIXIE imaged the bremsstrahlung from the highest-energy electrons reaching deepest into the atmosphere, showing that the X-ray band maps the most energetic precipitation directly where the visible and ultraviolet bands trace the bulk precipitation higher up, a band-specific view of the auroral energy distribution.
4. **The ionosphere is a source, not only a sink.** The polar-wind measurements quantified the cold ions flowing outward from the polar cap into the magnetosphere, showing that the ionosphere supplies plasma upward to the magnetosphere as well as receiving precipitation, an ionospheric-outflow substrate distinct from the imaging substrates earlier on the axis.
5. **A fleet of spacecraft builds a multi-point view no single craft can.** Polar observed the polar magnetosphere while Wind monitored the solar wind upstream and Geotail observed the magnetotail, showing that a coordinated fleet can build a multi-point picture of the solar-terrestrial system from complementary vantages, the GGS-fleet coordination that Polar joins.
6. **A four-observation axis can hold in-situ, partial-imaging, full-imaging, and combined vantages together.** The reopened magnetosphere axis now holds the FAST in-situ view, the DE-1 auroral-oval imaging, the IMAGE inner-magnetosphere imaging, and the Polar combined multi-wavelength imaging and in-situ observation, showing that an axis can build complementary vantages from local sampling through global imaging to combined observation across its observations.
7. **A long operational interval follows the polar magnetosphere across a solar cycle.** Polar operated from 1996 through 2008, more than a decade, following the aurora and the polar plasma through many geomagnetic events, showing that a long-lived mission can build a continuous record of the polar magnetosphere across a large part of a solar cycle.
8. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across a distinct-palette mission.** Because Polar reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.188 template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the multi-wavelength polar imaging with a distinct X-ray-violet / UV-indigo / visible-green palette of deep polar dark, X-ray violet, UV indigo, visible green, aurora teal, and polar blue, making the distinct-palette mission a clean build.

## Surprises

- **The aurora has an X-ray face.** The aurora is familiar as a visible and ultraviolet glow, but Polar's PIXIE imager rendered it whole in X-rays, mapping the bremsstrahlung of the most energetic precipitating electrons, so the catalog's first global X-ray view of the aurora came from a band the eye never sees.
- **A high-latitude polar owl mirrors a high-latitude polar-orbiting spacecraft.** The Snowy Owl, which keeps a commanding watch over the bright open ground of the high latitudes, enters as the polar-vantage mirror to the polar-orbiting mission, so the catalog pairs a high-latitude raptor with a polar-magnetosphere imager.
- **The ionosphere feeds the magnetosphere from below.** The polar-wind measurements showed the cold ionospheric ions flowing outward from the polar cap into the magnetosphere, so the ionosphere is a quantified source of magnetospheric plasma rather than only a destination for precipitation.

## Dedication

v1.189 Polar is dedicated to the team that built and flew the Global Geospace Science Polar spacecraft: the GGS Polar science team who designed a mission to image the aurora from above while sampling the polar magnetosphere from within; the PIXIE imaging team who produced the first global X-ray images of the aurora; the Ultraviolet Imager and Visible Imaging System teams who rendered the same aurora in the ultraviolet and visible bands; the thermal-ion teams who built the TIDE and TIMAS instruments that measured the polar-wind outflow; the particle, field, and wave instrument teams who sampled the polar cusp and the polar cap; and the NASA Goddard Space Flight Center team who managed the program and the spacecraft. It is dedicated as well to the wider community of magnetospheric physicists whose work Polar advanced, the multi-wavelength imaging and the in-situ sampling that joined the polar aurora to the particles and fields that produce it. Polar launched in 1996, flew a highly elliptical polar orbit over the northern pole, imaged the polar light across three bands while sampling the polar cusp and polar wind, and operated through 2008 within the Global Geospace Science fleet alongside Wind and Geotail.
