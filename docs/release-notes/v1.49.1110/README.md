## Mission Overview

Aquarius is the obs#13 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's sea-surface-salinity mission, the instrument that mapped the saltiness of the global ocean from space, flying aboard the Argentine SAC-D spacecraft. ★ It was a joint NASA + CONAE (Comisión Nacional de Actividades Espaciales, Argentina) Earth-observing mission: NASA's Aquarius instrument was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL built the scatterometer and managed Aquarius) and Goddard Space Flight Center (GSFC built the radiometer and ran post-launch operations); CONAE provided the SAC-D spacecraft (the bus built by INVAP in Argentina) and several of its own instruments, with contributions from Brazil (INPE), Canada (CSA), France (CNES), and Italy (ASI). The instrument read the ocean two ways at once: the three dual-polarization L-band radiometers (~1.413 GHz) measured the sea surface's microwave brightness temperature — brighter where the water was fresher, dimmer where it was saltier — and the polarimetric L-band scatterometer (~1.26 GHz) measured the wind-driven roughness of the waves to correct the radiometer, the instrument sweeping three push-broom beams (28.7/37.8/45.6° off-nadir). Together they turned the faint microwave glow of the sea into a map of its salt. The mission launched 10 June 2011 (14:20 UTC) on a Delta II (7320-10C) from Vandenberg (SLC-2W), California, and reached a sun-synchronous orbit at roughly 657 km altitude with a ~7-day repeat. The story is the Delta II launch from Vandenberg, the climb to the sun-synchronous orbit, the radiometer reading the faint microwave brightness of the sea, the scatterometer reading the roughness to correct for it, the salt of the ocean gathered into the first NASA global monthly maps of sea surface salinity, and the run of some four years before it ended. The science was central: the radiometer measured the brightness temperature of the sea surface — set by its salinity and temperature — while the scatterometer measured the surface roughness so that the wind's effect could be removed, and together they retrieved the sea surface salinity, an essential climate variable tied to the global water cycle and ocean circulation. Aquarius's firsts were about being the first NASA-DEDICATED mission, the global monthly mapping, and the combined technique: it was the first NASA mission and instrument dedicated to measuring sea surface salinity from space; it produced the first NASA global monthly maps of sea surface salinity; and it retrieved salinity by the combined L-band radiometer + scatterometer approach. ★ The "first NASA-dedicated" result is load-bearing: scope it strictly to "the first NASA mission dedicated to measuring sea surface salinity from space"; it is NOT "the first salinity from space" — ESA's SMOS, launched November 2009, measured sea surface salinity from space earlier. ★ The combined-technique result is equally load-bearing: the radiometer read the salinity, the scatterometer corrected for the wind-driven surface roughness, and the two together retrieved the salt. ★ The global-monthly-mapping result is load-bearing: the first NASA global monthly maps of sea surface salinity, gathered across 2011–2015. ★★★ Aquarius is RETIRED (2011–2015), so the catalog frames its whole story in the PAST (Aquarius launched 10 June 2011, reached its sun-synchronous orbit, measured and mapped the sea surface salinity, and ended in June 2015 — the prime three-year science mission completed November 2014, the mission ended after the SAC-D RTU/onboard-power failure of June 2015). ## Surprises

- **One instrument read the ocean's salt by listening to its faint microwave glow.** Aquarius read the sea surface salinity not by touching the water but by measuring the faint microwave brightness temperature of the sea surface with an L-band radiometer — brighter where the water was fresher and dimmer where it was saltier — turning a quantity that is hard to measure from a ship into a global monthly map.
- **Two L-band instruments, one to read the salt and one to remove the wind.** Aquarius carried an L-band radiometer (~1.413 GHz) and an L-band scatterometer (~1.26 GHz). The radiometer read the sea surface's microwave brightness, set by its salinity; the scatterometer read the wind-driven roughness of the waves, which also brightens the surface — so the scatterometer's reading could be removed from the radiometer's, leaving the salinity signal alone.
- **The same L-band band, two very different targets.** Aquarius and its axis sibling SMAP both use an L-band radiometer, but they read very different things: Aquarius read the salt of the OCEAN, while SMAP reads the moisture of the LAND's SOIL. The same physics of L-band microwave radiometry, turned to the sea in one case and to the ground in the other.
- **An estuarine oyster that filters the salty water and lives by its salinity.** The Olympia Oyster (*Ostrea lurida*), the Pacific Northwest's only native oyster, is a small bivalve of the brackish estuaries and tideflats where river water meets the sea — filtering the salty water for its food and sensitive to its changing salinity, native to Puget Sound, Willapa Bay, and the estuaries from British Columbia to California — a creature that lives in the salt of the sea Aquarius measured at the surface (behavioral / ecological description only; distinct from the introduced Pacific oyster *Magallana/Crassostrea gigas*).
- **A salt-marsh grass that sheds the salt through its leaves.** The Saltgrass (*Distichlis spicata*), a low, wiry, salt-tolerant grass of Pacific Northwest coastal salt marshes and tidal flats, lives in saline soil and sheds excess salt through specialized salt glands on its leaves — a true halophyte of the briny margin, native to PNW coastal salt marshes (ecological description only; distinct from pickleweed, jaumea, and arrowgrass) — bound with the throughline of the salt of the sea, the thing that handles the brine.

## Dedication

v1.293 Aquarius is dedicated to the people at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory and Goddard Space Flight Center, at CONAE and INVAP in Argentina, and to the partners in Brazil, Canada, France, and Italy who built and flew the instrument that read the salt of the global ocean from space. The teams who built two L-band instruments — one to read the faint microwave brightness of the sea surface, brighter where the water was fresher and dimmer where it was saltier, and one to measure the roughness of the waves so the wind could be removed from the reading — and tuned them so well that a quantity once gathered only by ship became the first NASA global monthly maps of the ocean's salinity. The crews who carried it aloft on a Delta II from Vandenberg and set it in its sun-synchronous orbit. The people who kept the watch over the sea's salt for some four years, past the three it was designed for, and the scientists who built the satellite record of ocean salinity tied to the water cycle and the circulation of the sea. The instrument is quiet now, and that record of the ocean's salt belongs to them.
