## Mission Overview

STEREO is the obs#3 sustainer of the HELIOPHYSICS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA twin-spacecraft mission whose defining records are the first stereoscopic, three-dimensional views of the Sun, the first 3-D imaging and tracking of coronal mass ejections from the Sun to Earth, and the first simultaneous view of the entire Sun. The two nearly identical observatories — STEREO-A "Ahead" and STEREO-B "Behind" — were built and integrated by the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory (JHU APL) under NASA's Solar Terrestrial Probes program, with mission management at NASA Goddard — NOT ESA / Dornier, and the firsts concern stereoscopic imaging, not the builder. A heliophysics mission near Earth's orbit, the pair launched together 25 October 2006 (26 Oct 2006 00:52 UTC) on a single Delta II 7925-10L from Cape Canaveral (SLC-17B). STEREO-A entered a heliocentric orbit slightly INSIDE Earth's (faster → it drifts AHEAD of Earth); STEREO-B slightly OUTSIDE (slower → it drifts BEHIND), each drifting ~22° per year so the two separated ~44°/year, the stereoscopic baseline widening with every orbit. The instrument set was SECCHI (the Sun Earth Connection Coronal and Heliospheric Investigation — the EUVI extreme-UV imager, the COR1/COR2 coronagraphs, and the HI1/HI2 heliospheric imagers), IMPACT (in-situ particles and fields), PLASTIC (plasma and suprathermal ion composition), and SWAVES (radio and plasma waves). The story is the single Delta II launch, the twins parting ways — one drawing ahead of Earth, one falling behind — and the widening baseline that let two observatories see the Sun and the CMEs it launches from two vantage points at once, the way two eyes give depth. The science is central: STEREO combined the views from its two spacecraft into three-dimensional pictures of the Sun, imaged and tracked coronal mass ejections in 3-D as they traveled from the Sun out toward Earth (improving space-weather forecasting), and, on 6 February 2011 when the twins reached ~180° apart, returned the first simultaneous view of the entire Sun across the full 360°. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: STEREO is a SOLAR / heliophysics mission, NOT a space telescope, and re-stamps NO space-telescope-axis anchor; it is a NASA mission under the Solar Terrestrial Probes program, built by JHU APL, NOT a joint ESA/NASA mission; its firsts are stereoscopic IMAGING of the Sun and CMEs from two vantage points (distinct from Ulysses, which flew OVER the poles and surveyed the heliosphere in situ, and from Parker, which dove THROUGH the corona near the equator). ★ The page is framed in MIXED tense: STEREO-A continues to operate and return data (present tense, ~20 years on orbit); contact with STEREO-B was lost on 1 October 2014 during a planned reset before a solar conjunction (briefly reacquired 21 August 2016; recovery attempts ended 17 October 2018) — past tense for B; and the firsts are PAST achievements. ## Surprises

- **Two spacecraft, one rocket, two vantage points.** STEREO launched both nearly identical observatories together on a single Delta II 7925-10L on 25 October 2006, then let orbital mechanics part them — STEREO-A onto a slightly smaller, faster orbit that drew ahead of Earth, STEREO-B onto a slightly larger, slower orbit that fell behind — each by ~22° per year, so the stereoscopic baseline widened from near-zero toward 180° as the mission went on.
- **The Sun in depth, the way two eyes give depth.** STEREO gave the first stereoscopic, three-dimensional views of the Sun (first 3-D images released 23 April 2007), combining the views from its two spacecraft the way two eyes triangulate depth — the first time the Sun and the structures above it could be seen in true three dimensions from two simultaneous vantage points.
- **Coronal mass ejections tracked in 3-D from the Sun to Earth.** STEREO made the first 3-D imaging and tracking of coronal mass ejections, following an eruption in three dimensions as it traveled from the Sun out toward Earth through the coronagraphs and the heliospheric imagers — a direct improvement to space-weather forecasting.
- **The entire Sun, seen at once.** On 6 February 2011 the twins reached ~180° separation, on opposite sides of the Sun, and STEREO returned the first simultaneous view of the entire Sun across the full 360° — the whole solar surface imaged at one moment for the first time.
- **A small native ground mantis that judges depth with two eyes.** The Agile Ground Mantis (*Litaneutria minor*), a small, fast, ground-dwelling praying mantis native to the dry interior Pacific Northwest's sagebrush-steppe (the Columbia Plateau of eastern Washington and Oregon, north into British Columbia — Canada's only native mantid), has true stereoscopic (three-dimensional) vision, judging depth by combining the views from its two large, widely-set eyes before it strikes (documented natural history only; distinguished from the introduced European *Mantis religiosa* and Chinese *Tenodera sinensis*) — a mirror of the mission that put two spacecraft on two vantage points to see the Sun in depth.
- **A branched dry-steppe shrub among which the watcher waits.** Antelope Bitterbrush (*Purshia tridentata*), a much-branched dry-country shrub of the interior PNW sagebrush-steppe and open ponderosa slopes (rose family), with small three-lobed ("tridentate") leaves and pale yellow flowers, a key wildlife browse plant (ecological description only, distinguished from sagebrush *Artemisia* and rabbitbrush *Ericameria*) — the kind of fixed dry-steppe shrub among which the stereoscopic ground mantis hunts and waits, bound with STEREO's two-vantage-point view by depth from two eyes.

## Dedication

v1.268 STEREO is dedicated to the people across NASA and the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory who built and flew the twin spacecraft that gave the first three-dimensional views of the Sun. The engineers who built two nearly identical observatories and set them on one rocket, then let orbital mechanics carry one ahead of Earth and one behind, each by a little more every year, until the pair could see the Sun from two sides at once. The navigators who held both craft on their slowly parting paths; the instrument teams behind SECCHI, IMPACT, PLASTIC, and SWAVES; and the scientists who fused the two views into a single picture in depth — the Sun and the storms it launches seen the way two eyes give depth, a coronal mass ejection tracked in three dimensions from the Sun out toward Earth, and, on a day in February 2011 when the twins reached opposite sides of the Sun, the whole of it imaged at once. One craft still keeps watch and returns its half of the view, years after the planned mission ended. They put two eyes on the Sun and resolved it in depth.
