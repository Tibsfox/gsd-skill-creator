## Surprises

- **Two spacecraft, one rocket, two vantage points.** STEREO launched both nearly identical observatories together on a single Delta II 7925-10L on 25 October 2006, then let orbital mechanics part them — STEREO-A onto a slightly smaller, faster orbit that drew ahead of Earth, STEREO-B onto a slightly larger, slower orbit that fell behind — each by ~22° per year, so the stereoscopic baseline widened from near-zero toward 180° as the mission went on.
- **The Sun in depth, the way two eyes give depth.** STEREO gave the first stereoscopic, three-dimensional views of the Sun (first 3-D images released 23 April 2007), combining the views from its two spacecraft the way two eyes triangulate depth — the first time the Sun and the structures above it could be seen in true three dimensions from two simultaneous vantage points.
- **Coronal mass ejections tracked in 3-D from the Sun to Earth.** STEREO made the first 3-D imaging and tracking of coronal mass ejections, following an eruption in three dimensions as it traveled from the Sun out toward Earth through the coronagraphs and the heliospheric imagers — a direct improvement to space-weather forecasting.
- **The entire Sun, seen at once.** On 6 February 2011 the twins reached ~180° separation, on opposite sides of the Sun, and STEREO returned the first simultaneous view of the entire Sun across the full 360° — the whole solar surface imaged at one moment for the first time.
- **A small native ground mantis that judges depth with two eyes.** The Agile Ground Mantis (*Litaneutria minor*), a small, fast, ground-dwelling praying mantis native to the dry interior Pacific Northwest's sagebrush-steppe (the Columbia Plateau of eastern Washington and Oregon, north into British Columbia — Canada's only native mantid), has true stereoscopic (three-dimensional) vision, judging depth by combining the views from its two large, widely-set eyes before it strikes (documented natural history only; distinguished from the introduced European *Mantis religiosa* and Chinese *Tenodera sinensis*) — a mirror of the mission that put two spacecraft on two vantage points to see the Sun in depth.
- **A branched dry-steppe shrub among which the watcher waits.** Antelope Bitterbrush (*Purshia tridentata*), a much-branched dry-country shrub of the interior PNW sagebrush-steppe and open ponderosa slopes (rose family), with small three-lobed ("tridentate") leaves and pale yellow flowers, a key wildlife browse plant (ecological description only, distinguished from sagebrush *Artemisia* and rabbitbrush *Ericameria*) — the kind of fixed dry-steppe shrub among which the stereoscopic ground mantis hunts and waits, bound with STEREO's two-vantage-point view by depth from two eyes.
