### Part B: Black-headed Grosbeak -- The Groove Reset (FIRST CARDINALIDAE, Both-Sex Singing, Acoustic Convergence, Bill as Dual Instrument)

Full deep research covering the Black-headed Grosbeak as the engine's first Cardinalidae, the introduction of both-sex singing, the acoustic convergence concept (grosbeak-robin pair), the groove reset as twenty-fourth acoustic element, and the bill as dual-purpose instrument (seed-cracker and vocal resonator):

- **FIRST CARDINALIDAE -- 15th Family in the Engine, Heavy-Billed Seed-Cracking Lineage:** *Pheucticus melanocephalus* (Swainson, 1827). From Greek *pheuktikos* ("shy") + *melas* (black) + *kephalos* (head). The family Cardinalidae contains approximately 53 species worldwide, defined by heavy conical bills, seed-crushing jaw musculature, and distinctively structured palates. Nine-primaried oscine passerines in the New World tanager clade. The engine now spans 15 families: Ardeidae (0), Troglodytidae (1), Turdidae (2/7/18/19), Paridae (3/9), Strigidae (4), Sittidae (5), Tyrannidae (6), Regulidae (8), Aegithalidae (10), Passerellidae (11), Fringillidae (12), Vireonidae (13), Parulidae (14-17/20), Picidae (21-22), Cardinalidae (23). The grosbeak is the first family outside the warbler-sparrow-finch cluster. Its massive conical bill represents a different ecological strategy -- heavy seed-cracking, fruit-processing, large-insect capture -- that no previous engine species possesses at this scale.

- **THE BILL AS DUAL-PURPOSE INSTRUMENT -- Seed-Cracker AND Vocal Resonator, 35-50 N Bite Force:** Bill depth ~15-17 mm (nearly twice a junco's ~9 mm). Width ~14-16 mm. Jaw adductor muscles generate bite forces estimated at 35-50 N (Herrel et al. 2005; Van der Meij and Bout 2004), among the highest of any North American passerine of comparable body mass. The seed-cracking process: seed placement in lower mandible groove, mandibular occlusion, husk separation by muscular tongue, 2-3 seconds per sunflower seed. But the massive bill has acoustic consequences. In passerines, bill morphology affects the resonant properties of the vocal tract -- the bill acts as a horn, filtering and shaping the frequencies produced by the syrinx. Larger, deeper bills produce lower-frequency, more resonant sounds (Podos 2001; Herrel et al. 2009). The grosbeak's deep bill contributes to the low-frequency richness of its warble -- the "robin-like" quality partly arises because the grosbeak's large bill produces a similarly warm resonance to the robin's equally substantial bill. The bill is simultaneously a tool and a resonator. The seed-cracker IS the sound shaper. Third instance in the engine of anatomy serving dual functions (after the flicker's hyoid and bill at degrees 21-22), but the grosbeak's dual function is more subtle -- the bill shapes the song passively, as a resonator, not actively, as a striker.

- **THE SONG -- Rich Robin-Like Warble With Slurs, 1.5-4.5 kHz, 10-20 Phrases Per Bout:** Fundamental frequency range ~1.5-4.5 kHz, most energy at 2.0-3.5 kHz. Song duration 2-5 seconds (up to 6-8 seconds extended). 10-20 rapidly delivered phrases per bout, each a slurred whistle rising or falling in pitch. Phrases connected without distinct pauses -- continuous stream. Each phrase contains a continuous pitch glide (upslur, downslur, or arcing slur). Tempo ~3-5 phrases/second -- near the perceptual fusion threshold, same boundary region as the Oregon Junco's trill (degree 11). Timbre: rich, warm, full-bodied, substantial low-frequency content, smooth harmonic structure. Less thin than warbler songs (degrees 14-17/20), less ethereal than the Hermit Thrush (degree 7), less buzzy than the Pine Siskin (degree 12). The grosbeak's timbre is warm and rounded -- a quality arising from the bill's acoustic filtering. Register: mid-range, centered on 2.5-3.5 kHz, placing it squarely in the vocal register occupied by the American Robin, the Hermit Thrush, and other rich-voiced woodland passerines.

- **ACOUSTIC CONVERGENCE ACROSS FAMILIES -- Grosbeak-Robin Pair, 40 Million Years of Independent Evolution:** The grosbeak's resemblance to the American Robin's song operates on four levels. Shared frequency range: both center songs in the 2.0-3.5 kHz band. Shared phrase structure: both deliver songs of rapidly alternating rising and falling slurred phrases. Shared rhythmic cadence: both deliver phrases at ~3-5/second. Key differences: the grosbeak has higher phrase variety (rarely repeats within a bout), slightly richer/more mellow tone, continuous flow without phrase groups, and longer songs (2-5+ seconds vs. 2-3 seconds). The convergence is not mimicry. Cardinalidae and Turdidae diverged approximately 40 million years ago. The resemblance arises from convergent acoustic evolution: two species occupying similar acoustic niches in mixed forest, with similarly large bills producing similar vocal-tract resonances, arriving at similar song structures through independent evolutionary paths. The bill shapes the sound. The habitat selects for mid-frequency, rich-toned warbles. Two lineages converged on the same solution. New concept for the engine: acoustic convergence across families. The grosbeak (degree 23) and robin (degree 25) form a convergence pair -- different families, similar sounds.

- **BOTH SEXES SING -- First Species With Elaborate Female Song, Vocal and Reproductive Symmetry:** Males sing loud, elaborate warbles from exposed perches for territorial advertisement. Females also sing -- structurally similar warbles, typically shorter (1-3 seconds vs. 2-5 seconds), softer, less elaborate. Female song occurs on the nest (singing while incubating, documented by Ritchison 1983), in countersinging with the male (alternating bouts during territory establishment and pair bonding), and post-fledging (both parents sing near fledglings). Hypotheses: ancestral retention of dual-sex singing, mate coordination linked to shared incubation, nest defense, and mutual mate assessment in species where both sexes contribute heavily to parental care. The shared incubation pattern is equally remarkable: both sexes develop brood patches, males incubate during midday (~10 AM to 2 PM), females incubate morning/late afternoon/overnight. Males sing while incubating -- the paradox of simultaneously hiding (cryptic nest behavior) and broadcasting (conspicuous territorial song). Odom et al. (2014, *Nature Communications*) established that female song is likely the ancestral state in oscine passerines. The grosbeak retains what many temperate-zone species have lost.

- **THE GROOVE RESET -- 24th Acoustic Element (Part B Side), Melody Returns After Percussion:** After the hammer (degree 21, vocal metronomic pulse) and the drum (degree 22, substrate-dependent broadband percussion), the grosbeak's rich rolling warble resets the engine to tonal melody. The groove reset is not retreat. The melody is richer for having been away. The grosbeak's warble is the most harmonically complex, most varied, most flowing melodic content since the Hermit Thrush at degree 7. In music production, the groove reset is the moment after the drum solo when the full band re-enters. The drums have been soloing (degrees 21-22). Now the melody returns. The groove incorporates everything the engine has learned: melodically rich (like degrees 7 and 18), rhythmically flowing (like degrees 14 and 17), warm-toned (like degree 2), structurally complex (10-20 varied slurred phrases per bout). The sound of the engine exhaling after holding its breath through percussion.

- **MIXED FOREST -- Broadest Ecosystem Category, The Forest Most People Hear First:** Mixed forest encompasses deciduous-conifer woodland, second-growth forest, suburban mature shade-tree corridors, riparian zones (cottonwood, alder, bigleaf maple alongside Douglas fir), and foothill transition zones (oak savanna grading into conifer forest). This is the dominant lowland habitat type in settled PNW wherever fire suppression, development, or natural succession has produced a blend of native deciduous species and conifers. The forest of suburban Seattle, the Willamette Valley margins, the San Juan Island hillsides. The habitat where most people first hear a Black-headed Grosbeak. Acoustic environment: variable canopy structure favoring mid-frequency propagation, moderate reverberation, and in suburban settings, anthropogenic noise that selects for loud, rich, mid-frequency warbles -- precisely what the grosbeak produces.

- **NEOTROPICAL MIGRANT -- Summer Voice, Late April to Mid-September, Peak Song Late May Through Mid-July:** Winters in western Mexico (Pacific slope, Sinaloa to Oaxaca). Males arrive first, establishing territories through song. Females arrive 5-10 days later. Incubation 12-14 days, nestling 11-12 days, one brood per season. Peak song period: approximately 6-8 weeks of full territorial singing. Nine of 22 unique species in the engine are neotropical migrants (41%). The residents provide the year-round acoustic foundation. The migrants provide the summer chorus. The grosbeak is the richest-voiced of the summer arrivals.

- **MONARCH BUTTERFLY PREDATION -- Cardenolide Resistance, Mexican Overwintering Sites:** One of the few bird species that regularly feeds on toxic Monarch butterflies at overwintering sites in central Mexico. Monarchs sequester cardenolides from milkweed. The grosbeak has evolved partial resistance, selectively eating less-toxic abdominal tissues while discarding wing and thorax. The same bird that sings in a Seattle backyard in June feeds on toxic butterflies in a Mexican fir forest in January.

- **BROWN-HEADED COWBIRD RESISTANCE -- Bill as Egg-Ejector, Moderate Rejecter:** Grosbeaks reject approximately 30-40% of experimentally introduced cowbird eggs (Rothstein 1975; Peer and Sealy 2004). Rejection includes egg ejection (grasping the cowbird egg in the massive bill and removing it) and nest desertion. The bill that cracks seeds and shapes song also ejects parasitic eggs. Three functions of the grosbeak bill -- feeding, singing, nest defense -- converge in one anatomical structure.

- **THIRD CONSECUTIVE E=4 (Part B) -- Longest Sustained Energy Since E=2 Plateau, Three Forms of Acoustic Power:** The engine crossed from E=3 to E=4 at degree 21 and has not dropped. The E=4 plateau carries different character than the E=2 plateau (degrees 0-7): E=2 was quiet to moderate sounds in closed-canopy habitats, the engine building vocabulary; E=4 is loud sounds in open to mixed habitats, the engine in full voice. The grosbeak's E=4 is a different quality than the flicker's. The flicker's energy is percussive -- sharp, impulsive, mechanically amplified. The grosbeak's energy is vocal -- sustained, melodic, biologically powered. E=4 through percussion (21-22) and E=4 through rich song (23) demonstrate that high energy does not require a single mechanism.

### The Degree 23 Parallel: Small-Town Voice, Big-Stage Groove

The resonance between Allen Stone and the Black-headed Grosbeak operates on four primary axes:

1. **The groove reset -- melody returning after percussion.** Allen Stone follows Foxy Apollo (percussion-forward Jazz/Funk) in the S36 engine, just as the grosbeak follows the flicker drum in SPS. Voice returns. The groove resets. The drum solo ends. The singer steps to the mic.

2. **Small-town origin to big-stage voice.** Allen Stone came from Chewelah, WA (pop. ~2,600) to Seattle. The grosbeak migrates from Mexican wintering grounds to PNW breeding territories. Both arrive from elsewhere and fill the summer soundscape with powerful vocal presence. The Chewelah-to-Seattle path maps to the Mexico-to-Puget-Sound migration. Rural origin, urban destination, powerful voice.

3. **Energy 4 through vocal richness.** Neither Allen Stone nor the grosbeak achieve their energy through percussion or amplification. The voice IS the instrument. Third consecutive E=4, now carried by melodic power rather than mechanical force. Stone's gospel-trained voice moves bodies in a venue. The grosbeak's rich warble fills a mixed-forest canopy.

4. **Soul as convergent genre.** Soul music converges gospel, R&B, jazz, and blues -- multiple traditions arriving at a unified sound from different origins. The grosbeak's song converges on robin-like character from independent evolutionary origins. Allen Stone's soul sound converges church gospel and secular soul through self-education. The grosbeak's warble converges with the robin's through parallel acoustic evolution. Convergence as creative strategy -- whether the medium is culture or biology.

## Retrospective

### What Worked

- **Allen Stone is extensively documented, and the documentation depth recovered sharply.** 32 sources (vs. 21 at degree 22). KEXP interviews, NPR features, Billboard profiles, Seattle Times articles, BET coverage, Berklee announcement, multiple long-form interviews. Documentation depth correlates with career maturity and label affiliation -- a labeled, nationally-touring artist generates more press than a grassroots local band.

- **The rural origin thread produced the richest structural finding.** Stone being from Chewelah is not merely biographical -- it restructures the engine's geographic model, introduces a new artist-city pattern (The Rural Draw), documents a new trajectory (Boomerang), and forces the engine to reckon with rural-to-urban migration as a distinct category. The most geographically productive degree since the Central District concentration at degrees 0-6.

- **The label analysis filled a 22-degree gap.** Twenty-two degrees without a meaningful label entry, then three label models documented in a single degree (self-released, independent-amplifier, major-director). Stone's career provides the comparison data the engine was missing. The Capitol Records cautionary tale is well-documented in Stone's own words from multiple interviews.

- **The Berklee circle closes naturally.** Jones (0) and Frisell (1) trained at Berklee. Stone (23) was invited to teach there. The coincidence is not forced -- it emerges from the data. The institutional pipeline thread now includes a Berklee node connecting the engine's first degree to its twenty-fourth.

- **New species after the flicker pair is the right structural move.** The engine spent two degrees on one species (Northern Flicker, degrees 21-22). Degree 23 delivers a new species, a new family, and a new sound type. Taxonomic breadth resumes expansion. 22 unique species across 24 degrees.

- **The return to tonal melody after percussion is smooth.** The groove reset works. After broadband percussion (degree 22), the grosbeak's rich warble feels like an exhale -- familiar territory (voice, pitch, harmony) with new richness. The transition from noise to melody is welcome, not abrupt.

- **Acoustic convergence across families opens a genuinely new analytical dimension.** The grosbeak-robin pair is a testable prediction: when the robin arrives at degree 25, the engine will have two data points for the same acoustic solution, generated independently. First controlled comparison of convergent sound in the engine.

- **Both-sex singing is a genuinely new behavioral concept.** No previous species introduced this. The engine's behavioral vocabulary now includes vocal symmetry -- both partners singing, both incubating. Enriches the model of bird communication beyond the male-dominated paradigm.

- **The Part A/B parallel operates on four axes with genuine structural resonance.** Groove reset, small-town to big-stage, vocal richness, convergent genre. The convergence-as-creative-strategy axis (soul as convergent genre, grosbeak as convergent song) is the most elegant: biological convergence paralleling cultural convergence.

- **Still only two non-passerines in twenty-four degrees.** The Great Blue Heron (Pelecaniformes, degree 0) and Northern Flicker (Piciformes, degrees 21-22). The grosbeak returns the engine to Passeriformes. The 20:2 non-passerine ratio is unchanged.

- **Still no marine or shoreline species.** Twenty-four degrees of "The Sound of Puget Sound" without a species from the Sound itself. The engine's most persistent geographic gap.

- **Still no raptor, still no corvid.** No Accipitridae, no Falconidae, no Steller's Jay, no crow, no raven. Among the most visually prominent and acoustically varied PNW birds.

- **Energy 5 remains absent.** Three degrees at E=4 but the engine's maximum energy level has not been reached. A gull, eagle, or corvid could break through.

- **Stone's specific Seattle neighborhood during his Seattle years (roughly 2008-2017) is not documented.** As a rural arrival, he may not have been identified with any single neighborhood. Tag: INFERRED.

## Lessons Learned

1. **The church can function as a conservatory without institutional infrastructure.** Stone's church training provided the two essential elements of formal music education: vocal technique (thousands of hours of singing) and audience comfort (leading worship services). What it did not provide -- theory, peer musicians, professional networks -- Stone built himself. The lesson: institutional music training is one pathway, not the only pathway. The engine should track alternative training pipelines at all subsequent degrees.

2. **The independent label is the sweet spot for soul music.** Stone's career spans self-released, independent (ATO), and major (Capitol). He chose the independent model twice. The major label wanted hits; the soul singer wanted authenticity. The lesson: track label affiliation at all subsequent degrees. Compare independent vs. major models when data permits. ATO's amplifier-not-director model is now the engine's baseline for healthy label relationships.

3. **Internet-mediated career ignition changes the velocity equation.** A viral YouTube video compressed what previously took years of live performance into months. The lesson: track whether subsequent artists show internet-mediated careers. The distinction between pre-internet and post-internet career trajectories may be the engine's most important temporal variable.

4. **Rural origins add a dimension that urban-only analysis cannot capture.** The concept of "the scene" that drove 22 prior degrees does not exist in Chewelah. Stone's trajectory forces the engine to model what happens when there IS no scene -- when the artist must assemble everything from recordings, in isolation, with no institutional support. The lesson: rural origin is not an absence of data; it is a different kind of data.

5. **Acoustic convergence across families is a testable prediction.** The grosbeak sounds like the robin. When the robin arrives at degree 25, the engine can compare them directly -- same acoustic solution, different evolutionary paths. The lesson: the convergence pair concept generates hypotheses that future degrees can test. Track all cross-family vocal resemblances.

6. **Both-sex singing challenges the male-dominated paradigm.** Every previous species with song had male-dominated or male-exclusive singing. The grosbeak breaks this. The lesson: the engine's behavioral vocabulary should not assume male-only song as default. Track female song at all subsequent degrees.

7. **The ADSR cycle's second iteration builds faster than the first.** The first cycle: silence (0) to cascade (1) to drone (2) -- slow accumulation. The second cycle: groove reset (22) to testimony (23) -- two degrees and already has rhythmic foundation plus semantic content. The acceleration maps to accumulated energy: three consecutive E=4 degrees provide momentum the first cycle's E=1-2 degrees did not have.

8. **Scene ecology continues to produce structural insights from consecutive degrees.** Degrees 18-23 (Maktub, FMR, Monge, Polyrhythmics, Foxy Apollo, Stone) now document six consecutive aspects of how a music scene operates: workshop, collective, individual, anchor, inheritor, rural arrival. The lesson: consecutive degrees in the same genre zone generate emergent structural analysis that no single degree can produce.

9. **The bill as dual-purpose instrument (ecological tool + acoustic resonator) is the most elegant morphological finding since the flicker pair.** The same anatomical structure that cracks seeds, processes fruit, and ejects cowbird eggs also shapes the spectral character of the song. The lesson: form follows function follows form. Track dual-function anatomy at all subsequent degrees.

10. **Source depth recovers with career maturity.** 32 sources at degree 23 vs. 21 at degree 22. The emerging-artist documentation gap identified at degree 22 was structural, not universal. Labeled, nationally-touring artists generate more press. The lesson: source depth is itself a variable that tracks career stage. Document it explicitly.
