# BBS Culture Educational Pack

Before the web, before the internet reached most homes, there were bulletin board systems. A modem, a phone line, and a computer running in someone's spare room connected communities that invented online culture from scratch. This pack tells their story.

## What You'll Learn

This pack covers the technical, cultural, and social history of bulletin board systems from their origins in 1978 through their decline in the late 1990s. It's part technical manual, part oral history, and part cultural study.

### The Technical Layer

- **Modem Technology** -- From 300 baud acoustic couplers to 33.6k V.34 modems. How data was encoded in sound, why baud rate and bits-per-second aren't the same thing, and the clever signal processing that squeezed ever more data through copper telephone wire.
- **BBS Software** -- RBBS, TBBS, Wildcat!, PCBoard, Renegade, Telegard, and dozens more. What each system offered, how sysops chose between them, and how the software shaped the communities that used it.
- **FidoNet and Networking** -- The store-and-forward mesh network that connected thousands of BBSes worldwide. Zone, net, node addressing. Echomail, netmail, and the nightly polling schedule that moved messages across continents while long-distance rates were lowest.
- **Door Games** -- Legend of the Red Dragon, TradeWars 2002, Barren Realms Elite, and the ecosystem of multiplayer games that ran as external programs "through the door" of the BBS software.

### The Cultural Layer

- **The Sysop Experience** -- Running a BBS meant dedicating a phone line, managing users, moderating content, and keeping hardware running 24 hours a day. The sysop was simultaneously system administrator, community manager, and host.
- **ANSI Art and the Art Scene** -- The creative explosion of ANSI and ASCII art that turned terminal screens into canvases. Groups like ACiD, iCE, and Blocktronics pushed the medium to its limits, creating elaborate login screens, menu systems, and standalone art packs.
- **The Warez Scene** -- Software piracy, release groups, 0-day distributions, and the complex ethics of a community that simultaneously valued technical skill and transgressed intellectual property law.
- **Phreaking and Hacking** -- The overlap between BBS culture and the telephone hacking community. Blue boxes, red boxes, and the curious relationship between exploring systems and exploiting them.

### The Legacy Layer

- **From BBS to Web** -- How BBS culture directly influenced early web communities, forum software, social media platforms, and online gaming. The patterns established by sysops in the 1980s echo in every Discord server and subreddit today.
- **The Telnet Revival** -- Modern BBSes accessible via telnet and SSH, the retro-computing community that keeps them running, and what's gained and lost when the medium changes but the culture persists.
- **Lessons for Modern Communities** -- What BBS operators learned about community management, content moderation, trust, and identity that Silicon Valley keeps rediscovering the hard way.

## Prerequisites

None. General familiarity with computers is helpful but not required. The technical sections explain everything from the ground up.

## Primary Sources

This pack draws on interviews with former sysops, archived message bases, contemporary publications (Boardwatch Magazine, Computer Shopper BBS listings), and the documentary film BBS: The Documentary by Jason Scott. Where possible, original BBS software and door games are available for hands-on exploration through emulation.

## Estimated Duration

The complete pack is approximately 80 hours of material. The technical and cultural layers can be studied independently or interwoven, depending on your interests.
