# NATURE-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

NATURE-101 assessment focuses on the quality of observation, accuracy of identification, depth of ecological understanding, and commitment to outdoor practice. Assessment emphasizes field skills and journal quality over memorization of facts. The nature journal is the primary portfolio artifact -- a living record of the learner's growing relationship with the natural world.

---

## Formative Assessment (Ongoing)

Continuous feedback to guide learning:

### Nature Journal Review

Review journals regularly (weekly or biweekly). Look for:

- **Accuracy:** Are sketches true to life? Do they capture the right number of petals, the correct leaf arrangement, actual proportions?
- **Detail:** Are entries getting more detailed over time? Are measurements, colors, and textures recorded?
- **Questions:** Does the journal contain "I wonder..." questions? Are questions becoming more specific and investigable?
- **Consistency:** Is the learner journaling regularly? Are date, time, location, and weather recorded?
- **Growth:** Compare early entries to recent ones. Is the naturalist eye developing?

### Observation Checks

During field activities, observe whether the learner:

- Notices things others miss (developing naturalist eye)
- Uses senses systematically rather than casually glancing
- Records observations immediately rather than relying on memory
- Makes predictions before checking ("I think this is an oak because...")
- Returns to earlier observations to add new information

### Probing Questions

Use these questions throughout instruction:

**Observation (M1):**
- "What do you notice that you didn't see at first?"
- "How has your sit spot changed since last week?"
- "What would you need to observe to answer your question?"

**Plants & Fungi (M2):**
- "How do you know this is an oak and not a maple?"
- "Why are mushrooms growing here and not over there?"
- "What will this plant look like in three months?"

**Animals & Insects (M3):**
- "What is that bird doing? Why?"
- "What animal made these tracks? How do you know?"
- "Why are there more insects near the water?"

**Ecosystems (M4):**
- "What would happen if we removed all the decomposers?"
- "Why is this forest different from that meadow?"
- "How does the stream connect to the surrounding land?"

**Conservation (M5):**
- "What threatens this ecosystem?"
- "How does your citizen science data help researchers?"
- "What would it take to restore this habitat?"

### Identification Tracking

Maintain a running list of species the learner can identify:

- **Trees:** How many species? By which field marks?
- **Wildflowers:** How many? Which seasons?
- **Birds:** How many by sight? By sound?
- **Insects:** Which orders? Which families?
- **Fungi:** Any? Which groups?

Track growth in identification confidence and accuracy over time.

## Summative Assessment (End of Module)

### Species Portfolio

Students compile a portfolio of species they can identify in the field:

**Contents:**
- Journal entries documenting each species encounter
- Field sketches with identifying features labeled
- Photographs (if available)
- Habitat and seasonal notes for each species
- At least one "species portrait" -- a detailed entry about a species studied over time

**Criteria:**
- Minimum species count appropriate to grade level (10 for elementary, 25 for middle, 50+ for high school)
- Identifications are accurate (verified by field guide or expert)
- Journal entries include date, location, weather, habitat, and behavioral notes
- Quality matters more than quantity -- depth of knowledge about fewer species trumps a long list of casual sightings

### Ecosystem Investigation

Students conduct a detailed study of one local ecosystem:

**Structure:**
1. **Description:** Where is it? What type of habitat? Physical features (soil, water, topography)?
2. **Species List:** What organisms live here? Producers, consumers, decomposers?
3. **Food Web:** How do species connect? Who eats whom?
4. **Seasonal Changes:** How does the ecosystem change across seasons?
5. **Threats:** What pressures does this ecosystem face?
6. **Recommendations:** What would you do to protect or restore it?

**Criteria:**
- Investigation is based on direct field observation (not internet research alone)
- Species list reflects actual observations at the site
- Food web is accurate and includes multiple trophic levels
- Seasonal changes reflect real observations over time
- Threats are specific and evidence-based (not generic)
- Recommendations are practical and locally appropriate

### Citizen Science Record

Students document their contributions to citizen science platforms:

**Contents:**
- Screenshots or links to iNaturalist/eBird/GLOBE observations
- Summary statistics: how many observations, how many species, identification accuracy rate
- Reflection: What did you learn from community feedback? How did AI and expert identifications change your understanding?
- Analysis: What patterns do you see in your data? What's common? What's rare?

**Criteria:**
- Minimum observation count appropriate to grade level
- Observations include quality photos, accurate locations, and identification attempts
- Learner engages with community feedback (corrects identifications, asks questions)
- Reflection shows learning and growth

### Nature Journal Review (Summative)

Comprehensive review of the nature journal as a whole:

**Criteria:**
- Entries span multiple weeks or months of regular observation
- Entries show progression from simple to detailed
- Sketches improve in accuracy over time
- Questions become more specific and investigable
- Ecological connections between entries are noted
- Journal reflects personal engagement, not just assignment completion

## Rubric Levels

Use these rubric descriptions across all assessments. Adapt as needed for specific assignments.

### Beginning

Student demonstrates initial engagement with nature:

- **Observation:** Notices some features but misses relationships; observations may be superficial or inconsistent
- **Identification:** Can name a few common species (robin, oak, dandelion) but relies on others for identification
- **Journaling:** Entries are brief, infrequent, or lack detail; sketches may not match the actual organism
- **Ecological thinking:** Sees individual organisms but does not connect them to habitat or food web
- **Field skills:** Needs guidance to find organisms; may struggle with patience or sustained attention outdoors
- **Stewardship:** Aware that nature should be protected but cannot articulate specific threats or actions

**Example:** "I saw a bird in a tree. It was brown." (Observation is real but lacks detail, identification, context.)

### Developing

Student demonstrates growing naturalist skills:

- **Observation:** Systematically uses senses; notices details like color, shape, size, texture; beginning to record weather and habitat
- **Identification:** Can identify 10-20 common species using field guides; learning to use field marks
- **Journaling:** Regular entries with sketches that capture key features; measurements and questions appearing; date/time/location recorded
- **Ecological thinking:** Recognizes some relationships (this caterpillar eats this plant; this bird eats insects); beginning to see food chains
- **Field skills:** Can use a hand lens, binoculars, and field guides; developing patience for sit spot practice
- **Stewardship:** Can name specific threats to local ecosystems; understands concept of invasive species; beginning citizen science participation

**Example:** "Song sparrow singing from the top of the elderberry bush (Sambucus nigra). 7:15 AM, 58F, partly cloudy. I think it's defending territory because it returns to the same perch every morning. Three other sparrows nearby but they don't sing from this bush." (Detailed observation with identification, habitat, behavior, and hypothesis.)

### Proficient

Student demonstrates solid naturalist competence:

- **Observation:** Detailed, systematic observations; notices patterns across time and space; designs observations to test hypotheses
- **Identification:** Can identify 30-50+ local species across multiple groups (trees, birds, insects, wildflowers); uses field marks confidently; identifies some species by sound or track
- **Journaling:** Rich, detailed journal with accurate sketches, quantitative data, ecological notes, and thoughtful questions; journal shows clear progression over months
- **Ecological thinking:** Constructs food webs; explains energy flow; identifies ecological relationships (predation, mutualism, parasitism); understands succession and seasonal cycles
- **Field skills:** Can conduct independent field surveys; uses standardized protocols; contributes quality citizen science data
- **Stewardship:** Can assess habitat health; identifies specific invasive species and their impacts; proposes evidence-based conservation actions

**Example:** "Decomposition station week 6: apple 95% gone (visible fungi, insect larvae, darkened soil beneath). Leaf 70% skeletonized (fungal hyphae visible, millipedes and springtails present). Cotton cloth 30% degraded (mold colonies, weakened fibers). Plastic bag 0% change. Rate comparison confirms what I expected: simple organic matter decomposes fastest because decomposer enzymes can break down sugars and cellulose. The plastic polymer chains are too long for any local decomposer. This makes me wonder how microplastics affect soil organisms."

### Advanced

Student demonstrates exceptional naturalist depth:

- **Observation:** Observations are research-quality; designs and executes multi-week observation protocols; notices patterns that contribute new local ecological knowledge
- **Identification:** Extensive species repertoire (100+); identifies difficult groups (warblers by song, grasses by seed head, beetles by family); serves as identification resource for others
- **Journaling:** Journal is a scientific tool; contains original data, phenological records, behavioral observations, and ecological analyses; suitable for sharing with researchers
- **Ecological thinking:** Models ecosystems quantitatively; understands trophic cascades, edge effects, and metapopulation dynamics; connects local observations to biogeographic patterns
- **Field skills:** Conducts independent research projects; data quality meets professional standards; mentors younger naturalists
- **Stewardship:** Designs and implements conservation projects; communicates effectively with land managers; understands policy and management trade-offs

**Example:** "Two-year phenology comparison for sugar maple (Acer saccharum) at my sit spot: bud break 2025 April 8, 2026 March 29 (10 days earlier). Leaf-out 2025 April 22, 2026 April 14 (8 days earlier). This matches NOAA temperature data showing March 2026 was 2.1C warmer than March 2025. The earlier leaf-out means the maple is photosynthesizing 8-10 extra days before competing canopy trees shade it. I submitted this observation to USA-NPN (National Phenology Network) and it matches regional trends of advancing spring phenology at 2.3 days per decade."

## Formative Assessment Strategies

### Observation Checklists

Use during field activities:

- [ ] Learner makes detailed observations before identifying
- [ ] Learner records observations in journal with date, time, weather
- [ ] Learner sketches accurately (counts petals, captures proportions)
- [ ] Learner asks "I wonder..." questions
- [ ] Learner notices things without prompting
- [ ] Learner connects current observation to previous knowledge

### Quick Prompts

5-minute ungraded reflections:

- "Sketch the last bird you saw. What was it doing?"
- "Name three trees you can identify. How do you tell them apart?"
- "What's different about your sit spot this week vs. last week?"
- "Draw a food chain that includes an organism you observed today"

### Peer Teaching

Structured knowledge sharing:

- "Teach your partner to identify this tree. What are the field marks?"
- "Show your partner your favorite journal entry. Explain what you observed and what you learned."
- "Compare your species lists. What did one person find that the other missed?"

### Self-Assessment

Student reflection:

- "What am I getting better at? What still challenges me?"
- "Look at my first journal entry and my most recent. What's different?"
- "Where do I see myself on the rubric? What would move me to the next level?"
- "What species do I want to learn to identify next?"

## Portfolio Suggestions

### The Nature Journal Portfolio

The nature journal itself IS the portfolio. Supplement with:

- **Species list:** Running list of all identified species with dates and locations
- **Best-of selections:** 5-10 journal entries that show strongest work
- **Growth comparison:** Early entry alongside recent entry with reflection on growth
- **Citizen science records:** Screenshots or printouts of iNaturalist/eBird submissions
- **Project documentation:** Ecosystem study, conservation project, or research findings

### Digital Supplement (Optional)

- Photographs of organisms observed
- Audio recordings of birdsong or frog calls
- Time-lapse photos of decomposition or plant growth
- Maps of survey routes and species locations

## Grading Guidance

### Formative Work

Do not grade formative assessments (journal checks, observation checklists, quick prompts). They are for feedback and growth, not judgment. Provide specific, encouraging feedback:

- Good: "Your oak sketch captures the lobed leaves perfectly. Next time, try adding the bark texture too -- it's one of the best ways to identify oaks in winter."
- Vague: "Nice drawing!"

### Summative Work

Grade summative assessments on demonstrated learning, not on artistic skill or handwriting:

- Does the journal show regular outdoor observation?
- Are identifications accurate?
- Does the learner explain ecological relationships?
- Is there evidence of growth over time?

### Weight

Suggested breakdown:
- Nature journal quality and consistency: 40%
- Species identification portfolio: 25%
- Ecosystem investigation or citizen science project: 25%
- Class participation and field behavior: 10%

Adjust based on your situation.

## Special Considerations

### Differentiation

- **For reluctant outdoor learners:** Start with short, structured activities; build toward longer independent observation
- **For strong artists:** Channel into scientific illustration; emphasize accuracy alongside beauty
- **For strong writers:** Encourage detailed written observations alongside sketches
- **For strong in science:** Deepen quantitative analysis, ecological modeling, research design
- **For students with mobility limitations:** Adapt outdoor activities; use window observations, accessible trails, and close-to-building habitats

### Weather and Seasons

Nature study happens in all weather. Assessment should reflect this:

- Rain activities are different from sunny-day activities (fungi, worms, streamflow)
- Winter identification (by bark and bud) is a different skill from summer identification (by leaf and flower)
- Short daylight hours in winter require scheduling adjustments
- Extreme weather (severe storms, extreme cold) may require indoor journal review sessions

### Safety

Assess field safety as part of naturalist competence:

- Can the learner identify poison ivy/oak/sumac?
- Does the learner check for ticks after outdoor sessions?
- Does the learner maintain safe distance from wildlife?
- Does the learner tell others where they're going before field work?
