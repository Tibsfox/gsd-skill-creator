# NATURE-101: Nature Studies & Naturalism -- Foundational Knowledge Pack

**Date:** 2026-02-20
**Status:** Initial Vision
**Depends on:** SCI-101 (Scientific Inquiry), ENVR-101 (Environmental Science & Sustainability)
**Context:** Nature studies is the oldest form of science -- observing, wondering, recording, and understanding the living world around you. This pack serves anyone who wants to learn to read the landscape like a book: families, homeschoolers, classrooms, youth groups, and lifelong learners who want to deepen their connection to the natural world through patient, disciplined observation.

---

## Vision

Before there were laboratories, there were naturalists walking through forests with notebooks. Charles Darwin spent years observing barnacles. John Muir filled journals with sketches of glaciers and wildflowers. Rachel Carson turned tidepool observations into a revolution in environmental awareness. Maria Sibylla Merian painted caterpillars transforming into butterflies in seventeenth-century Suriname. The tradition of naturalism -- going outside, looking carefully, asking questions, and recording what you find -- is the foundation upon which all life sciences were built.

This pack revives that tradition. Nature studies is the art and science of paying attention to the natural world. It teaches you to slow down, observe closely, ask good questions, and keep a nature journal. It develops identification skills -- knowing the trees, birds, insects, and wildflowers around you by name and habit. It builds ecological literacy -- understanding how living things connect to each other and to their habitat. And it cultivates stewardship -- the deep, personal connection to wild places that makes people want to protect them.

Nature studies is not about memorizing species lists or passing identification quizzes. It is about developing a relationship with the living world. A naturalist notices the first robin of spring, knows which direction moss grows on trees (and why the old rule is wrong), can identify the owl calling at dusk, and understands why the creek runs muddy after a storm. These are not trivial accomplishments -- they represent a deep and practical understanding of how the world works.

## Problem Statement

Children are increasingly disconnected from the natural world. Richard Louv named this "nature deficit disorder" -- a generation that spends more time indoors with screens than outdoors with trees. The consequences are measurable: reduced physical activity, increased anxiety, diminished attention spans, and a growing inability to identify even common local species.

Science education has compounded the problem by moving learning indoors. Students study ecosystems from textbooks and diagrams rather than by sitting quietly in a forest. They learn about photosynthesis from animations rather than by watching a seed sprout in their own garden. Field skills -- tracking, plant identification, bird watching, nature journaling -- are rarely taught in schools because they don't fit neatly into standardized tests.

The result is a paradox: students can name a hundred Pokemon but not ten local trees. They know about endangered species on other continents but can't identify the birds in their own backyard. Environmental awareness exists in the abstract -- students know they should "save the planet" -- but ecological understanding, the actual knowledge of how ecosystems work in the places where they live, is thin.

This pack addresses the gap directly. It puts students outside. It teaches them to observe before they theorize, to sketch before they label, and to wonder before they search for answers. It builds the field skills that transform passive nature appreciation into active ecological literacy. And it connects to the great tradition of naturalism that produced some of humanity's most profound scientific insights.

## Core Concepts

The five essential ideas that everything in this pack builds from:

1. **Observation & Field Skills:** The foundation of all natural history. Learning to use your senses systematically: seeing color, shape, movement, and pattern; hearing birdsong and insect sounds; smelling soil and flowers; feeling bark and leaf texture. Developing the patience to sit still and watch. Building the discipline to record what you observe in a nature journal with sketches, notes, and questions.

2. **Plant Identification & Botany:** Knowing the green world around you. Learning to identify trees by bark, leaf shape, and silhouette. Recognizing wildflowers by petal count, color, and habitat. Understanding fungi as decomposers. Following the seasons of growth: bud break in spring, fruiting in summer, seed dispersal in autumn, dormancy in winter. Learning which plants are edible, which are medicinal, and which are dangerous.

3. **Animal Behavior & Zoology:** Knowing the animals that share your landscape. Learning to identify birds by sight, sound, and flight pattern. Studying insects -- the most diverse and ecologically important group of animals. Reading mammal tracks and signs. Understanding animal behavior: territory, migration, nesting, foraging, predation, and symbiosis. Building the habit of watching quietly instead of chasing.

4. **Ecology & Ecosystems:** Understanding how living things connect. Learning food webs -- who eats whom. Understanding the water cycle, nutrient cycling, and decomposition. Recognizing that every organism has a role. Seeing how removing one species affects the whole community. Understanding habitat: what makes a forest different from a meadow, a pond different from a stream, a desert different from a rainforest.

5. **Stewardship & Conservation:** Caring for wild places. Understanding what threatens ecosystems: habitat loss, pollution, invasive species, climate change. Learning practical conservation skills: invasive species removal, habitat restoration, citizen science data collection. Developing the ethic that knowledge of the natural world carries responsibility to protect it. Contributing to community science projects that produce real data for real conservation decisions.

## Skill Tree Architecture

Nature studies builds progressively from sensory awareness to scientific contribution:

```
Foundation (K-2)
  +-- Sensory Exploration (seeing, hearing, touching, smelling nature)
  +-- Nature Walks (guided observation in familiar places)
  +-- Simple Identification (big categories: tree, flower, bird, bug, mammal)
  +-- Nature Journaling Begins (drawings, colors, "I noticed..." sentences)
  +-- Seasonal Awareness (what changes month to month)

Elementary (3-5)
  +-- Systematic Observation (using hand lenses, binoculars, field guides)
  +-- Plant Identification (10+ local trees, 10+ wildflowers by name)
  +-- Bird Watching Basics (10+ common birds by sight and sound)
  +-- Insect Study (life cycles, habitat preferences, collecting techniques)
  +-- Nature Journaling Develops (field sketches, measurement, questions)
  +-- Food Web Understanding (who eats whom in local ecosystems)
  +-- Habitat Comparison (forest vs. meadow vs. wetland vs. urban)

Middle School (6-8)
  +-- Advanced Identification (using dichotomous keys, regional guides)
  +-- Animal Tracking & Sign Reading (prints, scat, nests, burrows)
  +-- Ecosystem Analysis (transects, quadrats, species surveys)
  +-- Phenology (timing of biological events across seasons)
  +-- Citizen Science Participation (iNaturalist, eBird, etc.)
  +-- Nature Journaling Matures (scientific illustration, data tables)
  +-- Ecology Concepts (succession, carrying capacity, niche, trophic levels)

High School (9-12)
  +-- Research-Quality Field Methods (point counts, mark-recapture, plots)
  +-- Statistical Analysis of Field Data (diversity indices, population estimates)
  +-- Biogeography (why species are where they are)
  +-- Conservation Biology (endangered species, habitat fragmentation)
  +-- Restoration Ecology (active habitat management)
  +-- Nature Journaling as Scientific Tool (field notebooks for research)
  +-- Independent Research Projects

College+ (13+)
  +-- Professional Field Techniques (GPS, GIS, remote sensing)
  +-- Taxonomic Specialization (botany, ornithology, entomology, mycology)
  +-- Ecological Modeling & Quantitative Ecology
  +-- Conservation Planning & Policy
  +-- Contribution to Scientific Literature
  +-- Mentoring the Next Generation of Naturalists
```

## Module 1: Outdoor Observation -- Field Skills, Nature Journaling, and Using Your Senses

### What It Teaches

- How to observe systematically using all five senses
- The discipline of sitting still and watching
- Nature journaling: sketching, writing, measuring, and questioning
- Weather awareness: reading clouds, wind, temperature, and seasons
- Basic orientation: compass directions, sun position, landmarks
- The difference between looking and seeing

### Interactive Elements

- **Activity: Sit Spot -- 15 Minutes of Silence.** Find a comfortable spot outdoors. Sit still for 15 minutes. Don't move. Don't talk. Just observe. When the time is up, draw and write about everything you noticed -- sounds, movements, smells, textures, colors, temperatures, wind direction. Repeat at the same spot weekly and track how observations deepen over time.

- **Activity: Nature Journal First Entry.** Use a blank notebook. Write the date, time, location, weather, and temperature at the top. Sketch one thing you observe -- a leaf, a bird, a rock, a cloud. Label your sketch with colors, measurements, and questions. Don't worry about artistic quality; worry about accuracy. A field sketch that captures the right number of petals is more valuable than a beautiful drawing with the wrong number.

- **Activity: Sensory Walk.** Walk a familiar path but focus on one sense at a time. First walk: only listen. Second walk: only look at colors. Third walk: only feel textures. Fourth walk: only smell. Compare notes. What did you discover when you focused? What do you normally miss?

- **Exploration Point:** How does your sit spot change across the seasons? What's different about the same place in January vs. July? Track temperature, leaf cover, animal activity, and plant growth weekly for at least one full season.

### Technical Implementation Notes

This module emphasizes outdoor time. Activities cannot be completed indoors. The nature journal is the primary artifact -- a living document that grows throughout the learner's time with this pack. Skill-creator should observe journaling frequency, detail quality, and question sophistication over time. Dashboard could track "days outside" and "journal entries" as primary metrics.

## Module 2: Plants & Fungi -- Identification, Life Cycles, Seasons, and the Green World

### What It Teaches

- Tree identification by bark, leaf shape, branching pattern, and silhouette
- Wildflower identification by petal count, color, habitat, and bloom time
- Grass, fern, and moss recognition
- Fungi: mushrooms, bracket fungi, molds, and their role as decomposers
- Seeds, fruits, and dispersal mechanisms
- Seasonal cycles: dormancy, bud break, flowering, fruiting, leaf drop
- Basic plant anatomy: roots, stems, leaves, flowers, seeds
- Edible and medicinal plants (with safety emphasis)
- Invasive plants and their ecological impacts

### Interactive Elements

- **Activity: Tree Identification Walk.** Visit a park, forest, or neighborhood with at least 10 tree species. Use a field guide or app to identify each tree. For each tree, record: name, leaf shape (simple or compound, lobed or unlobed), bark texture, branching pattern, and approximate height. Make a bark rubbing and a leaf pressing. Return in each season to observe how the tree changes.

- **Activity: Seed Collection & Sprouting.** Collect seeds from at least five different plants in autumn. Record where each seed was found, what it looks like, and how it might disperse (wind, water, animal, gravity). Plant seeds in pots and observe germination. Which germinate first? Which need cold stratification? Journal the growth process with sketches and measurements.

- **Activity: Fungus Hunt.** After a rain, search for fungi in your area -- on fallen logs, in leaf litter, on living trees, in lawns. Sketch each fungus you find. Note color, size, shape, gill or pore structure, substrate, and location. Photograph specimens for identification (never eat wild mushrooms without expert guidance). Discuss: why do fungi appear after rain? What are they doing for the ecosystem?

- **Exploration Point:** Choose one tree and observe it every week for an entire year. Record the date, temperature, and what the tree looks like. When do buds appear? When do leaves unfurl? When do flowers open? When do fruits form? When do leaves change color? When do they fall? This "tree year" is one of the most powerful nature study projects.

### Technical Implementation Notes

Plant identification is highly regional. The pack should support customization for different biomes and geographic regions. Skill-creator could track identification accuracy and species repertoire growth. Consider integration with iNaturalist for real-time identification feedback. Seasonal activities need to be flagged by hemisphere and climate zone.

## Module 3: Animals & Insects -- Bird Watching, Insect Study, Mammal Tracking, and Animal Behavior

### What It Teaches

- Bird identification by sight, sound, flight pattern, and habitat
- Bird behavior: feeding, singing, nesting, territory, migration
- Insect identification by body plan (beetles, butterflies, bees, flies, dragonflies, etc.)
- Insect life cycles: complete and incomplete metamorphosis
- Mammal tracking: footprints, scat, trails, dens, feeding signs
- Reptile and amphibian identification in local habitats
- Animal behavior observation: feeding, resting, social interaction, alarm calls
- Nocturnal animals: owls, bats, moths, fireflies
- The ethics of wildlife observation: distance, disturbance, and respect

### Interactive Elements

- **Activity: Backyard Bird Count.** Choose a 30-minute window. Sit in one spot and record every bird you see or hear. Note species (use a field guide or app), count, behavior (feeding, singing, flying, perching), and direction. Repeat weekly at the same time and location. Over months, see how the bird community changes with seasons. Submit data to eBird for citizen science.

- **Activity: Bug Hotel Builder.** Build a habitat for solitary bees, beetles, and other beneficial insects using natural materials: hollow stems, pine cones, bark, drilled wood blocks, straw. Place it in a sheltered spot in the garden. Observe which insects move in over weeks and months. Sketch and identify the residents. Discuss: why do these insects need shelter? What do they do for the ecosystem?

- **Activity: Night Walk.** On a warm evening, go outside with a flashlight (red filter preferred to preserve night vision). Listen for owls, tree frogs, and crickets. Look for moths at porch lights. Search for fireflies in summer. Observe bats at dusk. Use a headlamp to spot spider eyes reflecting in the grass. Record everything you find. Discuss: what animals are active at night? Why?

- **Exploration Point:** Choose one animal species and become an expert on it. Read about its biology, habitat, diet, behavior, and conservation status. Observe it in the field whenever possible. Keep a journal of sightings, behaviors, and questions. Present your findings as a "species portrait" with illustrations and field notes.

### Technical Implementation Notes

Bird identification benefits from audio integration -- birdsong recordings linked to species. Mammal tracking requires regional track guides. Insect identification is complex; focus on order-level recognition before species-level. Skill-creator could track species lists and identification confidence. Ethical observation guidelines should be woven into every activity.

## Module 4: Ecosystems & Connections -- Food Webs, Water Cycles, Soil Ecology, and Ecological Relationships

### What It Teaches

- Food webs and trophic levels: producers, herbivores, predators, decomposers
- Energy flow through ecosystems
- Water cycle at the local level: rain, runoff, infiltration, evaporation
- Soil ecology: soil organisms, decomposition, nutrient cycling
- Biomes and habitats: what makes each ecosystem unique
- Ecological relationships: predation, competition, mutualism, parasitism, commensalism
- Succession: how ecosystems change over time after disturbance
- The role of keystone species
- Seasonal cycles and their ecological drivers
- Microhabitats: the world under a rock, inside a log, at the edge of a pond

### Interactive Elements

- **Activity: Food Web Card Game.** Create cards for organisms in your local ecosystem: plants, herbivores, predators, decomposers, fungi, bacteria. On each card write the organism's name, what it eats, and what eats it. Connect the cards with string to create a food web. Pull out one card (simulate extinction) and trace what happens to every connected organism. Which removals cause the most disruption? Why?

- **Activity: Decomposition Observation Station.** Set up a decomposition experiment: place different materials (apple, leaf, paper, plastic, bread, cotton) on the ground under a mesh cover. Check weekly. Record which items decompose first, what organisms appear (fungi, insects, worms), and how the materials change in color, texture, and mass. After 8 weeks, compare results. Discuss: what does decomposition do for the ecosystem?

- **Activity: Stream Study.** Visit a local stream or pond. Observe and record: water speed, clarity, temperature, bottom substrate (rocks, sand, mud). Look for aquatic organisms: dragonfly larvae, caddisfly cases, water striders, fish, snails, algae. Sketch what you find. Discuss: what does the presence of these organisms tell you about water quality? Clean streams have different communities than polluted ones.

- **Exploration Point:** Map the ecosystem within a one-meter square. Use string to mark off a quadrat on the ground. Identify and count every living thing inside: plants, insects, spiders, worms, fungi, mosses. Draw a map showing where each organism is. Estimate the total number of organisms per square meter, then extrapolate to your whole yard, park, or forest. The numbers are often astonishing.

### Technical Implementation Notes

Ecosystem concepts require building from concrete (direct observation) to abstract (system diagrams, mathematical models). Younger learners observe and describe; older learners quantify and model. Skill-creator should observe the progression from "I see a caterpillar eating a leaf" to "herbivory reduces primary productivity by X%." Water quality monitoring could integrate with real citizen science databases.

## Module 5: Conservation & Citizen Science -- Habitat Stewardship, Data Collection, and Restoration

### What It Teaches

- Threats to ecosystems: habitat loss, fragmentation, pollution, invasive species, climate change
- Conservation principles: protection, restoration, management
- Citizen science: contributing real data to real research
- Invasive species identification and management
- Habitat restoration techniques
- Data collection protocols: systematic observation, standardized methods
- Environmental ethics: land ethic, indigenous knowledge, intergenerational responsibility
- Local conservation: what's happening in your watershed, your county, your state
- Career paths in natural history and conservation

### Interactive Elements

- **Activity: Citizen Science Data Submission.** Register for iNaturalist (free). Go outside and photograph organisms you find. Upload photos with location, date, and your identification. Let the community verify or correct your ID. Over weeks, build a personal species list. Discuss: how does your data help scientists? How do aggregated observations reveal patterns? Explore the iNaturalist map of your area to see what others have found.

- **Activity: Trail Clean-Up & Habitat Survey.** Combine community service with science. Walk a trail and collect litter (wear gloves, bring bags). Simultaneously survey the habitat: what species are present? Where is habitat degraded? Where is it healthy? Map the trail showing clean areas and problem areas. Present findings to a local conservation group or park authority with specific recommendations.

- **Activity: Invasive Species Survey.** Learn to identify 3-5 invasive species in your area (consult your state's invasive species list). Walk through a natural area and map where invasives are found. Measure their coverage. Discuss: where did these species come from? Why are they successful? What damage do they cause? What are the options for control? If appropriate, participate in an organized removal effort.

- **Exploration Point:** Design and carry out a small conservation project. Examples: build and install nest boxes for cavity-nesting birds; create a pollinator garden with native plants; monitor a stream for water quality over several months; map the urban tree canopy in your neighborhood. Document the project in your nature journal. Share results with a local conservation organization.

### Technical Implementation Notes

Citizen science integration is key to this module. Activities should connect to real platforms (iNaturalist, eBird, GLOBE Observer). Data collection protocols must be standardized enough to produce usable data. Skill-creator should track progression from casual observation to protocol-adherent data collection. Conservation ethics require careful treatment -- emphasize evidence-based stewardship, not activism.

## Assessment Framework

### How Do We Know Progress Is Happening?

| Level | Indicator | Assessment Method |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| Beginning | Can name a few common species; makes simple observations | Conversation, journal review, guided walks |
| Developing | Uses field guides; journals regularly; notices seasonal changes | Journal quality, identification accuracy, observation detail |
| Proficient | Identifies 50+ local species; explains ecological relationships; contributes citizen science data | Species lists, ecosystem models, data submissions, independent projects |
| Advanced | Conducts independent field research; mentors others; contributes to conservation | Research quality, mentoring effectiveness, conservation impact |

### Nature Journal as Primary Portfolio

The nature journal is the single most important artifact in this pack. It is both the learning tool and the evidence of learning:

- **Quantity:** How often does the learner journal? Weekly? Daily?
- **Detail:** Are sketches accurate? Do they capture the right number of petals, the correct leaf arrangement, the actual proportions?
- **Questions:** Does the journal contain questions? "Why does this bird sing at dawn?" "What caused these tracks?" "Why are mushrooms growing on this log?"
- **Growth:** Compare early entries to later ones. Is observation becoming more detailed? Are identifications more confident? Are ecological connections being noticed?

### Formative Assessment (During Learning)

- Can the learner sit still for 15 minutes and make productive observations?
- Does the learner use field guides or identification resources independently?
- Does the learner notice things others miss? (Emerging naturalist eye)
- Can the learner explain why something is the way it is? (Moving from observation to understanding)
- Does the learner ask questions that could be investigated? (Scientific thinking)

### Summative Assessment (Evidence of Mastery)

- **Species Portfolio:** A collection of 20-50+ species the learner can identify, with journal entries showing when, where, and how each was identified. Quality matters more than quantity.
- **Ecosystem Study:** A detailed investigation of one local ecosystem: species list, food web, habitat description, seasonal changes, threats, and conservation recommendations.
- **Citizen Science Record:** Documentation of data submitted to citizen science platforms, with reflection on what the data contributes.
- **Nature Journal Review:** Assessment of journal quality over time, looking for growth in observation detail, identification accuracy, ecological understanding, and question sophistication.

## Parent Guidance

### If You Don't Know Nature...

You don't need to be a naturalist to teach nature studies. You need curiosity and willingness to go outside. The single most effective thing you can do is say: "I don't know -- let's find out." When your child asks "What kind of bird is that?" and you have no idea, that's a perfect learning moment. Get a field guide. Download Merlin Bird ID. Look it up together.

Nature studies is one of the few subjects where parent and child can genuinely learn together. You don't need to be ahead of your student. You need to be beside them, modeling curiosity and persistence.

### Key Phrases to Encourage

- "What do you notice?"
- "Let's look more closely."
- "I wonder why..."
- "What else do you see?"
- "Can you draw that?"
- "Let's come back and check on it."
- "What's different from last time?"
- "I don't know -- let's find out."

### Common Misconceptions

- **"Nature study means going to a nature center or park."** Nature is everywhere -- your yard, a vacant lot, a crack in the sidewalk. Urban nature is still nature. Start where you are.

- **"You need to know species names to do nature study."** Names are useful but not essential. Observation comes first. You can study an unknown flower for weeks before identifying it -- and you'll learn more that way than if you looked up the name immediately.

- **"Children need to be entertained outdoors."** Boredom is productive. When children run out of things to do, they start noticing things. The sit spot practice works because it deliberately removes stimulation and forces attention on what's actually there.

- **"Nature study requires wilderness."** Most naturalists do their best work close to home. Darwin studied earthworms in his own garden. Your backyard ecosystem is as complex and worthy of study as any national park.

- **"Dangerous animals make outdoor study unsafe."** Know your local hazards (ticks, venomous snakes, poison ivy) and take appropriate precautions. The risk of outdoor study is dramatically overstated. The risk of never going outside is dramatically understated.

### When to Get Help

If your learner is developing a serious interest in a specific area (ornithology, botany, entomology, mycology), connect them with:
- Local naturalist clubs and Audubon chapters
- University extension programs and herbaria
- State parks and nature centers with education programs
- Museum collections and volunteer programs
- Master Naturalist certification programs (available in most U.S. states)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can someone learn nature studies without going outside?**
A: No. That would be like learning to swim without water. Books, videos, and apps are supplements, not substitutes. The core of nature study is direct observation of the living world. You must go outside. Start with 15 minutes in your yard.

**Q: How long does it take to go through this pack?**
A: Nature study is a lifelong practice, not a course to complete. Foundation activities (K-2) might take 20-40 hours of outdoor time. Building identification skills (3-5) takes a year of regular outdoor observation. Developing ecological literacy (6-8) takes two or more years. Becoming a skilled naturalist is the work of decades, and that's part of the beauty -- there's always more to learn.

**Q: Is this for homeschooling or classroom use?**
A: Both, and also for youth groups, families, scouts, nature clubs, and independent learners. The pack works anywhere someone is willing to go outside regularly and observe carefully. Homeschoolers have a natural advantage -- flexible schedules allow outdoor time in all weathers and seasons. Classrooms may need to schedule regular outdoor sessions.

**Q: My child hates bugs. Can we skip the insect module?**
A: Insects are the most diverse and ecologically important group of animals on Earth. Skipping them means missing most of the animal kingdom. That said, start where your child is comfortable. Begin with butterflies and dragonflies (usually well-received) and work toward beetles and spiders. Familiarity often converts fear into fascination. The bug hotel activity is a great icebreaker -- building a home for insects makes them less frightening.

**Q: Do we need expensive equipment?**
A: No. The essential equipment is: a notebook, a pencil, and time outdoors. Optional but helpful: a hand lens (magnifying glass, $5-10), binoculars ($20-100), and a field guide for your region ($15-25). A smartphone camera is useful for identification. Everything else is optional. The best naturalists in history worked with paper and pen.

**Q: Is nature study just for rural areas?**
A: Absolutely not. Urban nature is rich and fascinating. Cities have birds, insects, trees, weeds, fungi, and complex ecosystems. A vacant lot is an ecosystem. A crack in the sidewalk is a microhabitat. Pigeons, sparrows, and starlings exhibit complex behaviors worth studying. Urban nature study often reveals surprising biodiversity -- and it's the nature most people actually live with.

**Q: How is this different from environmental science (ENVR-101)?**
A: ENVR-101 teaches environmental systems from the top down -- climate, cycles, pollution, sustainability. NATURE-101 works from the bottom up -- individual species, local ecosystems, hands-on field skills. ENVR-101 gives you the framework to understand global environmental challenges. NATURE-101 gives you the field knowledge to understand the ecosystem in your own backyard. They complement each other beautifully.

**Q: What about safety outdoors?**
A: Know your local hazards and prepare accordingly. Learn to identify poison ivy/oak/sumac. Check for ticks after outdoor sessions. Know what venomous snakes live in your area and how to avoid them. Carry water and sun protection. Tell someone where you're going. These precautions are simple and effective. The outdoors is not inherently dangerous -- it's just a different environment that requires appropriate awareness.

**Q: Can adults use this pack?**
A: Yes. Many adults have never learned to identify the trees on their street or the birds at their feeder. Nature study is equally valuable at any age. Some of the greatest naturalists began their serious observation as adults. The sit spot practice, nature journaling, and identification skills are enriching at every stage of life.
