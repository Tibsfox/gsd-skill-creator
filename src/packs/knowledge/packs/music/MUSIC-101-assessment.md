# MUSIC-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

MUSIC-101 assessment focuses on **musical understanding and expression**, not technical accuracy or performance anxiety. We assess whether learners can:

- Feel and maintain rhythm
- Think in pitches
- Understand how sounds work together
- Listen actively
- Create something original

We do NOT assess whether their voice is "trained," whether they can play complex repertoire, or whether they achieve concert-level precision.

---

## Rubric Levels

Assessment in MUSIC-101 uses four levels of proficiency:

### Beginning
The learner is exploring and building foundational skills. They may not yet show consistent success, but they are engaged and trying.

**Rhythm:**
- Maintains a steady beat for short periods with support
- May lose the beat with tempo changes
- Responds rhythmically to music but may lag or rush

**Melody & Pitch:**
- Sings with approximate pitch; may be off by several notes
- Recognizes high/low pitch differences
- Matches simple pitches with effort

**Harmony & Ensemble:**
- Listens to others but doesn't yet coordinate well
- May struggle to sing a different part than the melody
- Participates in ensemble but may blend with dominant melody

**Listening & Analysis:**
- Hears music and responds emotionally
- Notices some musical elements (loud/soft, fast/slow)
- May struggle to identify form or structure

**Creation:**
- Explores with instruments and voice
- Creates short patterns or melodies with prompting
- Shares work but may be self-conscious

### Developing
The learner shows increasing consistency and skill. They understand core concepts and can apply them with moderate independence.

**Rhythm:**
- Maintains a steady beat reliably in most tempos
- Distinguishes pulse from rhythm
- Creates simple rhythmic patterns that others can follow
- Notates basic rhythm with some accuracy

**Melody & Pitch:**
- Sings mostly in tune with familiar melodies
- Identifies scale patterns and solfège syllables
- Matches pitches with increasing accuracy
- Begins to understand intervals

**Harmony & Ensemble:**
- Sings a different part with some independence
- Maintains their part while listening to others
- Understands basic chord construction
- Blends reasonably well in small groups

**Listening & Analysis:**
- Identifies form structures (verse/chorus, ABA)
- Recognizes genres
- Notices instrumentation and orchestration
- Discusses emotional response with some musical vocabulary

**Creation:**
- Composes simple melodies and song ideas
- Uses digital tools with support
- Improvises over simple chord progressions
- Accepts feedback and attempts revision

### Proficient
The learner demonstrates consistent skill and understanding. They can work independently and apply knowledge to new situations.

**Rhythm:**
- Maintains a steady beat across all tempos and styles
- Reads and interprets rhythm notation accurately
- Creates and leads rhythmic patterns for groups
- Understands and performs syncopation and polyrhythm
- Improvises rhythmic variations while maintaining pulse

**Melody & Pitch:**
- Sings in tune with expression and control
- Recognizes and creates melodic contours
- Uses solfège to think in pitches
- Understands intervals and their functions
- Improvises melodic passages

**Harmony & Ensemble:**
- Sings multiple parts with accuracy and independence
- Understands chord progressions and harmonic function
- Arranges melodies for different ensembles
- Blends seamlessly in group settings
- Helps others find their parts

**Listening & Analysis:**
- Identifies complex forms and unusual structures
- Discusses music using accurate terminology
- Analyzes how compositional choices create effect
- Appreciates music across cultures and genres
- Makes informed interpretive choices

**Creation:**
- Composes multi-section pieces with intention
- Uses digital tools effectively and creatively
- Improvises convincingly in different styles
- Revises work systematically based on feedback
- Collaborates productively with peers

### Advanced
The learner shows sophisticated understanding and skill. They make creative choices, help others learn, and might pursue music further.

**Rhythm:**
- Understands and performs complex rhythmic structures
- Creates polyrhythmic compositions
- Arranges rhythms for ensemble
- Teaches rhythm concepts to others
- Makes insightful interpretive choices about tempo and feel

**Melody & Pitch:**
- Sings with artistic expression and advanced technique
- Composes melodies with intentional contour and phrasing
- Thinks in multiple keys and modes
- Understands and applies voice leading principles
- Improvises sophisticatedly in various styles

**Harmony & Ensemble:**
- Creates harmonic arrangements with interest and sophistication
- Understands advanced harmony (secondary dominants, substitutions, etc.)
- Leads ensemble rehearsals and helps others
- Makes informed orchestration choices
- Composes and arranges for varied ensembles

**Listening & Analysis:**
- Identifies subtle compositional techniques
- Discusses music with nuance and depth
- Understands historical and cultural contexts deeply
- Critiques performances with insight and respect
- Recognizes connections between musical styles

**Creation:**
- Composes complex, coherent pieces
- Uses digital tools with mastery
- Improvises creatively in multiple styles
- Mentors others through the composition process
- Considers how their music affects listeners

## Formative Assessment

Formative assessment happens **ongoing** during instruction. It is informal observation that guides teaching decisions.

### Listening & Response
- Do students respond rhythmically to music?
- Do they attempt activities with effort, even if imperfect?
- Can they explain their musical choices?
- Do they listen to peers respectfully?

### Peer Feedback
- Can students offer constructive comments on peers' work?
- Do they phrase feedback kindly?
- Can they identify what worked well, not just problems?
- Do they accept feedback about their own work?

### Observation Checklist
During rhythm activities:
- [ ] Student maintains beat
- [ ] Student responds to tempo changes
- [ ] Student creates a pattern others can follow
- [ ] Student participates respectfully

During melody work:
- [ ] Student sings with approximate accuracy
- [ ] Student matches pitches with effort
- [ ] Student creates something original
- [ ] Student responds to coaching

During harmony & ensemble:
- [ ] Student listens to others
- [ ] Student maintains their part
- [ ] Student blends with the group
- [ ] Student helps others succeed

During listening:
- [ ] Student focuses during listening
- [ ] Student notices musical elements
- [ ] Student discusses thoughtfully
- [ ] Student respects diverse preferences

During creation:
- [ ] Student explores and experiments
- [ ] Student makes intentional choices
- [ ] Student shares work with confidence
- [ ] Student revises based on feedback

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment happens **at key points** — end of module, unit, or semester. It captures what the learner has achieved overall.

### Performance Portfolio
A collection of student work across the semester showing growth:
- Video recordings of rhythm performances
- Recordings of singing (solo and ensemble)
- Compositions and arrangements
- Listening journals
- Peer feedback forms
- Reflection statements

**Portfolio Assessment Prompt**: "What shows your growth as a musician? What are you proudest of? What do you want to improve?"

### Composition Project
A student-initiated composition showing integration of multiple skills:
- Written or recorded composition
- Explanation of creative choices
- Reflection on revision process
- Connection to learning outcomes

**Prompt Variation by Level**:
- Beginning: "Compose an 8-beat rhythm and teach it to the class"
- Developing: "Compose a melody with 3 sections and explain the form"
- Proficient: "Compose a complete song with melody, rhythm, and harmony"
- Advanced: "Compose an ensemble arrangement showing understanding of harmony and texture"

### Ensemble Performance
A group performance showing collaborative skill:
- Small ensemble (3-6 people)
- Prepared piece or improvisation
- Peer evaluation of collaboration

### Listening & Analysis Test (Optional)
- Listen to a piece and identify form, instruments, genre, era
- Answer questions about compositional choices
- Explain emotional response with musical vocabulary

## Assessment by Module

### Module 1: Rhythm & Movement

**Can they...**
- Maintain a steady beat across different tempos?
- Distinguish pulse from rhythm?
- Create a rhythmic pattern that others can follow?
- Read and write basic rhythm notation?

**How to assess:**
- Individual beat-keeping (clap along with recordings at different tempos)
- Rhythm games (echo patterns, create patterns)
- Notation quiz (identify quarter notes, eighth notes, rests)
- Group performance (drum circle, body percussion)

### Module 2: Melody & Voice

**Can they...**
- Match pitches and sing in tune?
- Understand and use scale patterns?
- Create an original melody?
- Read and write notes on a staff?

**How to assess:**
- Pitch matching exercises (sing back notes played on instrument)
- Melody composition (create a 4-bar melody on a given rhythm)
- Singing test (sing "Happy Birthday" or familiar song in tune)
- Solfège quiz (identify and sing scale degrees)

### Module 3: Harmony & Ensemble

**Can they...**
- Identify and name chords?
- Create a harmonic accompaniment for a melody?
- Sing a different part than the melody?
- Arrange a song for multiple voices/instruments?

**How to assess:**
- Chord identification (hear chords and name them)
- Part-singing performance (sing assigned part in ensemble)
- Arrangement project (create a 2-3 part arrangement)
- Peer feedback (listen to another group's arrangement and evaluate)

### Module 4: Listening & Analysis

**Can they...**
- Recognize form structures (verse/chorus, ABA)?
- Identify genres and styles?
- Discuss emotional and expressive meaning?
- Make informed judgments about music?

**How to assess:**
- Listening journal (take notes during listening, identify form)
- Genre identification game
- Comparison essay (explain differences between two pieces)
- Reflection discussion (what did you notice? what did you feel?)

### Module 5: Creation & Technology

**Can they...**
- Compose an original piece or song?
- Use digital music software?
- Improvise over chords or rhythms?
- Revise their work based on feedback?

**How to assess:**
- Composition portfolio (collect written compositions, recordings)
- Digital project (beat creation, song arrangement in GarageBand or similar)
- Performance (share composition with class)
- Reflection (explain creative choices and revision process)

## What NOT to Assess

MUSIC-101 does NOT grade:

- **Voice quality**: Pretty voice, trained voice, "good voice" — these are accidents of birth and training, not musicality
- **Performance perfectionism**: Whether notes are played exactly right in concert performance — this creates anxiety and kills joy
- **Comparison to peers**: Whether this student is "better" than that student — this is damaging and untrue
- **Possession of "talent"**: Whether student is "naturally musical" — everyone is; some just have more training

MUSIC-101 DOES grade:
- Effort and engagement
- Understanding of concepts
- Creativity and expression
- Collaboration and respect
- Willingness to revise

## Reporting Progress

### For Students
"You've shown growth in [rhythm/melody/etc.]. Here's what you're doing well: [specific observation]. Here's what to work on: [specific suggestion]. Keep going!"

### For Parents
"Your child is developing musical understanding. They can [specific skill]. We're working on [specific goal]. At home, you can support by [specific suggestion]."

### For School Records
Use rubric levels (Beginning/Developing/Proficient/Advanced) for report cards. Avoid letter grades if possible; they oversimplify music learning.

## Differentiation & Accessibility

### For Learners Who Are Deaf or Hard of Hearing
- Use visual representations of rhythm, pitch, and form
- Provide tactile experiences (feel vibrations, tap pulses)
- Use sign language in ensemble settings
- Provide written explanations and notes
- Consider cochlear implants or hearing aids if used by student

### For Learners with Motor Differences
- Offer alternative participation modes (listening, discussion, digital creation)
- Use speech-to-text for composition ideas
- Provide adapted instruments or digital alternatives
- Allow performance in any modality (signing, rolling, using switches)

### For Advanced Learners
- Challenge with complex forms and advanced harmony
- Invite composition and arrangement projects
- Offer leadership roles (assistant teacher, peer mentor)
- Explore music history, theory, or production in depth

### For Struggling Learners
- Start with simpler rhythms and melodies
- Provide more repetition and scaffolding
- Use familiar songs as entry points
- Celebrate small wins and effort
- Check for hearing or auditory processing issues

## Key Assessment Principles

1. **Focus on growth**, not perfection. Where are they now? Where are they heading?
2. **Celebrate effort and engagement**. The student who tries and fails learned more than the one who didn't try.
3. **Provide specific feedback**. "Good job" is not feedback. "You maintained the beat for the whole 8 bars — that's what steady really means" is feedback.
4. **Assess understanding, not just performance**. Can they explain why that chord works? Can they hear when they go off pitch and correct it?
5. **Use multiple modes**. Some students shine in solo performance, others in ensemble. Some through written work, others through discussion.
6. **Assess collaboration**. Music in this pack is often group work. Can they listen to others? Support peers? Accept feedback?
7. **Revise and re-assess**. If a student doesn't meet a standard, teach it differently and try again. One attempt is not a final judgment.

### Ensemble Collaboration Checklist
- [ ] I listened to others' parts
- [ ] I maintained my part accurately
- [ ] I helped someone who was struggling
- [ ] I accepted feedback kindly
- [ ] I participated fully

### Listening Journal Prompt
Listen to [piece]. Then answer:
1. What do you hear? (instruments, voice, sounds)
2. What's the form? (verse/chorus, ABA, other)
3. What's the feeling or mood?
4. What surprised you or caught your attention?
5. Would you listen again? Why or why not?

## Assessment & Equity

MUSIC-101's assessment approach is intentionally designed to be equitable:

- No expensive instruments required to demonstrate skill
- Multiple modes of participation (singing, movement, discussion, creation, listening)
- No bell curve; all students can achieve proficiency
- Assessment based on understanding, not performance anxiety
- Effort and growth matter as much as current skill level
- Accommodations are standard practice, not "special"

Every student deserves to be heard, seen, and celebrated for their musical understanding.
