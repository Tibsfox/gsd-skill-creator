# ECON-101 Assessment Framework

## Guiding Principles

Assessment in ECON-101 focuses on reasoning, not terminology. Can learners think like economists? Can they use economic concepts to understand real situations? Can they make sound financial decisions? Assessment emphasizes growth and understanding over perfection and speed.

---

## Rubric Levels: Economic Reasoning

Assessment across all modules uses four proficiency levels: Beginning, Developing, Proficient, and Advanced. These rubric levels apply to the core competencies of economic thinking.

### Beginning

**Characteristics:**
- Recognizes that economics deals with choices and scarcity, but explanation is incomplete
- Identifies what happens in an economic situation but not why
- Makes financial decisions without explicit reasoning or trade-off analysis
- Struggles to apply economic concepts to new situations
- May confuse wants with needs or miss opportunity costs

**Feedback:** "You identified what changed, but let's dig into why. What incentives were at work? What trade-offs mattered?"

**Support:** Provide explicit connection between the concept and the situation. Use concrete examples with clear cause-and-effect.

### Developing

**Characteristics:**
- Demonstrates partial understanding of scarcity, opportunity cost, and supply/demand
- Identifies some factors affecting an economic decision but may miss others
- Applies economic reasoning to familiar contexts but struggles with novel ones
- Recognizes trade-offs but doesn't weigh them systematically
- Shows awareness of multiple perspectives in a situation

**Feedback:** "Your reasoning is on track. Now consider the perspective of the person on the other side of this decision. What would they want?"

**Support:** Help learner add depth by considering multiple perspectives, calculating trade-offs more precisely, or connecting to similar situations they've studied.

### Proficient

**Characteristics:**
- Clearly articulates scarcity and opportunity cost in various contexts
- Applies supply and demand reasoning to predict price changes
- Analyzes financial decisions by weighing costs, benefits, and risks
- Considers effects on multiple groups (buyers, sellers, workers, communities)
- Explains economic phenomena using evidence and reasoning

**Feedback:** "Your analysis is solid. You've considered multiple perspectives and grounded your reasoning in evidence. Well done."

**Support:** Challenge with more complex scenarios, connections across modules, or real-world applications.

### Advanced

**Characteristics:**
- Demonstrates sophisticated economic reasoning across diverse contexts
- Recognizes when economic thinking applies and when other factors dominate
- Anticipates unintended consequences of economic decisions or policies
- Integrates economics with other knowledge (psychology, history, science, ethics)
- Evaluates competing economic arguments using evidence and logic

**Feedback:** "Excellent analysis. You've identified a nuance many people miss: that policy creates both winners and losers even if the net effect is beneficial. How would you address that distributional issue?"

**Support:** Engage with advanced applications, policy analysis, research, and mentoring of peers.

## Module-Specific Assessment

### Module 1: Scarcity & Economic Thinking

**Key Questions to Assess:**
- Can the learner identify examples of scarcity in their own life?
- Do they distinguish wants from needs, and does that distinction influence their decisions?
- Can they articulate opportunity costs?
- Do they apply scarcity thinking to situations without prompting?

**Evidence:**
- In discussion: Listen for spontaneous use of phrases like "If I choose X, I can't have Y" or "I only have so much time"
- In writing: Explanations of personal choices that mention what was given up
- In activities: Desert Island and Scavenger Hunt performance and reasoning

**Proficient Example:** "I spent my Saturday afternoon studying instead of playing video games. The opportunity cost was not being with my friends. It was worth it because I'll get a better grade, but I also felt like I missed out. Next time maybe I can study with friends."

---

### Module 2: Markets & Exchange

**Key Questions to Assess:**
- Can the learner apply supply and demand reasoning to real prices?
- Do they predict price changes correctly from supply or demand shifts?
- Can they distinguish competitive markets from monopolies and understand the difference?
- Do they recognize market failures?

**Evidence:**
- Analysis of classroom marketplace outcomes: Who got rich? Why? What prices changed?
- Supply & Demand Price Discovery: Correct identification of supply vs. demand shifts
- In discussion: Can they explain real-world price changes (gas prices, housing, smartphones)?

**Proficient Example:** "When everyone wanted iPhones but Apple limited supply, prices stayed high. Then when many companies made smartphones, competition increased and Apple had to lower prices. It's supply and demand — fewer sellers means higher prices, more sellers means lower prices."

---

### Module 3: Money & Financial Systems

**Key Questions to Assess:**
- Can they explain why money was invented and how it evolved?
- Do they understand interest and the time value of money?
- Can they calculate interest correctly?
- Do they evaluate credit decisions by comparing interest costs?

**Evidence:**
- History of Money Timeline: Explanation of how money solved problems
- Interest Calculator: Correct calculations and interpretation
- In discussion: Understanding of how credit works and its trade-offs

**Proficient Example:** "Borrowing $10,000 at 8% interest costs me about $4,500 in interest over 5 years. At 3% interest, it's only $1,600. So 5% higher rate costs me almost $3,000 more. That's why shopping for the best interest rate matters."

---

### Module 4: Personal Financial Literacy

**Key Questions to Assess:**
- Can they create a realistic budget?
- Do they evaluate financial decisions by weighing costs, benefits, and risks?
- Do they understand trade-offs in borrowing, saving, and investing?
- Can they recognize behavioral biases in financial decision-making?

**Evidence:**
- Budget Building Workshop: A realistic, detailed budget that shows trade-offs
- Investment Portfolio Simulation: Thoughtful asset allocation that reflects goals and risk tolerance
- In discussion: Can they explain why they'd make a certain financial choice?

**Proficient Example:** "I'm considering borrowing for college. The opportunity cost is working instead of studying full-time. But the benefit is higher earning potential long-term. If the program costs $50,000 and my salary gain is $15,000/year more, I break even in about 3.5 years. After that it's profit. It's worth borrowing."

---

### Module 5: Macroeconomics & Civic Economics

**Key Questions to Assess:**
- Can they measure and interpret key economic indicators?
- Do they understand what causes business cycles?
- Can they analyze policy and predict effects?
- Do they recognize how policies distribute benefits and costs?

**Evidence:**
- GDP Measurement Activity: Correct calculation and understanding of limitations
- Inflation Impact Role Play: Recognition that inflation affects groups differently
- Policy Analysis Essay: Evaluation of a policy using evidence and reasoning

**Proficient Example:** "A minimum wage increase helps low-wage workers by raising their income. But it might cause businesses to hire fewer people or raise prices. The benefit is clear for employed low-wage workers, but it could hurt teenagers looking for first jobs. You can't say it's simply good or bad — it depends on perspective and evidence."

## Formative Assessment Strategies

### 1. Economic Reasoning Discussions

**What:** Regular conversations (not quizzes) about economic questions.

**How:**
- "If a new restaurant opens on your block, what happens to nearby restaurants? Why?"
- "Your friend wants to drop out of school to work. What are the opportunity costs?"
- "Why did the price of gas go up? What would have to happen for it to come back down?"

**Look For:**
- Use of economic concepts correctly
- Recognition of incentives and trade-offs
- Consideration of multiple perspectives
- Evidence-based reasoning

---

### 2. Decision Case Studies

**What:** Real or realistic scenarios where learners analyze using economic reasoning.

**Examples:**
- "A job offers $40,000 with good benefits or $50,000 with no benefits. Which is better and why?"
- "Should your state raise the minimum wage? Who benefits and who loses? What's your evidence?"
- "Your parent is considering buying a house. What economic factors should they consider?"

**Process:**
1. Present scenario
2. Learners analyze using economic tools
3. Discuss multiple perspectives
4. Debrief: What economic concepts did you use?

---

### 3. Observation of Financial Decision-Making

**What:** Notice when learners spontaneously demonstrate economic thinking.

**Red Flags (signs of learning):**
- Asks "Is it worth it?" before making a choice
- Thinks about opportunity cost ("What else could I do with this money?")
- Recognizes incentives affecting behavior
- Questions an assumption ("But what if prices go up?")
- Considers effects on multiple groups

**Record:** Jot notes on when learners show these behaviors. It's evidence of learning that formal assessments might miss.

---

### 4. Market Simulations and Experiments

**What:** The activity itself provides assessment. Observe and record:
- Do learners make strategic decisions?
- How do they respond to price changes?
- Do they recognize patterns?
- Can they explain what happened afterward?

## Summative Assessment Options

### 1. Personal Financial Plan

**Scope:** A realistic budget, savings goals, borrowing decisions, and investment strategy for the next 5-10 years.

**Components:**
- **Income:** Current or projected income (job, allowance, other sources)
- **Expenses:** Actual or estimated spending across categories
- **Goals:** What financial milestones do you want to reach? (emergency fund, college, house, travel)
- **Borrowing Decisions:** Should you borrow for education, car, home? What's your reasoning?
- **Investing:** How will you save for long-term goals? What's your risk tolerance?

**Grading Rubric:**
- **Realism** (Beginning/Developing/Proficient/Advanced): Are numbers realistic? Do they reflect actual or credible scenarios?
- **Trade-Offs** (B/D/P/A): Does the plan show awareness of competing needs? How are priorities justified?
- **Decision Quality** (B/D/P/A): Are borrowing and investing decisions well-reasoned? What's the evidence?
- **Adaptability** (B/D/P/A): How does the plan respond to different scenarios (income increases/decreases, emergency expenses)?

---

### 2. Policy Analysis Essay

**Scope:** Evaluate an economic policy proposal (carbon tax, minimum wage, universal basic income, trade agreement, inflation target, etc.).

**Structure:**
1. **The Policy:** What is being proposed?
2. **The Economic Reasoning:** What economic problem does it try to solve? What mechanism would work?
3. **Effects on Different Groups:** Who benefits? Who loses? By how much?
4. **Trade-Offs:** What do we gain and what do we give up?
5. **Evidence:** What data or research supports or opposes this policy?
6. **Your Evaluation:** Given all this, is it a good policy? What's your reasoning?

**Grading Rubric:**
- **Understanding** (B/D/P/A): Does the essay demonstrate solid grasp of the policy and economics?
- **Evidence Use** (B/D/P/A): Does it cite data or research? Are sources credible?
- **Perspective-Taking** (B/D/P/A): Does it consider multiple viewpoints fairly?
- **Reasoning** (B/D/P/A): Does the argument connect evidence to conclusions logically?
- **Nuance** (B/D/P/A): Does it acknowledge uncertainty and trade-offs, or present it as simple?

### 3. Capstone Economic Citizenship Project

**Scope:** Research a local or national economic issue and propose evidence-based solutions.

**Examples of Issues:**
- Youth unemployment in your community
- Gentrification and housing affordability
- Regional wealth gaps
- Agricultural economics and food security
- Trade and its effects on local manufacturing

**Project Outputs** (choose one or combine):
- **Written Report:** Research document with problem statement, economic analysis, and solutions
- **Oral Presentation:** 10-15 minute presentation to an audience
- **Visual Poster:** Infographic or poster with data and findings
- **Multimedia:** Video, podcast, or interactive experience
- **Policy Brief:** Summary and recommendation in a format for decision-makers

**Grading Rubric:**
- **Research Quality** (B/D/P/A): Depth and credibility of sources
- **Economic Analysis** (B/D/P/A): Application of economic concepts to the issue
- **Solutions** (B/D/P/A): Proposed solutions are realistic and grounded in evidence
- **Communication** (B/D/P/A): Clear explanation of problem and reasoning
- **Engagement** (B/D/P/A): Connections to learners' communities and lives

## Feedback Strategies

Feedback should be **specific, actionable, and growth-oriented**.

### Feedback on Economic Reasoning

**Instead of:** "Good job analyzing supply and demand."

**Try:** "You correctly identified that demand increased, which raised prices. Now push deeper: Why did demand increase? What caused that shift? Would supply have been affected too?"

### Feedback on Financial Decisions

**Instead of:** "Your budget is too generous on entertainment."

**Try:** "Your budget allocates 15% to entertainment. That's fine if it reflects your priorities, but let's check: Do you have an emergency fund? Are you saving for your goals? Does this trade-off feel right to you?"

### Feedback on Policy Analysis

**Instead of:** "You didn't consider the other side."

**Try:** "You made a strong case for why this policy helps workers. Now let's think like a business owner: What would concern them about this policy? How might they respond?"

---

## Grading & Reporting

### Alternative to Points

Instead of: "You got 34 out of 40 points."

Consider: "You demonstrated proficient-level economic reasoning. Here's where you're ready to advance, and here's where you could go deeper."

### Progress Tracking

Track three dimensions:
1. **Conceptual Understanding:** Can learners use economic concepts correctly?
2. **Application:** Can they apply these concepts to new situations?
3. **Integration:** Can they combine concepts and perspectives to analyze complex problems?

Show progress on each over time.

### Self-Assessment

Include learner self-reflection:
- "What economic concepts did you use this unit?"
- "Where do you still have questions?"
- "What real-world decision could you now make better?"

---

## Special Considerations

### Equity in Assessment

- Financial assessments assume some income and savings capacity. Acknowledge that not all learners have equal resources.
- Avoid assumptions about family economic circumstances.
- Use diverse examples of financial situations and goals.
- Celebrate economic strategies used by families in different circumstances.

### Neurodiversity

- Provide multiple ways to demonstrate understanding (discussion, writing, visual, hands-on)
- Allow extended time for processing economic concepts (systems thinking is complex)
- Break complex scenarios into smaller pieces
- Provide structured notes and reference materials during assessments

### English Learners

- Use visual representations (graphs, diagrams, pictures) alongside text
- Pre-teach key vocabulary (opportunity cost, equilibrium, inflation, etc.)
- Provide sentence frames for discussion ("If supply increases, then ___" or "The opportunity cost of ___ is ___")
- Allow responses in multiple languages if possible

---

## Reassessment & Growth

Learners should have opportunities to revisit and improve:
- Redo a policy analysis after feedback
- Revise a budget after circumstances change
- Retry a decision case study after additional learning
- Reflect on economic choices they've made and what they'd do differently

This models real-world financial decision-making: you learn, you adjust, you improve.
