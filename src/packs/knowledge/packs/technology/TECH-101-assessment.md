# TECH-101: Assessment Framework

## Overview

Assessment in TECH-101 focuses on understanding, application, and design thinking rather than memorization. The goal is to evaluate whether learners can think technologically: identify how tools work, analyze systems, design solutions, trace digital processes, and evaluate technology's role in society.

---

## Beginning

Learners at this level are developing foundational awareness of technology as more than gadgets.

**Tool Understanding:** Identifies common tools and can name their basic purpose. Uses simple machines with guidance. Recognizes that tools help people do things.

**Systems Awareness:** Can name some parts of familiar systems (a kitchen, a school). Identifies obvious inputs (food goes in the oven) and outputs (cooked food comes out). Does not yet see connections between system parts.

**Design Thinking:** Can follow step-by-step building instructions. Builds from a model or template. Does not yet independently define problems or generate solutions.

**Digital Awareness:** Uses digital devices for basic tasks (games, video). Understands on/off and basic input (keyboard, touch). Does not yet understand how devices work internally.

**Societal Awareness:** Recognizes that technology affects daily life. Can name technologies they use. Does not yet consider trade-offs or impact.

**Assessment Methods at This Level:**
- Observation during hands-on activities
- Verbal explanation: "What does this tool do? Why is it shaped this way?"
- Drawing: sketch a tool and label what it does
- Sorting: group objects by purpose or type

---

## Developing

Learners at this level are building connections between concepts and beginning to reason about technology.

**Tool Understanding:** Explains mechanical advantage for simple machines. Identifies simple machines within compound devices. Can predict which tool is best for a given task and explain why.

**Systems Thinking:** Maps system components, identifies basic feedback loops. Predicts some consequences of changing system parts. Beginning to see systems as interconnected rather than isolated.

**Design Capability:** Defines problems clearly. Brainstorms multiple solutions (at least three). Builds and tests prototypes with guidance. Beginning to iterate based on test results.

**Digital Literacy:** Understands that computers store information as numbers (binary concept). Can trace a message through a network at a simplified level. Follows algorithms step by step.

**Societal Awareness:** Describes how specific technologies changed how people live. Identifies at least one positive and one negative consequence of a technology. Beginning to consider multiple perspectives.

**Assessment Methods at This Level:**
- Design portfolio with sketches, notes, and test results
- System diagram with labeled inputs, outputs, and at least one feedback loop
- Oral or written explanation of mechanical advantage
- Binary encoding exercises (convert 5 numbers, decode a message)
- Short essay or discussion: "How did [technology] change [community/activity]?"

---

## Proficient

Learners at this level think technologically: they analyze systems, design solutions, and evaluate technology critically.

**Tool Mastery:** Designs compound machines for specific tasks. Calculates mechanical advantage. Evaluates tool fitness for purpose across multiple criteria (efficiency, safety, cost).

**Systems Analysis:** Analyzes complex systems with multiple feedback loops. Predicts unintended consequences of system changes. Optimizes systems for competing variables (speed vs. quality, cost vs. durability).

**Design Proficiency:** Completes full design cycles independently from problem identification through multiple iterations. Considers user needs, constraints, and trade-offs throughout. Documents design decisions with rationale.

**Digital Competence:** Reasons about algorithm efficiency (why some approaches are faster). Understands encryption and security concepts. Evaluates digital systems for privacy and reliability trade-offs.

**Critical Evaluation:** Evaluates technology through ethical, environmental, and equity lenses. Constructs evidence-based arguments about technology policy. Identifies bias in how technology is designed or deployed.

**Assessment Methods at This Level:**
- Complete design project with documentation (problem, research, iterations, final reflection)
- System analysis report: map a real-world system, identify failure modes, propose improvements
- Technology impact essay with evidence from multiple perspectives
- Algorithm comparison: analyze two approaches to the same problem, explain trade-offs
- Peer design review: evaluate another learner's design against criteria

---

## Advanced

Learners at this level create, teach, and contribute to technological understanding.

**Innovation:** Creates novel tool designs or system architectures. Identifies problems others have not noticed and proposes solutions. Teaches mechanical and systems principles to others effectively.

**Systems Mastery:** Models complex systems with multiple interacting feedback loops. Identifies systemic risks and cascading failure modes. Designs systems with built-in resilience and adaptability.

**Design Leadership:** Leads design projects for teams. Balances competing constraints at expert level. Mentors others through the design process. Designs for accessibility, sustainability, and equity.

**Digital Sophistication:** Designs digital systems at a conceptual level. Understands system architecture, scalability, and reliability. Reasons about societal implications of digital infrastructure decisions.

**Thought Leadership:** Synthesizes historical, ethical, and environmental perspectives into coherent technology philosophy. Proposes technology policy with awareness of trade-offs and stakeholder impact. Contributes original analysis to technology discourse.

**Assessment Methods at This Level:**
- Original design project addressing a real community need
- Systems modeling with simulation or detailed analysis
- Mentoring portfolio (evidence of teaching others)
- Technology policy proposal with stakeholder analysis
- Publication or presentation for authentic audience

## Formative Assessment

Ongoing assessment strategies used during learning to guide instruction and provide feedback.

### Design Portfolio Review
Review learner portfolios at regular intervals. Look for evidence of growth in:
- Problem definition clarity
- Number and quality of brainstormed solutions
- Prototype quality and testing rigor
- Willingness to iterate based on data
- Documentation quality and reflection depth

### System Analysis Observations
During systems-thinking activities, observe whether learners:
- Identify all major components without prompting
- Correctly label inputs, outputs, and processes
- Notice feedback loops without being told to look for them
- Predict consequences accurately
- Consider stakeholders beyond the obvious

### Technical Explanation Quality
Ask learners to explain how something works. Evaluate:
- Accuracy of explanation
- Use of appropriate vocabulary
- Ability to adjust explanation for different audiences
- Connection between principles (e.g., linking mechanical advantage to the design of a specific tool)

### Transfer Indicators
Watch for learners applying concepts from one module in another context:
- Recognizing feedback loops in digital systems (M2 concept in M4 context)
- Applying design thinking to societal problems (M3 concept in M5 context)
- Identifying simple machines in digital hardware (M1 concept in M4 context)

---

## Summative Assessment

Evidence collected at the end of a unit or module to evaluate mastery.

### Design Project
Complete a full design project with written documentation including:
- Problem statement with evidence of user research
- At least three brainstormed solutions with evaluation criteria
- Prototype description and test results
- Iteration evidence (what changed and why)
- Final reflection on what worked, what didn't, and what was learned

### System Analysis Report
Analyze a real-world system (school cafeteria, local water system, package delivery network):
- Complete system map with all components, inputs, outputs
- At least two feedback loops identified and classified (positive/negative)
- Two unintended consequences identified with reasoning
- One proposed improvement with trade-off analysis

### Technology Impact Presentation
Research and present on how a specific technology changed a community:
- Historical context (what existed before)
- Description of the technology and how it works
- Evidence of positive and negative impacts
- At least two stakeholder perspectives represented
- Personal evaluation with supporting argument

---

## Portfolio Suggestions

A technology portfolio demonstrates growth across all five modules. Suggested artifacts:

1. **Simple Machine Collection:** Photographs or drawings of simple machines found in daily life, with labels identifying type and mechanical advantage
2. **System Map:** A detailed diagram of a system the learner chose to analyze, showing all components and feedback loops
3. **Design Project Documentation:** Complete design cycle documentation for at least one project, from problem identification through iteration
4. **Digital System Trace:** A diagram showing how information travels through a digital system, with explanations at each step
5. **Technology Impact Analysis:** A written or presented analysis of a technology's effect on society, with evidence and multiple perspectives
6. **Reflection Journal:** Ongoing entries about technology observations, questions, and connections discovered during learning
