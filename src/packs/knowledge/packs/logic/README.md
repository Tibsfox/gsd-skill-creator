# LOG-101: Logic & Formal Systems

**A Foundational Knowledge Pack on Valid Reasoning, Arguments, and Proof**

---

## Quick Start

**What is LOG-101?** A comprehensive curriculum on logical thinking: from sorting games and pattern recognition in kindergarten through formal proof and symbolic systems in college.

**Who is it for?** Learners from age 4 to 16+ and their teachers, mentors, and parents.

**What will learners do?**
- Play logic games and solve puzzles
- Learn to recognize fallacies in real arguments
- Construct valid arguments defending their own claims
- Master symbolic logic notation and truth tables
- Write mathematical proofs using logical techniques

**Time commitment?** 20–150 hours depending on grade level and depth.

---

## Five Modules

1. **Logical Thinking Foundations** (PreK–5): Sorting, patterns, if-then reasoning
2. **Arguments & Reasoning** (3–9): Premises, conclusions, deductive vs. inductive
3. **Fallacies & Critical Evaluation** (6–12): Naming fallacies, media literacy, propaganda analysis
4. **Symbolic Logic & Truth Tables** (9–14): Propositional logic, notation, equivalences
5. **Proof & Formal Systems** (9–16+): Direct proof, contradiction, induction, axiomatics

- Identify premises and conclusions in arguments

- Construct valid arguments defending their claims

## Assessment & Success

Learners are assessed on **reasoning quality**, not answer memorization.

**Formative assessment** (ongoing):
- Discussions: Are students spotting fallacies? Defending claims?
- Activities: Can students construct valid arguments?
- Peer critique: Do students give constructive feedback on reasoning?

**Summative assessment** (end-of-unit):
- Argument construction: Write a multi-premise argument
- Fallacy identification: Recognize fallacies in provided texts
- Symbolic logic: Construct truth tables, prove equivalences
- Proof writing: Prove statements using appropriate techniques

## Parent & Mentor Guidance

### Why Logic Matters
Logic is the foundation of clear communication, critical thinking, problem-solving, and understanding nearly any field. A learner who can think logically is equipped to navigate a complex world full of competing claims.

### How to Support
1. **Play logic games**: Sudoku, chess, logic puzzles — these are logic training in disguise
2. **Ask "Why?" and "How do you know?"**: Push learners to explain their thinking
3. **Spot fallacies together**: When watching TV or reading news, ask: "Is that a fair argument? Any fallacies?"
4. **Construct arguments**: "You want X. Give me three reasons why I should allow it."
5. **Play devil's advocate**: Help learners defend against criticism

### Red Flags (Growth Opportunities)
- Struggles to give reasons for claims
- Assumes if something sounds good, it must be true
- Dismisses counterarguments without engaging
- Confuses "I believe it" with "It's true"

These are not failures — they're opportunities to practice logical thinking.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Isn't logic just philosophy?**
A: Logic appears in philosophy, but also in mathematics (proof), computer science (algorithms), law (reasoning), and everyday persuasion. It's a tool used across all domains.

**Q: Can young children really learn logic?**
A: Absolutely. Young learners reason logically every day. We make that reasoning explicit and systematic through games and activities.

**Q: Will this make my learner overly pedantic?**
A: The opposite. Good logic instruction teaches when to be precise and when to communicate naturally. It's a tool, not a straitjacket.

**Q: How does this relate to mathematics?**
A: Logic is the language and foundation of mathematics. Proof—the heart of mathematics—is logic in action. Both are taught in tandem.

**Q: Is this useful in real life?**
A: Very much. It helps with clear communication, detecting manipulation, problem-solving, and understanding complex topics.

## Troubleshooting

**Learner struggles with if-then language?**
→ Use real-world examples: "If it rains, then we stay inside." Revisit this concept regularly at increasing levels of abstraction.

**Learner finds fallacies boring or abstract?**
→ Use current events, social media, and advertisements. Finding fallacies in real arguments is much more engaging than textbooks.

**Learner struggles with symbolic notation?**
→ Take more time. Notation is shorthand for ideas they already understand in English. Introduce gradually. Use visual aids.

**Learner gets frustrated with proof writing?**
→ Start with simple, guided proofs. Celebrate small steps. Remind them that learning to write proofs takes time and practice.
