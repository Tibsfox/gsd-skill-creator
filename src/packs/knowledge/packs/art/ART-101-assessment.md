# ART-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

Assessment in ART-101 measures growth and creative development, not innate talent or comparison to peers. We assess:

1. **Observational Skill** — Can the student slow down and notice details?
2. **Intentional Choice-Making** — Does the student make deliberate decisions about technique, color, composition?
3. **Technical Control** — Does the student demonstrate growing command of tools and materials?
4. **Creative Thinking** — Is the student experimenting, taking risks, and generating ideas?
5. **Reflection & Articulation** — Can the student articulate what they did, why, and what they learned?
6. **Collaboration & Critique** — Does the student give and receive feedback constructively?

---

## Rubric: Observational Drawing

### Beginning

- Draws freely without concern for accuracy
- Uses marks and lines without clear intent
- Observes for short periods before sketching
- Can describe drawing in simple terms ("I drew a face")
- Gains confidence from attempting drawing

**What the teacher does:** Celebrate willingness to draw. Ask gentle questions: "What did you notice about the eyes?" Model close observation.

### Developing

- Observes for longer periods; pays attention to details
- Translates observations into recognizable images
- Makes some accurate proportions (face has eyes, nose, mouth in correct locations)
- Describes observations while drawing ("The shadow is here")
- Shows growing confidence; attempts more complex subjects

**What the teacher does:** Ask "What did you notice that surprised you?" Encourage longer observation before drawing.

### Proficient

- Observes systematically; notices details (textures, light, proportions)
- Accurately represents what is seen (proportions, angles, spatial relationships)
- Demonstrates control of line and value
- Compares own work to object: "My proportions are off; let me look again"
- Chooses subjects that challenge themselves

**What the teacher does:** Ask "What made you choose that approach?" Encourage continued growth by providing more complex observation tasks.

### Advanced

- Observes with extraordinary attentional focus and records minute details
- Captures not just likeness but character (drawings feel alive)
- Renders value and spatial relationships with sophistication
- Adjusts approach based on self-evaluation
- Mentors peers in observation techniques

**What the teacher does:** Challenge with complex subjects. Ask about artistic choices: "How did you decide where to put the darkest value?"

## Rubric: Color, Value & Composition

### Beginning

- Chooses colors without evident planning
- Uses color because it's available, not because of intent
- Fills entire paper; little awareness of negative space
- Responds to prompts about color ("That's blue" vs. "That feels calm")
- Explores paint and color mixing playfully

**What the teacher does:** Celebrate exploration. Ask: "What color did you use most?" Introduce color vocabulary gently.

### Developing

- Makes some intentional color choices
- Attempts to show light and dark (value)
- Places subject on paper with some awareness of composition
- Explains color choices with guidance ("I used yellow because the sun...")
- Practices color mixing to achieve desired colors

**What the teacher does:** Ask questions prompting intentional choice: "Why did you pick that color?" Introduce composition basics (foreground/background).

### Proficient

- Makes intentional color choices that support meaning or mood
- Creates smooth value gradients; uses value to show dimension
- Applies basic composition principles (balance, emphasis, movement)
- Articulates why certain colors or values work ("Cool colors make it feel calm")
- Deliberately places focal point to guide viewer attention

**What the teacher does:** Deepen analysis: "How would this feel if you swapped warm and cool colors?" Introduce advanced principles (rule of thirds, contrast).

### Advanced

- Uses sophisticated color relationships (complementary, analogous, triadic)
- Controls value subtly to create atmospheric perspective and mood
- Understands how composition affects emotional response
- Makes intentional "wrong" color choices for artistic effect
- Analyzes artworks for compositional and color strategies; applies discoveries to own work

**What the teacher does:** Provide more complex prompts. Ask: "How are you using color to tell the story?" Challenge to analyze and imitate master artists' techniques.

## Rubric: Materials & Making

### Beginning

- Explores materials with curiosity and enthusiasm
- Uses materials safely with guidance
- Tries different techniques (painting, sculpting, collaging)
- May struggle with basic control but persists
- Speaks about what material "does" ("Clay is squishy")

**What the teacher does:** Celebrate exploration. Provide safety guidance. Normalize struggle: "That's hard; let's try again."

### Developing

- Demonstrates basic competence with 2-3 media
- Understands how different materials afford different possibilities
- Follows multi-step directions with some support
- Repairs simple mistakes (re-gluing, repainting)
- Chooses materials intentionally for a project

**What the teacher does:** Teach specific techniques. Ask: "Why did you choose that material?" Expand material palette gradually.

### Proficient

- Shows technical control with multiple media
- Selects materials strategically for intended effect
- Solves problems (material isn't working; tries different approach)
- Creates three-dimensional work demonstrating spatial thinking
- Combines media intentionally (collage + drawing, sculpture + painting)

**What the teacher does:** Introduce advanced techniques. Ask: "How does the material shape your idea?" Challenge with new materials or constraints.

### Advanced

- Demonstrates mastery-level control of chosen media
- Understands material properties deeply (clay's plasticity, paint viscosity)
- Experiments with non-traditional materials creatively
- Teaches peers techniques
- Develops distinctive personal style through material expertise

**What the teacher does:** Support independent exploration. Ask: "What media do you want to specialize in?" Facilitate advanced projects.

## Rubric: Creative Thinking & Risk-Taking

### Beginning

- Generates many ideas (quantity over quality)
- Tries things without self-consciousness
- Accepts feedback without defensiveness
- Recovers quickly from "mistakes"
- Plays with ideas playfully

**What the teacher does:** Celebrate risk-taking. Normalize "failures" as experiments. Say: "What did you learn from that?"

### Developing

- Generates ideas with some variety
- Takes risks but with some hesitation
- Responds to feedback; makes some adjustments
- Attempts subjects slightly outside comfort zone
- Shows willingness to experiment with new techniques

**What the teacher does:** Encourage more risk: "What would happen if you tried...?" Celebrate brave attempts.

### Proficient

- Generates multiple ideas; considers alternatives
- Takes meaningful risks; isn't discouraged by partial failure
- Incorporates feedback thoughtfully; sometimes ignores feedback if it conflicts with vision
- Experiments deliberately with new techniques/styles
- Makes intentional choices that break conventional rules

**What the teacher does:** Push further: "Could you push that idea even further?" Ask about artistic vision: "Where are you going with this?"

### Advanced

- Develops original ideas rooted in personal curiosity or concern
- Takes sophisticated risks that show artistic vision
- Advocates for artistic choices; explains why feedback doesn't fit intent
- Experiments constantly; has identifiable artistic interests/themes
- Inspires peers through creative courage

**What the teacher does:** Support independent artistic direction. Challenge: "What's the big idea behind this body of work?"

## Rubric: Reflection & Articulation

### Beginning

- Describes work simply ("I made a painting")
- Responds to prompts about their work
- Identifies one thing they like about their work
- Speaks with encouragement from teacher
- Shows awareness that they made choices

**What the teacher does:** Ask simple questions: "What did you do?" "What was hard?" "What do you like about it?"

### Developing

- Describes what they made and how they made it
- Explains some choices ("I used blue because...")
- Identifies strengths and one area to improve
- Speaks with moderate confidence
- Reflects on effort and engagement

**What the teacher does:** Ask deeper questions: "Why did you make that choice?" "What would you do differently?"

### Proficient

- Clearly articulates artistic intent
- Explains choices with reference to elements/principles ("I used asymmetrical balance to make it feel dynamic")
- Identifies specific strengths and growth areas
- Reflects thoughtfully on what they learned
- Speaks confidently about own work

**What the teacher does:** Ask: "What's the story behind this?" "How does this connect to other work?" Introduce artist statements.

### Advanced

- Articulates sophisticated artistic vision and themes
- References art history or contemporary artists in explaining choices
- Provides nuanced self-critique (celebrates growth but identifies subtle improvements)
- Makes connections between this work and body of work
- Speaks eloquently and persuasively about artistic intent

**What the teacher does:** Ask: "How does this fit into your larger artistic interests?" Support portfolio development and exhibition.

## Rubric: Giving & Receiving Critique

### Beginning

- Listens to feedback without dismissal
- Responds with "thank you" (not defensiveness)
- Observes peer work with interest
- Makes simple comments ("That's nice")
- Recovers quickly when feedback isn't what was hoped

**What the teacher does:** Model respectful listening. Normalize that feedback isn't judgment. Celebrate willingness to hear others.

### Developing

- Listens actively; asks clarifying questions
- Offers basic feedback ("I like the colors")
- Notices and appreciates peer work
- Makes some adjustments based on feedback
- Balances pride in work with openness to improvement

**What the teacher does:** Teach feedback structure: "I notice... I like... I wonder..." Encourage specificity over vagueness.

### Proficient

- Gives specific, evidence-based feedback ("The contrast between light and dark makes it feel dramatic")
- Asks thoughtful questions ("What would happen if you changed the composition?")
- Receives feedback gracefully; considers it before deciding to use
- Explains own vision without defensiveness
- Contributes thoughtfully to group critique

**What the teacher does:** Deepen critique: "Why do you think that choice works?" Challenge to balance honesty with kindness.

### Advanced

- Provides nuanced feedback addressing technique, concept, and impact
- Asks questions that deepen the artist's thinking
- Advocates for own artistic vision while remaining open to alternatives
- Facilitates peer critique; teaches others how to give constructive feedback
- Engages in sophisticated discussion about artistic choices and intent

**What the teacher does:** Ask: "What questions could we ask that would push this further?" Support peer teaching and curation.

## Formative Assessment

### Observation Checklists

**During Individual Work:**
- Is the student engaged and focused?
- Are they observing before drawing, or jumping in?
- Do they persist when work is hard, or give up quickly?
- Are they making intentional choices?
- Do they ask questions or ask for help appropriately?

**During Group Critique:**
- Does the student listen respectfully?
- Do they offer feedback with evidence?
- Do they speak about their own work clearly?
- Do they respond to feedback without defensiveness?

### Sketchbook Reviews

Check sketchbooks for:
- Variety of ideas and approaches
- Evidence of observation and reflection
- Growth over time
- Willingness to experiment
- Quality of brainstorming/ideation

### Self-Assessment Prompts

Regular prompts for reflection:
- "What choice in this piece are you most proud of?"
- "What was challenging? How did you work through it?"
- "What would you do differently if you made this again?"
- "What did you learn from feedback?"
- "How does this piece show growth compared to earlier work?"

---

## Summative Assessment

### Portfolio Review

At the end of a unit or semester:
- Student selects pieces representing growth
- Student writes artist statement
- Student presents portfolio
- Portfolio demonstrates:
  - Range of media/techniques explored
  - Evidence of technical growth
  - Thoughtful self-selection
  - Articulate reflection

### Project Rubric (Example)

For major projects, use multi-dimensional rubric:

| Dimension | Beginning | Developing | Proficient | Advanced |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Observation** | Free exploration | Attentive looking | Detailed, accurate observation | Extraordinary attention to subtlety |
| **Technique** | Basic mark-making | Growing control | Skillful use of tools/media | Sophisticated technical mastery |
| **Composition** | Random placement | Some awareness | Intentional arrangement | Sophisticated design |
| **Creativity** | Conventional approach | Some experimentation | Intentional risk-taking | Original vision |
| **Reflection** | Simple description | Explained choices | Articulate intent | Nuanced self-critique |

## Assessment Methods by Module

### Module 1: Seeing & Drawing

**Formative:**
- Observation journal (daily or weekly)
- Timed sketches (speed reduces perfectionism)
- Proportional accuracy check (compare drawing to object)

**Summative:**
- Portfolio of observational drawings (3-5 pieces showing growth)
- Self-assessment: "How has my observational skill improved?"

### Module 2: Color, Value & Composition

**Formative:**
- Color mixing experiments and documentation
- Value scale creation and refinement
- Composition sketches (thumbnails showing different arrangements)

**Summative:**
- Complete painting/artwork demonstrating intentional color/value/composition choices
- Artist statement explaining color and composition decisions

### Module 3: Materials & Making

**Formative:**
- Media exploration journal (notes on what each material does)
- Technique practice (multiple pieces using same medium)
- Problem-solving observations (how did they respond to material challenges?)

**Summative:**
- Three completed pieces using three different media
- Self-assessment: "Which medium do I prefer? Why?"

### Module 4: Art in Context

**Formative:**
- Artwork analysis prompts (reading images for historical/cultural meaning)
- Timeline contributions (artworks + context)
- Discussion participation (art history conversations)

**Summative:**
- Artist biography presentation
- Comparative analysis essay/presentation (same theme across cultures)
- Reflection: "How has studying art history influenced my own work?"

### Module 5: Creative Process & Portfolio

**Formative:**
- Brainstorming session documentation (ideas generated)
- Critique participation (feedback given and received)
- Revision notes (how did feedback shape iteration?)

**Summative:**
- Complete portfolio with artist statement
- Presentation of portfolio with explanation of selections
- Self-reflection on growth throughout the course

## Differentiation & Support

### For Students Struggling with Confidence

- Start with smaller paper (less intimidating)
- Shorter observation periods (high-precision practice in short bursts)
- Frequent celebration of small successes
- Peer models of work that shows "imperfect" drawing
- Private feedback before public sharing

### For Students Excelling

- Longer observation times (more complex subjects)
- Advanced techniques (multiple media simultaneously, art history research)
- Teaching roles (mentoring peers, explaining techniques)
- Self-directed projects (student-designed challenges)
- Exhibition and external feedback opportunities

### For Students with Perfectionism

- Speed-based activities (ideation sprints, quick sketches)
- Multiple attempts at same subject to show variation is good
- Emphasis on process over product
- Sharing "failures" as learning experiences
- Focus on effort and growth, not outcome quality

### For Students with Sensory or Fine Motor Challenges

- Large tools (thick crayons, chunky brushes, large paper)
- Different media (clay instead of drawing, digital instead of traditional)
- Hand-over-hand guidance (teacher demonstrates with physical support)
- Assistive technology (magnifiers, adapted tools)
- Modified activities (collage instead of drawing; verbal descriptions instead of written)

## Communication with Parents/Guardians

### Parent-Friendly Report Template

**Your child's growth in art:**

- [Student name] has been exploring [media/techniques] in art class.
- A strength I've noticed: [specific observation and why it matters]
- An area for continued growth: [challenge and how you can support]
- How you can support at home: [specific suggestion—materials, conversations, activities]
- What we're working on next: [coming unit/focus]

**Sample:** "Asha has been working on observational drawing. She's developing great focus and noticing small details. Next, we'll explore how to show depth through perspective. You can support by asking her to sketch things she observes at home and talking about what she notices."

---

## Conclusion

Assessment in ART-101 is about nurturing growth, confidence, and creative thinking—not measuring talent. We celebrate risk-taking, honor the iterative process, and recognize that all students are capable of visual expression.

The rubrics above are guides, not rigid standards. What matters most is that students leave feeling capable, curious, and confident in their ability to observe, create, and think visually.
