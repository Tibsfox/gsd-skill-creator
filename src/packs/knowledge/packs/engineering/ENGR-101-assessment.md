# ENGR-101: Engineering & Design -- Assessment Framework

This assessment framework evaluates engineering design process skills, not factual recall. The focus is on how learners approach problems, not what they know about engineering terminology.

## Beginning

The learner is starting to develop engineering thinking. At this level:

- Can name some steps in the design process but may not follow them in order
- Tends to jump straight to building without defining the problem or brainstorming alternatives
- When something fails, tries random changes rather than analyzing why it failed
- May identify one or two constraints but misses others that are important
- Describes designs verbally but struggles to document them in writing or sketches
- Treats failure as a negative outcome rather than useful information
- Works with one material or approach without considering alternatives
- Needs significant guidance to complete a design cycle

**What success looks like at Beginning level:** The learner can build something that partially addresses a stated need and can describe what they built, even if they skipped most of the design process.

## Developing

The learner is building engineering habits with guidance. At this level:

- Follows the design process when prompted but may skip steps without reminders
- Can write a basic design brief with 2-3 requirements and 1-2 constraints
- Generates 2-3 solution ideas before selecting one, though ideas may be similar variations
- Tests prototypes but may not measure systematically or record data
- When something fails, can identify what went wrong but not always why
- Beginning to understand that materials have different properties and tradeoffs
- Can make one or two improvements based on test results
- Documents some of the design process but leaves gaps in the record

**What success looks like at Developing level:** The learner can complete a guided design cycle from problem definition through at least one iteration, with measurable improvement between versions.

## Proficient

The learner independently applies engineering thinking. At this level:

- Uses the full design process without prompting: define, ideate, prototype, test, iterate
- Writes clear design briefs with measurable success criteria and multiple constraints
- Generates 5+ genuinely different solution approaches before narrowing down
- Uses decision matrices or other structured evaluation to select solutions
- Builds quick, cheap prototypes focused on answering specific design questions
- Tests systematically against stated criteria and records data carefully
- Performs root cause analysis when prototypes fail (not just "it broke")
- Makes targeted, data-driven changes between iterations
- Selects materials based on relevant properties and justifies choices with tradeoff reasoning
- Maintains an engineering notebook documenting the full design cycle
- Can explain design decisions to others with clear reasoning

**What success looks like at Proficient level:** The learner can take a novel problem, complete the full design cycle independently, produce a working solution that meets most success criteria, and explain what they would improve with more time.

## Advanced

The learner demonstrates sophisticated engineering judgment. At this level:

- Adapts the design process to fit the problem rather than following it rigidly
- Considers stakeholders, environmental impact, accessibility, and ethics in problem definition
- Applies knowledge from multiple domains (materials, mechanisms, systems) in integrated designs
- Manages complex projects with many interacting constraints and subsystems
- Uses quantitative analysis (calculations, simulations, statistical testing) to predict and evaluate performance
- Mentors other learners through the design process, identifying where they are stuck
- Recognizes tradeoffs between competing constraints and makes justified judgment calls
- Documents designs thoroughly enough for someone else to reproduce them
- Can critique designs constructively, identifying both strengths and areas for improvement
- Knows when to stop iterating and can justify "good enough" decisions

**What success looks like at Advanced level:** The learner can lead a team through a complex, multi-constraint design challenge, producing a well-documented solution with quantitative performance evidence and a clear rationale for all major design decisions.

## Formative Assessment

Ongoing assessment strategies used during learning activities:

- **Design Brief Review:** At the start of each project, review the design brief for completeness. Are requirements measurable? Are constraints identified? Are success criteria specific enough to test against? Provide feedback before building begins.
- **Brainstorming Check:** After ideation, count distinct solution approaches. Are they genuinely different or minor variations? Push for at least one "wild" idea that challenges assumptions.
- **Prototype Checkpoint:** Before testing, ask: what specific question does this prototype answer? If the answer is "everything," the prototype is trying to do too much. Guide toward focused prototypes.
- **Testing Observation:** Watch how learners test. Are they measuring against criteria or just playing? Are they recording data or relying on memory? Are they controlling variables?
- **Failure Response:** When something breaks, observe the immediate reaction. Do they analyze or just rebuild? Prompt with "What happened? Where did it fail? Why do you think it failed there?"
- **Iteration Quality:** Between versions, check that changes are targeted and justified by data. Random changes indicate the learner is not connecting test results to design decisions.
- **Engineering Notebook Review:** Periodically review notebooks for completeness, clarity, and evidence of reflection. Notebooks should tell the story of the design process, not just show the final product.

## Summative Assessment

Evidence of mastery gathered at key milestones:

- **Engineering Portfolio:** A collection of 3-5 design projects showing the full cycle from problem definition through iteration. Each project includes design brief, brainstorm evidence, prototype photos or sketches, test data, failure analysis, and iteration documentation. Portfolio should show growth over time.
- **Novel Design Challenge:** Given a problem the learner has not seen before, with specific constraints and a time limit, complete the full design process independently. Assessed on process quality (did they follow the cycle?) and outcome quality (does the solution meet criteria?).
- **Peer Teaching:** Explain the engineering design process to someone who has not learned it. Guide them through a simple challenge. Effective teaching demonstrates deep understanding.
- **Design Review Presentation:** Present a completed project to an audience, explaining the problem, key design decisions, test results, iterations, and what you would improve. Audience asks questions. Assessed on depth of reasoning and ability to justify decisions.
- **Reflection Essay:** Analyze a past project in depth. What worked and why? What would you do differently and why? What did you learn from the failures? What engineering skills did you develop?

## Portfolio Suggestions

The engineering portfolio should demonstrate growth in design process skills across multiple projects. Suggested portfolio structure:

- **3 to 5 complete design projects** from different modules, showing breadth of engineering thinking
- **Each project includes:** design brief, brainstorm evidence (sketches, lists), prototype documentation (photos, drawings), test data (measurements, observations), failure analysis notes, iteration record (what changed and why), final reflection
- **A growth narrative:** a 1-page reflection on how your engineering thinking has changed from your first project to your most recent
- **A "Greatest Failure" entry:** your most spectacular or instructive failure, with thorough analysis of what you learned from it
- **A design you are proud of:** your best work, with an explanation of what makes it your best (not necessarily the one that worked perfectly, but the one where you learned the most or solved the hardest problem)

Portfolios are assessed on process quality (completeness and depth of design documentation), growth evidence (improvement visible across projects), and reflection depth (quality of analysis about what was learned and how thinking changed).
