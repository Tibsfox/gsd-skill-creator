## Vision

Engineering is the discipline of creating solutions that work in the real world under real constraints. While science asks "why does this happen?" engineering asks "how can I make something that does what I need?" It is the oldest form of applied intelligence: every bridge, every tool, every shelter, every machine, every piece of software is an engineering artifact. Engineering thinking is not reserved for professionals with degrees -- it is the systematic process of defining problems, imagining solutions, building prototypes, testing them, and improving them based on what you learn.

This pack teaches engineering as a way of thinking, not as a body of facts. The engineering design process is universal: it applies whether you are building a treehouse, designing a wheelchair ramp, programming a robot, or creating a water filtration system. At every level, from kindergarten to college, learners practice the same cycle -- define, ideate, prototype, test, iterate -- with increasing sophistication in constraints, materials, analysis, and documentation.

The goal is not to produce engineers (though some learners will become engineers). The goal is to produce people who can look at a problem, understand what constraints they face, generate multiple possible solutions, build something, test it honestly, and improve it. That is engineering thinking, and it is one of the most powerful cognitive tools a human being can develop.

## Problem Statement

Engineering is one of the most underrepresented disciplines in pre-college education. Most students never encounter formal engineering instruction until college -- if they encounter it at all. The consequences are significant:

**The Science Fair Confusion.** Most "science fairs" actually involve engineering projects (build a volcano, construct a bridge, design an experiment apparatus) but frame them as "science." This conflation means students never learn the engineering design process as a distinct, systematic methodology. They build things without understanding why the process of building matters.

**The Math-Science Pipeline Fallacy.** The dominant educational model treats engineering as something you do after you learn enough math and science. This is backwards. Engineering is how humans have always learned -- by making things. A child stacking blocks is engineering. A teenager modifying a bicycle is engineering. Waiting until college to teach the design process wastes a decade of natural engineering instinct.

**Failure as Stigma, Not Data.** In engineering, a failed prototype is not a mistake -- it is data. It tells you what does not work and points you toward what might. But most educational environments treat failure as something to avoid. Students who internalize "failure = bad" cannot become effective engineers, designers, or problem-solvers. This pack explicitly reframes failure as the most valuable part of the process.

**Constraint Blindness.** Real engineering always operates under constraints: budget, time, materials, safety, environmental impact, user needs. Students who have never practiced designing under constraints produce impractical solutions. They optimize for one variable (usually coolness) without understanding tradeoffs. Constraint-based thinking is a learnable skill, and it must be practiced early.

**The Gender and Access Gap.** Engineering has historically been framed as masculine and technical, excluding enormous populations from developing engineering thinking. By introducing engineering as universal problem-solving (not as "hard math for smart people"), this pack aims to make engineering identity accessible to every learner regardless of gender, background, or prior academic performance.

## Core Concepts

The five essential ideas that everything in this pack builds from:

1. **Define the Problem:** Before you can solve anything, you must understand what you are solving. This means identifying the need, understanding who has the need, defining what success looks like, and cataloging the constraints (time, money, materials, safety, environmental impact). A well-defined problem is half-solved. A poorly-defined problem leads to elegant solutions to the wrong question.

2. **Generate Solutions:** Once the problem is defined, generate as many potential solutions as possible before choosing one. This requires divergent thinking -- suspending judgment, combining ideas, considering unconventional approaches. The best solution is rarely the first idea. It is usually the fifth or tenth idea, informed by the flaws of the earlier ones. Feasibility analysis helps narrow the field: which solutions are actually buildable given the constraints?

3. **Prototype & Build:** An idea in your head is not engineering. Engineering requires making something real -- a physical model, a digital simulation, a working circuit, a cardboard mockup, a code prototype. The act of building reveals problems that thinking alone cannot find. Materials have properties. Connections have limits. Gravity is unforgiving. Prototyping is where theory meets reality.

4. **Test & Evaluate:** A prototype must be tested against the original success criteria, not against your hopes. Testing requires honesty, measurement, and comparison. Did the bridge hold the required weight? Did the water filter remove the target contaminants? Did the app complete the task in under 3 seconds? Failure analysis is the most important engineering skill: when something fails, understanding why it failed is more valuable than the prototype itself.

5. **Iterate & Improve:** Engineering is never one-and-done. Every test produces data. Every failure reveals a path forward. Iteration means redesigning based on evidence, not guessing. It means making targeted changes (not starting over) and testing again. The design cycle is a spiral, not a line: each pass through define-ideate-build-test produces a better solution. Optimization means finding the best tradeoff among competing constraints, not maximizing a single variable.

## Module 1: The Engineering Design Process

### What It Teaches

- How to identify and define a problem worth solving
- How to write clear requirements and success criteria
- How to catalog constraints (budget, time, materials, safety, environment, user needs)
- How to distinguish between needs and wants in a design brief
- How to conduct user research and empathy interviews
- How to brainstorm without premature judgment
- How to evaluate ideas against constraints using decision matrices
- How to select a solution and justify the choice

### Why This Module Comes First

Everything in engineering begins with defining the problem correctly. Students who skip problem definition build things nobody needs or things that fail because they optimized for the wrong variable. This module establishes the discipline of understanding before building -- the single most important habit in engineering.

### Interactive Elements

- **Activity: The Marshmallow Challenge** -- Build the tallest freestanding structure from spaghetti, tape, string, and a marshmallow. Teaches that prototyping beats planning when materials are unfamiliar. The marshmallow always goes on top first in successful teams (test early, not late).
- **Activity: Design Brief Workshop** -- Given a real problem (design a better backpack for a student with a disability), write a complete design brief with requirements, constraints, and success criteria. Practice distinguishing needs from wants.
- **Activity: Customer Needs Interview** -- Interview a real person about a real frustration. Translate their complaints into engineering requirements. Practice empathy as an engineering skill.
- **Exploration Point:** Compare how different engineering disciplines (civil, mechanical, electrical, software) define problems. What language do they use? What constraints are universal?

### Technical Implementation Notes

The design process module uses a structured template system in GSD-OS. Learners fill in design brief templates with increasing complexity by grade level. Skill-creator observes which steps students skip (usually testing criteria) and which they over-invest in (usually brainstorming without constraints). Progress is measured by completeness and quality of design documentation, not by the quality of the final product.

### Grade-Level Progression

**Foundation (PreK-2):** "What is the problem?" "What do we need our thing to do?" "What can we use to build it?" Simple verbal problem statements. One or two constraints (it must stand up; it must hold a ball).

**Elementary (3-5):** Written design briefs with 3-4 constraints. Brainstorming lists with at least 5 ideas before selecting. Simple decision matrices (pros/cons for each idea). Testing against stated criteria.

**Middle School (6-8):** Formal design briefs with user research. Weighted decision matrices. Feasibility analysis. Engineering notebooks with dated entries. Peer review of design briefs.

**High School (9-12):** Professional-format design documents. Stakeholder analysis. Risk assessment. Budget and timeline estimation. Design reviews with external feedback.

**College+ (13+):** Industry-standard requirements documents. Trade studies. Systems engineering frameworks. Regulatory compliance considerations.

## Module 2: Materials & Structures

### What It Teaches

- Properties of common materials (wood, metal, plastic, fabric, paper, concrete, composites)
- How material properties affect design choices (strength, weight, cost, workability, durability)
- Basic structural principles (compression, tension, torsion, shear)
- Why triangles are the strongest shape in structures
- How load paths work -- tracing forces through a structure
- How structures fail and what failure modes look like
- How to select materials based on constraints
- Basics of material testing (bend tests, load tests, drop tests)

### Why Materials Matter

You cannot design what you cannot build, and you cannot build what you do not understand. Materials are the vocabulary of engineering. A student who understands that wood is strong in compression but weak in tension makes fundamentally different design choices than one who treats all materials as interchangeable. This module gives learners the material literacy they need to make real things.

### Interactive Elements

- **Activity: Strongest Bridge from 100 Popsicle Sticks** -- Design and build a bridge using exactly 100 popsicle sticks and glue. Test to failure. Analyze where and why it broke. Teaches load paths, structural triangulation, and material limits.
- **Activity: Material Strength Testing Lab** -- Test paper, cardboard, balsa wood, aluminum foil, and string for tension, compression, and bending strength. Create a materials comparison chart. Learn that every material has tradeoffs.
- **Activity: Earthquake-Resistant Tower** -- Build a tower from marshmallows and toothpicks that survives simulated shaking. Introduces the concept of dynamic loading and structural resilience.
- **Exploration Point:** Find three structures in your neighborhood. What materials are they made from? Why those materials and not others? What constraints drove the material choices?

### Technical Implementation Notes

Materials module integrates with a virtual materials database in GSD-OS. Each material has property cards (strength, weight, cost, environmental impact, workability). Learners can compare materials side-by-side and make selections for their design projects. Skill-creator tracks which material properties students consider in their decisions and flags when students ignore critical properties (like cost or environmental impact).

### Grade-Level Progression

**Foundation (PreK-2):** Exploring materials by touch and observation. "Is it hard or soft? Heavy or light? Does it bend?" Building with blocks, cardboard, tape, clay. Noticing which materials work for which jobs.

**Elementary (3-5):** Simple material testing. Comparing strength of paper tubes vs. flat paper. Building bridges and towers with constraints. Introduction to structural shapes (arches, triangles). Material selection for simple projects.

**Middle School (6-8):** Formal material properties (tensile strength, compressive strength, elasticity). Load testing with measurement. Introduction to composites. Failure analysis with root cause identification. Material selection matrices.

**High School (9-12):** Stress and strain concepts. Factor of safety. Material science fundamentals (crystalline structure, polymers, alloys). Computer-aided structural analysis. Environmental lifecycle of materials.

**College+ (13+):** Advanced materials science. Finite element analysis. Material selection databases (Ashby charts). Composite design. Smart materials and nanomaterials.

## Module 3: Mechanisms & Systems

### What It Teaches

- Simple machines (lever, wheel & axle, pulley, inclined plane, wedge, screw)
- How gears work -- gear ratios, speed vs. torque tradeoffs
- Linkages and how motion is transmitted and transformed
- Basic circuits -- voltage, current, resistance, series vs. parallel
- Sensors and actuators -- how machines sense and respond to the world
- Control systems -- feedback loops, on/off control, proportional control
- Systems thinking -- how components interact to produce emergent behavior
- How to decompose a complex system into subsystems

### Why Mechanisms Matter

Every machine, every robot, every automated system is built from simple mechanisms combined in clever ways. A student who understands gears, linkages, and circuits can look at any machine and understand how it works. More importantly, they can design new machines by combining known mechanisms in new configurations. This module transforms students from users of technology into understanders and creators of technology.

### Interactive Elements

- **Activity: Build a Simple Machine Chain Reaction** -- Design and build a Rube Goldberg machine using at least 4 of the 6 simple machines. Each stage triggers the next. Teaches energy transfer, mechanical advantage, and the importance of reliability at each stage.
- **Activity: Design a Gear Train** -- Using physical or virtual gears, design a gear train that achieves a specified speed reduction or increase. Calculate gear ratios. Understand the speed-torque tradeoff. Build and test.
- **Activity: Circuit Challenge** -- Build circuits that accomplish specific tasks (light an LED, spin a motor, trigger a buzzer when a switch is pressed). Progress from series to parallel to combination circuits.
- **Activity: Feedback Loop Demonstration** -- Build a simple thermostat-like system using a sensor and actuator. Observe how feedback creates self-regulating behavior. Discuss real-world examples (thermostats, cruise control, biological homeostasis).
- **Exploration Point:** Pick any machine in your house (toaster, bicycle, washing machine). Identify every simple machine inside it. How do they work together?

### Technical Implementation Notes

Mechanisms module uses interactive simulations in GSD-OS for gear trains, linkages, and circuits. Virtual breadboards allow circuit design without physical components. Skill-creator observes students' ability to predict system behavior before testing (hypothesis formation) and tracks the gap between prediction and reality (calibration improvement over time).

### Grade-Level Progression

**Foundation (PreK-2):** Exploring simple machines in everyday objects. Ramps, levers, wheels. "What makes it easier to move?" Building with gears and pulleys using toy sets. Cause-and-effect chains.

**Elementary (3-5):** Identifying all 6 simple machines. Measuring mechanical advantage of levers and pulleys. Building simple circuits with batteries, wires, and bulbs. Chain reaction machines. Introduction to gear ratios through hands-on exploration.

**Middle School (6-8):** Calculating mechanical advantage and gear ratios. Series and parallel circuits with measurement. Introduction to sensors and actuators. Programmable systems (basic robotics). Linkage design and motion analysis.

**High School (9-12):** Complex mechanism design. Electronic circuit analysis (Ohm's law, Kirchhoff's laws). Control systems and feedback. Pneumatics and hydraulics. Computer-controlled systems. Integration of mechanical and electrical subsystems.

**College+ (13+):** Advanced mechanism design. Power electronics. PID control. Embedded systems. Mechatronics. Systems integration and testing. Industrial automation.

## Module 4: Prototyping & Testing

### What It Teaches

- How to build quick, rough prototypes that answer specific questions
- The difference between proof-of-concept, functional, and production prototypes
- How to design tests that actually measure what matters
- How to collect data during testing (measurement, observation, recording)
- Failure analysis -- systematic methods for understanding why something broke
- How to iterate based on test results (not guesses)
- Documentation of the design-build-test cycle
- When to stop iterating and declare "good enough"

### Why Prototyping & Testing Is Its Own Module

Many students (and adults) think engineering is mostly design. In reality, engineering is mostly building and testing. The ability to quickly create a prototype that answers a specific design question -- without over-investing in polish or completeness -- is the skill that separates effective engineers from everyone else. This module teaches learners to build cheap, fast, and ugly prototypes that produce valuable data.

### Interactive Elements

- **Activity: Paper Airplane Redesign Iteration** -- Fold a paper airplane. Measure flight distance. Identify one thing to change. Refold with that change. Measure again. Repeat 5 times. Graph the results. Teaches the power of systematic iteration vs. random changes.
- **Activity: Crash Test Dummy Safety Improvement** -- Design and build a protective enclosure for an egg (the "passenger") on a toy car. Crash the car into a wall. Measure damage. Redesign. Teaches safety engineering, failure analysis, and iteration under constraint.
- **Activity: Prototype Sprint** -- Given a problem statement, build three different quick prototypes in 30 minutes. Test all three. Pick the best one to develop further. Teaches that multiple prototypes beat one polished prototype.
- **Activity: Failure Museum** -- Collect and display failed prototypes with analysis cards explaining what was learned from each failure. Celebrates failure as data and normalizes the iterative process.
- **Exploration Point:** Interview someone who builds things for a living (carpenter, mechanic, programmer, chef). How many times do they try something before it works? How do they learn from failures?

### Technical Implementation Notes

Prototyping module is the most hands-on module in the pack. GSD-OS provides digital engineering notebooks for documenting the build-test-iterate cycle, with photo upload capability for prototype documentation. Templates guide learners through structured failure analysis (what happened, why it happened, what to change). Skill-creator tracks the number of iteration cycles and whether changes are data-driven or random.

### Grade-Level Progression

**Foundation (PreK-2):** Build something. Test it. "Did it work? What happened? What should we try different?" Simple verbal reflection. Rebuilding with one change. Celebrating "it broke!" as useful information.

**Elementary (3-5):** Building prototypes from simple materials. Testing with basic measurement (rulers, scales, timers). Recording results in tables. Making one change at a time. Before-and-after comparison. Drawing designs and labeling changes between iterations.

**Middle School (6-8):** Multiple prototype strategies (sketch models, cardboard mockups, computer models). Controlled testing with variables isolated. Data tables and graphs. Formal failure analysis reports. Engineering notebooks with dated entries. Peer testing and feedback.

**High School (9-12):** CAD-to-prototype workflows. Statistical analysis of test results. Design of experiments (controlled variables, sample sizes). Technical writing for test reports. Design reviews with external audiences. Cost-benefit analysis of iterations.

**College+ (13+):** Rapid prototyping technologies (3D printing, laser cutting, CNC). Design validation and verification. Reliability testing. User acceptance testing. Production readiness assessment. Documentation for manufacturing.

## Module 5: Engineering Ethics & Impact

### What It Teaches

- Engineers have responsibility for the safety and welfare of the public
- Every engineering decision has consequences beyond the immediate project
- Environmental impact assessment -- understanding what building things costs the planet
- Human factors and accessibility -- designing for all users, not just the "average" user
- The difference between "can we build it?" and "should we build it?"
- Case studies of engineering failures and their consequences
- Professional ethics codes and why they exist
- Equity in engineering -- who benefits and who bears the costs?

### Why Ethics Is Part of Engineering, Not Separate

Engineering ethics is not a philosophy course bolted onto a technical curriculum. It is an integral part of every engineering decision. When a civil engineer decides on a factor of safety, that is an ethical decision about how much risk is acceptable. When a software engineer decides what data to collect, that is an ethical decision about privacy. This module teaches that ethics is not separate from engineering -- it IS engineering, because engineering without ethics produces bridges that fall, software that discriminates, and products that harm.

### Interactive Elements

- **Activity: The Trolley Problem, Engineering Edition** -- Present real engineering dilemmas (self-driving car collision decisions, dam construction displacing communities, chemical plant location near residential areas). Discuss tradeoffs. There are no easy answers, and that is the point.
- **Activity: Accessibility Audit** -- Audit a real space (school, park, store) for accessibility. Identify barriers. Propose engineering solutions with cost estimates. Teaches that design choices include or exclude people.
- **Activity: Environmental Impact Report** -- For a proposed project (building a new road, manufacturing a product, developing software that requires server farms), estimate the environmental impact. Consider materials, energy, waste, and end-of-life.
- **Exploration Point:** Research an engineering failure (Challenger disaster, Tacoma Narrows Bridge, Therac-25 radiation machine). What went wrong technically? What went wrong ethically? Could it have been prevented?

### Technical Implementation Notes

Ethics module uses case study methodology extensively. GSD-OS presents dilemmas with no single correct answer and tracks how students reason through tradeoffs. Skill-creator observes whether students consider multiple stakeholders, whether they identify non-obvious consequences, and whether their ethical reasoning matures over time (from "rules" to "principles" to "judgment").

### Grade-Level Progression

**Foundation (PreK-2):** "Is it fair?" "Who could get hurt?" "Did we think about everyone?" Simple discussions about sharing materials, building things that everyone can use, and not breaking other people's things.

**Elementary (3-5):** Introduction to safety in design. "What could go wrong?" Environmental awareness -- where do materials come from and where do they go? Designing for people with different abilities. Taking responsibility for design choices.

**Middle School (6-8):** Case studies of engineering failures (age-appropriate). Stakeholder analysis -- who is affected by this design? Environmental impact basics. Introduction to professional ethics. Discussing technology's unintended consequences.

**High School (9-12):** Formal engineering ethics frameworks. Environmental impact assessment. Risk-benefit analysis. Case studies with nuance and complexity. Professional responsibility and whistleblowing. Global impact of engineering decisions. Design for social justice.

**College+ (13+):** Professional ethics codes (NSPE, IEEE, ACM). Regulatory frameworks. Liability and legal responsibility. Sustainable engineering. Global development ethics. Technology policy and governance. Research ethics.

## Assessment Framework

### How Do We Know Progress Is Happening?

| Level | Indicator | Assessment Method |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| Beginning | Can name the steps of the design process | Conversation, labeling, matching activities |
| Developing | Can follow the design process with guidance | Guided projects, design brief completion, structured reflection |
| Proficient | Can independently apply the design process to new problems | Independent projects, engineering notebooks, design reviews |
| Advanced | Can teach the design process to others, adapt it to complex problems | Mentoring, complex multi-constraint projects, innovation |

### Formative Assessment (During Learning)

- **Design Brief Quality:** Are requirements clear? Are constraints identified? Are success criteria measurable?
- **Brainstorming Breadth:** How many distinct ideas are generated? Are they genuinely different approaches or minor variations?
- **Prototype Quality:** Does the prototype answer a specific question? Is it built quickly and cheaply (not over-polished)?
- **Testing Rigor:** Are tests measuring the right things? Is data being collected systematically?
- **Iteration Evidence:** Are changes based on test data? Is there documentation of what changed and why?
- **Failure Response:** When something breaks, does the student analyze why or just try again randomly?

### Summative Assessment (Evidence of Mastery)

- **Engineering Portfolio:** A collection of design projects showing the full cycle from problem definition through iteration. Each project includes design brief, brainstorm evidence, prototype photos/sketches, test data, failure analysis, and iteration documentation.
- **Design Challenge:** Given a novel problem with specific constraints, complete the full design process independently within a time limit.
- **Peer Teaching:** Explain the design process to someone who has not learned it. Effective teaching demonstrates deep understanding.
- **Reflection Essay:** Analyze a past project. What worked? What would you do differently? What did you learn from failures?

## Parent Guidance

### If You Do Not Know Engineering...

Good news: you do not need to be an engineer to facilitate engineering learning. Engineering is fundamentally about solving problems, and every parent solves problems every day. When you fix a leaky faucet, organize a closet, plan a trip within a budget, or figure out how to fit furniture through a doorway, you are doing engineering.

Your role is to ask questions, not provide answers:
- "What are you trying to make it do?"
- "What's stopping it from working?"
- "What else could you try?"
- "What did you learn from that?"

The most important thing you can do is resist the urge to fix it for them. Let them struggle. Let them fail. Let them figure it out.

### Key Phrases to Encourage Engineering Thinking

- "What is the problem you are trying to solve?"
- "What are the constraints? What can't you change?"
- "Can you think of three different ways to solve this?"
- "What would happen if you tried...?"
- "Why do you think it broke? Where exactly did it fail?"
- "What would you change if you built it again?"
- "What data do you have? What does it tell you?"
- "Is this good enough, or should we iterate again?"

### Common Misconceptions

- **"Engineering is just building stuff."** Building is one step. Engineering includes defining the problem, researching, brainstorming, evaluating, testing, and iterating. Building without the other steps is not engineering -- it is tinkering (which is valuable, but different).

- **"If it broke, you failed."** In engineering, a broken prototype is the most valuable thing you can produce. It tells you exactly what does not work. The only real failure is not learning from the breakage.

- **"You need to be good at math to do engineering."** Math helps at advanced levels, but the core engineering design process requires no math at all for young learners. Counting, measuring, and comparing are enough to start. Mathematical sophistication grows naturally as problems become more complex.

- **"Engineering is for boys."** Engineering is for everyone who wants to solve problems. Full stop. The historical gender imbalance in engineering is a product of culture, not capability. This pack actively frames engineering as universal.

- **"There is one right answer."** In engineering, there are many possible solutions, each with different tradeoffs. Two engineers can solve the same problem with completely different designs, and both can be "right" for different reasons. Judgment matters more than knowledge.

### When to Get Help

If your learner is consistently frustrated (not productively challenged, but genuinely stuck and unhappy), look for:
- Local maker spaces with mentors
- FIRST Robotics teams (FIRST LEGO League for younger students)
- Engineering is Elementary (EiE) curriculum resources
- Community college engineering courses open to high school students
- PBS Design Squad Nation videos for inspiration
- Local engineers who might mentor (many engineering societies have outreach programs)

## Connection to Other Packs

This pack directly connects to and complements:

- **MATH-101 (Mathematics):** Mathematical reasoning underlies engineering analysis. Measurement, geometry, algebra, and statistics are all tools that engineers use daily. Engineering provides the "why do I need math?" answer that many students crave.

- **SCI-101 (Science):** Science discovers the principles; engineering applies them. Understanding physics (forces, energy, materials) enables better engineering. Engineering projects motivate scientific inquiry ("why did my bridge break?" leads to learning about forces).

- **CODE-101 (Coding & Computer Science):** Software engineering is engineering. The design process applies identically to code: define the problem, design a solution, implement, test, iterate. Many modern engineering projects combine physical and digital systems.

- **ART-101 (Visual Arts & Design):** Engineering and art share design thinking. Aesthetics matter in engineering -- users prefer beautiful solutions. Sketching and visualization are core engineering skills.

- **MFAB-101 (Maker & Fabrication):** This pack enables MFAB-101. Once students understand the engineering design process, they are ready to learn advanced fabrication techniques (3D printing, laser cutting, CNC machining, electronics).

- **TRADE-101 (Trades & Craftsmanship):** Engineering thinking applies to every trade. Carpentry, plumbing, electrical work, and welding all follow the design process. Understanding engineering makes tradespeople more effective problem-solvers.

ENGR-101 appears in the GSD-OS learning dashboard as a wrench icon in brown (#795548), positioned in the Core Academic ring. It connects visually to MATH-101 and SCI-101 (recommended prior knowledge) and to MFAB-101 and TRADE-101 (packs it enables). Progress is shown as completion percentage across all five modules, with a secondary indicator showing the number of complete design cycles documented.

### Skill-Creator Integration

- **Pattern Observation:** Skill-creator watches for students who consistently skip steps in the design process (usually testing or iteration) and prompts them to slow down and complete the full cycle.
- **Success Promotion:** When a student develops an effective approach to failure analysis, prototyping, or brainstorming, skill-creator can promote that approach as a reusable skill template.
- **Cross-Pack Connections:** Engineering activities that naturally involve math (measurement, calculation), science (materials, forces), or coding (automation, control) trigger cross-pack skill suggestions.

### Activity Generation

When `gsd new-project` scaffolds activities for ENGR-101, it generates:
- A design brief template appropriate to the learner's grade level
- Material constraints matched to available supplies
- Testing criteria that are measurable and grade-appropriate
- Iteration tracking templates for the engineering notebook
- Reflection prompts that guide failure analysis

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can someone learn engineering without learning math and science first?**
A: Yes, absolutely. The engineering design process requires no math or science at the foundation level. Young children engineer constantly when they build with blocks, design blanket forts, or figure out how to get a toy out of a tight space. Math and science become increasingly useful as engineering problems become more complex, but they are not prerequisites for starting. This pack recommends MATH-101 and SCI-101 as prior knowledge, not requirements.

**Q: How long does it take to go through this pack?**
A: It depends on depth and grade level. Foundation learners (PreK-2) might spend 30-50 hours across all modules over a school year. Elementary learners (3-5) might spend 50-80 hours. Middle school (6-8) might spend 80-120 hours. High school (9-12) might spend 120-180 hours. These are estimates -- engineering is best learned by doing, and some projects take longer than others.

**Q: Is this for homeschooling or classroom use?**
A: Both. The activities are designed to work with individual learners (homeschool), small groups (co-ops, after-school programs), and full classrooms. Most activities can be scaled from 1 to 30 learners. The key resource requirements are everyday materials (cardboard, tape, popsicle sticks, paper, string) rather than specialized equipment.

**Q: My child hates building things. Can we skip engineering?**
A: Engineering is not limited to physical building. Software engineering, systems engineering, process engineering, and design engineering all involve minimal physical construction. A child who dislikes using their hands might love designing systems, writing requirements, or analyzing failures. Try the problem-definition and testing modules first -- they emphasize thinking over building. If a child genuinely dislikes all aspects of engineering thinking, focus on other packs and return to engineering when a real problem motivates it.

**Q: What materials do we need?**
A: Most activities use everyday materials: cardboard, tape, glue, popsicle sticks, paper, string, rubber bands, paper clips, straws, marshmallows, and toothpicks. For electronics activities, a basic kit with batteries, wires, LEDs, switches, and a buzzer costs under $20. No specialized or expensive equipment is required. The whole point is that engineering works with what you have.

**Q: How is this different from a "maker" curriculum?**
A: Maker curricula focus on building and creating (which is wonderful). Engineering adds the systematic design process: define the problem before building, test against criteria, iterate based on data. Making is "build cool things." Engineering is "solve real problems by building things systematically." This pack builds the engineering thinking that makes making more effective.

## Evolution of This Pack

### Version 1.0 (Current)

- Initial vision and five core modules
- Engineering design process as central framework
- Parent guidance for non-engineer families
- Assessment framework based on design process skills
- Vetted resource list focused on free and accessible materials

- Maker space equipment guides for schools and libraries
- Advanced assessment rubrics with portfolio analysis

*This knowledge pack establishes engineering as systematic, creative problem-solving under constraints. The engineering design process -- define, ideate, prototype, test, iterate -- is a universal tool for creating things that work in the real world. Every learner deserves to develop this capability, regardless of whether they become professional engineers.*
