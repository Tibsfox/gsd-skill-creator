# HIST-101: History & Civilization -- Assessment Framework

This assessment framework evaluates historical thinking skills, not factual recall. The focus is on how learners analyze evidence, reason about causation, consider perspectives, and construct arguments -- not whether they can recite dates, names, and events.

## Beginning

The learner is starting to develop historical thinking. At this level:

- Can identify that some things are "old" and some things are "new" but struggles to place events in chronological order
- Tends to accept a single account of the past as "what happened" without questioning source or perspective
- When asked why something happened, offers a single cause without considering contributing factors
- May confuse primary and secondary sources or not understand the distinction
- Describes the past in terms of "good guys" and "bad guys" rather than understanding context
- Judges historical actors entirely by modern standards without considering their context
- Can retell a historical narrative but cannot analyze it
- Needs significant guidance to engage with historical sources

**What success looks like at Beginning level:** The learner can describe events in the past using simple chronological language (before, after, long ago) and can identify one reason why a historical event happened, even if the reasoning is simplistic.

## Developing

The learner is building historical thinking habits with guidance. At this level:

- Can place events in chronological order and construct simple timelines
- Beginning to distinguish between primary sources (created at the time) and secondary sources (created later)
- When analyzing a source, can identify who created it and when, but may not consider purpose or bias
- Can identify 2-3 causes of a historical event with prompting
- Recognizes that different people experienced the same event differently, but struggles to explain why
- Beginning to use evidence from sources to support claims about the past
- Can compare two time periods and identify obvious differences, but may miss continuities
- Tends to describe what happened rather than analyze why it happened or what it means

**What success looks like at Developing level:** The learner can analyze a primary source with guided questions (who created it, when, for whom, why), can identify multiple causes for an event, and can recognize that at least two different perspectives exist on the same event.

## Proficient

The learner independently applies historical thinking skills. At this level:

- Analyzes sources systematically: identifies author, audience, purpose, context, and potential biases without prompting
- Corroborates claims by comparing multiple sources and noting agreements and contradictions
- Constructs multi-causal explanations that distinguish between triggers and underlying conditions
- Considers multiple perspectives on events and can explain why different groups experienced them differently
- Writes evidence-based historical arguments with a clear thesis supported by source evidence
- Understands that all historical narratives are selective constructions, not neutral reports of fact
- Can identify continuity and change across time periods and evaluate the significance of turning points
- Addresses counterarguments in written and oral historical reasoning
- Distinguishes between description (what happened), analysis (why it happened), and evaluation (what it means)
- Recognizes when their own perspective or assumptions might be influencing their interpretation

**What success looks like at Proficient level:** The learner can take a historical question, find and analyze relevant sources independently, construct a multi-causal explanation or evidence-based argument, consider at least two perspectives, and present findings clearly with proper source attribution.

## Advanced

The learner demonstrates sophisticated historical judgment. At this level:

- Evaluates historiographic debates: understands how and why scholarly interpretations change over time
- Conducts independent primary source research using archives, collections, and databases
- Constructs original historical arguments that contribute to understanding of a topic
- Analyzes how power, identity, and social position shape which histories are told and which are silenced
- Holds multiple perspectives simultaneously while making reasoned judgments about significance and meaning
- Understands the philosophical questions underlying historical practice (objectivity, truth, memory, narrative)
- Mentors other learners in historical thinking skills, diagnosing where they are stuck
- Recognizes the ethical responsibilities of writing and teaching history
- Can adapt analytical methods to different types of evidence (textual, visual, material, oral, digital)
- Produces work that could be shared with a scholarly or public audience

**What success looks like at Advanced level:** The learner can conduct original research on a historical question, engage with scholarly debate, produce a well-argued interpretation supported by primary source evidence, and reflect thoughtfully on the methodological choices involved.

## Formative Assessment

Ongoing assessment strategies used during learning activities:

- **Source Analysis Check:** When learners encounter a new source, observe whether they automatically ask: Who created this? When? For whom? Why? What is it not telling me? Provide feedback on analysis depth and prompt for missing steps.
- **Causal Reasoning Depth:** After learners explain why an event happened, count the number of causes identified. Are they all the same type (political) or do they span multiple domains (economic, social, environmental)? Push for deeper and broader causal reasoning.
- **Perspective Recognition:** When discussing historical events, ask: Whose perspective is this account written from? Whose voice is missing? Can you imagine how someone in a different position experienced this? Observe whether learners can shift perspectives without prompting.
- **Evidence Use:** When learners make claims about the past, check whether they cite specific evidence. Are they paraphrasing accurately? Are they distinguishing between what the source says and what they infer from it?
- **Argument Quality:** When learners write or present historical arguments, evaluate: Is there a clear thesis? Is evidence relevant and properly attributed? Is there analysis (not just quotation)? Are counterarguments addressed?
- **Continuity Awareness:** When discussing change over time, check whether learners also identify what stayed the same. Prompt for continuity alongside change.
- **Presentism Check:** When learners judge historical actors, observe whether they consider the actor's context. Prompt with: "What did people at that time believe? What information did they have? What pressures were they under?"

## Summative Assessment

Evidence of mastery gathered at key milestones:

- **Historical Portfolio:** A collection of 3-5 pieces demonstrating growth in historical thinking skills. Should include at least one source analysis, one causal explanation, one perspective-taking piece, and one evidence-based argument. Portfolio should show development over time, not just final products.
- **Independent Research Project:** Choose a historical question, conduct research using both primary and secondary sources, construct an evidence-based argument, and present findings. Assessed on question quality, source selection and analysis, argument structure, and depth of historical reasoning.
- **Source Analysis Exam:** Given an unfamiliar primary source and a set of contextual information, produce a thorough analysis: identify the source type, author, audience, purpose, and context; evaluate reliability; explain what the source reveals about its time period; and identify what additional sources would be needed to corroborate or challenge its claims.
- **Peer Teaching:** Explain a historical thinking skill (source analysis, causal reasoning, perspective-taking) to someone who has not learned it, then guide them through a practice exercise. Effective teaching demonstrates deep understanding of the skill.
- **Historiographic Essay (Advanced):** Survey how scholarly interpretation of a historical topic has changed over time. Analyze what drove the changes in interpretation and what this reveals about history as a discipline.

## Portfolio Suggestions

The historical thinking portfolio should demonstrate growth in inquiry skills across multiple types of historical reasoning. Suggested portfolio structure:

- **3 to 5 complete pieces** showing different historical thinking skills (source analysis, causal explanation, perspective-taking, argumentation, continuity and change)
- **Each piece includes:** the historical question addressed, the sources used (with citations), the analysis or argument produced, and a reflection on the thinking process
- **A growth narrative:** a 1-page reflection on how your historical thinking has changed from your earliest piece to your most recent -- what can you do now that you could not do before?
- **A source analysis showcase:** your best piece of source analysis, with an explanation of what makes it strong (did you identify something others missed? did you successfully corroborate across sources?)
- **A perspective you changed:** a piece where you started with one interpretation and changed your mind based on evidence -- with an honest account of what changed your thinking

Portfolios are assessed on reasoning quality (depth and sophistication of historical thinking), evidence use (quality of source analysis and citation), growth evidence (visible improvement across pieces), and reflection depth (quality of metacognitive analysis about how thinking changed).
