# PHYS-101: Assessment Framework

This document defines the assessment framework for the Physics Foundational Knowledge Pack. Assessment in physics should emphasize conceptual understanding and experimental reasoning over formula memorization. A learner who can explain why something happens, design an experiment to test it, and connect it to other phenomena demonstrates deeper mastery than one who can only solve textbook problems.

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing awareness of physical phenomena. They can identify and describe what they observe using everyday language but have not yet connected observations to formal physics concepts.

**Indicators:**
- Identifies physical phenomena in everyday life (things falling, rolling, bouncing)
- Uses everyday language to describe observations ("it went fast," "it got hot")
- Recognizes that physical events have causes ("something made it move")
- Sorts materials by observable physical properties (heavy/light, rough/smooth)
- Asks questions about why physical events happen

**Evidence looks like:**
- Drawing or describing what happened during an experiment
- Pointing out examples of pushes, pulls, or energy changes in daily life
- Asking "why" questions about physical phenomena
- Grouping objects by physical characteristics

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building mental models. They make predictions before experiments, connect new observations to things they already know, and begin using physics vocabulary appropriately.

**Indicators:**
- Makes predictions before experiments and explains reasoning
- Connects observations to prior knowledge ("this is like when...")
- Uses physics vocabulary in context (force, energy, wave, circuit)
- Identifies patterns in experimental data
- Distinguishes observation from inference

**Evidence looks like:**
- Written predictions with justifications before conducting experiments
- Correct use of physics terms in explanations
- Simple data tables and graphs from experiments
- Identifying when a prediction was wrong and suggesting why
- Describing cause-and-effect relationships in physical systems

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level use models and mathematics to explain and predict physical phenomena. They can design experiments, analyze data quantitatively, and communicate their reasoning clearly.

**Indicators:**
- Uses physical models (free body diagrams, energy bar charts, circuit diagrams) to analyze situations
- Applies mathematical relationships to make quantitative predictions
- Designs controlled experiments with identified variables
- Analyzes data using graphs, calculations, and statistical reasoning
- Communicates physics reasoning clearly in writing and speech

**Evidence looks like:**
- Lab reports with hypothesis, procedure, data analysis, and conclusion
- Correct application of physics equations to solve problems
- Self-designed experiments with controlled variables
- Clear written explanations connecting evidence to conclusions
- Teaching a concept to a peer and answering their questions

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level apply physics principles to novel and complex situations. They critique experimental designs, synthesize concepts across modules, and contribute original thinking.

**Indicators:**
- Applies physics principles to unfamiliar or real-world scenarios
- Critiques experimental designs and identifies sources of error
- Synthesizes concepts across multiple modules (e.g., using energy conservation and wave theory together)
- Proposes and investigates original questions
- Evaluates the limitations and assumptions of physical models

**Evidence looks like:**
- Independent research projects investigating original questions
- Peer review of other learners' lab reports with constructive feedback
- Essays connecting physics concepts to technology, engineering, or societal issues
- Solutions to open-ended problems with multiple valid approaches
- Mentoring younger or less experienced learners

## Formative Assessment

Formative assessment happens during learning. Its purpose is to provide feedback that helps learners and mentors adjust their approach. Physics formative assessment should be low-stakes, frequent, and focused on understanding rather than correctness.

### Prediction Journals
Before every experiment or demonstration, learners write what they think will happen and why. After observing the outcome, they compare their prediction to reality and reflect on any gaps. This practice builds the habit of making and testing mental models.

**Frequency:** Every experiment or demonstration
**What to look for:** Are predictions becoming more specific over time? Is the learner using physics concepts in their reasoning? Are reflections identifying which assumptions were wrong?

### Conceptual Questions
Pose questions that require explanation without calculation. Examples:
- "If you drop a heavy ball and a light ball from the same height in a vacuum, which hits the ground first? Explain."
- "A car is moving at constant velocity. What can you say about the forces on it?"
- "Why does your car lurch forward when you brake suddenly?"

**Frequency:** Weekly
**What to look for:** Depth of reasoning, correct identification of relevant principles, ability to distinguish correct from incorrect intuitions.

### Peer Explanation
Learners explain a concept or experiment result to a partner who hasn't studied it yet. The partner asks clarifying questions. If the explanation is clear and accurate, understanding is solid.

**Frequency:** End of each topic or module
**What to look for:** Accuracy of explanation, ability to handle follow-up questions, use of examples and analogies, avoidance of jargon without explanation.

### Error Analysis
When an experiment produces unexpected results or a prediction is wrong, learners analyze why. They identify which assumption was incorrect, what they overlooked, or what variable they failed to control.

**Frequency:** Whenever predictions fail
**What to look for:** Growth in analytical thinking, willingness to embrace errors as learning opportunities, ability to revise mental models based on new evidence.

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment evaluates mastery at the end of a module or unit. It should be comprehensive, allowing learners to demonstrate understanding through multiple modalities.

### Lab Portfolio
A curated collection of the learner's best experimental work. Should include:
- At least one experiment per module
- Hypothesis, procedure, data, analysis, and conclusion for each
- Reflection on what was learned and what would be done differently
- Evidence of growth over time (compare early and late entries)

**Assessment criteria:** Completeness, accuracy, depth of analysis, quality of reflection, evidence of scientific thinking.

### Design Challenges
Open-ended problems that require applying multiple physics concepts:
- "Design a container that keeps ice frozen for the longest time" (heat transfer, insulation, energy)
- "Build a bridge from popsicle sticks that supports the most weight" (forces, materials, structural analysis)
- "Create a Rube Goldberg machine with at least 5 energy transformations" (energy conservation, forces, motion)

**Assessment criteria:** Correct application of physics principles, creativity, systematic approach, ability to explain design choices.

### Conceptual Essays
Written explanations of physical phenomena that demonstrate deep understanding:
- "Explain why the sky is blue, using what you know about waves and light"
- "Describe the energy transformations that happen when you ride a bicycle uphill and then coast back down"
- "Why can astronauts float inside the International Space Station?"

**Assessment criteria:** Accuracy, depth, clarity, connection between concepts, and ability to address common misconceptions.

### Oral Defense
The learner presents a topic they have mastered and answers questions from peers and mentors. This assesses:
- Depth of understanding (can they go beyond surface explanations?)
- Flexibility of thinking (can they approach the topic from different angles?)
- Communication skills (can they explain clearly to different audiences?)
- Confidence with the material (can they handle unexpected questions?)

## Portfolio Suggestions

A physics learning portfolio might include:

1. **Experiment log:** Record of all experiments conducted, including failed ones
2. **Prediction journal:** Collection of predictions, outcomes, and reflections
3. **Concept maps:** Visual representations of how physics concepts connect
4. **Problem solutions:** Worked examples showing reasoning, not just answers
5. **Design documentation:** Plans, prototypes, and evaluations for design challenges
6. **Reflection essays:** Periodic reflections on what has been learned and what remains challenging
7. **Teaching artifacts:** Notes or recordings from peer explanation sessions
8. **Real-world connections:** Photographs or descriptions of physics observed in everyday life
9. **Question collection:** A running list of physics questions the learner wants to investigate
10. **Growth evidence:** Side-by-side comparisons of early and late work showing improvement
