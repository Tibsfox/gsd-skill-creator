# TRADE-101 Assessment Framework

## Assessment Philosophy

Assessment in TRADE-101 is **competency-based**: Can the student perform the task safely and correctly? We evaluate **what students can do**, not what they can recite.

All assessment is **safety-first**. A student cannot earn advanced ratings while cutting corners on safety.

---

## Rubric Levels

### Beginning

The student is learning foundational concepts and basic techniques.

**Tool Mastery & Safety:**
- Identifies most common tools by sight, but may confuse similar tools
- Uses tools with reminders about safety procedures
- Needs supervision during tool use
- Follows written safety instructions when directed
- May forget safety steps and needs correction

**Precision & Measurement:**
- Measures with guidance (ruler, tape measure)
- Accuracy within 1/2 inch on ruler measurements
- Understands concept of measurement but execution is inconsistent
- Makes errors reading measurements
- Doesn't check work before cutting/assembly

**Project Execution:**
- Follows simple 2-3 step instructions with adult support
- Starts project but may not complete without encouragement
- Produces projects with visible flaws (rough cuts, gaps, poor finishing)
- Gets frustrated when something doesn't fit perfectly

**Understanding & Reasoning:**
- Knows WHAT to do (follows procedure)
- Doesn't explain WHY steps matter
- Treats safety rules as arbitrary restrictions
- Doesn't understand why material properties matter
- Focuses on finishing speed rather than quality

---

### Developing

The student is building competency and working with increasing independence.

**Tool Mastery & Safety:**
- Identifies tools correctly and matches to common uses
- Follows safety protocols independently for familiar situations
- Uses hand tools confidently; power tools with supervision
- Explains basic safety reasons (avoid this to prevent injury)
- Occasionally forgets a step but corrects when reminded

**Precision & Measurement:**
- Measures independently to 1/4 inch accuracy
- Uses rulers, tape measures, and squares correctly
- Checks measurements before cutting
- Understands need for accuracy but sometimes rushes
- Makes consistent effort to work to dimension

**Project Execution:**
- Follows multi-step plans with occasional clarification
- Completes projects without constant encouragement
- Produces workable projects with acceptable appearance
- Executes steps in order and checks progress
- Makes minor mistakes that don't affect final function

**Understanding & Reasoning:**
- Understands WHY most steps matter
- Follows safety rules because they make sense, not from fear
- Asks questions about material choice and method
- Grasps that precision prevents rework
- Takes pride in quality but balances speed

---

### Proficient

The student demonstrates solid competency and works with substantial independence.

**Tool Mastery & Safety:**
- Identifies all tools confidently and explains proper use
- Selects appropriate tools for specific tasks without direction
- Uses power tools safely under minimal supervision
- Anticipates hazards before they arise
- Mentors others on safe tool use
- Understands root causes of safety rules

**Precision & Measurement:**
- Measures consistently to 1/16 inch accuracy
- Understands tolerances and why precision matters
- Checks work before and after cutting/assembly
- Reads complex measuring instruments (calipers, levels, squares)
- Adjusts approach if measurement shows error

**Project Execution:**
- Plans before cutting; thinks through sequence
- Troubleshoots when something doesn't fit
- Recovers from minor mistakes without major rework
- Completes projects with professional appearance and durability
- Works independently on multi-step projects
- Explains decisions about materials and methods

**Understanding & Reasoning:**
- Understands systems and how components interconnect
- Explains WHY joints need to be tight, why grain matters, how pressure works
- Recognizes when something doesn't meet standards and fixes it
- Applies knowledge to new situations
- Asks clarifying questions when a procedure is unclear
- Balances speed and quality appropriately

---

### Advanced

The student demonstrates mastery, independence, and teaching capability.

**Tool Mastery & Safety:**
- Optimizes tool selection for efficiency and accuracy
- Demonstrates advanced techniques (stropping chisels, maintaining saws, setting up equipment)
- Troubleshoots tool problems and makes adjustments
- Mentors peers and emphasizes safety without being pedantic
- Understands tool design principles and limitations
- Maintains tools in workshop condition

**Precision & Measurement:**
- Measures to design tolerances specified in drawings
- Understands cumulative error and plans to minimize it
- Reads and creates precise technical drawings
- Uses advanced measuring instruments (micrometer, depth gauge, spirit level)
- Explains measurement uncertainty and works within it
- Verifies that completed work meets design intent

**Project Execution:**
- Designs custom projects with clear intent
- Reads complex technical drawings and interprets them correctly
- Makes professional-quality work suitable for real use or display
- Troubleshoots complex problems systematically
- Manages multiple projects simultaneously
- Delivers on timeline and budget

**Understanding & Reasoning:**
- Explains engineering tradeoffs: cost vs. quality, strength vs. weight, speed vs. durability
- Designs solutions to novel problems
- Reads building codes and safety standards
- Understands why standards exist and when they apply
- Mentors others in design thinking and problem-solving
- Contributes to continuous improvement of workshop practices

---

## Formative Assessment

Ongoing observation and feedback during learning.

**Observable Skills:**

1. **Safety Culture & Awareness**
   - Notices hazards before being told
   - Corrects own unsafe practices proactively
   - Reminds peers of safety protocols
   - Understands consequences of violations

2. **Measurement & Precision**
   - Checks measurement before executing
   - Catches errors and adjusts
   - Achieves required accuracy consistently
   - Understands why precision matters for this task

3. **Project Planning**
   - Plans sequence before starting
   - Organizes materials before beginning
   - Reads instructions/drawings carefully
   - Adjusts plan if something doesn't work as expected

4. **Problem-Solving**
   - Systematically diagnoses why something failed
   - Tests hypotheses rather than randomly trying fixes
   - Learns from mistakes and adjusts approach
   - Asks smart questions when stuck

5. **Craftsmanship**
   - Takes pride in appearance and function
   - Finishes details; doesn't just "get it done"
   - Presents completed work with confidence
   - Maintains workspace organization

6. **Independence & Responsibility**
   - Works without constant supervision
   - Takes initiative on tasks
   - Cleans and stores tools properly
   - Seeks help appropriately (not immediately, not never)

---

## Summative Assessment

Formal evaluation at natural stopping points (project completion, end of module).

**Project Evaluation Rubric:**

| Criterion | Beginning | Developing | Proficient | Advanced |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Safety** | Follows rules with reminders | Follows independently | Anticipates hazards | Maintains safety culture for others |
| **Precision** | Within 1/2" | Within 1/4" | Within 1/16" | Design-specified tolerances |
| **Functionality** | Partially works | Works with minor issues | Works as intended | Exceeds specifications |
| **Appearance** | Rough, visible flaws | Acceptable, minor flaws | Professional, clean | Showcase quality |
| **Independence** | Needs constant direction | Occasional guidance needed | Works independently | Mentors others |
| **Problem-Solving** | Requests help quickly | Tries fixes, asks for help | Diagnoses and fixes independently | Teaches troubleshooting |
| **Understanding** | Follows procedure | Explains basic reasoning | Explains design decisions | Designs solutions |

## Portfolio Suggestions

Students should maintain documentation of their learning:

1. **Photos of Completed Projects**
   - Before and after shots
   - Close-ups of joints and details
   - Progress photos through building stages

2. **Measurement Logs**
   - Record actual vs. designed dimensions
   - Show understanding of tolerances
   - Demonstrate consistency improving over time

3. **Tool Proficiency Record**
   - Which tools can you use safely?
   - Checkboxes for: identified, used with supervision, used independently, taught to others

4. **Safety Incident Log**
   - Record near-misses (learning opportunities)
   - Document what you noticed, what you did, what you learned
   - Shows developing safety awareness

5. **Design Sketches**
   - Early sketches of projects
   - Revision notes as you refine ideas
   - Final drawings and material lists

6. **Reflection Prompts**
   - What was hardest about this project?
   - What would you do differently next time?
   - How do you know your work is good?
   - What did you learn about this material/tool?

## Module-Specific Assessment Focus

### Module 1: Tools, Safety & Workshop Practice

**Key Assessment Questions:**
- Can student identify tools by sight and explain use?
- Does student follow safety protocols consistently?
- Can student explain WHY safety rules exist (not just THAT they exist)?
- Does student maintain workshop organization?

**Success Evidence:**
- 100% correct tool identification
- Zero safety violations over time
- Student proactively corrects unsafe practices
- Student can explain consequences of violations

---

### Module 2: Woodworking & Fabrication

**Key Assessment Questions:**
- Can student measure to required precision?
- Does student produce straight cuts and clean joints?
- Can student complete a multi-step project independently?
- Does student understand why grain direction matters?

**Success Evidence:**
- Measurements within 1/16 inch on high school work
- Joints fit without gaps
- Projects function as designed and look professional
- Student explains grain direction effects and adapts approach

---

### Module 3: Electrical Fundamentals

**Key Assessment Questions:**
- Can student explain voltage, current, resistance in own words?
- Does student design circuits that work correctly?
- Can student use a multimeter safely and accurately?
- Can student troubleshoot a broken circuit?

**Success Evidence:**
- Circuits light up on first try
- Multimeter readings are accurate
- Student can diagnose why a circuit failed
- Student understands grounding and shock hazards

---

### Module 4: Plumbing & Mechanical Systems

**Key Assessment Questions:**
- Can student assemble PVC systems without leaks?
- Does student understand pressure and flow?
- Can student explain why drainage slope matters?
- Can student troubleshoot a leak?

**Success Evidence:**
- Pressure test shows zero leaks
- Student explains pressure/height relationship
- Water drains properly in test system
- Student correctly diagnoses and fixes common problems

---

### Module 5: Welding, Metalwork & Construction

**Key Assessment Questions:**
- Does student produce clean, strong welds/joints?
- Can student read technical drawings?
- Does student understand material properties?
- Can student explain structural safety concepts?

**Success Evidence:**
- Welds are clean, with good fusion and no major defects
- Student reads drawings accurately and produces parts to spec
- Student selects materials appropriately for application
- Student explains load paths and failure modes

## Special Considerations

### Safety Overrides Everything

A student working unsafely cannot pass, regardless of technical skill. Safety is non-negotiable.

### Age-Appropriate Assessment

Assessment standards adjust for age:
- **K-2:** Tool identification and basic safety; no power tools
- **3-5:** Hand tool competency; measurement to 1/4 inch; simple projects
- **6-8:** Multiple tools; measurement to 1/8-1/4 inch; multi-step projects
- **9-12:** Advanced techniques; precision to 1/16 inch; independent design

### Celebrating Success

Recognize achievements:
- Display completed projects prominently
- Share photos/videos of successful work
- Celebrate safety milestones ("100 days without an incident")
- Recommend advanced students as peer teachers

### Addressing Struggles

If a student is falling behind:
1. Assess which specific skill is limiting progress (safety? measurement? reading directions?)
2. Provide targeted practice on that skill
3. Pair with a peer mentor for guided practice
4. Consider alternative entry point (different activity, tool, or module)
5. Never compromise safety to move forward

## Assessment Tools

- **Observation Checklists** — Daily habits and safety awareness
- **Project Rubrics** — Multi-point scales for completed work
- **Practical Tests** — Can you use this tool? Measure correctly?
- **Portfolio Review** — Growth over time
- **Peer Assessment** — "Is this work safe? Good quality?"
- **Self-Reflection** — Student evaluation of own work quality

---

## Grading (if required)

In project-based learning, traditional letter grades are less meaningful. Consider:

- **Competency-Based Grades:** Novice, Developing, Proficient, Advanced (per module or skill)
- **Project Grades:** Pass/Revise (revise until professional quality)
- **Narrative Feedback:** Specific comments on what the student did well and what to work on next
- **Skills Checklist:** "Student can [specific skill]" with date of proficiency

---

## Parent/Guardian Communication

Communicate progress using examples, photos, and clear language:

> "Maria is now measuring consistently within 1/4 inch and completed her birdhouse with professional-quality finishing. She's ready to move into carpentry projects with more complex joinery. Next she'll learn about wood grain direction and how it affects cutting."

Emphasize safety, growth, and next steps — not grades or comparisons.

---

## Conclusion

TRADE-101 assessment emphasizes **what students can do safely and correctly**. It values precision, independence, and understanding over speed. It celebrates craftsmanship and problem-solving. Most importantly, it ensures that safety is never compromised.

The goal is not to produce master electricians by age 12. The goal is to produce students who understand trades, respect safety, can work with real tools and materials, and are prepared for apprenticeship when they're ready.
