# THEO-101: Theology & Religion Studies -- Assessment Framework

## Assessment Philosophy

Assessment in theology and religion studies evaluates **understanding, analysis, and respectful engagement** -- never belief, devotion, or personal faith. There are multiple valid perspectives on most questions. Quality is measured by accuracy, depth, comparative skill, and the ability to engage with religious diversity respectfully and substantively.

### Core Principles

1. **Assess understanding, not belief.** We never evaluate whether learners believe the "right" things. We assess whether they accurately understand what traditions teach.
2. **Multiple valid perspectives.** In comparative work, the best answers demonstrate nuance, not a single "correct" view.
3. **Comparative skill is the highest-order competency.** Moving from describing one tradition to comparing across traditions represents genuine growth.
4. **Respectful engagement is non-negotiable.** Dismissing, trivializing, or proselytizing in assessment tasks is a criteria failure regardless of factual accuracy.
5. **Primary sources matter.** Assessments at higher levels should reference actual texts, not just secondhand descriptions.

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are just starting to encounter the world's religious diversity. They may have familiarity with one tradition (their own or their family's) but limited awareness of others.

**Knowledge & Understanding:**
- Can name a few religious traditions (e.g., Christianity, Islam, Buddhism)
- Recalls basic facts (e.g., "Muslims fast during Ramadan") but may confuse or conflate traditions
- May rely on stereotypes or oversimplifications about unfamiliar traditions
- Understands that people have different religions but may not understand why

**Comparison & Analysis:**
- Identifies obvious similarities ("they both have a holy book") but struggles with differences
- Treats all religions as essentially the same or as completely unrelated
- Limited ability to explain why traditions differ on a given topic

**Textual Engagement:**
- Can listen to and retell simple sacred stories
- May not distinguish between sacred text and ordinary storytelling
- Limited vocabulary for discussing religious concepts

**Respectful Engagement:**
- May express curiosity but also confusion or discomfort with unfamiliar practices
- May make judgments about traditions based on limited information
- Beginning to understand that respect does not require agreement

**Typical Evidence:** Participation in story circles, basic identification tasks, drawing activities, simple "same and different" comparisons.

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building a foundation of knowledge about multiple traditions and beginning to compare them meaningfully.

**Knowledge & Understanding:**
- Can describe 4-5 major world religions with reasonable accuracy
- Understands core beliefs, key figures, and major celebrations for several traditions
- Beginning to recognize internal diversity within traditions ("not all Christians believe the same thing")
- Distinguishes between religion, culture, and ethnicity

**Comparison & Analysis:**
- Can compare celebrations, stories, or practices across 2-3 traditions
- Identifies both similarities and differences but may still oversimplify
- Beginning to understand that comparison is not ranking
- Can place some traditions in historical context

**Textual Engagement:**
- Can read age-appropriate excerpts from sacred texts with guidance
- Identifies genre (story, law, poetry, parable) with some support
- Beginning to understand that texts are interpreted differently by different communities

**Respectful Engagement:**
- Shows genuine curiosity about unfamiliar traditions
- Asks questions respectfully and without judgment
- Can discuss religious topics without dismissing or advocating
- Beginning to bracket personal views when studying academically

**Typical Evidence:** Tradition comparison charts, sacred text reading responses, religion passport completion, journal entries, participation in guided discussions.

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level demonstrate solid religious literacy -- accurate knowledge, meaningful comparison, and respectful engagement with complexity.

**Knowledge & Understanding:**
- Can describe the world's major religions accurately, including internal diversity
- Understands historical development, theological concepts, and contemporary expressions
- Recognizes the difference between insider (emic) and outsider (etic) perspectives
- Understands how religion intersects with culture, politics, art, and ethics

**Comparison & Analysis:**
- Compares theological concepts, practices, and ethical positions across 3+ traditions with nuance
- Identifies both common ground and genuine, irreducible differences
- Analyzes how religious ideas have shaped historical events and social structures
- Can evaluate media portrayals of religion for accuracy and fairness

**Textual Engagement:**
- Reads passages from sacred texts with attention to genre, context, and interpretation
- Understands hermeneutics -- that texts are always interpreted, never simply "read"
- Compares passages from different traditions on the same theme with insight
- Recognizes translation and compilation as interpretive acts

**Respectful Engagement:**
- Engages with challenging topics (religious violence, gender, science-religion) with nuance
- Represents traditions other than their own accurately and empathetically
- Participates in structured interfaith dialogue productively
- Distinguishes between scholarly description and personal advocacy

**Typical Evidence:** Comparative essays, sacred text analysis papers, interfaith dialogue participation, research projects, media literacy analyses, portfolio of comparative work.

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level demonstrate sophisticated religious studies competency -- original analysis, theological reasoning, and nuanced engagement with the field's most challenging questions.

**Knowledge & Understanding:**
- Deep knowledge of multiple traditions, including minority and indigenous religions
- Understands theological arguments within and between traditions
- Can explain scholarly approaches to religion (historical-critical, phenomenological, sociological, feminist, postcolonial)
- Recognizes "religion" itself as a constructed category with political implications

**Comparison & Analysis:**
- Conducts original comparative analysis of theological, ethical, or historical questions
- Engages with the exclusivism/inclusivism/pluralism debate with sophistication
- Analyzes primary sources in their historical and literary context
- Evaluates competing scholarly interpretations of religious phenomena
- Understands the relationship between religion and modernity, secularization, and revival

**Textual Engagement:**
- Applies multiple scholarly methods to sacred text interpretation
- Understands the history of canon formation, textual criticism, and translation theory
- Engages with commentary traditions within and across religions
- Reads secondary scholarly literature critically

**Respectful Engagement:**
- Facilitates interfaith dialogue, not just participates
- Engages with intellectual humility about their own assumptions and biases
- Addresses controversial topics with both honesty and care
- Models the scholarly disposition: curious, rigorous, respectful, self-aware

**Typical Evidence:** Original research papers, seminar presentations, fieldwork reports, capstone projects, facilitated dialogue sessions, advanced textual analysis, critical book reviews.

## Formative Assessment

Ongoing assessment strategies for tracking growth throughout the learning process:

### Discussion Participation
- **What to observe:** Accuracy of religious knowledge, quality of comparisons, respectful engagement, willingness to ask questions, ability to represent unfamiliar traditions fairly.
- **Frequency:** Every session involving group discussion.
- **Documentation:** Discussion rubric or observation notes.

### Sacred Text Response Journals
- **What to observe:** Comprehension, contextual awareness, comparison to other texts, personal reflection that distinguishes scholarly understanding from personal belief.
- **Frequency:** After each text reading session.
- **Documentation:** Journal entries with teacher feedback.

### Quick Knowledge Checks
- **What to observe:** Can students accurately identify traditions, symbols, celebrations, and key concepts?
- **Frequency:** At the start or end of each module section.
- **Documentation:** Low-stakes quizzes or matching activities.

### Comparison Exercises
- **What to observe:** Quality of comparison (not just "same" and "different" but why), attention to nuance, avoidance of oversimplification.
- **Frequency:** After each comparative activity.
- **Documentation:** Comparison charts with written explanations.

## Summative Assessment

### Tradition Representation Portfolio
Build a portfolio demonstrating knowledge of multiple religious traditions. Include:
- Accurate descriptions of at least 4 traditions
- Comparative analysis on at least 2 themes
- Sacred text readings with contextual analysis
- Personal reflection on what religious literacy means

### Interfaith Dialogue Project
Participate in or design an interfaith dialogue session. Demonstrate:
- Accurate representation of an assigned tradition
- Respectful listening and questioning
- Identification of common ground and genuine differences
- Reflection on what was learned from the dialogue

### Research Investigation
Investigate a question at the intersection of religion and society:
- Choose a topic (religion and art, religion and law, religion and conflict, religion and science)
- Research perspectives from multiple traditions
- Present findings with scholarly sources
- Analyze how religious literacy changes understanding of the topic

### Comparative Theology Essay
Write an essay comparing how 3+ traditions approach a theological or ethical question:
- Accurate representation of each tradition's position
- Analysis of similarities and genuine differences
- Engagement with primary sources where possible
- Demonstration of intellectual humility and scholarly distance

## Portfolio Suggestions

### Foundation Level (K-2)
- Drawings of sacred stories from different traditions
- "My Religion Passport" with stamps from each tradition visited
- Photos or drawings of sacred spaces
- Simple "same and different" comparisons

### Elementary Level (3-5)
- Comparison charts for celebrations, symbols, and practices
- Sacred story retellings in own words
- Golden Rule collection from multiple traditions
- Journal entries from World Religions Passport activities

### Middle School Level (6-8)
- Sacred text reading responses with contextual analysis
- Religion-in-the-news analysis pieces
- Comparative timeline of religious history
- Media literacy analysis: how is religion portrayed?

### High School Level (9-12)
- Interfaith dialogue participation records and reflections
- Comparative theology essays
- Research projects on religion and society
- Ethics case studies with multiple religious perspectives
- Position papers representing assigned traditions

### College+ Level
- Original research papers
- Fieldwork reports from religious community visits
- Advanced textual analysis with scholarly apparatus
- Capstone project: religion and a global challenge
- Critical reviews of scholarly works in religious studies

## Assessment Accommodations

- **English Language Learners:** Provide key religious vocabulary in learner's first language; allow responses in first language where appropriate; use visual aids and multilingual sacred text excerpts.
- **Learners with Religious Backgrounds:** Validate their knowledge while challenging them to understand other traditions with equal depth; distinguish between devotional knowledge and academic analysis.
- **Learners from Secular Backgrounds:** Validate their perspective while building understanding of why religion matters to practitioners; include non-religious worldviews in all comparative work.
- **Learners with Sensitivities:** Some topics (death, suffering, religious violence) may be triggering. Provide content warnings, alternative activities, and private reflection options.
- **Diverse Abilities:** Offer multiple modes of expression (written, oral, visual, dramatic) for demonstrating understanding. Sacred stories can be drawn, acted, or narrated, not just written about.

---

*Assessment in THEO-101 measures the growth of religious literacy: the ability to understand, compare, and engage respectfully with the world's religious traditions. Understanding, not belief. Respect, not agreement. Knowledge, not advocacy.*
