## Executive Summary

**DIGLIT-101: Digital Literacy & Information Science** is a foundational knowledge pack designed to build competence in navigating, evaluating, and creating information in digital environments. Unlike traditional "computer class" instruction focused on software applications, this pack emphasizes the critical thinking and systems understanding necessary to be an informed digital citizen in the 21st century.

**Key Focus Areas:**
- Digital systems foundations: understanding hardware, software, networks, and how the internet works
- Information literacy: finding, evaluating, and fact-checking sources in digital spaces
- Digital communication: responsible, effective use of email, messaging, and collaborative tools
- Online safety & citizenship: privacy, security, cyberbullying, digital footprint management
- Data and algorithms: how data is collected, processed, and used; algorithmic bias; AI awareness

**Who Should Use This Pack:** Learners of all ages from kindergarten through college who need to understand digital technology not as a tool to learn other subjects, but as a domain of knowledge and practice in itself. Parents, educators, and mentors who want to go beyond "screen time rules" to develop digital competence and judgment.

**Learning Outcomes:** After engaging with this pack, learners will:
- Understand fundamental concepts of how digital systems work
- Evaluate the credibility and bias in online information
- Use digital tools safely and responsibly
- Recognize and respond to online risks
- Understand how algorithms and data shape their digital experiences
- Make informed decisions about their digital footprint and privacy

## Vision & Philosophy

### What Is Digital Literacy?

Digital literacy is not about learning the latest software. It's about developing the conceptual understanding and critical thinking necessary to make informed decisions in a digital world.

A digitally literate person:
- **Understands systems:** Not just "how to use" technology, but why it works the way it does
- **Evaluates information critically:** Recognizes bias, identifies reliable sources, fact-checks claims
- **Communicates effectively:** Uses digital tools appropriately for different contexts and audiences
- **Protects themselves and others:** Makes conscious decisions about privacy, security, and online behavior
- **Understands implications:** Recognizes how algorithms, data, and AI shape their experiences and the world

### The Problem with Current Approaches

Most digital literacy instruction falls into two ineffective extremes:

**Extreme 1: App Training**
Students are taught to click buttons in Word, Google Slides, or whatever tool is currently popular. They learn software, not concepts. When the software updates or a new tool emerges, their knowledge becomes obsolete. They can type a document but don't understand how networks work or how to evaluate a source.

**Extreme 2: Screen Time Restriction**
Parents and schools restrict "screen time" without teaching critical engagement. Students understand that devices are bad, not how to use them well. When they gain independence, they often struggle with online risks and poor information evaluation because they were never taught to engage critically.

### Our Approach: Conceptual Understanding + Critical Thinking

DIGLIT-101 teaches the underlying concepts and develops judgment. Students learn:
- How digital systems actually work (starting with observable mechanics, progressing to underlying principles)
- How to think critically about information and sources
- How to use tools responsibly and effectively
- How to understand their own digital footprint and make conscious choices about privacy
- How to recognize and respond to online manipulation and risks

This approach is timeless. The specific tools change, but critical thinking about information and digital systems remains relevant throughout life.

## Problem Statement

### Why Digital Literacy Matters

We live in a world where:
- **Information is abundant but unreliable.** Anyone can publish anything online. Students must distinguish between credible sources and misinformation.
- **Technology is ubiquitous.** Digital devices and networks are part of every aspect of modern life — education, work, health, social connection, entertainment.
- **Data is currency.** Personal information is collected, sold, and used to influence behavior. Understanding data privacy is a critical life skill.
- **Algorithms shape reality.** Search results, social media feeds, and recommendations are shaped by algorithmic decisions. Understanding algorithmic bias is necessary to make informed decisions.
- **Online risks are real.** Cyberbullying, scams, identity theft, and other online harms affect people of all ages. Awareness and skills are necessary for safety.
- **Digital citizenship is moral.** How we behave online affects others. Understanding digital ethics and respect is part of growing up in a connected world.

### The Gap in Current Education

Despite the importance of digital literacy, most schools treat it as:
- A technical elective (if offered at all)
- A unit within other classes (e.g., "Using Google Docs in English class")
- A response to crisis (e.g., "Cyberbullying awareness week")
- An app-focused activity (e.g., "Learn to code in Python")

What's often missing:
- **Integrated curriculum:** Digital literacy is treated as separate from "real" subjects rather than foundational to understanding modern information and systems
- **Critical thinking focus:** The emphasis is on "safe" behavior rather than developing judgment
- **Age-appropriate progression:** Students either get too little (passive consumption rules) or too much (blame for not understanding complex systems)
- **Understanding systems:** Students learn what to do but not why, so they struggle to adapt to new situations

### The Opportunity

By teaching digital literacy as a foundational knowledge domain — with clear conceptual progressions, authentic assessments, and integrated real-world examples — we enable learners to:
- Develop judgment, not just follow rules
- Transfer learning to new tools and situations
- Become informed digital citizens who understand their own agency and responsibility
- Recognize and resist manipulation
- Make conscious choices about technology in their lives

## Core Competencies

DIGLIT-101 is organized around five interconnected core competencies:

### 1. Digital Foundations
**Understanding How Digital Systems Work**

The foundation. Without understanding the basics of hardware, software, networks, and how the internet operates, all other digital literacy is superficial.

**Conceptual Core:**
- Devices and operating systems: what hardware is, what software does, how they work together
- Networks and the internet: how devices connect, how data travels, domain names and IP addresses
- Information representation: how computers store and manipulate information (binary, digital files)
- Troubleshooting basics: how to identify and solve common technology problems

**Skill Progression:**
- Foundation (K-2): Devices have different purposes. Technology sometimes breaks and can be fixed. Instructions matter.
- Elementary (3-5): Hardware and software are different. Networks connect computers. Information can be sent electronically.
- Middle (6-8): Operating systems manage hardware. Internet uses protocols. Devices store information digitally.
- High School (9-12): Network architecture and routing. Digital information representation. System troubleshooting and security implications.
- College+: Networking principles. Security in complex systems. Emerging technologies and their implications.

### 2. Information Literacy & Research
**Evaluating, Finding, and Using Information Effectively**

In a world of information overload, the ability to find reliable information and evaluate credibility is crucial.

**Conceptual Core:**
- Search strategies: how search engines work, how to search effectively, understanding sponsored results
- Source evaluation: evaluating author credibility, checking bias, identifying reliable sources
- Fact-checking and verification: recognizing common misinformation patterns, fact-checking techniques
- Synthesizing information: understanding different perspectives, avoiding echo chambers
- Media literacy: understanding how images, videos, and other media can mislead or manipulate

**Skill Progression:**
- Foundation (K-2): Different sources have different information. Some sources are reliable. It's good to ask grown-ups.
- Elementary (3-5): Websites have authors. Not all information on the internet is true. Better questions get better answers.
- Middle (6-8): Evaluating author expertise and bias. Understanding how search algorithms work. Fact-checking techniques.
- High School (9-12): Recognizing misinformation tactics. Understanding algorithmic amplification. Synthesizing information from multiple sources.
- College+: Advanced research skills. Understanding information ecosystems. Contributing to reliable knowledge.

### 3. Digital Communication & Creation
**Communicating Effectively and Responsibly in Digital Spaces**

Digital communication is pervasive and requires different skills than face-to-face or written communication.

**Conceptual Core:**
- Email and professional communication: composing clear emails, understanding etiquette, managing inboxes
- Collaborative tools: using shared documents, managing version control, asynchronous collaboration
- Digital media creation: creating content responsibly, understanding different media formats
- Online etiquette and presence: understanding norms for different contexts, being respectful online
- Copyright and attribution: understanding intellectual property, proper citation, giving credit

**Skill Progression:**
- Foundation (K-2): Sending messages. Asking for help online. Being polite on screens.
- Elementary (3-5): Composing clear emails. Sharing documents with others. Understanding digital etiquette.
- Middle (6-8): Professional email. Collaborative document management. Creating and sharing digital media.
- High School (9-12): Advanced communication across platforms. Understanding digital norms. Copyright and attribution.
- College+: Advanced collaboration. Content creation for different audiences. Understanding digital identity.

### 4. Online Safety & Digital Citizenship
**Protecting Yourself and Making Responsible Choices Online**

Safety and responsibility are not about restricting access but about developing judgment.

**Conceptual Core:**
- Password security and authentication: strong passwords, two-factor authentication, recognizing phishing
- Privacy protection: understanding privacy settings, data collection, making conscious choices
- Cyberbullying and online harassment: recognizing harmful behavior, supporting targets, understanding impact
- Digital footprint: understanding what information is public, the permanence of online actions
- Online scams and malware: recognizing common schemes, protecting devices and accounts
- Digital citizenship and ethics: understanding rights and responsibilities, recognizing online manipulation

**Skill Progression:**
- Foundation (K-2): Passwords are private. The internet has rules. Being kind online matters.
- Elementary (3-5): Creating strong passwords. Understanding privacy. Recognizing unkind behavior.
- Middle (6-8): Two-factor authentication. Privacy settings. Cyberbullying awareness and response.
- High School (9-12): Advanced security. Digital footprint management. Online ethics and rights.
- College+: Advanced privacy concepts. Understanding manipulation techniques. Contributing to healthy online communities.

### 5. Information Systems & Data
**Understanding How Data and Algorithms Shape Digital Experiences**

In the age of AI, understanding data and algorithms is as important as understanding electricity in the industrial age.

**Conceptual Core:**
- Data collection and storage: what data is collected, where it's stored, who has access
- Data processing and analysis: how data is used to understand patterns, make predictions, personalize experiences
- Algorithmic systems: how algorithms make decisions, algorithmic bias, explainability
- AI and machine learning: what AI is, what it can and cannot do, limitations and risks
- Data-driven decision making: understanding data-driven claims, recognizing correlation vs. causation

**Skill Progression:**
- Foundation (K-2): Computers remember information. Patterns help us predict. Machines follow rules.
- Elementary (3-5): Collecting data helps us understand. Computers look for patterns. Algorithms are instructions.
- Middle (6-8): Data representation and analysis. How recommendations work. Algorithmic bias concepts.
- High School (9-12): Statistical thinking. Understanding algorithmic decision-making. AI capabilities and limitations.
- College+: Advanced data science concepts. Understanding AI systems. Ethical implications of data use.

## Module Overview

DIGLIT-101 is organized into five modules, each focusing on one core competency. Modules are designed to be taught in sequence for a linear pathway, or mixed/revisited in a spiral pathway as learners deepen their understanding.

### Module 1: Digital Foundations

**Focus:** How digital systems work — hardware, software, networks, and information representation.

**Essential Questions:**
- How do computers store and process information?
- What is software and how does it differ from hardware?
- How are computers connected? How does information travel across the internet?
- What happens when technology doesn't work, and how do we fix it?

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Understand basic hardware components and their functions
- Explain the difference between hardware and software
- Describe how networks connect devices and transmit information
- Troubleshoot common technology problems
- Understand the basics of digital representation (files, directories, naming conventions)

**Module Activities:** (2-3 per age band)
- Hardware scavenger hunt: identifying components inside devices
- Internet traceroute: physically tracing how data travels
- Network mapping: drawing how devices in a home/school connect
- Troubleshooting scenarios: diagnosing and solving simulated technology problems
- Operating system exploration: understanding the role of OS in device management

**Duration:** 10-30 hours depending on grade level and depth

### Module 2: Information Literacy & Research

**Focus:** Finding reliable information and evaluating credibility in digital spaces.

**Essential Questions:**
- How do search engines work? Why do they show certain results?
- How do I know if information is reliable?
- What does it mean for a source to be biased?
- How do I spot misinformation and misleading media?

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Use search engines effectively with strategic keywords and filters
- Evaluate source credibility using multiple criteria
- Apply fact-checking techniques to verify claims
- Recognize common misinformation patterns and tactics
- Synthesize information from multiple sources to form balanced understanding

**Module Activities:** (2-3 per age band)
- Search engine experiments: comparing results, understanding algorithms
- Source evaluation challenges: determining credibility of various sources
- Fact-checking investigations: verifying viral claims
- Media analysis: deconstructing misleading images and headlines
- Research projects: finding reliable information on authentic topics

**Duration:** 15-40 hours depending on grade level and complexity

### Module 3: Digital Communication & Creation

**Focus:** Communicating responsibly and effectively using digital tools.

**Essential Questions:**
- How does communication differ across digital platforms?
- What makes digital communication professional vs. casual?
- How do I create and share content responsibly?
- What does it mean to respect intellectual property?

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Compose clear, professional emails appropriate to context
- Collaborate effectively using shared documents and tools
- Create digital content (documents, presentations, media) with attention to audience
- Understand and apply copyright and attribution principles
- Recognize and adapt to norms of different digital communities

**Module Activities:** (2-3 per age band)
- Email writing workshops: composing professional messages
- Collaborative document projects: managing shared work
- Digital media creation: producing various content types
- Presentation creation: designing effective visual communication
- Copyright and citation practice: properly attributing sources

**Duration:** 15-40 hours depending on grade level

### Module 4: Online Safety & Digital Citizenship

**Focus:** Making responsible choices online and protecting personal security.

**Essential Questions:**
- How do I keep my accounts and devices secure?
- What is a digital footprint and how does it affect me?
- What constitutes cyberbullying and what can I do about it?
- What are my rights and responsibilities online?

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Create and maintain strong passwords and use multi-factor authentication
- Manage privacy settings and make conscious decisions about personal information
- Recognize and respond to cyberbullying and online harassment
- Understand the permanence and implications of digital actions
- Recognize online scams and protect against malware
- Understand digital rights and responsibilities

**Module Activities:** (2-3 per age band)
- Password strength testing: creating and analyzing password security
- Privacy audits: examining social media and app permissions
- Cyberbullying response scenarios: practicing appropriate reactions
- Digital footprint analysis: understanding what's searchable about you
- Scam identification exercises: recognizing common schemes
- Digital citizenship discussions: exploring ethics and rights online

**Duration:** 15-40 hours depending on grade level

### Module 5: Information Systems & Data

**Focus:** Understanding how data and algorithms shape digital experiences.

**Essential Questions:**
- What data is collected about me online? By whom?
- How do algorithms decide what I see?
- What is AI and what can it do?
- How can data and algorithms be biased? What are the implications?

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Describe what personal data is collected and how it's used
- Explain how algorithms make decisions and personalize content
- Understand the capabilities and limitations of AI
- Recognize algorithmic bias and its consequences
- Evaluate data-driven claims with statistical thinking
- Understand ethical implications of data use and AI systems

**Module Activities:** (2-3 per age band)
- Data collection tracking: identifying what data is gathered
- Algorithm analysis: understanding how recommendations work
- Algorithmic bias exercises: recognizing and discussing bias
- AI experiments: testing AI systems and their limitations
- Data-driven claim evaluation: understanding statistics and causation
- Ethics discussions: implications of surveillance and algorithmic decision-making

**Duration:** 15-40 hours depending on grade level

## Skill Tree Architecture

DIGLIT-101 uses a vertical scaffold model where learners revisit the same core concepts at increasing sophistication as they develop. Rather than "Computer Basics," "Intermediate Computing," "Advanced Computing," we organize by:

**Horizontal slice (age band):**
- Foundation (K-2): Awareness of devices, simple rules and patterns
- Elementary (3-5): Understanding basic systems, following procedures, noticing cause-and-effect
- Middle School (6-8): Understanding how systems work, making informed decisions
- High School (9-12): Critical analysis, understanding implications, making complex judgments
- College+ (13-16): Advanced concepts, research literacy, contributing to discourse

**Vertical slice (core competency):**
- Same essential question across all levels, but increasing complexity and nuance
- Spiral revisiting allows deeper understanding over time
- Transfer across competencies — skills learned in one module support others

**Example: Understanding Data Use**

| Age Band | Competency Focus | Essential Activity |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|
| K-2 | Awareness | "Computers remember things we tell them" — storing favorite games/settings |
| 3-5 | Pattern Recognition | Collecting and sorting data (favorite colors, weather patterns) |
| 6-8 | System Understanding | Understanding how websites remember preferences; exploring cookies and settings |
| 9-12 | Critical Analysis | Analyzing Facebook's data collection policies; comparing privacy practices |
| 13+ | Contribution | Researching data regulation; contributing to data ethics discussions |

This architecture allows a 5-year-old and a 15-year-old to work on the same module, with activities differentiated by sophistication rather than different topics.

## Learning Pathways

### Linear Pathway (Recommended for Sequential Learning)

**Module 1 → Module 2 → Module 3 → Module 4 → Module 5**

**Rationale:** Builds from foundational understanding of systems, to finding/evaluating information, to communicating responsibly, to staying safe, to understanding the bigger picture of data and algorithms. Each module builds on previous conceptual understanding.

**Best for:** Comprehensive programs, semester-long courses, organized curriculum integration

### Spiral Pathway (Recommended for Depth and Revisiting)

Cycle through all five modules multiple times at increasing depth:
- **First spiral:** Foundations — focus on awareness and basic concepts
- **Second spiral:** Intermediate — focus on application and problem-solving
- **Third spiral:** Advanced — focus on analysis and critical thinking

**Rationale:** Learners benefit from revisiting concepts multiple times with increasing sophistication. A second experience with "how search engines work" (Module 2) is much richer after understanding networks (Module 1) and algorithms (Module 5).

**Best for:** Ongoing programs, annual revisits, integrated whole-school approaches

### Focused Pathway (Recommended for Specific Needs)

Select 1-2 modules based on immediate needs:
- **Just added to social media?** Start with Module 4 (Online Safety) and Module 2 (Information Literacy)
- **Learning to do research?** Start with Module 2 (Information Literacy) and optionally Module 1 (Foundations)
- **Interested in AI/Data?** Start with Module 5, then Module 1 for foundations
- **Using digital tools for a project?** Start with Module 3 (Communication & Creation) and optionally Module 2

**Rationale:** Different learners have different entry points. A focused pathway allows addressing immediate concerns while providing access to depth.

**Best for:** Shorter programs, response to specific situations, afterschool enrichment

## Assessment Philosophy

### What We Assess

We assess **digital judgment**, not software proficiency. Questions like "Can you use Word?" are less important than "Can you evaluate information credibility?" or "Do you understand the privacy implications of sharing information online?"

### How We Assess

**Formative Assessment (Ongoing, during learning):**
- Observation: During activities, note questions asked, decisions made, understanding demonstrated
- Self-reflection: Journals prompting students to think about their own digital practices
- Peer discussion: Conversations about digital scenarios and ethical dilemmas
- Skill demonstrations: Can they follow security practices? Search effectively? Evaluate sources?

**Summative Assessment (End of unit/module):**
- Digital citizenship portfolio: Collection of reflections, scenarios, and decisions
- Investigation projects: Research real information on authentic topics using appropriate evaluation
- Decision-making cases: Written or discussion-based response to realistic scenarios
- Presentations: Teaching others about a digital literacy concept

### Assessment Rubrics

**Beginning:** Following rules and procedures with guidance
- Follows security rules when reminded
- Uses search with basic keywords
- Recognizes obvious misinformation when pointed out
- Participates in discussions with guidance

**Developing:** Applying understanding to new situations
- Maintains security practices independently
- Uses advanced search strategies
- Evaluates source credibility using learned criteria
- Discusses digital ethics thoughtfully

**Proficient:** Independent judgment and critical thinking
- Makes security decisions autonomously
- Synthesizes information from multiple sources
- Explains why sources are trustworthy or not
- Considers implications and teaches others

**Advanced:** Teaching, leadership, and contribution
- Teaches digital security practices to others
- Conducts research on information quality and manipulation
- Contributes to discussions about digital ethics
- Models responsible digital citizenship

## Accessibility & Inclusion

DIGLIT-101 is designed to be accessible to all learners regardless of:

**Disability:**
- Activities include alternatives (keyboard navigation, text descriptions, audio options)
- Concepts are explained multiple ways (visual, auditory, kinesthetic)
- Pacing allows time for processing

**Language Background:**
- Core concepts are explained clearly with minimal jargon
- Visual supports accompany text
- Opportunities for discussion and explanation in different languages

**Socioeconomic Status:**
- No requirement for expensive hardware or latest technology
- Activities use common household materials and free tools
- Acknowledges different access levels to technology at home

**Neurodiversity:**
- Activities can be done with or without technology
- Clear expectations and structure
- Multiple ways to demonstrate understanding

## Parent & Mentor Guidance

### Why This Matters for Families

Digital literacy isn't a school subject — it's a life skill. Parents who understand these concepts can:
- Support their child's learning at home
- Model good digital practices
- Have informed conversations about technology and safety
- Make conscious family decisions about device use

### Common Parent Questions

**Q: Isn't this just teaching tech that will be outdated?**

A: We're teaching underlying concepts and critical thinking, not specific tools. Understanding "how search engines work" remains relevant even when the interface changes. Understanding "how to evaluate source credibility" applies to any future information platform.

**Q: Should I let my child use social media?**

A: Rather than "yes" or "no," DIGLIT-101 emphasizes helping your child make conscious choices. What matters? Your child understands the platform, can manage privacy settings, recognizes risks like cyberbullying and oversharing, and you have ongoing conversations about it.

**Q: How do I balance technology with outdoor play and other activities?**

A: Digital literacy isn't about MORE screen time — it's about BETTER screen time. A child who can thoughtfully evaluate information and use tools responsibly is engaging with technology more healthily than one who mindlessly scrolls.

**Q: What if I don't understand technology myself?**

A: You don't need to be a tech expert. Your child's learning doesn't depend on you knowing how to code or fix computers. What matters is: model curiosity ("Let's figure this out together"), ask good questions, and have conversations about digital choices.

### Family Activities

- **Dinner table discussion:** "What's something interesting you found online today? How do you know it's true?"
- **Digital audit:** Together, check privacy settings on devices and accounts. Discuss what information is private.
- **Scam awareness:** Show your child a suspicious email or text. Ask "What's suspicious about this?" and discuss what a real version might look like.
- **Media analysis:** Watch an ad together. Discuss what it's trying to convince you to do and how it tries to do it.
- **Teach-back:** Ask your child to explain a digital concept to you. Their explanation reveals their understanding and builds their ability to explain clearly.

## Common Questions

### Is coding part of digital literacy?

Coding is a valuable skill but not required for digital literacy. DIGLIT-101 focuses on understanding digital systems and making informed decisions, not programming. Coding can be an extension for interested learners, but it's not core to the pack.

### How does this connect to other subjects?

Digital literacy is inherently cross-curricular:
- **In English:** Evaluating sources for research; understanding media analysis
- **In Science:** Understanding how data informs scientific claims; recognizing bias in scientific communication
- **In Social Studies:** Understanding how information shapes civic participation; recognizing propaganda
- **In Math:** Understanding statistics and data; recognizing misleading graphs

Every subject benefits from digital literacy skills.

### How much technology do we need for this pack?

Some activities require internet access and devices, but many don't. DIGLIT-101 includes unplugged alternatives where appropriate:
- Networking concepts can be explored without internet (physical routing exercises)
- Search and evaluation can be practiced with printed sources
- Digital citizenship concepts apply to all communication, not just online

This allows access for learners with limited device or internet access.

### How do we assess learning if we're focusing on judgment rather than knowledge?

Assessment focuses on demonstrated decision-making:
- **Can students apply knowledge to new situations?** (evaluating a source they haven't seen before)
- **Can students explain their reasoning?** (why they think something is reliable)
- **Do they recognize complexity and nuance?** (understanding that "not biased" and "definitely true" are often not applicable)
- **Can they teach others?** (explaining concepts to peers)

These are harder to grade on a multiple-choice test but more meaningful measures of learning.

### What if a family has serious privacy concerns?

DIGLIT-101 respects that families have different values about technology use. The pack:
- Teaches how systems work (factual information)
- Presents multiple perspectives on privacy, data use, and online risks
- Empowers families to make their own decisions
- Never mandates technology use

A family that chooses minimal technology can still engage with the concepts through discussion and critical thinking.

## Conclusion

Digital literacy is no longer optional. Every person — regardless of age or intended career path — needs to understand digital systems, evaluate information, communicate responsibly, stay safe online, and understand how data and algorithms shape their world.

DIGLIT-101 provides a comprehensive, developmentally appropriate framework for building these essential competencies. Rather than teaching tools that become obsolete, we teach concepts that endure. Rather than teaching "safe" compliance, we develop judgment and agency.

The goal is not to create digital experts, but informed, capable, responsible digital citizens.
