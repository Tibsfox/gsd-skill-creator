# PROB-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

This assessment framework evaluates problem-solving process and strategy, not correctness of answers. A learner who uses excellent strategy but reaches a wrong answer has demonstrated stronger problem-solving skills than one who gets the right answer by luck or memorized procedure. Progress is measured by the quality of thinking: Does the learner understand before solving? Do they select strategies deliberately? Do they monitor progress and adjust? Do they reflect and transfer?

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing awareness of problem solving as a process. They attempt problems through trial and error, can describe what is being asked with support, and are beginning to recognize that different approaches exist.

**Understanding Problems:**
- Attempts problems immediately without pausing to understand
- Can describe what the problem is asking with guided questioning
- Identifies some given information but may miss key details
- Recognizes when they do not understand, though may not know how to proceed

**Strategy Use:**
- Relies primarily on trial and error
- Uses one approach and stops if it does not work (or waits for help)
- Can follow a strategy when guided step by step
- Begins to recognize that drawing a picture or making a list can help

**Creative Thinking:**
- Generates one or two ideas when brainstorming
- Tends toward familiar solutions rather than novel ones
- Can identify that a problem could be seen differently with prompting
- Participates in creative activities with encouragement

**Collaboration:**
- Participates in group problem solving when directed
- Shares ideas when asked but may not volunteer
- Listens to others' ideas but may not build on them
- Understands that different people may have different approaches

**Metacognition:**
- Can report whether they found a problem easy or hard
- Describes what they did but not why they did it
- Recognizes when they are stuck but has limited strategies for getting unstuck
- With prompting, can identify one thing they might try differently

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building conscious awareness of problem-solving strategies. They pause to understand problems before solving, select from a small repertoire of strategies, and can describe their thinking process.

**Understanding Problems:**
- Pauses to read/hear the problem before starting to solve
- Restates problems in own words with reasonable accuracy
- Identifies most known and unknown information
- Asks some clarifying questions before proceeding

**Strategy Use:**
- Names and uses 2-3 problem-solving strategies (e.g., draw a diagram, guess and check)
- Selects a strategy before starting, though selection may not be optimal
- Persists with a strategy for a reasonable time before switching
- Can explain why they chose a particular strategy

**Creative Thinking:**
- Generates multiple ideas during brainstorming (3-5)
- Applies SCAMPER prompts with guidance to produce variations
- Recognizes when a problem could be reframed, though may need help doing so
- Makes connections between problems in similar domains

**Collaboration:**
- Contributes ideas voluntarily in group settings
- Listens to and considers others' approaches
- Begins to build on teammates' ideas ("What if we also...")
- Shares work and takes on assigned roles within a group

**Metacognition:**
- Describes the strategy they used and whether it worked
- Recognizes when they are stuck and tries at least one alternative
- Compares their approach with a peer's and notes differences
- Reflects on what was easy and hard about a problem after solving

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level demonstrate independent, strategic problem solving. They use multiple strategies flexibly, monitor their own progress, explain their reasoning, and begin to transfer strategies across domains.

**Understanding Problems:**
- Consistently restates problems before solving, identifying knowns, unknowns, and constraints
- Distinguishes between the stated problem and deeper underlying issues
- Identifies and sets aside irrelevant information
- Describes what a successful solution would look like before beginning

**Strategy Use:**
- Names and uses 5+ strategies, selecting based on problem characteristics
- Switches strategies when the current approach is not making progress
- Combines multiple strategies within a single problem
- Evaluates which strategy was most effective after solving and explains why

**Creative Thinking:**
- Generates many diverse ideas during brainstorming (6+)
- Applies SCAMPER prompts independently to produce creative variations
- Reframes problems independently to reveal new solution paths
- Draws analogies between problems in different domains to inspire solutions

**Collaboration:**
- Facilitates group problem solving by eliciting ideas from all members
- Builds on others' ideas constructively and offers alternatives respectfully
- Distributes tasks based on team members' strengths
- Manages disagreement productively, focusing on ideas rather than people

**Metacognition:**
- Monitors progress in real time and adjusts approach when needed
- Explains reasoning process clearly, including why alternatives were rejected
- Recognizes structural similarities between problems across domains
- Reflects on growth over time, identifying strategies that have become stronger

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level demonstrate mastery of problem-solving as a transferable meta-skill. They tackle genuinely novel and complex problems, mentor others, create new approaches, and apply strategies flexibly across all domains.

**Understanding Problems:**
- Analyzes problem structure formally, classifying problems by type and identifying analogous solved problems
- Identifies hidden assumptions and questions whether they are valid
- Specifies success criteria precisely before attempting solutions
- Recognizes when a problem is ill-defined and takes steps to clarify it

**Strategy Use:**
- Draws from a comprehensive strategy repertoire, including domain-specific and general-purpose approaches
- Creates novel strategies by combining or modifying existing ones
- Evaluates strategy efficiency (not just effectiveness) and selects optimal approaches
- Teaches strategies to others, adapting explanations to the learner's level

**Creative Thinking:**
- Generates highly original ideas that challenge conventional assumptions
- Uses systematic creativity frameworks (SCAMPER, lateral thinking, design thinking) fluently
- Draws analogies across distant domains, identifying deep structural parallels
- Evaluates creative ideas rigorously, balancing novelty with feasibility

**Collaboration:**
- Leads collaborative problem-solving sessions, creating structures that leverage group intelligence
- Mentors less experienced problem solvers, modeling strategies and productive struggle
- Navigates complex group dynamics including conflicting perspectives and power imbalances
- Synthesizes diverse contributions into solutions that exceed what any individual could produce

**Metacognition:**
- Demonstrates sophisticated awareness of own cognitive processes, biases, and blind spots
- Transfers strategies across domains deliberately and reflects on what enabled the transfer
- Evaluates the quality and limitations of their own solutions with intellectual honesty
- Contributes to the development of problem-solving pedagogy by articulating what works and why

## Formative Assessment

Formative assessment occurs during learning and provides feedback that guides improvement. In PROB-101, formative assessment focuses on the quality of the problem-solving process, not the correctness of the answer.

### Think-Aloud Assessment

Observe learners as they narrate their thinking while solving. Listen for:
- Do they restate the problem before attempting a solution?
- Do they name the strategy they are using?
- Do they monitor progress and notice when they are stuck?
- Do they switch strategies when the current approach is not working?
- Do they check their solution against the original problem?

### Strategy Journal Review

Periodic review of learners' strategy journals. Look for:
- Are entries becoming more detailed and reflective over time?
- Is the learner using a growing repertoire of strategies?
- Does the learner evaluate strategy effectiveness honestly?
- Does the learner recognize patterns across problems?

### Peer Observation

Learners observe a partner solving a problem and provide feedback:
- What strategies did you see your partner use?
- When did your partner get stuck, and how did they respond?
- What would you have done differently, and why?
- What did you learn from watching your partner's approach?

### Getting-Unstuck Check-Ins

When learners report being stuck, assess their self-awareness:
- What have you tried so far?
- What do you think is the source of the difficulty?
- What strategy have you not tried yet?
- Can you think of a simpler version of this problem?

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment evaluates cumulative growth in problem-solving competence at the end of a unit or module.

### Problem-Solving Portfolio

A collection of work demonstrating growth in strategic problem solving:

**Required components:**
- Strategy journal entries showing progression from limited to diverse strategy use
- A solved problem with detailed written reflection on strategy selection, monitoring, and evaluation
- An unsolved problem with reflection on what was tried, why it did not work, and what might be tried next
- Evidence of transfer: the same strategy applied to problems in two different domains with reflection

**Optional components:**
- A problem designed for a classmate that requires a specific strategy to solve efficiently
- A creative solution that uses an unconventional approach with justification
- A collaborative problem-solving reflection describing group dynamics and individual contributions

### Novel Problem Challenge

Present a genuinely unfamiliar problem (not a variation of a practiced type). Evaluate:

1. **Understanding:** Did the learner restate the problem, identify knowns/unknowns, and clarify constraints?
2. **Strategy selection:** Did the learner consider multiple strategies and select one with reasoning?
3. **Execution:** Did the learner carry out the strategy systematically?
4. **Monitoring:** Did the learner check progress and adjust when needed?
5. **Reflection:** Did the learner evaluate their approach regardless of whether they reached the answer?

### Strategy Teaching Assessment

Have the learner teach a problem-solving strategy to a younger student or peer:

- Does the learner explain the strategy clearly with appropriate examples?
- Can the learner adapt their explanation when the student does not understand?
- Does the learner demonstrate the strategy on a problem while narrating their thinking?
- Does the learner help the student apply the strategy to a new problem without doing it for them?

## Portfolio Suggestions

### Foundation Level (PreK-2)
- Drawings of how they solved a puzzle or building challenge
- "I tried... then I tried..." narratives about persistence
- Sorting activities showing known vs. unknown information
- Photos of built solutions with verbal descriptions of the process

### Elementary Level (3-5)
- Strategy journal entries naming the strategy used and whether it worked
- "Work backwards" and "draw a diagram" solution examples
- Peer comparison notes: how a partner solved the same problem differently
- Simple analogies: "This problem is like [familiar problem] because..."

### Middle School Level (6-8)
- Multi-strategy solutions showing strategy selection reasoning
- Creative solutions using SCAMPER or reframing with justification
- Collaborative problem-solving reflections on group dynamics
- Cross-domain transfer examples with explicit structural comparison

### High School Level (9-12)
- Complex problem analyses with systems maps and stakeholder identification
- Original problem designs with solution guides and strategy annotations
- Strategy teaching portfolio showing mentorship of younger learners
- Tradeoff evaluation essays for wicked problems with explicit ethical reasoning
