# DIGLIT-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

This assessment framework evaluates digital literacy as judgment and critical thinking, not memorization of facts or proficiency with tools. Progress is measured by evidence that learners understand digital systems, evaluate information critically, make safe choices online, and recognize implications of algorithmic systems.

A learner who asks critical questions about how social media works, evaluates source credibility independently, and understands privacy implications is making progress — regardless of whether they know technical jargon or can use the latest app.

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing awareness of digital concepts and learning to follow guidelines and procedures.

**Digital Foundations:**
- Identifies basic hardware components when shown or labeled
- Recognizes that devices have different purposes and functions
- Understands that technology sometimes breaks but can be fixed
- Follows troubleshooting procedures with guidance

**Information Literacy:**
- Notices that some sources might be more reliable than others
- Follows basic search instructions to find information
- Recognizes obvious misinformation when pointed out
- Asks for help when unsure about information online

**Digital Communication:**
- Sends emails or messages with basic structure and clarity
- Uses collaborative tools with guidance
- Asks questions when unsure about tone or appropriateness
- Participates in group work with support

**Online Safety:**
- Follows security rules when reminded (passwords, privacy settings)
- Recognizes obviously unkind behavior online
- Tells adults about concerning online experiences
- Participates in discussions about digital citizenship

**Data & Algorithms:**
- Recognizes that computers remember information
- Notices when ads or recommendations appear online
- Understands basic concept that data is collected
- Asks questions about how systems work

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building systems understanding and applying guidelines more independently.

**Digital Foundations:**
- Describes how basic hardware components work together
- Understands difference between hardware and software
- Traces how information travels on a network
- Applies troubleshooting steps to solve common problems
- Recognizes when to seek help for complex problems

**Information Literacy:**
- Uses search strategies beyond single keywords
- Evaluates source credibility using 2-3 criteria (author, date, reputation)
- Fact-checks claims using simple methods
- Identifies obviously biased sources
- Gathers information from multiple sources

**Digital Communication:**
- Composes professional emails appropriate to context
- Collaborates using shared documents with minimal errors
- Adapts communication style for different audiences
- Cites sources when appropriate
- Recognizes and follows community norms online

**Online Safety:**
- Maintains security practices independently (strong passwords, privacy settings)
- Recognizes signs of cyberbullying and responds appropriately
- Understands that online actions have consequences
- Makes conscious decisions about what to share online
- Identifies suspicious emails and online scams

**Data & Algorithms:**
- Describes what data is collected in daily activities
- Explains how recommendations are personalized
- Understands basic concept of algorithmic decision-making
- Recognizes that algorithms are designed by humans
- Asks questions about fairness and bias

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level demonstrate deep understanding and critical thinking about digital systems.

**Digital Foundations:**
- Explains how networks and the internet work at architectural level
- Understands concepts like IP addresses, routers, protocols
- Applies systematic troubleshooting to complex problems
- Researches how new technologies work
- Explains digital concepts to others

**Information Literacy:**
- Evaluates sources using multiple sophisticated criteria
- Synthesizes information from diverse sources
- Fact-checks claims independently and thoroughly
- Recognizes subtle bias and propaganda techniques
- Understands how algorithms and echo chambers shape information landscape
- Teaches others about evaluating information

**Digital Communication:**
- Adapts communication across multiple platforms and contexts
- Collaborates effectively on complex projects
- Understands copyright and intellectual property
- Creates clear, compelling digital content
- Provides constructive feedback to peers
- Reflects on own digital communication practices

**Online Safety:**
- Makes autonomous security decisions
- Understands and manages digital footprint
- Responds compassionately to cyberbullying
- Helps peers understand online safety
- Recognizes manipulation tactics
- Understands digital rights and responsibilities

**Data & Algorithms:**
- Understands how personal data is collected, stored, and used
- Explains algorithmic decision-making and its implications
- Recognizes algorithmic bias and discusses fairness
- Evaluates data-driven claims using statistical thinking
- Understands ethical implications of AI systems
- Discusses implications for privacy, autonomy, justice

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level demonstrate mastery and can teach, research, and contribute to digital literacy.

**Digital Foundations:**
- Understands complex digital systems deeply
- Researches and learns about new technologies independently
- Explains technical concepts clearly to non-technical audiences
- Contributes to discussions about technology policy
- Recognizes implications of technical design choices

**Information Literacy:**
- Conducts sophisticated research projects
- Evaluates information ecosystems and how they shape understanding
- Recognizes and resists manipulation tactics
- Contributes to discussions about misinformation and truth
- Teaches others about information evaluation
- Understands information literacy as connected to justice and democracy

**Digital Communication:**
- Produces high-quality digital content for authentic audiences
- Leads collaborative projects involving multiple stakeholders
- Mentors others in digital communication
- Understands implications of own digital presence
- Recognizes power dynamics in digital communication

**Online Safety:**
- Models excellent digital citizenship
- Teaches others about online safety
- Advocates for fair and just online policies
- Contributes to healthy online communities
- Understands connection between personal safety and systemic issues
- Recognizes rights and responsibilities of digital platforms

**Data & Algorithms:**
- Understands data science concepts and limitations
- Researches algorithmic systems and their societal implications
- Contributes to discussions about AI ethics and policy
- Recognizes intersections of data use with justice issues
- Evaluates information and systems critically and systemically

## Formative Assessment

Formative assessment occurs during learning and provides feedback that guides improvement. In DIGLIT-101, formative assessment focuses on thinking, judgment, and developing understanding.

### Observation During Activities

During investigations and discussions, observe:
- Do they ask critical questions?
- Do they distinguish observation from inference?
- Do they cite evidence when making claims?
- Do they recognize complexity and nuance?
- Do they change their thinking when presented with new information?
- Do they help peers understand concepts?

### Questioning & Discussion

Ask questions that reveal thinking:
- "Why do you think that source is reliable/unreliable?"
- "What would change your mind about this claim?"
- "How might someone use this technique to manipulate people?"
- "What are the limitations of this data or study?"
- "Who benefits from this algorithm working this way?"

### Self-Reflection

Have learners reflect on:
- What they learned about how digital systems work
- How their understanding has changed
- Digital choices they made and why
- Questions they still have
- How they'll apply learning in their own digital life

### Skill Demonstrations

Ask learners to demonstrate competence:
- **Search:** Find reliable information on a topic
- **Evaluate:** Assess credibility of sources without guidance
- **Create:** Produce digital communication appropriate to context
- **Protect:** Manage privacy and security settings
- **Analyze:** Explain how an algorithm or data system works

### Peer Discussion & Feedback

Facilitate discussions where learners:
- Discuss digital scenarios and ethical dilemmas
- Provide feedback on each other's information evaluation
- Collaborate on projects
- Teach each other concepts

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment evaluates cumulative learning at the end of a unit or module.

### Digital Literacy Portfolio

A collection of work demonstrating growth in digital thinking:

**Required Components:**
1. **Learning Journal Entries** — Reflections on how understanding has changed (3-5 entries over the module)
2. **Information Evaluation Assignment** — Evaluating sources on a topic of interest, explaining credibility reasoning
3. **Problem-Solving Case Study** — Responding to a realistic digital scenario (scam, cyberbullying, privacy dilemma, algorithmic issue)
4. **Self-Assessment** — Evaluating own digital literacy and identifying areas of growth

**Optional Components:**
- A teaching artifact (explaining a concept to younger learners or peers)
- A research project about digital systems or online safety
- Digital communication samples showing growth
- A reflection on own digital practices and goals

### Investigation Projects

Projects that demonstrate applied understanding:

1. **Information Evaluation Project:** Select a controversial topic. Find 5 sources with different perspectives. Evaluate their credibility. Write analysis explaining why you trust some sources more than others and how different sources shaped your understanding.

2. **Digital Systems Research:** Research how a digital system works (social media algorithm, search engine, email, online voting, etc.). Create a presentation explaining the system to peers who know nothing about it.

3. **Online Safety Case Study:** Research a real cybersecurity incident, cyberbullying case, or privacy violation. Explain what happened, why it matters, how it could have been prevented, and what should change.

4. **Algorithmic Bias Analysis:** Find an example of algorithmic bias (hiring, lending, content moderation, etc.). Research how it happened and what impact it had. Propose potential solutions.

5. **Digital Citizenship Project:** Identify a problem in your digital community (school social media, gaming community, online classroom, etc.). Propose and implement a solution.

### Performance Assessments

Learners demonstrate competence in authentic contexts:

1. **Information Evaluation Scenario:** Given a claim, find evidence, evaluate sources, and present your conclusion about its credibility.

2. **Digital Communication Project:** Write professional emails, collaborate on a shared document, or present information online — showing attention to audience and context.

3. **Security Decision-Making:** Given privacy scenarios, demonstrate understanding by setting privacy settings, creating strong passwords, and explaining decisions.

4. **Algorithmic Analysis:** Examine a real algorithmic system (search results, recommendations, content moderation) and explain how it works and what biases it might have.

5. **Teaching Others:** Explain a digital literacy concept to younger students or less digitally literate family members.

## Portfolio Suggestions

### Foundation Level (K-2)
- Observation drawings of devices and their parts
- Stories about online experiences (good and concerning)
- Pictures of "safe" vs. "unsafe" online behavior
- Discussions about devices and what they do
- Simple problem-solving scenarios

### Elementary Level (3-5)
- Observation journals about technology use
- Information evaluation activities (sorting credible vs. questionable sources)
- Email writing samples
- Stories about online experiences and reflections
- Basic fact-checking projects
- Drawings or descriptions of network connections

### Middle School Level (6-8)
- Full investigation portfolios with source evaluation
- Case studies of digital citizenship issues
- Problem-solving scenarios with written responses
- Research projects about digital systems
- Reflection on own digital practices and goals
- Peer feedback on information evaluation

### High School Level (9-12)
- Research-quality projects on digital literacy topics
- Critical analysis of algorithmic systems
- Case studies with policy implications
- Digital communication samples across contexts
- Reflection on implications of technology use
- Contribution to digital citizenship discussions or initiatives

## Grading Rubric

If numerical grades are required, assess based on evidence of understanding and judgment:

| Criterion | Beginning (1) | Developing (2) | Proficient (3) | Advanced (4) |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|----------------|-------------|
| **Digital Understanding** | Recalls basic facts; follows procedures | Explains concepts with support; applies to familiar situations | Explains systems deeply; applies to new situations | Researches, teaches, contributes to discussions |
| **Critical Thinking** | Accepts information without evaluation | Questions information; applies some criteria | Evaluates multiple perspectives; recognizes complexity | Analyzes systems implications; recognizes interconnections |
| **Information Evaluation** | Trusts/distrusts sources without reasoning | Uses simple criteria to evaluate | Applies sophisticated evaluation criteria | Synthesizes diverse sources; understands information ecosystems |
| **Digital Citizenship** | Follows rules with reminding | Follows rules independently; recognizes impact on others | Makes autonomous ethical decisions; helps others | Models excellence; contributes to community standards |
| **Application & Transfer** | Applies learning only to taught examples | Applies learning to similar situations | Applies learning to new situations | Applies across domains; transfers to real-world decisions |

## Important Assessment Principles

### Assessment ≠ Measurement of Facts

We are NOT assessing:
- Whether students know the name of the CPU
- Whether they can use Microsoft Word
- Whether they memorize password rules
- Whether they know the definition of "algorithm"

### Assessment = Evidence of Thinking

We ARE assessing:
- Whether they understand how systems work
- Whether they evaluate sources critically
- Whether they make safe, conscious decisions online
- Whether they recognize implications and complexity

### Authentic Contexts Matter

Assess in realistic contexts:
- Evaluating real sources, not fictional ones
- Solving actual problems, not textbook scenarios
- Communicating for real purposes, not exercises
- Protecting their own digital presence, not hypothetical identities

### Dialogue > Grades

The most valuable assessment is conversation:
- Asking why they evaluated a source as credible
- Discussing digital choices and implications
- Reflecting on learning and growth
- Identifying areas to deepen understanding

---

## Conclusion

DIGLIT-101 assessment emphasizes judgment, understanding, and the ability to think critically about digital systems. Rather than testing what students know, we document evidence of how they think about digital literacy, make decisions, and contribute to their digital communities.

The goal is not to create digital experts, but to assess whether learners are developing the thinking skills to be informed, capable, responsible digital citizens.
