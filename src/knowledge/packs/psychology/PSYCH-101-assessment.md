# PSYCH-101 Assessment Framework

This document outlines how to assess learning and growth in Psychology & Human Development across all modules and grade levels.

---

## Overview: Four-Level Rubric

All assessment in PSYCH-101 uses a four-level rubric that applies across modules and grade levels, with age-appropriate indicators.

### Beginning
- Can name and recognize emotions, concepts, and developmental stages
- Understands basic definitions and examples
- Describes situations simply using psychology vocabulary
- Shows awareness that psychology applies to real life
- May need support applying concepts consistently

### Developing
- Can explain psychological concepts in their own words
- Applies concepts to familiar situations with guidance
- Recognizes examples and implications of psychological principles
- Shows growing self-awareness and empathy
- Can answer "why" questions about behavior with support

### Proficient
- Can explain complex psychological concepts clearly
- Applies understanding to solve real social and personal challenges
- Designs investigations or projects using psychological principles
- Teaches concepts to peers effectively
- Shows consistent self-awareness, empathy, and growth mindset

### Advanced
- Can integrate concepts across multiple modules
- Critiques and evaluates psychological research and claims
- Applies concepts to novel or complex situations
- Mentors others in psychological thinking and emotional intelligence
- Contributes to dialogue on ethics, applications, and limitations of psychology

## Module 1: Brain, Perception & Cognition

### Beginning
- Identifies optical illusions and notices they trick your eyes
- Knows that the brain receives information through senses
- Can describe memory as forgetting sometimes happens
- Recognizes the word "bias" in simple examples

### Developing
- Explains that the brain actively interprets what it perceives
- Describes attention as paying attention to some things and missing others
- Explains that people forget because memory doesn't work like a video recording
- Identifies one or two cognitive biases in real examples

### Proficient
- Explains how perception is an active construction by the brain
- Describes inattentional blindness and why it happens
- Explains how memory systems work (sensory, working, long-term) and strategies to improve retention
- Identifies multiple cognitive biases in decision-making and media
- Applies understanding to evaluate eyewitness testimony or advertising claims

### Advanced
- Critiques illusions and explains the neuroscience behind them
- Discusses implications of attention limitations for safety, learning, and testimony
- Designs memory experiments or study methods based on research
- Analyzes advertising and media for exploitation of cognitive biases
- Connects perception and cognition to broader psychological phenomena

---

## Module 2: Learning, Memory & Study Skills

### Beginning
- Knows the terms "classical conditioning" and "operant conditioning"
- Identifies rewards and punishments in simple stories
- Tries a new study strategy
- Notices that they learn by watching others sometimes

### Developing
- Explains classical conditioning with an example (theirs or given)
- Identifies reward and punishment in their own behavior
- Applies one effective study strategy to new material
- Reflects on how they learned a specific skill
- Shows awareness that not all study methods work equally

### Proficient
- Explains both classical and operant conditioning with personal examples
- Identifies and applies evidence-based study strategies (spaced repetition, active recall, interleaving)
- Designs and tests a study method experiment
- Develops metacognitive awareness of their own learning
- Teaches study strategies to peers and explains why they work

### Advanced
- Analyzes behavior patterns in terms of conditioning principles
- Critiques study advice and evaluates it against research
- Designs learning environments or activities optimized for memory and retention
- Mentors others in metacognition and self-regulated learning
- Connects learning principles to broader motivation and achievement

---

## Module 3: Emotion, Motivation & Well-being

### Beginning
- Names basic emotions (happy, sad, angry, scared)
- Identifies what emotion they're feeling in the moment
- Tries one emotion regulation strategy
- Knows some activities feel more fun than others

### Developing
- Locates emotions in their body; describes what triggered them
- Applies emotion regulation strategies when supported
- Distinguishes between activities they do for enjoyment vs. obligation
- Shows awareness of intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation
- Reflects on growth mindset vs. fixed mindset in learning

### Proficient
- Explains emotions as information; uses emotion regulation toolkit independently
- Recognizes patterns in their emotional responses across contexts
- Strategically builds intrinsic motivation in areas of importance
- Demonstrates growth mindset when facing challenges
- Teaches emotion regulation or resilience strategies to peers

### Advanced
- Analyzes emotional patterns and what drives them; makes intentional changes
- Helps others develop emotion regulation and resilience
- Critiques extrinsic reward systems and designs intrinsically motivating environments
- Applies well-being research (positive psychology, mindfulness, resilience) to own life and community
- Contributes to conversations about mental health and emotional intelligence

---

## Module 4: Human Development Across the Lifespan

### Beginning
- Names some developmental stages (childhood, adolescence, adulthood)
- Describes what changes happen during one stage
- Notices that people are different at different ages
- Shows curiosity about why people act the way they do

### Developing
- Describes characteristics of 2-3 developmental stages
- Explains one change (cognitive, emotional, social) that happens at each stage
- Applies developmental understanding to explain behavior
- Recognizes attachment and identity as important
- Reflects on their own developmental stage and tasks

### Proficient
- Explains major developmental theories (Piaget, Erikson) and their application
- Connects developmental stage to behavior and challenges in multiple examples
- Conducts interviews or observations showing understanding of developmental differences
- Recognizes own developmental tasks and supports others in theirs
- Discusses how development and culture interact

### Advanced
- Critiques developmental theories and discusses cultural variations
- Analyzes complex situations through developmental lens
- Mentors younger individuals recognizing their developmental stage
- Contributes to conversations about life transitions, aging, and lifespan development
- Integrates developmental understanding with other psychological principles

---

## Module 5: Social Psychology & Relationships

### Beginning
- Recognizes conformity (people doing what the group does)
- Notices stereotypes in media
- Shows empathy for others' feelings
- Identifies what they like about friends/relationships

### Developing
- Explains conformity with examples
- Recognizes stereotypes and discusses why they're inaccurate
- Practices perspective-taking in social situations
- Discusses what makes relationships healthy
- Reflects on their own social behavior and influences

### Proficient
- Explains conformity, group dynamics, and social influence with real examples
- Recognizes and interrupts stereotypes and biases
- Applies empathy and perspective-taking to resolve conflicts
- Analyzes relationships using psychological principles
- Teaches peers about social influence and bias
- Demonstrates prosocial behavior and helping

### Advanced
- Critiques famous social psychology experiments and their ethics
- Analyzes group dynamics in complex situations (teams, organizations, social movements)
- Helps others develop empathy and perspective-taking
- Contributes to conversations about prejudice, social justice, and intergroup relations
- Applies social psychology to real-world social challenges

## Formative Assessment (During Learning)

Formative assessment helps learners know they're on track and informs instruction.

### Emotion & Thought Journals
Ongoing journaling where learners reflect on:
- What emotions they notice daily (Emotion Thermometer)
- How they're applying study strategies
- Moments when they recognized cognitive biases
- Changes in their understanding of themselves or others

**Evidence of Progress:** Increasingly detailed reflection; recognition of patterns; application of concepts to new situations

### Quick Concept Checks
Brief formative checks throughout learning:
- "Explain this optical illusion using what you know about perception"
- "What study strategy could help you learn this? Why?"
- "What emotion is this character experiencing? What clues tell you that?"
- "What developmental stage is this? How do you know?"

**Evidence of Progress:** Growing accuracy, depth, and independence in responses

### Peer Discussions & Peer Teaching
Learners explain concepts to each other, teach strategies, and discuss applications.

**Evidence of Progress:** Clarity of explanation; ability to answer peers' questions; integrating feedback

### Self-Designed Investigations
Learners design their own mini-studies:
- Test which study strategy works for them
- Observe conformity in their environment
- Map their own emotion patterns
- Interview someone about development

**Evidence of Progress:** Quality of questions, thoughtfulness of design, insights from data

### Direct Observation
Teachers/mentors notice:
- When learners recognize and apply emotion regulation strategies
- When they show empathy or perspective-taking
- When they catch themselves in cognitive biases
- When they help peers understand concepts

**Evidence of Progress:** Frequency and independence of applying concepts; helping others

## Summative Assessment (Evidence of Mastery)

Summative assessment demonstrates overall learning at key points.

### PSYCH-101 Portfolio
Comprehensive collection of learning across modules, including:

**Required Elements:**
1. **Vision Statement:** "What I learned about how humans work and how it changes me"
2. **Module Investigations:** 1-2 key investigations or reflections per module (5 total)
3. **Emotion & Motivation Journal:** Sampling of entries showing self-awareness and growth
4. **Study Strategy Analysis:** What works for you and why (from Module 2 experiments)
5. **Relationship Reflection:** How psychology helps you understand your own relationships
6. **Application Project:** "How does psychology explain something important in my life?"

**Evaluation:** Portfolio demonstrates understanding across modules, self-awareness, ability to apply concepts, and growth over time.

### Teach-Back: Explaining Psychology to Someone New
Learner explains one psychological concept clearly to someone unfamiliar with it:
- Chooses a concept they understand well
- Explains it clearly with examples
- Answers questions
- Adjusts explanation based on audience understanding

**Evaluation:** Clarity, accuracy, use of examples, ability to simplify without losing meaning

### Personal Application Project
Learner selects a personal or community challenge and applies psychology:
- Identifies psychological principles relevant to the situation
- Gathers evidence or observes the situation carefully
- Proposes how psychological understanding could help
- Reflects on what they learned

**Examples:**
- "How can I use emotion regulation to handle test anxiety?"
- "What's happening in my friend group? How can I understand and help?"
- "How does my community show conformity? Is that helpful or problematic?"
- "What developmental needs are my younger siblings showing? How can I support them?"

**Evaluation:** Application of accurate concepts, depth of thinking, evidence of growth, realism about limitations

### Ethical Analysis
Learner discusses ethical questions in psychology:
- What responsibilities do psychologists have when studying human behavior?
- When is persuasion ethical vs. manipulative?
- How can psychology help without harming?
- What questions can't psychology answer?

**Evaluation:** Nuance, recognition of multiple perspectives, grounded in psychological knowledge

### Portfolio-Based Assessment (Grades 6+)
Learners compile evidence of learning across the year and reflect on growth.

**Sections:**
1. Best work samples (investigations, reflections, projects)
2. Growth evidence (early attempts vs. current work showing improvement)
3. Self-assessment (rubric rating and explanation of why)
4. Reflection on applications to own life
5. Goals for continued growth

**Evaluation:** Honest self-assessment, recognition of growth, evidence supporting conclusions

## Grade-Level Emphasis

While all modules use this four-level rubric, emphasis shifts with grade level:

### Foundation (K-2)
- Emphasis on emotion recognition and naming
- Concrete, observable psychology (what I see, feel, do)
- Guided reflections and simple discussions
- Play-based activities and observation

### Elementary (3-5)
- Emphasis on self-awareness and learning strategies
- Beginning to understand others' perspectives
- Guided investigations and reflections
- More independent journals and projects

### Middle School (6-8)
- Emphasis on understanding self in context of development and group
- Growing ability to critique media and recognize bias
- More independent investigations and analysis
- Deeper reflection on relationships and motivations

### High School (9-12)
- Emphasis on integrating concepts across modules
- Engaging with research methods and evidence evaluation
- Contributions to discussions on ethics and applications
- Complex projects showing sophisticated understanding

### College+ (13+)
- Emphasis on research-level thinking and contribution
- Advanced analysis of psychological theories and evidence
- Potential research projects or field placements
- Consideration of career and application paths

## Strategies for Different Learners

### For Verbal Learners
- Discussions and peer teaching
- Podcasts or audiobooks on psychology topics
- Recorded reflections
- Teaching others

### For Visual-Spatial Learners
- Concept maps and diagrams
- Videos and animations
- Visual journals and sketches
- Media analysis

### For Kinesthetic Learners
- Hands-on activities and experiments
- Role-playing and drama
- Movement and physical activities
- Building models or creating physical representations

### For Introverted Learners
- Written reflections and journals
- Individual investigations
- Small group discussions
- Asynchronous sharing (written instead of verbal)

### For Learners Who Struggle Socially
- Emotion regulation toolkit development
- One-on-one relationship coaching
- Structured social interaction practice
- Case study analysis instead of live social experiments

### For Advanced Learners
- Research projects with statistical analysis
- Mentoring younger learners
- Critiquing psychological studies
- Exploring specialized topics (neuroscience, cultural psychology, etc.)

---

## Using Assessment Data

### For Learners
- "What did I learn? Where can I grow?"
- "What strategies are working for me?"
- "How has my understanding changed?"

### For Parents/Mentors
- "What is my child learning about themselves?"
- "Where might they need support?"
- "How can I facilitate deeper learning?"

### For Teachers/Facilitators
- "What concepts do students understand well?"
- "Where is additional instruction needed?"
- "How can I tailor instruction to different needs?"

---

## No-Blame, Growth-Focused Assessment

Important: Assessment in PSYCH-101 is about growth, not judgment.

- Mistakes are learning opportunities
- Struggles are normal and expected
- Everyone learns at different paces
- Current level is a starting point, not a ceiling
- Feedback is specific and supportive, not critical

---

## Special Considerations

### Mental Health & Sensitive Content
Some concepts (emotion regulation, adolescent development, mental health) may surface personal struggles. Assessment should:
- Never force disclosure of personal mental health information
- Recognize that some reflections may be private
- Provide resources for those who need support
- Create psychologically safe environments

### Ethical Constraints
Some real social experiments in psychology were unethical (Milgram, Stanford). When discussing:
- Frame as historical lessons in research ethics
- Discuss what protections exist now
- Do not replicate unethical research
- Respect boundaries of all participants

---

## Summary

PSYCH-101 assessment prioritizes:
✓ Self-awareness and growth
✓ Application to real life
✓ Evidence-based thinking
✓ Empathy and perspective-taking
✓ Continuous improvement over "grades"

The goal is learners who understand themselves and others with increasing depth, apply psychological knowledge to real challenges, and continue learning about the endlessly fascinating science of human behavior.
