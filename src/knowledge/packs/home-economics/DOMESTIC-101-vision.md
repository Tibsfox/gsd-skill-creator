# DOMESTIC-101: Home Economics & Life Skills -- Foundational Knowledge Pack

## Vision

The skills you actually need to live well -- cooking a meal from scratch, keeping a clean and organized home, managing money, mending clothes, and planning for the future. These are the skills that grandparents once taught, that schools have largely abandoned, and that young adults desperately need. Not gendered, not outdated -- essential for everyone.

Home economics is not a relic of the 1950s. It is the practical knowledge that makes independent living possible: the ability to feed yourself nutritious food, maintain a clean and functional living space, manage your finances, repair rather than replace, and handle the thousand small tasks that daily life demands. When these skills are missing, people become dependent on convenience products, delivery services, and expensive professionals for things they could easily do themselves.

DOMESTIC-101 teaches these skills without nostalgia, without gender assumptions, and without moralizing. Every human being -- regardless of gender, background, or future career -- needs to know how to cook, clean, budget, sew a button, and plan ahead. These are not women's skills or men's skills. They are human skills.

---

## Problem Statement

### The Skills Gap

Home economics programs have been cut from most schools in the United States and many other countries. What was once a standard part of education -- learning to cook, sew, clean, budget, and manage a household -- has been replaced by nothing. The assumption is that families will teach these skills, but many families themselves lack them, having grown up during the same period of decline.

The result is a generation of young adults who:

- **Cannot cook basic meals.** Over-reliance on processed foods, fast food, and delivery services. Poor nutrition follows naturally from inability to prepare food. The connection between cooking skills and health outcomes is well-documented.

- **Cannot manage money.** Financial illiteracy leads to credit card debt, inability to budget, poor savings habits, and vulnerability to predatory financial products. Young adults are entering the workforce without understanding compound interest, insurance, or taxes.

- **Cannot maintain a household.** Cleaning, organizing, basic maintenance, and seasonal tasks are mysteries. The result is disorganized, uncomfortable living spaces and expensive service calls for problems that take five minutes to fix.

- **Cannot repair or mend.** A missing button means a discarded shirt. A loose screw means a replaced piece of furniture. A clogged drain means a plumber. The throw-away culture is reinforced by inability to fix things.

- **Cannot plan or prepare.** Emergency preparedness, meal planning, seasonal maintenance, long-term financial planning -- these require foresight and organizational skills that are not innate. They must be taught.

### Why It Matters

These skills are not luxuries. They are the foundation of:

- **Health:** Cooking is the single most impactful health behavior after not smoking. People who cook eat better, period.
- **Financial stability:** Budgeting, saving, and avoiding debt are skills, not personality traits. They can be taught.
- **Environmental sustainability:** Repair, reuse, reduce waste, grow food, preserve food -- all require skills.
- **Independence:** Self-sufficiency is not about doing everything yourself. It is about having the capability to do what needs doing.
- **Resilience:** When supply chains break, when income drops, when emergencies hit -- practical skills are what keep people fed, sheltered, and functional.
- **Community:** Cooking for others, sharing skills, helping neighbors fix things, teaching children -- these build social bonds.

### The Gender Question

Historically, home economics was taught primarily to girls, reinforcing the idea that domestic work was women's work. This was wrong then and it is wrong now. The solution is not to eliminate home economics education but to make it universal and gender-neutral.

Every person needs to eat. Every person needs clean clothes. Every person needs a functional living space. Every person needs to manage money. These are not gendered activities. DOMESTIC-101 teaches them as universal life skills, essential for all people regardless of gender identity, family structure, or career path.

A young man who cannot sew a button is as poorly prepared for adult life as a young woman who cannot change a tire. DOMESTIC-101 rejects the entire framework of gendered domestic skills and instead asks: what does every human need to know to live well?

## Core Concepts

### 1. Food & Cooking

Meal planning, preparation, food safety, nutrition, cultural foodways, and preserving. Cooking is the foundational domestic skill -- the one with the most direct impact on health, happiness, and daily life.

**What learners will understand:**
- Food is not mysterious. Cooking follows learnable principles.
- Meal planning saves money, reduces waste, and improves nutrition.
- Food safety prevents illness. Temperature, time, and cleanliness matter.
- Every culture has food wisdom worth learning and celebrating.
- Preserving food (freezing, canning, drying) extends what you grow or buy.
- Cooking is creative, social, and deeply satisfying.

**Progression:**
- **K-2:** Kitchen safety, simple no-cook recipes, helping adults in the kitchen, naming foods and their origins, tasting and exploring flavors.
- **3-5:** Following recipes, measuring ingredients, basic cooking techniques (boiling, baking, mixing), meal planning basics, food safety rules, cultural food exploration.
- **6-8:** Independent cooking of complete meals, adapting recipes, shopping on a budget, food preservation basics, understanding nutrition labels, cooking for others.
- **9-12:** Advanced cooking techniques, meal planning for a household, cooking from different cuisines, food budgeting, batch cooking, entertaining, preservation methods.
- **College+:** Cooking as life management -- feeding yourself well on limited time and budget, cooking for health conditions, developing a personal food philosophy, teaching others.

### 2. Textiles & Clothing

Sewing basics, mending, laundering, wardrobe management, and fabric knowledge. The ability to maintain, repair, and care for clothing and textiles.

**What learners will understand:**
- Clothing has a lifecycle: acquire, wear, care for, repair, retire.
- Basic sewing skills (buttons, hems, tears) extend the life of garments enormously.
- Laundry is a skill: sorting, temperature, stain treatment, drying, folding.
- Wardrobe management means knowing what you have, what you need, and what to let go.
- Fabric knowledge helps you buy wisely and care for garments properly.
- Fast fashion has environmental and human costs. Repair and reuse matter.

**Progression:**
- **K-2:** Sorting clothes (clean/dirty, light/dark), folding simple items, identifying fabric textures, learning button concepts, helping with laundry.
- **3-5:** Hand sewing basics (threading a needle, running stitch), sewing on a button, sorting laundry by color and fabric, reading care labels, basic stain treatment.
- **6-8:** Mending tears and holes, replacing buttons and snaps, machine sewing basics, complete laundry process, wardrobe inventory, understanding fabric types.
- **9-12:** Alterations and adjustments, advanced mending, sewing simple projects, evaluating clothing quality before purchase, capsule wardrobe concepts, sustainable fashion awareness.
- **College+:** Independent textile management -- maintaining a wardrobe, making repair decisions, understanding cost-per-wear, teaching skills to others.

### 3. Household Management

Cleaning, organizing, time management, maintenance, and seasonal tasks. The systems and routines that keep a living space functional, healthy, and comfortable.

**What learners will understand:**
- A clean home is a health measure, not a moral achievement.
- Cleaning is systematic: different surfaces, different methods, different frequencies.
- Organization is about systems, not perfection. Everything needs a place.
- Maintenance prevents expensive repairs. Small tasks done regularly save big problems later.
- Seasonal tasks (deep cleaning, weatherizing, organizing) require planning.
- Shared living requires shared responsibility and clear systems.

**Progression:**
- **K-2:** Picking up after yourself, making your bed, helping with simple cleaning tasks, learning where things belong, developing routines.
- **3-5:** Cleaning surfaces (wiping, sweeping, vacuuming), organizing personal spaces, understanding cleaning products and safety, developing chore habits, helping with household tasks.
- **6-8:** Complete room cleaning, bathroom and kitchen cleaning, laundry start to finish, basic organization systems, creating cleaning schedules, understanding household maintenance basics.
- **9-12:** Full household cleaning routines, deep cleaning techniques, home maintenance basics (changing filters, unclogging drains, tightening hardware), organizing shared spaces, creating maintenance calendars.
- **College+:** Managing a household independently -- cleaning schedules, maintenance routines, landlord communication, shared living agreements, seasonal tasks, hiring help when appropriate.

### 4. Personal Finance & Budgeting

Earning, saving, spending wisely, debt avoidance, and planning. The financial literacy that schools assume someone else will teach.

**What learners will understand:**
- Money is a tool, not a goal. Understanding it gives you choices.
- Budgeting is planning: income minus expenses equals savings (or debt).
- Saving is a habit that starts small and compounds over time.
- Debt is expensive. Understanding interest rates and avoiding unnecessary debt is critical.
- Smart shopping means comparing prices, understanding value, and resisting marketing.
- Financial planning is for everyone, not just wealthy people.

**Progression:**
- **K-2:** Understanding what money is, coins and bills, simple saving concepts, wants vs. needs, the idea of earning.
- **3-5:** Making change, simple budgeting with allowance or earnings, saving for goals, understanding prices and value, comparison shopping.
- **6-8:** Creating and tracking a budget, understanding income and expenses, saving accounts, introduction to compound interest, understanding advertising and marketing tactics, price-per-unit comparison.
- **9-12:** Real-world budgeting (rent, utilities, food, transportation), understanding credit and debt, insurance basics, taxes basics, financial goal setting, avoiding predatory financial products, retirement concepts.
- **College+:** Independent financial management -- monthly budgeting, emergency fund building, understanding employment benefits, investing basics, long-term financial planning, teaching others.

### 5. Self-Sufficiency & Resourcefulness

Repair vs. replace, making do, DIY skills, emergency preparedness, and the mindset of capability rather than dependency.

**What learners will understand:**
- Many things can be fixed. The first question should be "can I repair this?" not "where do I buy a new one?"
- Basic tools and their uses are learnable by anyone.
- Emergency preparedness is not paranoia -- it is responsibility.
- Gardening connects you to food, nature, and patience.
- Resourcefulness is a mindset: seeing possibilities rather than problems.
- Self-sufficiency does not mean doing everything alone. It means having the capability to choose.

**Progression:**
- **K-2:** Simple tool identification, helping with garden tasks, understanding what emergencies are, learning to ask for help appropriately, developing problem-solving habits.
- **3-5:** Using basic tools safely (hammer, screwdriver, measuring tape), planting and growing simple foods, making a family emergency plan, simple repair tasks with guidance, creative reuse of materials.
- **6-8:** Basic home repairs (tightening screws, replacing batteries, unclogging drains), container gardening, assembling an emergency kit, bicycle maintenance, repair vs. replace decision-making.
- **9-12:** Intermediate repairs (patching walls, replacing fixtures, basic plumbing), food preservation, comprehensive emergency preparedness, garden planning, evaluating DIY vs. professional help, teaching repair skills to others.
- **College+:** Independent maintenance and repair, community skill-sharing, sustainable living practices, emergency planning for your household, mentoring others in practical skills.

## Skill Tree

### Foundation (K-2): Discovery & Helping

At this level, learners are discovering the domestic world around them. They help adults, learn names for things, develop basic habits, and begin to understand that a household requires work and everyone can contribute.

**Key outcomes:**
- Kitchen safety awareness and simple food preparation
- Helping with laundry sorting and folding
- Picking up, organizing personal space, basic cleaning
- Understanding what money is and simple saving concepts
- Identifying basic tools and their purposes
- Developing routines and habits

### Elementary (3-5): Skills & Understanding

Learners develop real skills: following recipes, sewing a button, cleaning a room, managing a small budget, using tools safely. They understand WHY these skills matter and begin to take pride in competency.

**Key outcomes:**
- Following recipes and basic cooking techniques
- Hand sewing basics, laundry process, care labels
- Complete room cleaning, personal organization systems
- Budgeting allowance or earnings, comparison shopping
- Using basic tools safely, planting and growing food
- Understanding household systems and contributing to them

### Middle School (6-8): Independence & Competency

Learners can independently perform most domestic tasks. They cook complete meals, do their own laundry, clean thoroughly, manage a simple budget, and handle basic repairs. They are developing the competency that will carry them into independent living.

**Key outcomes:**
- Independent cooking of complete meals, meal planning
- Complete laundry process, mending, wardrobe management
- Full household cleaning, basic maintenance tasks
- Creating and tracking budgets, understanding credit concepts
- Basic repairs, emergency kit assembly, garden management
- Taking responsibility for personal living space

### High School (9-12): Mastery & Application

Learners apply domestic skills to real-world scenarios: budgeting for apartment living, cooking for a household, maintaining a home, planning finances for the future. They understand the systems behind daily life and can make informed decisions.

**Key outcomes:**
- Advanced cooking, meal planning for a household, entertaining
- Alterations, advanced mending, sustainable fashion awareness
- Deep cleaning, home maintenance calendar, seasonal tasks
- Real-world budgeting, credit management, financial planning
- Intermediate repairs, food preservation, emergency preparedness
- Teaching skills to others, shared living management

### College+ (Adult): Integration & Leadership

Adults integrate domestic skills into their full lives -- managing households, raising families, building community, and passing skills to the next generation. Domestic competency becomes a source of confidence, savings, health, and connection.

**Key outcomes:**
- Cooking as life management and creative expression
- Independent textile and wardrobe management
- Complete household management including maintenance
- Full financial independence and planning
- Self-sufficient approach to problems and challenges
- Mentoring and teaching practical skills to others

## Modules

### Module 1: Kitchen Skills & Cooking

From boiling water to meal planning, food safety, and recipes. The most fundamental domestic skill -- the one that affects your health, your budget, your social life, and your daily happiness more than any other single competency.

**What It Teaches:**
- Kitchen safety (knives, heat, food handling, allergies)
- Basic cooking techniques (boiling, sauteing, baking, roasting, mixing)
- Following and adapting recipes
- Meal planning and grocery shopping
- Food safety and storage
- Cooking on a budget
- Cultural foodways and diverse cuisines
- Cooking for others and sharing food

**Why It Matters:**
People who cook eat better. This is one of the most robust findings in nutrition research. Cooking gives you control over what goes into your food, how much you spend, and what traditions you carry forward. It is also one of life's genuine pleasures -- the smell of bread baking, the satisfaction of a meal shared, the creativity of improvising with what you have.

**Key Learning Outcomes:**
- Demonstrate safe kitchen practices appropriate to age and task
- Prepare increasingly complex meals independently
- Plan meals for a defined period considering nutrition, budget, and time
- Follow and adapt recipes for different needs (dietary restrictions, ingredient substitutions, scaling)
- Practice food safety from shopping through storage through preparation through cleanup
- Appreciate diverse food traditions and cooking methods

**Interactive Elements:**
- Recipe challenges: cook a meal from a mystery basket of ingredients
- Meal planning workshops: plan a week of meals on a budget
- Cultural food exchanges: research and prepare dishes from different cuisines
- Food safety investigations: temperature checks, cross-contamination awareness
- Cooking demonstrations and shared meals

**Technical Implementation Notes:**
Cooking activities require age-appropriate safety modifications. Younger learners work alongside adults. Middle schoolers begin independent cooking with supervision. High schoolers cook independently. All activities include allergen awareness and dietary accommodation notes.

### Module 2: Textiles, Clothing & Care

Sewing buttons, mending tears, laundry, and wardrobe basics. The skills that keep you dressed well without spending more than you need to.

**What It Teaches:**
- Hand sewing basics: threading a needle, running stitch, backstitch
- Sewing on buttons, hooks, and snaps
- Mending tears, patching holes, repairing seams
- Laundry: sorting, washing, drying, folding, ironing
- Reading care labels and understanding fabric types
- Stain treatment and garment care
- Wardrobe management: what you have, what you need, what to let go
- Sustainable fashion awareness: quality over quantity, repair over replace

**Why It Matters:**
The average American throws away 81 pounds of clothing per year. Much of it could have been repaired, cared for better, or never purchased in the first place. Basic textile skills -- sewing a button, mending a tear, treating a stain, doing laundry properly -- save money, reduce waste, and give you confidence that your appearance is in your own hands.

**Key Learning Outcomes:**
- Thread a needle and perform basic hand stitches
- Sew on a button, hook, or snap securely
- Mend a tear or hole using appropriate technique
- Complete the full laundry process from sorting through folding
- Read and follow care labels
- Treat common stains before washing
- Evaluate clothing quality and make informed purchasing decisions
- Understand the environmental impact of textile consumption

**Interactive Elements:**
- Button sewing race: timed challenge to sew a button securely
- Mending workshop: bring damaged garments and repair them together
- Laundry lab: sort, wash, dry, and fold a mixed load correctly
- Care label scavenger hunt: decode labels on garments
- Wardrobe audit: inventory what you have and identify gaps
- Sustainable fashion discussion: cost-per-wear analysis

**Technical Implementation Notes:**
Sewing activities use real needles and thread (with age-appropriate safety guidance). Younger learners may use blunt needles or lacing cards. Machine sewing is introduced in middle school with supervision. All activities adapt for motor skill differences.

### Module 3: Home Care & Organization

Cleaning routines, organizing spaces, and basic maintenance. The systems that keep a living space functional, healthy, and comfortable without consuming all your time.

**What It Teaches:**
- Cleaning different surfaces (counters, floors, glass, bathroom, kitchen)
- Understanding cleaning products and their purposes
- Developing cleaning routines and schedules
- Organizing spaces: everything has a place, systems over perfection
- Decluttering: deciding what to keep, donate, discard, repair
- Basic home maintenance: changing filters, tightening hardware, unclogging drains
- Seasonal tasks: deep cleaning, weatherizing, organizing
- Shared living: dividing responsibilities, communication, respect

**Why It Matters:**
A clean, organized living space directly affects mental health, physical health, productivity, and quality of life. It does not need to be perfect -- it needs to be functional. The difference between a chaotic living space and a functional one is not personality or virtue. It is systems: knowing what to clean, how to clean it, how often, and having a place for everything.

**Key Learning Outcomes:**
- Clean different rooms and surfaces using appropriate methods and products
- Develop and follow a regular cleaning routine
- Organize personal and shared spaces using practical systems
- Perform basic home maintenance tasks (changing light bulbs, tightening hardware, unclogging drains)
- Create and follow a seasonal maintenance calendar
- Communicate about shared living responsibilities
- Understand cleaning product safety

**Interactive Elements:**
- Room deep clean challenge: clean a room from ceiling to floor in a time limit
- Organization systems workshop: design a storage system for a real space
- Cleaning product investigation: what works, what is safe, what is green
- Maintenance skills stations: practice tightening, changing, unclogging
- Chore chart creator: design a shared responsibility system
- Seasonal cleaning calendar: plan deep cleaning and maintenance tasks

**Technical Implementation Notes:**
Cleaning activities use real cleaning products with safety guidance appropriate to age. Younger learners use water and mild soap. Middle schoolers learn about product categories. High schoolers use the full range. All activities include environmental considerations (green cleaning options).

### Module 4: Money Management

Budgeting, saving, smart shopping, avoiding debt, and financial planning. The financial literacy that transforms money from a source of stress to a tool for building the life you want.

**What It Teaches:**
- What money is and how it works
- Budgeting: income, expenses, saving, and the math that connects them
- Saving habits: emergency funds, goal-based saving, compound interest
- Smart shopping: price comparison, value assessment, resisting marketing
- Credit and debt: how they work, when they help, when they harm
- Insurance basics: what it is, what you need, how to compare
- Taxes: what they are, how they work, what you need to do
- Financial planning: goals, timelines, and the power of starting early

**Why It Matters:**
Financial stress is the leading cause of relationship conflict and a major contributor to mental health problems. Yet most people receive no formal financial education. They learn by trial and error -- often expensive error. Understanding budgeting, saving, credit, and planning is not complicated. It is straightforward math combined with good habits. But you have to be taught.

**Key Learning Outcomes:**
- Create and maintain a personal budget
- Develop and practice saving habits
- Compare prices and assess value before purchasing
- Understand how credit and interest work
- Identify and avoid predatory financial products
- Plan financially for short-term and long-term goals
- Understand basic tax obligations and processes
- Make informed financial decisions based on values and goals

**Interactive Elements:**
- Budget simulation game: manage a monthly budget with real-world scenarios
- Grocery price comparison: find best value across brands and stores
- Savings calculator: visualize compound interest over time
- Credit card simulation: see how minimum payments extend debt
- Insurance comparison workshop: evaluate different coverage options
- Financial goal setting: create a timeline and plan for a personal goal
- Tax basics workshop: fill out a simplified tax form

**Technical Implementation Notes:**
Financial activities use simulated money and scenarios for younger learners, transitioning to real budgets and financial tools for high schoolers. All activities avoid judgment about family financial situations. Include resources for financial hardship.

### Module 5: Self-Sufficiency & Practical Skills

Basic repairs, gardening, preserving, and emergency readiness. The mindset and skills of resourcefulness -- seeing possibilities rather than problems.

**What It Teaches:**
- Basic tool use: hammer, screwdriver, pliers, measuring tape, level
- Simple repairs: tightening, replacing, patching, unclogging
- When to DIY and when to call a professional
- Container and small-space gardening
- Food preservation basics: freezing, drying, simple canning
- Emergency preparedness: kit assembly, planning, communication
- Bicycle and vehicle basic maintenance
- Resourcefulness: repair vs. replace, creative reuse, making do

**Why It Matters:**
Self-sufficiency is not about doing everything yourself. It is about having the capability to handle common problems, make informed decisions about what needs professional help, and approach challenges with confidence rather than helplessness. A person who can tighten a loose screw, grow a tomato, assemble an emergency kit, and evaluate whether a repair is DIY or professional -- that person has options. Options reduce stress and build confidence.

**Key Learning Outcomes:**
- Identify and safely use basic household tools
- Perform simple repairs appropriate to age and context
- Evaluate repair vs. replace decisions based on cost, time, and skill
- Grow food in available space (container, window, garden, community plot)
- Preserve food using basic methods (freezing, drying)
- Assemble and maintain an emergency preparedness kit
- Develop a problem-solving mindset that asks "can I fix this?" before "where do I buy a new one?"

**Interactive Elements:**
- Fix-it workshop: bring broken items and repair them together
- Tool identification and use stations: practice with real tools on safe tasks
- Container garden project: plant, grow, and harvest something edible
- Emergency kit assembly: build a kit for your home or car
- Repair vs. replace debate: evaluate real scenarios and make decisions
- Resourcefulness challenge: solve a problem using only what you have

**Technical Implementation Notes:**
Tool activities require age-appropriate safety. Younger learners use plastic or supervised tools. Middle schoolers use real tools with guidance. High schoolers use tools independently. Gardening activities adapt for available space (windowsill to backyard). Emergency preparedness avoids fear-based messaging.

## Assessment Framework

### Philosophy

Assessment in DOMESTIC-101 evaluates practical competency: can you actually DO it? Not just describe it -- do it. Can you cook a meal? Can you sew a button that stays on? Can you create and follow a budget? Can you clean a bathroom? Can you fix a leaky faucet?

This is skills-based assessment. The proof is in the doing.

**What we assess:**
- Practical skill execution (can you do the thing?)
- Safety awareness (do you do it safely?)
- Planning and organization (can you plan ahead?)
- Problem-solving (can you figure out what to do when something goes wrong?)
- Growth over time (are you getting better?)
- Application to real life (do you use these skills outside of class?)

**What we do not assess:**
- Perfection (a slightly crooked hem that holds is a success)
- Speed (competency matters more than quickness)
- Conformity to one "right way" (there are many ways to cook an egg)
- Home circumstances (we assess skill, not privilege)
- Gender-based expectations (everyone learns everything)

### Rubric Overview

- **Beginning:** Learning about the skill, can identify what needs doing, needs significant guidance
- **Developing:** Can perform the skill with guidance, understands the process, building confidence
- **Proficient:** Can perform the skill independently, makes good decisions, applies skills to real life
- **Advanced:** Teaches others, innovates, integrates skills across domains, leads

See `DOMESTIC-101-assessment.md` for the complete rubric.

## Parent & Family Guidance

### Why This Matters for Your Family

Your child is learning the skills that make independent adult life possible. These are the skills that reduce stress, save money, improve health, and build confidence. Every skill your child learns now is one they will not have to scramble to learn later.

### How to Support Learning at Home

**Cooking:**
- Cook together. Let your child help at whatever level they can.
- Follow recipes together. Let your child read the instructions.
- Let your child plan a meal. Help them shop for it and prepare it.
- Celebrate effort, not perfection. A slightly burned pancake is still a success.
- Share your family's food traditions and recipes.

**Textiles & Clothing:**
- Teach your child to do laundry. Start with sorting and folding.
- When a button falls off, repair it together instead of discarding the garment.
- Show your child how to read care labels.
- Discuss clothing purchases: quality, care, need vs. want.

**Household:**
- Include your child in cleaning routines. Assign age-appropriate tasks.
- Model organization: show how you decide where things go.
- When something needs simple maintenance, involve your child.
- Discuss how household responsibilities are shared in your family.

**Money:**
- Talk about money openly. Explain budgeting decisions.
- Give your child opportunities to manage small amounts of money.
- When shopping, discuss price comparison and value.
- Help your child set savings goals and track progress.

**Self-Sufficiency:**
- When something breaks, try to fix it together before replacing it.
- Garden together, even if it is a single herb plant on a windowsill.
- Build an emergency kit together and discuss your family plan.
- Encourage your child to try before asking for help.

### Gender-Neutral Approach

DOMESTIC-101 teaches every skill to every learner regardless of gender. We ask families to reinforce this at home:

- Both boys and girls cook, sew, clean, budget, and repair.
- Domestic competency is not gendered -- it is universal.
- Avoid phrases like "that's women's work" or "boys don't sew."
- Model shared responsibility in your household.
- Celebrate competency in all domains for all people.

### Family Circumstances

We recognize that families have different circumstances:

- **Different living situations:** Apartment, house, shared housing, mobile home. All are valid. Skills adapt to space.
- **Different financial situations:** Budget activities accommodate all income levels. No shame, no assumptions.
- **Different cultural backgrounds:** Every culture has domestic wisdom. We celebrate all traditions.
- **Different family structures:** Single parents, two parents, grandparents, guardians, foster families. All families matter.
- **Different abilities:** Activities adapt for physical, sensory, and cognitive differences.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Learn By Doing

DOMESTIC-101 is fundamentally hands-on. You cannot learn to cook by reading about cooking. You cannot learn to sew by watching videos. You learn by doing -- making mistakes, figuring out what went wrong, and trying again.

Every module emphasizes:
1. **Demonstration:** See the skill performed (by teacher, video, or peer)
2. **Guided practice:** Try it with support and feedback
3. **Independent practice:** Do it yourself
4. **Application:** Use the skill in a real-life context
5. **Reflection:** What worked? What would you do differently?

### Scaffold for Success

Skills build on each other. You do not start with a five-course meal -- you start with boiling water. You do not start with tailoring -- you start with a button. Every module has a clear progression from simple to complex, with each step building on the last.

### Celebrate Competency

The goal is not perfection. It is competency. A meal that is slightly over-seasoned but safely prepared and nutritious is a success. A mended tear that is visible but holds is a success. A budget that is approximately correct but regularly used is a success. We celebrate doing over knowing, trying over avoiding, and progress over perfection.

### Connect to Real Life

Every skill learned in DOMESTIC-101 should be used in real life. This is not abstract knowledge -- it is practical capability. Homework is not a worksheet. Homework is cooking dinner, doing laundry, creating a budget, or fixing something that is broken. The classroom extends to the kitchen, the laundry room, the hardware store, and the garden.

### Culturally Responsive

Every culture has domestic wisdom. Every family has traditions around food, clothing, home, money, and resourcefulness. DOMESTIC-101 celebrates this diversity and invites learners to share their family's knowledge. There is no single "right way" to keep house -- there are many effective approaches informed by different cultures, climates, and circumstances.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### "Isn't home economics old-fashioned?"

The name may sound old-fashioned, but the skills are timeless. Cooking, cleaning, budgeting, mending, and maintaining a household are things every person needs to do for their entire life. What is old-fashioned is the idea that only certain people need these skills. DOMESTIC-101 is for everyone.

### "Shouldn't parents teach this?"

Ideally, yes. And many do. But many parents themselves lack these skills -- they grew up during the same period when home economics disappeared from schools. DOMESTIC-101 supports family learning. Parents and children can learn together.

### "Is this just for girls?"

Absolutely not. DOMESTIC-101 is explicitly gender-neutral. Every skill is taught to every learner. Boys cook. Girls use tools. Everyone budgets, cleans, sews, and plans. Domestic competency is a human requirement, not a gender role.

### "My child already knows how to cook / clean / budget."

Wonderful! DOMESTIC-101 provides structure and depth. Even experienced learners often have gaps (great cook but terrible with money, tidy room but cannot sew a button). The modular structure lets learners focus where they need to grow.

### "We cannot afford cooking ingredients / sewing supplies / tools."

DOMESTIC-101 uses common household materials wherever possible. Many activities require nothing beyond what is already in most homes. For activities that require materials, we provide alternatives and adaptations. No learner should be excluded due to cost.

### "How does this fit with academic standards?"

DOMESTIC-101 integrates naturally with math (budgeting, measurement, comparison shopping), science (food safety, nutrition, chemistry of cleaning), literacy (reading recipes, instructions, labels), and social studies (economics, cultural studies, community). It is inherently interdisciplinary.

### "What about food allergies and dietary restrictions?"

All cooking activities include allergen awareness and alternative ingredients. DOMESTIC-101 teaches safe food handling including allergen management. Learners with specific allergies or dietary restrictions (religious, ethical, medical) are accommodated in every activity.

### "Is this pack a full curriculum?"

DOMESTIC-101 can be used as a standalone curriculum (semester or year-long course) or integrated into existing classes. Individual modules and activities work well as supplements to science, math, health, or life skills courses.

## Conclusion

Home economics is not a relic. It is a lifeline.

Every skill in DOMESTIC-101 -- cooking, sewing, cleaning, budgeting, repairing -- is something that every person will need, every day, for the rest of their life. These skills save money, improve health, reduce waste, build confidence, and create independence. They are the foundation on which everything else in adult life rests.

DOMESTIC-101 brings these skills back into education where they belong -- not as gendered training, not as elective filler, but as essential knowledge for every human being. The goal is not to create perfect homemakers. The goal is to create capable, resourceful, independent people who can take care of themselves and contribute to their communities.

Cook a meal. Sew a button. Clean a room. Make a budget. Fix what is broken. These are not small things. These are the skills that make a life.
