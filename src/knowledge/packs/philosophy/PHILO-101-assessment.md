# PHILO-101: Philosophy -- Assessment Framework

This assessment framework evaluates philosophical thinking as a *process*, not a body of facts to memorize. Philosophy values the quality of reasoning, the depth of questioning, the willingness to consider other perspectives, and the intellectual honesty to follow arguments wherever they lead. Assessment in philosophy never penalizes a student for reaching an unpopular conclusion, only for reasoning poorly.

---

## Beginning

At the Beginning level, the learner is starting to develop philosophical awareness. They can identify that some questions are different from factual questions, and they are beginning to give simple reasons for their beliefs.

**Indicators:**
- Can identify a philosophical question when presented with one ("Is it ever okay to lie?" vs. "What is the capital of France?")
- Offers simple reasons for beliefs: "I think X because Y" (even if the reasoning is basic)
- Shows willingness to listen to others' viewpoints, even if not yet able to engage with them
- Can identify obvious unfairness in simple scenarios
- Recognizes that people sometimes disagree about what is right or beautiful
- Can name basic emotions and connect them to simple ethical situations (happy, sad, fair, unfair)

**Assessment Methods:**
- Observation during Question of the Day circles: Does the student contribute? Do they give reasons?
- Simple written or drawn responses to philosophical prompts
- Participation in fairness discussions with concrete scenarios
- Wonder Wall contributions: Is the student posting genuine questions?

---

## Developing

At the Developing level, the learner is building philosophical skills. They can construct basic arguments, consider more than one perspective, and engage with simple logical and ethical reasoning.

**Indicators:**
- Constructs basic arguments with identifiable premises and conclusions
- Considers at least two perspectives on a philosophical question before settling on a view
- Can spot some logical fallacies in obvious examples (e.g., ad hominem attacks in arguments)
- Applies simple ethical reasoning to dilemmas: "It would be wrong because..." with a reason beyond "because I said so"
- Distinguishes between opinions, beliefs, and claims supported by evidence
- Asks follow-up questions during discussions: "What do you mean?" "Can you give an example?"
- Recognizes that perception can be unreliable (optical illusions, tricks of memory)
- Can articulate basic aesthetic preferences with reasons: "I think this is beautiful because..."

**Assessment Methods:**
- Argument construction exercises: Can the student build a simple valid argument?
- Fallacy identification in curated examples (advertisements, movie dialogue)
- Written responses to ethical dilemmas showing multiple perspectives considered
- Philosophical question journal entries showing increasing depth over time
- Participation in structured discussions with evidence of building on others' ideas

---

## Proficient

At the Proficient level, the learner can engage in sustained philosophical inquiry. They construct multi-step arguments, apply ethical frameworks, evaluate evidence claims, and facilitate philosophical discussions.

**Indicators:**
- Constructs multi-step arguments and identifies weaknesses in others' arguments
- Applies ethical frameworks (consequentialism, deontology, virtue ethics) to real-world dilemmas and compares results
- Evaluates sources of knowledge critically: distinguishes testimony, perception, reasoning, and authority
- Uses skepticism constructively to test and strengthen beliefs rather than to dismiss all claims
- Facilitates philosophical discussions by asking probing questions and managing multiple perspectives
- Identifies logical fallacies in complex, real-world contexts (political speeches, news articles, social media)
- Gives reasoned aesthetic judgments that go beyond personal preference
- Engages with philosophical texts and extracts arguments from them
- Demonstrates intellectual humility: willingly changes position when presented with better reasoning
- Connects philosophical concepts across modules (e.g., how epistemology affects ethical reasoning)

**Assessment Methods:**
- Socratic seminar facilitation: Does the student guide inquiry effectively?
- Philosophical essays with clear argument structure and consideration of objections
- Applied ethics case analyses using multiple frameworks
- Evidence evaluation exercises: Can the student trace knowledge claims to their foundations?
- Cross-module projects connecting logic, ethics, and epistemology
- Peer assessment of argument quality in group discussions
- Philosophical portfolio showing growth in reasoning quality over time

---

## Advanced

At the Advanced level, the learner is an independent philosophical thinker. They construct original arguments, engage with primary texts, teach philosophical thinking to others, and apply philosophical reasoning to novel and complex situations.

**Indicators:**
- Constructs original philosophical arguments on self-chosen topics
- Engages critically with primary philosophical texts from multiple traditions
- Identifies unstated assumptions in arguments and evaluates their impact
- Applies formal logic (symbolic logic, truth tables) to evaluate argument validity
- Synthesizes multiple ethical frameworks to develop nuanced positions on complex issues
- Understands meta-philosophical questions: What is philosophy? What are its methods and limits?
- Teaches philosophical thinking effectively to younger or less experienced learners
- Engages with contemporary philosophical debates (AI consciousness, environmental ethics, social justice)
- Demonstrates intellectual courage: defends unpopular positions with strong reasoning
- Applies philosophical thinking spontaneously to new situations outside the classroom
- Appreciates diverse philosophical traditions (Western, Eastern, African, Indigenous) and can compare their approaches

**Assessment Methods:**
- Original philosophical inquiry paper: student pursues a self-chosen question through research and argumentation
- Primary text analysis and critique from multiple philosophical traditions
- Philosophical mentoring: teach a younger student philosophical thinking and document the process
- Participation in formal philosophical debates with structured argumentation
- Meta-philosophical reflection: What is the value of philosophy? How has your thinking changed?
- Advanced ethical analysis of contemporary issues with nuanced, multi-framework reasoning
- Symbolic logic proofs and formal argument evaluation

## Formative Assessment

Formative assessment in philosophy happens continuously through observation, conversation, and reflection. The goal is to help learners see their own growth in reasoning quality.

### Discussion Observation

During Socratic seminars and group discussions, observe:
- Does the student ask probing questions or only surface-level ones?
- Do they build on others' ideas or just wait for their turn to speak?
- Do they give reasons for their views or just state preferences?
- Do they consider counterarguments?
- Do they change their mind when presented with better reasoning?
- Do they demonstrate intellectual humility and curiosity?

### Philosophical Question Journal

Students maintain an ongoing journal of philosophical questions, reflections, and evolving arguments. Assess for:
- Depth of questions over time (moving from surface to structural)
- Quality of reasoning in reflections (premises, evidence, consideration of alternatives)
- Evidence of intellectual growth (changing views based on new reasoning, not just new information)
- Connections across philosophical domains (linking ethics to epistemology, logic to aesthetics)

### Argument Feedback

When students construct arguments (written or verbal), provide feedback on:
- Clarity of premises and conclusion
- Logical validity of the reasoning
- Consideration of objections and counterarguments
- Quality of evidence or examples offered
- Honesty about assumptions and limitations

---

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment in philosophy evaluates a learner's overall capacity for philosophical thinking, not their memorization of philosophical facts or positions.

### Philosophical Portfolio

A curated collection of the student's best philosophical work, including:
- Argument analyses (identifying and evaluating arguments in texts and media)
- Original arguments on self-chosen topics
- Ethical dilemma responses showing framework application
- Socratic seminar transcripts or reflections
- Philosophical question journal highlights
- Creative philosophical work (stories, art, thought experiments)

**Evaluation criteria:** Reasoning quality, depth of inquiry, growth over time, breadth of philosophical engagement, intellectual honesty.

### Applied Ethics Project

Students analyze a real-world ethical issue using the philosophical tools from the pack:
- Identify the moral question and why it matters
- Apply at least two ethical frameworks to the issue
- Consider multiple stakeholder perspectives
- Acknowledge limitations and uncertainties in their reasoning
- Arrive at a reasoned position and defend it against objections

**Evaluation criteria:** Framework application, perspective-taking, argument quality, intellectual humility, practical relevance.

### Socratic Seminar Facilitation

Students facilitate a philosophical discussion and are assessed on:
- Quality of opening question and follow-up questions
- Ability to draw out multiple perspectives without imposing their own
- Management of group dynamics (ensuring all voices are heard)
- Capacity to synthesize and advance the discussion
- Demonstration of the philosophical virtues: humility, curiosity, honesty, courage

---

## Portfolio Suggestions

A strong philosophical portfolio might include:

1. **Best Question:** The most interesting philosophical question the student has asked, with a reflection on why it matters
2. **Strongest Argument:** The best argument the student has constructed, with identified premises, evidence, and consideration of objections
3. **Ethical Analysis:** A case study analyzed using multiple ethical frameworks
4. **Changed Mind:** A reflection on a time when philosophical reasoning changed the student's view on something important
5. **Philosophical Dialogue:** A transcript or reflection from a Socratic seminar showing growth in discussion skills
6. **Creative Philosophy:** A story, artwork, or thought experiment that explores a philosophical theme
7. **Cross-Cultural Engagement:** An exploration of a philosophical idea from a tradition different from the student's own
8. **Meta-Reflection:** A reflection on what philosophy has taught the student about their own thinking
