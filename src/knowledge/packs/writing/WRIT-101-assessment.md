# WRIT-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

Assessment in WRIT-101 focuses on growth in reading and writing ability, not on literary terminology or test-taking skills. Progress is measured by the quality of thinking: Can the learner read thoughtfully, think analytically, and express ideas clearly? Can they revise meaningfully? Are they developing confidence and voice as both readers and writers?

A learner who asks better questions, designs fairer interpretations, reads more complex texts, revises more substantially, and writes with increasing clarity and voice is making progress—regardless of whether they can define "protagonist" or "metaphor."

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing initial engagement with texts and writing. They notice things in stories, ask simple questions, and are learning that writing communicates to others.

### Reading
- Listens to and enjoys stories and picture books
- Asks simple questions about what happened
- Notices obvious features (characters, problems, endings)
- Recalls key events from texts read aloud
- Identifies favorite books and authors

### Writing
- Tells stories aloud with enthusiasm
- Writes or draws simple ideas
- Understands that writing is different from drawing (conveys meaning with words)
- Attempts to write simple stories or captions
- Enjoys seeing their words become books

### Engagement & Thinking
- Participates in story discussions
- Shows emotions when reading (laughs at humor, cries at sad parts)
- Wants to know what happens next
- Interested in certain topics or characters

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building systematic approaches to reading and writing. They can identify story elements, answer questions about texts, and write longer pieces with guidance.

### Reading
- Reads independently with increasing fluency
- Identifies characters, settings, major events
- Answers literal comprehension questions consistently
- Makes simple predictions ("I think she'll come back because...")
- Notices and comments on interesting language or descriptions
- Begins to make connections between texts

### Writing
- Writes narratives with clear beginning, middle, and end
- Uses dialogue to show character and story
- Revises with guidance (adds details, fixes confusing parts)
- Writes in multiple forms (stories, poems, friendly letters)
- Attempts different endings or variations
- Edits for mechanics with support

### Engagement & Thinking
- Participates in discussions, sometimes building on others' ideas
- Asks questions about why characters act as they do
- Notices patterns or connections in stories
- Shows growing confidence in writing attempts

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level demonstrate independent analytical thinking about texts and increasing craft in writing. They can support interpretations with evidence, revise substantially, and develop personal voice.

### Reading
- Reads texts of increasing complexity with comprehension
- Analyzes character development and motivation
- Traces themes across stories
- Discusses how point of view affects reader understanding
- Recognizes symbolism and figurative language
- Supports interpretations with specific textual evidence
- Engages respectfully with multiple interpretations
- Makes connections across texts and to personal experience
- Asks sophisticated questions about author's choices

### Writing
- Writes across multiple forms with clear purpose
- Develops characters with depth and consistency
- Uses dialogue naturally to reveal character
- Creates vivid imagery and sensory detail
- Demonstrates personal voice (writing sounds like them, not generic)
- Revises substantially based on feedback
- Edits for clarity and style, not just mechanics
- Organizes ideas logically and clearly
- Completes multi-draft writing projects with evidence of growth

### Thinking & Analysis
- Moves beyond plot summary to interpretation
- Supports claims with specific evidence
- Recognizes that interpretation requires reasoning, not just feeling
- Appreciates multiple valid readings of texts
- Asks and answers their own questions while reading
- Shows curiosity about how writers make choices

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level demonstrate mastery of close reading, analytical thinking, and sophisticated writing. They read like writers, think like literary critics, and write with authority and grace.

### Reading
- Reads challenging texts independently and analytically
- Conducts literary analysis exploring multiple dimensions simultaneously
- Recognizes how historical/cultural context shapes texts
- Evaluates published interpretations and scholarship
- Applies multiple interpretive frameworks to deepen understanding
- Identifies and discusses authorial intent and technique
- Reads across genres, time periods, and cultures fluently
- Teaches reading skills and insights to others

### Writing
- Writes with distinctive, mature voice across multiple forms
- Constructs sophisticated arguments about texts with compelling evidence
- Uses language precisely and powerfully
- Integrates research and outside ideas seamlessly
- Revises extensively, rethinking rather than just refining
- Recognizes and breaks conventions intentionally for effect
- Produces writing suitable for publication or professional contexts
- Mentors other writers

### Thinking & Analysis
- Recognizes and explores ambiguity and complexity
- Understands interpretation as constructed, not discovered
- Considers author's choices within historical/cultural context
- Engages with texts as part of ongoing cultural conversations
- Synthesizes ideas from multiple texts and sources
- Contributes original insights to literary discourse

---

## Formative Assessment

Formative assessment occurs during learning and provides feedback that guides improvement.

### Reading Observations

Observe learners during independent reading and discussions:
- Do they ask genuine questions while reading?
- Can they locate specific evidence for their ideas?
- Do they listen to and build on classmates' interpretations?
- Do they change their thinking when presented with new evidence?
- Are they reading more fluently, reading longer books, tackling more complex texts?

### Annotation Review

Look at what learners mark and write on texts:
- Are annotations questions, observations, or just highlighting?
- Do annotations show genuine curiosity?
- Are they noticing details about craft (word choice, structure)?
- Are annotations becoming more sophisticated over time?

### Discussion Participation

During literature discussions and seminars:
- Do learners contribute ideas, not just answer teacher questions?
- Can they support their ideas with specific textual evidence?
- Do they listen and respond to others?
- Do they ask follow-up questions?
- Are they moving from factual questions toward interpretive ones?

### Reading Response Journals

If learners keep reading journals or reading logs:
- Are they making personal connections?
- Are they asking questions while reading?
- Are they noticing craft and technique?
- Is their thinking becoming more sophisticated?
- Are entries substantive or superficial?

### Writing Observations

As learners write:
- Do they attempt revision, or just accept first drafts?
- Can they identify strengths and weaknesses in their own work?
- Do they respond to feedback seriously?
- Are they experimenting with new forms or techniques?
- Is their voice becoming more distinctive?

### Peer Feedback Quality

Notice how learners give and receive feedback:
- Are comments about communication, not just mechanics?
- Do they distinguish between what works and what needs work?
- Are they receptive to suggestions?
- Do they revise based on feedback?

---

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment evaluates cumulative learning at end of unit, module, or semester.

### Portfolio

A collection of work demonstrating growth:

**Required components:**
- Reading artifacts: annotations, discussion notes, response journals, analytical essays
- Writing artifacts: drafts and revision history, finished pieces in 3+ forms
- Reflective statement: articulating growth and goals

**Assessment focus:**
- What's the range of texts engaged with?
- How does reading comprehension and analysis deepen over time?
- How does writing skill and voice develop?
- What's the quality of revision and thoughtfulness?

### Investigation Portfolio (Major Project)

A collection specifically demonstrating module learning:

**Components:**
- 3-4 pieces in different genres (fiction, poetry, analysis, or response)
- Evidence of revision (showing earlier and later versions)
- Reflection on what the portfolio shows about growth
- Optional: teaching materials for a concept learned

### Literary Analysis Essay

A major assignment demonstrating analytical thinking:

**Assessment criteria:**
- **Thesis:** Arguable, specific, moving beyond obvious observations
- **Evidence:** Specific, relevant, sufficient support for claims
- **Reasoning:** Clear explanation of how evidence supports thesis
- **Organization:** Ideas flow logically, building toward understanding
- **Craft:** Clear, engaging writing appropriate for academic context
- **Revision:** Evidence of rethinking, not just proofreading

---

## Rubric: Analytical Thinking

This rubric evaluates the depth and quality of analytical thinking about texts, across all forms of response (discussion, writing, annotation).

### Beginning
- Identifies literal details from texts
- Recognizes obvious patterns
- Answers direct questions about plot and characters
- Generalizes without supporting evidence

### Developing
- Identifies key details and supports with some examples
- Recognizes patterns and asks why they matter
- Connects details to larger ideas
- Supports interpretations with specific textual evidence
- Considers more than one perspective

### Proficient
- Analyzes how specific details contribute to larger meanings
- Synthesizes multiple details into coherent interpretations
- Considers historical/cultural context in interpretation
- Evaluates strength of evidence and reasoning
- Recognizes and explores complexity and ambiguity
- Supports claims with substantial, specific evidence

### Advanced
- Constructs sophisticated analyses revealing new insights
- Evaluates own interpretations critically
- Synthesizes multiple interpretations and perspectives
- Applies multiple analytical frameworks flexibly
- Makes original connections and discoveries
- Uses evidence with precision and authority

---

## Rubric: Writing Craft

This rubric evaluates the quality of writing across multiple dimensions.

### Beginning
- Writing communicates basic ideas
- Sentences are mostly correct
- Ideas are present but may be disorganized
- Word choice is simple but generally appropriate

### Developing
- Writing is clear and organized
- Sentence variety is developing
- Ideas are connected with logical transitions
- Word choice is more precise; some vivid language
- Grammar and mechanics are mostly correct
- Voice is emerging but still somewhat generic

### Proficient
- Writing is clear, engaging, and well-organized
- Sentences are varied and fluent
- Ideas are fully developed with specific details
- Word choice is precise and sometimes vivid
- Grammar and mechanics are correct
- Voice is distinctive and appropriate for purpose
- Revision shows rethinking and improvement

### Advanced
- Writing is compelling, with sophisticated organization
- Sentences are varied, rhythmic, and purposeful
- Ideas are deeply developed with vivid, specific details
- Word choice is precise, powerful, and nuanced
- Grammar and mechanics are polished
- Voice is mature, distinctive, and authoritative
- Extensive revision shows mastery of the craft

---

## Portfolio Rubric

This rubric evaluates the portfolio as a whole, demonstrating growth and range.

### Beginning (Does Not Demonstrate Proficiency)
- Portfolio shows limited range of work
- Writing shows minimal revision or development
- Reflection is superficial or missing
- Growth over time is not evident

### Developing (Approaching Proficiency)
- Portfolio shows some range of forms and topics
- Some pieces show evidence of revision
- Reflection identifies some strengths and areas for growth
- Growth is visible in later pieces

### Proficient
- Portfolio shows substantial range of forms and complexity
- Most pieces show evidence of meaningful revision
- Reflection articulates specific growth and learning
- Portfolio demonstrates clear progression toward sophistication
- Pieces are generally well-executed

### Advanced
- Portfolio shows impressive range and ambition
- All pieces show evidence of thoughtful revision
- Reflection demonstrates sophisticated self-assessment
- Portfolio reveals distinctive voice and interests
- Pieces are polished and well-crafted
- Portfolio tells a story of growth and development

## Assessment at Different Grade Levels

### K-2 (Foundation)
- Emphasis on engagement, fluency, and confidence
- Assessment through observation and informal conversation
- Portfolio includes drawings, early writing, recording of oral storytelling
- Growth metrics: How much? How long? How fluent?

### 3-5 (Elementary)
- Emphasis on comprehension, expression, and emerging analysis
- Mix of informal observation and more structured assessments
- Portfolio includes written responses, stories, poems, annotations
- Growth metrics: Complexity of texts read, quality of responses, revision

### 6-8 (Middle School)
- Emphasis on analysis, voice, and feedback responsiveness
- Formal assessments include essays, projects, discussions, portfolio
- Portfolio shows range of forms and evidence of revision
- Growth metrics: Sophistication of analysis, development of voice, revision quality

### 9-12 (High School)
- Emphasis on sophisticated analysis, writing craft, and literary thinking
- Formal assessments include major essays, projects, seminars, publications
- Portfolio demonstrates mastery and distinctive voice
- Growth metrics: Complexity of thinking and writing, command of multiple forms

## Informal Assessment Strategies

Beyond formal assignments, notice:

**Reading growth:**
- Are they reading more? Longer books? More challenging texts?
- Do they make more connections? Ask deeper questions?
- Are they reading across genres and time periods?

**Writing growth:**
- Do they write more? More varied forms?
- Is their voice becoming more distinctive?
- Do they revise more extensively?

**Confidence growth:**
- Are they willing to take risks (write poetry, tackle classics, try new genres)?
- Do they share work more willingly?
- Do they defend interpretations respectfully?

**Community growth:**
- Do they listen to and build on classmates' ideas?
- Do they give quality feedback?
- Do they contribute to a culture where reading and writing matter?

## Grading Considerations

### What to Grade / Assess
- Quality of thinking (analysis, interpretation)
- Writing craft (clarity, voice, organization)
- Participation in discussions and workshops
- Revision and response to feedback
- Growth over time (not just current performance)

### What NOT to Grade
- Personal taste (whether you like their favorite book)
- Speed of reading (some readers are slower, no less thoughtful)
- Grammar mechanics alone (teach mechanics, but don't grade writing solely on correctness)
- Whether they agree with the teacher's interpretation

### Grading Approaches
- **Standards-based:** Assess against rubric levels (Beginning/Developing/Proficient/Advanced)
- **Holistic:** Grade overall quality rather than point-by-point
- **Revision-focused:** Allow revision for grade improvement (effort = growth = higher grade)
- **Portfolio:** Assess portfolio as a whole showing cumulative learning
- **Minimal grading:** Focus on feedback and discussion rather than grades

### Reporting Progress
- Clear descriptions of what learner can do
- Specific examples of growth
- Next steps for continued development
- Honest assessment of strengths and challenges

## Self-Assessment & Reflection

Learners should regularly reflect on their learning:

**Reading reflection prompts:**
- What kinds of books do you love? What do they have in common?
- How has your reading changed this year?
- What's a question you asked while reading that made you think?
- How do you decide if an interpretation is valid?

**Writing reflection prompts:**
- What piece best shows who you are as a writer?
- How has your writing voice changed?
- What's been your biggest challenge as a writer? How are you addressing it?
- What are you proud of in your writing?

**Growth reflection prompts:**
- What have you learned about reading?
- What have you learned about writing?
- How have you changed as a reader/writer?
- What's next for you?

---

## Conclusion

Assessment in WRIT-101 serves learning, not sorting. The goal is to help learners recognize their growth, identify their strengths, and know what to work on next. Regular, specific feedback focused on clarity and impact matters more than grades. Revision is central—learners should revise based on feedback and assessment, understanding that improvement requires effort and iteration.

Learners who complete WRIT-101 should be confident readers who think deeply about texts, clear writers who revise purposefully, and engaged participants in literary community.
