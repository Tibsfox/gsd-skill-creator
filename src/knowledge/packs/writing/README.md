# WRIT-101: Literature & Creative Writing

## Quick Start

Welcome to WRIT-101 — a foundational knowledge pack for developing strong reading and writing skills through engagement with literature and creative expression.

### What This Pack Covers

Five integrated modules across K-College:

1. **Reading as Discovery** — Close reading, asking real questions, understanding how authors construct meaning
2. **Story & Narrative** — Character, point of view, conflict, dialogue, imagery, and pacing
3. **Poetry & Language** — Forms, imagery, figurative language, sound, and authentic voice
4. **The Writing Process** — Drafting, feedback, revision, editing, and developing personal voice
5. **Literary Analysis & Critical Response** — Moving beyond plot summary to interpret meaning

### For Learners

- Start with **Reading as Discovery** (Module 1) to develop close reading habits
- Then explore **Story & Narrative** (Module 2) and **Poetry & Language** (Module 3) in any order
- Integrate **The Writing Process** (Module 4) throughout — writing and reading reinforce each other
- Advance to **Literary Analysis & Critical Response** (Module 5) as you develop analytical skills

Each module includes concrete activities: annotation practices, writing workshops, poetry experiments, discussions, and portfolio development.

### For Teachers

This pack is designed for integration into existing language arts instruction or as the foundation for a standalone literature/writing course. Activities are scaffolded for K-College progression and can be adapted for your specific learners and texts.

**To use this pack:**

1. Select the appropriate module(s) for your learners' level
2. Choose activities that fit your curriculum and available time
3. Use the assessment framework to evaluate growth in reading and writing ability
4. Build a portfolio showing development over time
5. Participate in peer workshops and discussions — writing and reading improve together

### For Parents/Mentors

Support your child's reading and writing development at home:

- **Read together** — listen to audiobooks, discuss what you're reading, let them see you reading for pleasure
- **Write together** — journals, letters, stories, emails — all build writing fluency
- **Provide authentic audience** — family members to read their work, friends to discuss their ideas
- **Value process over perfection** — encourage drafting, revision, and sharing unfinished work
- **Find books they love** — graphic novels, mysteries, fantasy, poetry — all develop reading skills

## Learning Outcomes

By engaging with WRIT-101, learners will:

- **Read closely and analytically** — asking genuine questions, attending to authorial choices, supporting interpretations with evidence
- **Understand narrative craft** — recognizing how character, point of view, conflict, and other elements shape reader experience
- **Appreciate poetry and language play** — in diverse forms and across cultures
- **Write authentically across forms** — developing personal voice through regular practice and meaningful revision
- **Analyze literature deeply** — moving beyond plot summary to interpret meaning within historical and cultural context

## Getting Started with Activities

Each module includes 6+ concrete activities. Here are examples to get started:

### Reading as Discovery
- **Annotation Station** — Learn close reading by marking up a text, writing questions, and discussing observations
- **Genre Detective** — Compare story elements across different genres (fantasy, realistic fiction, historical fiction)

### Story & Narrative
- **Flash Fiction Challenge** — Write a complete story in 500 words, revise based on feedback
- **Character Interview** — Choose a character from a book, write their responses to questions revealing motivation and personality

### Poetry & Language
- **Found Poetry** — Create poems using words from newspapers, magazines, or other texts
- **Haiku Nature Walk** — Observe nature closely, write haiku capturing specific moments and sensations

### The Writing Process
- **Peer Workshop Circle** — Share drafts with classmates, give feedback, revise based on what you hear
- **Portfolio Development** — Collect your writing over time, reflect on growth and choices

### Literary Analysis & Critical Response
- **Theme Tracker** — Read a novel, keep a journal tracking how themes develop throughout the text
- **Comparative Essay** — Read two texts, analyze how they address similar themes differently

## Assessment & Portfolios

Assessment in WRIT-101 focuses on growth, not on test scores. A portfolio should include:

- **Reading artifacts:** Annotation samples, discussion notes, literary analysis essays
- **Writing artifacts:** Drafts and revision history, finished pieces in multiple forms, reflection on process
- **Growth evidence:** Earlier and later work showing increased sophistication

The assessment framework uses four rubric levels:
- **Beginning:** Developing foundational awareness
- **Developing:** Building systematic approaches
- **Proficient:** Demonstrating independent thinking
- **Advanced:** Mastery and teaching others

## Implementation Notes

### Time Commitment

- **Foundation (K-2):** 40-60 hours total (approximately 30 min/week for a full year)
- **Elementary (3-5):** 60-90 hours total (approximately 45 min/week for a full year)
- **Middle School (6-8):** 90-130 hours total (approximately 60-90 min/week for a full year)
- **High School (9-12):** 130-180 hours total (dedicated literature/writing course)

These are guidelines; adjust based on your learners' needs and interests.

### Materials Needed

Minimal materials required:
- Access to books (library, free online resources)
- Paper and writing implements
- Optional: computers for typing and collaboration
- Optional: audio equipment for poetry readings and discussions

### Texts to Pair With WRIT-101

**Picture Books & Early Readers:** Where the Wild Things Are, Click, Clack, Moo, Charlotte's Web, Ramona series

**Elementary:** Percy Jackson, Esperanza Rising, The Tale of Despereaux, Pax

**Middle School:** The Outsiders, The Giver, A Wrinkle in Time, Wonder, Monster

**High School:** To Kill a Mockingbird, 1984, The Great Gatsby, Their Eyes Were Watching God, contemporary novels and poetry
