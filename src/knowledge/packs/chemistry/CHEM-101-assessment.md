# CHEM-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

This assessment framework evaluates chemical understanding through the ability to observe, reason about, and explain the material world. Progress is measured by the quality of chemical thinking -- the ability to connect what is seen (macroscopic) to what is happening at the molecular level (microscopic), and to use that connection to predict, explain, and investigate. A learner who can explain why salt dissolves in water using molecular reasoning is making progress, regardless of whether they can write the chemical formula.

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing awareness of the material world. They notice that different materials have different properties, observe simple changes, and are building vocabulary for describing what they see.

**Matter & Properties:**
- Identifies materials as solid, liquid, or gas
- Describes materials using basic sensory properties (color, texture, hardness)
- Notices that mixing some things together causes changes (fizzing, color change)
- Sorts objects by a single property (hard/soft, rough/smooth)

**Atoms & Elements:**
- Understands that everything is made of tiny pieces too small to see
- Recognizes some common element names (gold, iron, oxygen, carbon)
- Knows that the periodic table lists all the elements
- Understands that different materials are made of different kinds of atoms

**Bonds & Structure:**
- Knows that atoms can stick together to form larger structures
- Identifies common substances by name (water, salt, sugar)
- Understands that mixing is different from combining chemically
- Recognizes that some materials dissolve and some do not

**Reactions & Energy:**
- Observes that some changes produce heat, light, or gas
- Distinguishes mixing (can separate) from reacting (new substance formed)
- Notices that cooking changes food in ways that cannot be undone
- Understands that fire requires something to burn

**Applied Chemistry:**
- Identifies chemistry in everyday life (cooking, cleaning, rusting)
- Knows that some substances are dangerous and require care
- Recognizes that materials can be natural or manufactured
- Shows curiosity about what things are made of

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building systematic understanding of chemical concepts. They observe with more structure, classify materials by multiple properties, understand basic atomic ideas, and can describe simple chemical reactions.

**Matter & Properties:**
- Measures properties systematically (mass, volume, temperature)
- Distinguishes physical changes from chemical changes with reasoning
- Classifies substances as mixtures or pure substances
- Designs simple separation procedures (filtering muddy water, evaporating salt water)

**Atoms & Elements:**
- Describes basic atomic structure (nucleus with protons and neutrons, electrons around it)
- Uses the periodic table to find element information (symbol, atomic number, group)
- Groups elements by type (metals, nonmetals, metalloids)
- Builds simple atomic models showing protons, neutrons, and electrons in shells

**Bonds & Structure:**
- Distinguishes ionic from covalent compounds by observable properties
- Builds simple molecular models using physical materials
- Understands that salt dissolves because water pulls apart the ions
- Knows that metals conduct electricity because electrons move freely

**Reactions & Energy:**
- Identifies common reaction indicators (gas production, color change, temperature change, precipitate)
- Understands conservation of mass conceptually ("atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed")
- Recognizes some reaction types (acid + base, combustion)
- Measures temperature changes during reactions

**Applied Chemistry:**
- Explains how a specific kitchen process works chemically (bread rising, rust forming)
- Identifies acids and bases in the household using indicators
- Understands that polymers are long chain molecules (plastic, rubber)
- Connects chemical concepts to one or two real-world applications

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level demonstrate independent chemical reasoning. They use atomic and molecular models to explain observations, predict reaction outcomes, and connect structure to properties systematically.

**Matter & Properties:**
- Uses particle models to explain state changes and dissolution
- Identifies unknown substances using systematic property testing
- Calculates density and uses it for substance identification
- Designs and conducts property investigations independently

**Atoms & Elements:**
- Predicts element behavior using periodic table trends (electronegativity, atomic radius, reactivity)
- Explains why elements in the same group behave similarly (same number of outer electrons)
- Draws electron configurations for the first 20 elements
- Connects electron arrangement to chemical bonding behavior

**Bonds & Structure:**
- Predicts bond type (ionic, covalent, metallic) from element positions on the periodic table
- Explains substance properties from molecular structure (why water is liquid, why diamond is hard)
- Uses VSEPR to predict simple molecular shapes (linear, bent, tetrahedral, pyramidal)
- Explains polarity and its effect on solubility ("like dissolves like")

**Reactions & Energy:**
- Balances chemical equations for common reaction types
- Classifies reactions by type and predicts products
- Explains reaction rate factors using collision theory
- Measures and interprets pH using indicators and scales

**Applied Chemistry:**
- Connects chemical principles to multiple real-world applications (materials, food, environment)
- Evaluates material choices using chemical property reasoning
- Explains environmental chemistry concepts (acid rain, greenhouse effect, water treatment)
- Designs simple investigations to answer applied chemistry questions

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level demonstrate mastery of chemical reasoning. They analyze complex systems, evaluate scientific claims, design investigations, and apply chemistry to solve authentic problems.

**Matter & Properties:**
- Analyzes complex mixtures and designs multi-step separation procedures
- Evaluates experimental methods for reliability and accuracy
- Teaches property identification and classification to others
- Uses property measurements to solve forensic-style identification challenges

**Atoms & Elements:**
- Explains periodic trends from electron configuration and nuclear charge
- Predicts properties of unfamiliar elements using periodic position
- Evaluates the historical development and limitations of atomic models
- Connects atomic structure to spectroscopic evidence (flame tests, emission spectra)

**Bonds & Structure:**
- Designs molecules with target properties by choosing appropriate atoms and bonds
- Explains intermolecular forces and their effects on boiling point, viscosity, and solubility
- Evaluates the strengths and limitations of different bonding models
- Connects molecular structure to biological function (enzyme shape, DNA structure)

**Reactions & Energy:**
- Performs stoichiometric calculations (moles, limiting reagents, percent yield)
- Analyzes equilibrium systems and predicts the effect of changes (Le Chatelier's principle)
- Evaluates energy sources from a chemical perspective (efficiency, environmental impact)
- Designs novel investigations to study reaction kinetics or thermochemistry

**Applied Chemistry:**
- Applies chemical principles to solve authentic real-world problems
- Evaluates commercial and environmental chemical claims using evidence
- Synthesizes knowledge from multiple chemistry modules to address complex questions
- Mentors others in chemical reasoning and investigation design

## Formative Assessment

Formative assessment occurs during learning and provides feedback that guides improvement. In CHEM-101, formative assessment focuses on the quality of chemical reasoning, not the recall of facts, formulas, or element names.

### Property Investigation Assessment

Observe learners during material investigation activities. Look for:
- Do they test multiple properties before drawing conclusions?
- Do they record observations systematically in tables?
- Do they distinguish physical from chemical properties in their descriptions?
- Do they use property data to make identifications, not just guesses?
- Do they design their own tests when given an unfamiliar material?

### Periodic Table Usage Check

When learners work with elements, evaluate:
- Do they use the periodic table as a prediction tool or only as a lookup reference?
- Can they explain WHY an element behaves the way it does, or only describe what it does?
- Do they notice patterns across groups and periods?
- Can they predict the behavior of an unfamiliar element from its position?

### Structure-Property Reasoning

During bonding and molecular structure activities, assess:
- Does the learner connect bond type to observable properties?
- Can they explain why a substance behaves the way it does at the molecular level?
- Do they use molecular models to make predictions about unfamiliar substances?
- Can they explain everyday phenomena (dissolving, melting, conductivity) using chemical structure?

### Reaction Analysis Check-Ins

During reaction activities, assess:
- Does the learner identify evidence of chemical change (not just physical change)?
- Can they explain what happens to atoms during a reaction?
- Do they apply conservation of mass when analyzing reactions?
- Can they predict products from reactant types?
- Do they connect energy changes to molecular-level events?

### Reflection Journals

Periodic journal entries where learners reflect on:
- A chemical concept they now understand that they did not before
- An everyday observation they can now explain using chemistry
- A question that a chemistry investigation raised but did not answer
- How their understanding of what "chemistry" means has changed

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment evaluates cumulative learning at the end of a unit or module. In CHEM-101, summative assessment should demonstrate the ability to observe, reason about, and investigate the chemical world.

### Investigation Portfolio

A collection of work demonstrating growth in chemical understanding:

**Required components:**
- A property investigation report identifying an unknown substance through systematic testing
- A molecular model collection with explanations of how structure determines properties
- A reaction investigation report (question, hypothesis, experiment, balanced equation, energy analysis, conclusion)
- An applied chemistry connection: explain a real-world phenomenon using chemical principles

**Optional components:**
- A periodic table project demonstrating understanding of trends and predictions
- An element biography researching the history, properties, and uses of a chosen element
- A kitchen chemistry investigation with chemical explanations
- A reflection on how studying chemistry has changed their perception of everyday materials

### Capstone Projects

Projects that demonstrate integrated mastery across multiple modules:

1. **Material Detective:** Given an unknown substance, design and conduct a complete investigation: test physical and chemical properties, determine composition (element, compound, or mixture), identify bonding type from properties, and write a full report with evidence-based conclusions.

2. **Kitchen Chemistry Explained:** Choose a cooking or food preservation process (baking bread, making cheese, pickling, caramelizing sugar, fermenting) and explain every step using chemical principles. Include molecular models, reaction equations, and energy analysis.

3. **Environmental Chemistry Analysis:** Investigate a local environmental chemistry issue (water quality, soil pH, air quality, material waste). Collect data, analyze using chemical principles, and propose evidence-based solutions.

4. **Element Story:** Research the complete story of an element: its discovery, atomic structure, bonding behavior, important compounds, industrial uses, biological role (if any), and environmental significance. Present as a narrative that connects atomic-level properties to real-world importance.

5. **Design a Material:** Given a set of desired properties (strong but lightweight, transparent, heat-resistant, biodegradable), use chemical reasoning to propose a material that would meet the requirements. Explain your reasoning using bonding, molecular structure, and intermolecular forces.

## Portfolio Suggestions

### Foundation Level (K-2)
- Drawings of materials sorted by properties with labels
- "What happened?" descriptions of kitchen experiments (baking soda + vinegar, dissolving sugar)
- Before-and-after comparisons of physical and chemical changes
- Collections of "things that dissolve" vs. "things that do not dissolve"

### Elementary Level (3-5)
- Property investigation data tables with multiple tested properties
- Atomic models built from craft materials with labeled parts
- Observations from kitchen chemistry experiments with attempted explanations
- Periodic table element cards with hand-drawn illustrations

### Middle School Level (6-8)
- Complete investigation reports with hypotheses, controlled variables, and data analysis
- Molecular models with structure-property explanations
- Balanced chemical equations with reaction type classification
- Applied chemistry projects connecting classroom concepts to real-world phenomena

### High School Level (9-12)
- Quantitative investigation reports with stoichiometric calculations
- Molecular geometry and polarity analyses with property predictions
- Thermochemistry and kinetics investigations with energy diagrams
- Research papers connecting chemistry to environmental, medical, or materials science topics
- Peer review of chemistry investigations demonstrating constructive critique
