# STAT-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

This assessment framework evaluates quantitative reasoning and financial literacy rather than rote calculation. Progress is measured by the quality of understanding, not the speed of computation. A learner who can explain what a number means in context, question whether a comparison is fair, and make informed financial decisions is making progress -- regardless of whether they can perform calculations quickly. The framework assesses two intertwined strands: accounting and financial reasoning (Modules 1, 2, 5) and statistical and probabilistic thinking (Modules 3, 4).

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing foundational awareness of numbers as tools for understanding the world. They can count money, identify basic financial concepts, and participate in simple data activities.

**Accounting & Financial Awareness:**
- Recognizes that money comes from somewhere and goes somewhere
- Can count coins and bills and make simple change
- Understands the basic concept of buying and selling
- Knows that businesses track money coming in and going out
- Can identify simple financial transactions (paying for something, receiving payment)

**Probability & Chance:**
- Uses everyday language for probability ("always," "never," "maybe," "sometimes")
- Understands that some things are certain and some are impossible
- Can participate in coin flipping and dice rolling activities
- Recognizes that results of a single flip or roll are unpredictable
- Sorts events into "likely" and "unlikely" categories

**Data & Information:**
- Participates in simple data collection (voting, tallying, counting)
- Reads simple pictographs and tally charts with guidance
- Can answer "How many?" questions from organized data
- Understands that counting things gives useful information
- Sorts objects or responses into categories

**Financial Decisions:**
- Understands the concept of saving (not spending money now to have it later)
- Can make simple spending decisions ("Do I want this or that?")
- Recognizes that money is finite (you cannot buy everything)
- Understands that some things cost more than others

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building systematic approaches to quantitative reasoning. They track transactions with increasing accuracy, compute basic statistics, understand simple probabilities, and apply financial concepts to personal decisions.

**Accounting & Financial Records:**
- Records transactions in a simple journal with guidance
- Understands that every transaction affects at least two accounts
- Categorizes expenses into basic groups (food, entertainment, savings)
- Reads simple financial summaries and identifies total income and expenses
- Creates basic budgets with fixed categories

**Probability & Statistical Thinking:**
- Expresses probability as a fraction or percentage
- Calculates probability for simple events (coin flips, dice rolls, card draws)
- Computes mean, median, and mode for small datasets
- Creates bar graphs and simple data tables
- Understands that larger samples give more reliable results

**Data Collection & Analysis:**
- Designs simple surveys with clear questions
- Collects and organizes data in tables
- Creates appropriate graphs for categorical and numerical data
- Identifies the "most common" and "least common" in a dataset
- Notices when a graph might be misleading (very obvious cases)

**Financial Decision-Making:**
- Creates a simple personal budget and tracks spending
- Understands the basic concept of interest (earning or paying for the use of money)
- Compares prices and calculates unit costs
- Recognizes the difference between needs and wants
- Understands that saving regularly builds over time

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level demonstrate solid quantitative reasoning. They maintain accurate financial records, conduct statistical investigations, apply probability thinking to real situations, and make informed financial decisions with awareness of trade-offs.

**Accounting & Financial Analysis:**
- Applies the accounting equation correctly to classify transactions
- Records transactions using double-entry bookkeeping (debit and credit)
- Reads and interprets basic financial statements (income statement, balance sheet)
- Calculates and interprets simple financial ratios (profit margin, current ratio)
- Understands the difference between cash-basis and accrual-basis accounting

**Probability & Inference:**
- Calculates conditional probability and applies independence concepts
- Distinguishes experimental from theoretical probability and explains discrepancies
- Understands and applies expected value in decision-making
- Recognizes common probability misconceptions (gambler's fallacy) in others' reasoning
- Designs and conducts probability experiments with multiple trials

**Statistical Investigation:**
- Designs complete statistical investigations (question, data collection, analysis, conclusion)
- Chooses appropriate measures of center and spread for different data types
- Creates and interprets histograms, box plots, and scatter plots
- Identifies sampling bias and explains how it affects conclusions
- Distinguishes correlation from causation with specific reasoning

**Financial Planning:**
- Calculates compound interest and explains its long-term effects
- Creates realistic budgets with appropriate categories and contingencies
- Compares financial products (savings accounts, credit cards) using interest rates and terms
- Understands credit scores and how financial behavior affects them
- Makes informed saving and spending decisions with awareness of opportunity costs

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level demonstrate sophisticated quantitative reasoning that integrates accounting, statistics, and financial literacy. They can teach others, analyze complex real-world situations, evaluate claims critically, and make nuanced decisions under uncertainty.

**Accounting & Advanced Financial Analysis:**
- Performs full accounting cycle from journal entries through financial statement preparation
- Conducts comparative financial analysis across time periods and between entities
- Interprets cash flow statements and explains why profitable companies can fail
- Applies cost-volume-profit analysis and break-even calculations
- Evaluates the quality and reliability of financial information

**Advanced Probability & Bayesian Thinking:**
- Applies Bayes' theorem to update probability estimates with new evidence
- Explains and avoids base rate neglect in real-world probability assessments
- Understands probability distributions and their applications
- Evaluates risk using expected value and variance
- Recognizes when probabilistic reasoning is appropriate vs. when it is misleading

**Advanced Statistical Reasoning:**
- Understands and interprets confidence intervals and hypothesis tests
- Evaluates experimental design and identifies threats to validity
- Reads and critically evaluates statistical claims in media and research
- Understands the limitations of statistical significance (p-hacking, effect size)
- Designs studies that address confounding variables and selection bias

**Advanced Financial Literacy:**
- Understands investment portfolio theory (diversification, risk-return trade-off)
- Evaluates complex financial products (mortgages, insurance, retirement accounts)
- Plans for long-term financial goals with realistic projections
- Identifies predatory financial practices and understands consumer protections
- Teaches financial concepts to others clearly and accurately

## Formative Assessment

Ongoing assessment strategies for monitoring progress and guiding instruction:

- **Quantitative Reasoning Journals:** Students maintain journals where they explain what numbers mean in context, not just how to calculate them. Review for depth of understanding, not calculation accuracy.
- **Transaction Recording:** Observe accuracy and consistency in bookkeeping activities. Look for understanding of why entries are made, not just mechanical recording.
- **Data Investigation Check-ins:** During statistical projects, check that students are making thoughtful methodological choices (appropriate measures, honest visualizations, bias awareness).
- **Probability Discussions:** Pose real-world probability scenarios and listen for reasoning quality, especially resistance to common fallacies.
- **Budget Reviews:** Review student budgets for realistic assumptions, appropriate categorization, and evidence of trade-off thinking.
- **Graph Interpretation Exercises:** Present graphs from real media and assess whether students can identify what the graph shows, what it hides, and whether it is honest.

---

## Summative Assessment

Evidence of learning collected at module or pack completion:

- **Financial Portfolio:** A collection of accounting records, budgets, and financial analyses created throughout Modules 1, 2, and 5, demonstrating growth in accuracy and sophistication over time.
- **Statistical Investigation Report:** A complete investigation from question through data collection, analysis, and conclusion, with honest discussion of limitations and potential biases.
- **Critical Analysis Essay:** Written analysis of a real-world financial or statistical claim from media (news article, advertisement, political argument), evaluating the evidence, identifying potential manipulation, and explaining what additional information would be needed.
- **Probability Experiment Report:** Design and results of a multi-trial probability experiment, with comparison of experimental and theoretical results and explanation of any discrepancies.

---

## Portfolio Suggestions

Portfolios demonstrate growth over time and provide holistic assessment beyond individual tasks:

- **Best Budget:** The best budget the student created during Module 5, with a written reflection on what they learned about financial trade-offs.
- **Data Story:** A statistical investigation where the student collected real data, analyzed it, and told the story the data revealed -- including what the data did not show.
- **Misleading Graph Collection:** A curated collection of misleading graphs the student found in media, with written analysis of each deception technique.
- **Accounting Records:** A complete set of bookkeeping records from a classroom store or simulation, demonstrating accurate double-entry recording and financial statement preparation.
- **Probability Reflection:** A written piece explaining a probability concept the student initially found counterintuitive (the Monty Hall problem, the birthday paradox, base rate neglect) and how their understanding changed through experimentation and reasoning.
- **Financial Literacy Action Plan:** A personal financial plan addressing saving, spending, and future goals, demonstrating practical application of concepts from Module 5.
