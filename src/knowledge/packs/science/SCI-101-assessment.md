# SCI-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

This assessment framework evaluates scientific inquiry skills rather than factual recall. Progress is measured by the quality of thinking, not the quantity of correct answers. A learner who asks better questions, designs fairer experiments, and reasons more carefully from evidence is making progress -- regardless of whether they know the names of specific scientists or the details of specific discoveries.

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing foundational awareness of scientific thinking. They notice things in the world around them, ask broad questions, and are learning to distinguish between what they observe and what they assume.

**Observation & Inquiry:**
- Notices obvious features of objects, organisms, and events
- Records simple observations using drawings or single words
- Asks broad questions ("Why is the sky blue?") without yet refining them
- Sorts objects by a single attribute (color, size, shape)

**Experimental Thinking:**
- Suggests ideas to try ("What if we add more water?")
- Identifies "what changed" in a simple before-and-after comparison
- Follows structured experiment procedures with guidance
- Makes simple predictions ("I think it will be bigger")

**Data & Evidence:**
- Reads simple data tables with guidance
- Recognizes biggest/smallest in a data set
- Sorts items into two groups based on one property
- Understands that counting and measuring give information

**Communication:**
- Describes what happened during an activity
- Draws pictures of observations and procedures
- Listens to others' descriptions and asks simple questions
- Shares findings verbally in small groups

**Nature of Science:**
- Knows that scientists investigate the natural world
- Understands that answers come from looking and testing, not just asking an adult
- Recognizes that different people might notice different things

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building systematic approaches to scientific inquiry. They observe with more structure, form simple hypotheses, collect and organize data, and are learning to communicate their findings.

**Observation & Inquiry:**
- Observes systematically, using multiple senses and recording methods
- Distinguishes observation from inference with increasing consistency
- Records observations with detail (quantitative and qualitative)
- Asks questions that can be investigated ("Does water temperature affect how fast sugar dissolves?")

**Experimental Thinking:**
- Formulates simple hypotheses with reasoning ("I think X because Y")
- Identifies the main variable being tested
- Designs fair tests with guidance (understanding that some things need to stay the same)
- Makes predictions with some specificity ("I think the red one will dissolve faster because...")

**Data & Evidence:**
- Creates data tables with labeled columns and rows
- Makes bar graphs and simple line graphs
- Calculates averages from multiple trials
- Identifies trends in data ("It went up each day")

**Communication:**
- Writes structured reports with sections (question, method, results, conclusion)
- Creates labeled diagrams and annotated graphs
- Presents findings to peers with visual aids
- Begins to give feedback on others' work using simple criteria

**Nature of Science:**
- Understands that scientific knowledge has changed over time
- Identifies some claims as pseudoscientific (with guidance)
- Knows about several famous scientists and their contributions
- Understands that experiments can give unexpected results, and that is okay

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level demonstrate independent scientific thinking. They design their own investigations, analyze data critically, construct evidence-based arguments, and evaluate others' work constructively.

**Observation & Inquiry:**
- Uses multiple observation methods (quantitative measurement, qualitative description, photography, sketching)
- Asks testable questions independently and refines them based on feasibility
- Classifies using multiple criteria simultaneously
- Designs observation protocols for novel situations

**Experimental Thinking:**
- Designs controlled experiments independently with identified variables
- Makes specific, measurable, falsifiable predictions
- Identifies confounding variables and plans appropriate controls
- Evaluates experimental designs for fairness and potential bias

**Data & Evidence:**
- Analyzes data using appropriate statistical measures (mean, median, range)
- Identifies outliers and discusses possible sources of error
- Distinguishes correlation from causation
- Draws evidence-based conclusions with explicit acknowledgment of uncertainty and limitations

**Communication:**
- Constructs CER (Claim-Evidence-Reasoning) arguments with supporting data
- Provides constructive, evidence-based peer review
- Adapts communication style and depth to different audiences
- Revises work meaningfully in response to feedback

**Nature of Science:**
- Explains how science self-corrects through peer review and replication
- Applies demarcation criteria to evaluate claims as scientific or not
- Discusses ethical considerations in research with nuance
- Understands that scientific theories are well-supported explanations, not guesses

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level demonstrate mastery of scientific inquiry. They design sophisticated investigations, evaluate published research, contribute to scientific discourse, and teach others.

**Observation & Inquiry:**
- Designs observation protocols for complex or novel situations
- Recognizes and accounts for observer bias in their own work
- Teaches observation skills to others effectively
- Integrates multiple data sources into comprehensive observational analyses

**Experimental Thinking:**
- Designs multi-variable experiments with appropriate statistical controls
- Evaluates published experimental designs critically, identifying strengths and weaknesses
- Proposes alternative experimental approaches to test the same hypothesis
- Understands and discusses the philosophy of falsifiability and its limits

**Data & Evidence:**
- Uses advanced statistical methods (significance testing, confidence intervals) appropriately
- Identifies bias in data collection, analysis, and interpretation
- Evaluates certainty and uncertainty rigorously across multiple studies
- Synthesizes evidence from multiple sources to draw meta-level conclusions

**Communication:**
- Produces research-quality reports suitable for publication or presentation
- Leads constructive review processes for peers
- Communicates complex scientific ideas to diverse audiences (scientists, public, policymakers)
- Mentors others in scientific writing and argumentation

**Nature of Science:**
- Analyzes paradigm shifts critically, understanding both the evidence and the social dynamics
- Evaluates science-society interactions (funding, policy, public trust) thoughtfully
- Contributes to ethical discussions about the responsibilities of scientific knowledge
- Understands the provisional nature of all scientific knowledge and the role of ongoing inquiry

## Formative Assessment

Formative assessment occurs during learning and provides feedback that guides improvement. In SCI-101, formative assessment focuses on the quality of scientific thinking, not the correctness of factual recall.

### Inquiry Discourse Assessment

Observe learners during discussions and investigations. Look for:
- Do they ask testable questions?
- Do they distinguish observation from inference in conversation?
- Do they cite evidence when making claims?
- Do they acknowledge uncertainty?
- Do they change their thinking when presented with new evidence?

### Experimental Design Review

When learners design experiments, evaluate:
- Is the hypothesis testable and falsifiable?
- Are variables identified clearly (independent, dependent, controlled)?
- Is the experimental procedure specific enough to be replicated?
- Is the data collection plan appropriate for the question?
- Are potential sources of error acknowledged?

### Data Analysis Check-Ins

During data analysis activities, assess:
- Is the learner choosing appropriate representations for the data type?
- Are calculations accurate and appropriately precise?
- Does the learner identify patterns, trends, and anomalies?
- Does the learner connect findings back to the original hypothesis?
- Does the learner acknowledge limitations of the data?

### Reflection Journals

Periodic journal entries where learners reflect on:
- What they learned about how science works
- How their thinking has changed during the investigation
- What they found surprising or confusing
- What they would do differently next time

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment evaluates cumulative learning at the end of a unit or module. In SCI-101, summative assessment should demonstrate the ability to conduct and communicate scientific inquiry.

### Investigation Portfolio

A collection of work demonstrating growth in scientific thinking:

**Required components:**
- Observation journal entries showing progression from simple to detailed, systematic recording
- A complete investigation report (question, hypothesis, experiment, data, analysis, conclusion, reflection)
- A peer review of another learner's work with constructive, evidence-based feedback
- A CER argument on a scientific topic with supporting data and acknowledgment of limitations

**Optional components:**
- A "Science vs. Not-Science" analysis of a real-world claim from the news
- A reflection on how the learner's understanding of science has evolved
- A case study analysis of a historical change in scientific understanding

### Capstone Projects

Projects that demonstrate integrated mastery across multiple modules:

1. **Original Investigation:** Design and conduct an original investigation from question to conclusion. Present findings in both written report and oral presentation format.

2. **Science Communication Challenge:** Take a scientific finding and communicate it to three different audiences: a scientist, a middle schooler, and a community member. Demonstrate ability to adjust depth, vocabulary, and emphasis without distorting the science.

3. **Critical Evaluation Report:** Select a published study (simplified for grade level) and evaluate it: identify the question, assess the experimental design, evaluate the data analysis, and critique the conclusions. Propose what additional evidence would strengthen or weaken the study's claims.

4. **Historical Case Study:** Research how scientific understanding of a specific topic (disease transmission, atomic structure, continental drift) has changed over time. Identify the evidence that drove major changes and the social factors that influenced acceptance or resistance.

## Portfolio Suggestions

### Foundation Level (K-2)
- Observation drawings with dates and descriptions
- "I wonder..." question collections
- Simple sorting and classification activities with photos
- Before-and-after comparisons (plants, weather, seasons)

### Elementary Level (3-5)
- Observation journals with increasing detail over time
- Simple experiment write-ups (question, what I did, what happened, what I learned)
- Data tables and bar graphs from classroom investigations
- Peer feedback notes (what was good, what could improve)

### Middle School Level (6-8)
- Full investigation reports with hypothesis, variables, data, and analysis
- CER arguments with cited data
- Peer review feedback demonstrating constructive critique
- Science vs. pseudoscience evaluation of a current claim

### High School Level (9-12)
- Research-quality investigation reports with statistical analysis
- Literature reviews of published studies
- Ethics case study analyses
- Scientific communication pieces adapted for different audiences
- Peer review portfolio demonstrating growth in evaluation skills
