# ENVR-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

ENVR-101 assessment focuses on evidence-based environmental reasoning: can students observe systems carefully, interpret data accurately, recognize cause-and-effect relationships, and propose solutions grounded in evidence? Assessment emphasizes reasoning over terminology memorization, process over single right answers, and systems thinking over isolated facts.

---

## Formative Assessment (Ongoing)

Continuous feedback to guide learning:

### Observation

Watch students at work. Do they:
- Notice details in ecosystems? (biodiversity, food relationships, habitat structure)
- Make predictions before testing? (scientific thinking)
- Adjust their understanding when new data appears? (intellectual honesty)
- Ask productive questions? ("What if...?" "How do you know...?" "What evidence...?")

### Probing Questions

Use these questions throughout instruction:

**Ecosystems & Biodiversity:**
- "How would removing this organism affect the ecosystem?"
- "What does this organism need to survive? Where does it find those things?"
- "Why do different ecosystems support different communities?"

**Earth Systems & Cycles:**
- "Where does this water/carbon/nitrogen come from? Where does it go?"
- "What would happen if we added fertilizer to this ecosystem?"
- "How long does it take for carbon to cycle through soil? Through rocks? Through the atmosphere?"

**Human Impact:**
- "Where does this pollution come from? How does it reach people?"
- "What are all the steps in removing this pollutant?"
- "Who is affected by this environmental problem? Who benefits from the cause?"

**Climate Science:**
- "What is the evidence for climate change? What is the evidence that humans caused it?"
- "What does this climate graph show? What doesn't it show?"
- "What are the tradeoffs in this energy source?"

**Sustainability:**
- "What makes this system sustainable? What makes it unsustainable?"
- "Who pays the costs of this solution? Who receives benefits?"
- "What would need to change for this proposal to work at scale?"

### Concept Mapping

Ask students to draw how concepts connect:
- Food chain → energy flow → trophic pyramid (M1)
- CO₂ in atmosphere → plants → animals → decomposition → back to atmosphere (M2)
- Pollution source → pathway → exposure → health impact (M3)
- Greenhouse gases → heat trapping → temperature increase → climate effects (M4)

### Data Interpretation

Present graphs, tables, or maps:
- "What does this graph show?" (read the data)
- "What does it NOT show?" (recognize limitations)
- "What caused this pattern?" (inference)
- "What would happen if this trend continued?" (prediction)

### Discussion & Explanation

Listen to student explanations:
- Do they explain reasoning clearly?
- Do they use evidence from observations or data?
- Do they acknowledge uncertainty?
- Do they recognize different perspectives?

## Summative Assessment (End of Module)

### Portfolio: Environmental Investigation

Students compile evidence of learning across the module:

**Contents:**
- Field observations (sketches, notes, photos)
- Data collection sheets (measurements, counts, classifications)
- Graphs and analysis (interpreting patterns, making inferences)
- Written explanations (how do findings connect to concepts?)
- Revised predictions (initial guess vs. what they learned)

**Criteria:**
- Observations are accurate and detailed
- Data collection is systematic and complete
- Analysis shows reasoning (not just reporting numbers)
- Explanations connect findings to broader concepts
- Student reflects on learning and misconceptions corrected

### Systems Modeling Project

Students build or design a model of an ecosystem, cycle, or energy system.

**Options:**
- Physical model: terrarium, water cycle in a bag, greenhouse effect jar, food web on poster
- Computational model: spreadsheet or graphing simulation of population dynamics or climate
- Design project: sustainable community, home energy plan, water treatment system
- Digital visualization: interactive diagram of cycle with all major processes labeled

**Criteria:**
- Model is accurate (represents reality, not just pretty)
- Model includes all major components and interactions
- Model shows how changing one part affects others (systems thinking)
- Student can explain how model works and its limitations
- Student makes predictions using the model

### Data Analysis Report

Students read real environmental data and communicate findings.

**Structure:**
1. **What data did you use?** (source, what it measures, when/where)
2. **What does it show?** (describe patterns)
3. **What does it NOT show?** (limitations)
4. **What caused this pattern?** (inference with evidence)
5. **What does it predict about the future?** (extrapolation with uncertainty)
6. **What actions might this data suggest?** (implications)

**Criteria:**
- Accurate interpretation of data
- Recognition of both clear patterns and uncertainty
- Reasonable inferences grounded in evidence
- Acknowledgment of alternative explanations
- Clear communication

### Policy Proposal or Critique

Students evaluate an environmental policy or propose a new one.

**Structure:**
1. **What is the problem?** (evidence)
2. **What is the proposed solution?** (policy)
3. **Will it work?** (feasibility, effectiveness)
4. **What are the tradeoffs?** (benefits, costs, who's affected)
5. **How would you improve it?** (or what alternative would work better?)

**Criteria:**
- Problem is clearly defined with evidence
- Solution logically addresses the problem
- Analysis of tradeoffs is balanced and thorough
- Considers multiple stakeholder perspectives
- Proposal is grounded in evidence and systems thinking

### Sustainability Design

Students design sustainable practices for home, school, or community.

**Structure:**
1. **Current state:** what is the system now? (energy, water, waste, food, transportation)
2. **Goals:** what does sustainability look like?
3. **Strategy:** how would you change the system?
4. **Barriers:** what prevents change?
5. **Action plan:** specific steps, timeline, success measures

**Criteria:**
- Design is systematic (addresses root causes, not symptoms)
- Recognizes and addresses tradeoffs
- Feasible given real constraints
- Accounts for human behavior and incentives
- Success measures are concrete and measurable

## Rubric Levels

Use these rubric descriptions across all assessments. Adapt as needed for specific assignments.

### Beginning

Student demonstrates limited understanding:

- **Observation:** Notices some features but misses relationships; observations may be incomplete or inaccurate
- **Evidence:** Makes claims without supporting evidence or uses irrelevant evidence
- **Reasoning:** Explains cause-and-effect simply, missing intermediate steps or feedback loops
- **Data:** Reads graphs/numbers mechanically without interpreting meaning
- **Prediction:** Makes predictions without basis or rationale
- **Perspectives:** Acknowledges only one viewpoint; doesn't see different interests or values
- **Solutions:** Proposes solutions that ignore consequences or feasibility

**Example:** "The greenhouse effect makes things hotter" (correct but incomplete; missing mechanism of CO₂ trapping heat)

### Developing

Student demonstrates emerging understanding:

- **Observation:** Systematically observes and records; sometimes connects observations to concepts
- **Evidence:** Supports claims with relevant evidence but may misinterpret or overgeneralize
- **Reasoning:** Explains relationships with some detail; recognizes some feedback or multi-step causation
- **Data:** Reads and interprets graphs/data; recognizes major patterns; may miss subtleties or alternative explanations
- **Prediction:** Makes predictions based on patterns; may not account for uncertainty
- **Perspectives:** Recognizes that different people have different perspectives; partially understands why
- **Solutions:** Proposes solutions with some consideration of consequences; may miss tradeoffs

**Example:** "The greenhouse effect happens because CO₂ traps heat in the atmosphere. More CO₂ makes Earth warmer. But it's complicated because clouds also affect temperature." (Correct mechanism, recognizes complexity)

### Proficient

Student demonstrates solid understanding:

- **Observation:** Carefully observes details; connects observations to concepts; draws reasonable inferences
- **Evidence:** Supports claims with relevant, accurate evidence; cites sources; distinguishes evidence from opinion
- **Reasoning:** Explains relationships with detail and accuracy; traces cause-and-effect through multiple steps; recognizes feedback loops
- **Data:** Accurately reads and interprets graphs/data; identifies trends and patterns; recognizes uncertainty and limitations
- **Prediction:** Makes predictions grounded in models or evidence; includes uncertainty ranges; revises with new data
- **Perspectives:** Understands different perspectives and recognizes legitimate value differences; appreciates why reasonable people disagree
- **Solutions:** Proposes solutions that address root causes; identifies and weighs tradeoffs; considers multiple stakeholders

**Example:** "Atmospheric CO₂ traps heat because its molecule shape absorbs infrared radiation. Increasing CO₂ from 280 ppm to 420 ppm has raised global temperature ~1°C, but the warming isn't uniform — poles warm faster, and some regions cool due to ocean circulation changes. Future warming depends on how fast we reduce emissions. All climate solutions involve tradeoffs: renewable energy is cleaner but requires land and mining; nuclear has low emissions but waste concerns; efficiency saves money but requires upfront investment."

### Advanced

Student demonstrates exceptional understanding:

- **Observation:** Observations are detailed and systematic; recognizes patterns others miss; designs observations to test hypotheses
- **Evidence:** Evaluates evidence critically; compares sources; recognizes that scientific disagreement can coexist with strong consensus
- **Reasoning:** Models complex relationships including feedback loops, emergent properties, and threshold effects; explains why simple explanations fail
- **Data:** Analyzes data at multiple levels; applies statistical reasoning; recognizes and quantifies uncertainty; evaluates data quality
- **Prediction:** Makes probabilistic predictions; understands model limitations and structural uncertainty; revises substantially with new information
- **Perspectives:** Synthesizes multiple perspectives; recognizes how values and interests shape viewpoints; sees common ground in disagreement
- **Solutions:** Designs solutions optimizing multiple objectives; identifies remaining uncertainties and risks; understands scale and systems implications

**Example:** "Climate change involves multiple coupled systems. CO₂ increases → temperature increases → atmospheric water vapor increases (amplifying feedback) → ice melts → less reflection (positive feedback) → permafrost thaws → methane released (positive feedback). But clouds also increase, which could increase or decrease warming depending on cloud type and height. Models project 1.5-4.5°C warming by 2100 under current policies, with the range reflecting uncertainty in climate sensitivity, future emissions, and feedback strengths. Every tenth of a degree matters because impacts accelerate nonlinearly. Solutions must work at global scale while respecting local contexts and equity. Current policy is insufficient to reach Paris goals, requiring rapid energy transition (challenging due to infrastructure lock-in), carbon removal (unproven at scale), and adaptation (necessary even with mitigation)."

## Formative Assessment Strategies

### Observation Checklists

Use during labs and field work:

- [ ] Student makes detailed observations
- [ ] Student records observations systematically
- [ ] Student notices patterns or relationships
- [ ] Student proposes explanations for observations
- [ ] Student asks follow-up questions

### Quick Writes

15-minute ungraded reflections:

- "Draw an energy pathway from the sun to a carnivore and label each step"
- "If we removed all the insects from an ecosystem, what would happen?"
- "What does this climate graph tell us? What doesn't it tell us?"
- "Describe one tradeoff in renewable energy"

### Peer Discussion

Structured conversations:

- "Teach your partner your explanation of the water cycle. Did they understand? Where was it unclear?"
- "Do you agree with this solution? Why or why not?"
- "What evidence would convince you of this claim?"

### Self-Assessment

Student reflection:

- "What do you understand well? What's still confusing?"
- "What changed your thinking in this module?"
- "Where do you see yourself on the rubric?"

## Accessibility in Assessment

Provide multiple ways for students to demonstrate understanding:

- **Writing:** essays, reports, quick writes
- **Speaking:** explanations to peers, presentations, discussions
- **Visual:** diagrams, graphs, models, drawings
- **Hands-on:** building models, conducting experiments, designing solutions
- **Digital:** spreadsheets, interactive simulations, videos

Scaffold as needed:
- Sentence frames: "The evidence shows ___. This means ___."
- Concept templates: cycle diagram with blanks to fill in
- Data templates: pre-formatted graph with axis labels, just add data
- Rubric checklist: "Did I include all components? Can I explain my reasoning?"

## Grading Guidance

### Formative Work

Don't grade formative assessments (observations, quick writes, practice). They're for feedback and learning, not judgment. Provide specific feedback:

- Good: "I like how you labeled the trophic levels. Can you also show the 10% rule — how much energy reaches each level?"
- Vague: "Good job!"

### Summative Work

Grade summative assessments (investigations, projects, reports) on learning objectives, not on neatness or effort:

- What evidence of understanding does this work show?
- Can the student explain their reasoning?
- Can the student connect findings to broader concepts?
- Does the student recognize limitations and uncertainty?

Use the rubric levels (Beginning/Developing/Proficient/Advanced) to communicate growth.

### Weight

Suggested breakdown:
- Investigations/observations: 40% (students learning from direct experience)
- Projects/modeling: 35% (students integrating and applying knowledge)
- Data analysis/policy: 15% (students reasoning with information)
- Class discussion/participation: 10% (students thinking out loud)

Adjust based on your situation.

## Special Considerations

### Differentiation

- **For students struggling with reading:** Provide text summaries or videos
- **For students strong in math:** Deepen quantitative analysis (statistics, modeling)
- **For students strong in writing:** Encourage detailed explanations and analysis
- **For students strong in visual/spatial thinking:** Emphasize diagrams, models, maps
- **For students with experience in environmental fields:** Encourage expertise in design and policy

### Sensitive Topics

Climate change, pollution, and environmental justice can raise strong emotions. Assessment should:

- Distinguish science (what the evidence shows) from values (how to respond)
- Acknowledge different perspectives on solutions without blurring facts
- Avoid shaming individual students for family practices
- Focus on understanding and solutions, not judgment

### Equity

- Ensure activities are accessible to all learners (not just wealthy families)
- Use local examples that students can relate to
- Recognize that some students have firsthand experience with environmental problems
- Include diverse voices in resources and examples

---

## Reflection & Iteration

Assess your assessment:

- Do students understand what good work looks like? (Share exemplars)
- Are rubrics clear? (Can students self-assess accurately?)
- Do assessments measure what matters? (Systems thinking? Evidence reasoning? Solutions?)
- Are assessments equitable? (Do all students have opportunity to show understanding?)
- What are students learning best from? (Field work? Labs? Data? Design?)
- What still confuses students? (Revise instruction or scaffolding)
