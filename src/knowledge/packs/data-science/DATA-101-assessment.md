# DATA-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

Assessment in DATA-101 focuses on reasoning with evidence and critical thinking, not formula memorization. We assess the ability to ask good questions, work with data thoughtfully, visualize findings clearly, and consider ethical implications.

---

## Rubric Levels

### Beginning

**Student demonstrates foundational understanding with support. Work shows:**
- Attempts to collect data but may not recognize quality issues
- Creates visualizations but they may be unclear or misleading
- Descriptions of data are vague ("The numbers are big" rather than "The range is 20-85")
- Limited awareness of limitations or alternative explanations
- Relies heavily on teacher guidance

**Example:** Student collects data about favorite colors, creates a bar chart, says "Most people like blue," but doesn't explain why or notice that the survey was only given at lunch (may miss students who eat elsewhere).

### Developing

**Student demonstrates understanding with some independence. Work shows:**
- Collects data with awareness of some quality issues
- Creates mostly clear visualizations; some choices are appropriate
- Describes data using summary statistics (mean, mode) but may not always interpret them accurately
- Shows some consideration of limitations ("I only asked my friends, so the results might be different for other groups")
- Can identify obvious patterns but misses subtler relationships

**Example:** Student designs a survey about homework time, collects responses from 20 students, creates a bar chart and calculates mean and median. Recognizes that only surveying one grade level might bias results. Still doesn't explain why median might be better than mean for this data.

### Proficient

**Student demonstrates solid understanding and independence. Work shows:**
- Collects data thoughtfully; identifies potential sources of bias and quality issues
- Creates accurate, clear visualizations; justifies chart type choices
- Calculates and interprets appropriate summary statistics (mean, median, range, etc.)
- Explains findings to others with evidence and acknowledges uncertainty
- Considers limitations: "This data only includes students present today, so it misses absent students"
- Recognizes patterns and can hypothesize explanations

**Example:** Student designs a survey about reading habits, samples from multiple grade levels, identifies potential non-response bias, creates a dashboard with multiple visualizations, calculates mean and median reading time, explains why the difference matters ("The median is 45 minutes, but the mean is 60 because a few students read much more"). Presents findings clearly with caveats.

### Advanced

**Student demonstrates sophisticated understanding and insight. Work shows:**
- Designs data collection to minimize bias; thoughtfully addresses quality and representativeness
- Creates compelling, well-designed visualizations that reveal nuance; anticipates misinterpretations
- Uses multiple statistical methods appropriately and interprets results with sophistication
- Distinguishes correlation from causation; considers confounding variables
- Communicates with clarity, precision, and appropriate uncertainty ("The data suggest... but we cannot rule out...")
- Asks insightful follow-up questions; proposes improvements
- Considers ethical implications: "Who is affected by how we use this data?"

**Example:** Student analyzes whether increased homework time predicts higher test scores. Collects data from multiple classes and years. Recognizes potential confounds (motivated students both do more homework AND study more). Creates visualizations showing relationship; calculates correlation but is careful not to claim causation. Proposes that an experiment (randomizing homework amount) would be needed to test causation. Reflects on ethics: "Before collecting student data, we should explain why and get permission."

## Rubric by Component

### Data Collection & Quality

| Beginning | Developing | Proficient | Advanced |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Collects data but doesn't consider how collection method affects results | Attempts to recognize bias but may miss sources | Identifies multiple sources of bias and quality issues; designs to minimize them | Designs collection to minimize bias; documents assumptions and limitations; considers representativeness |
| Doesn't organize data systematically | Organizes data but structure is unclear | Organizes clearly in tables; labels are consistent | Structures data for analysis; considers data types and missing values |

### Exploratory Analysis

| Beginning | Developing | Proficient | Advanced |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Attempts to describe data but descriptions are vague | Calculates summary statistics but may misinterpret | Calculates and interprets mean, median, range; identifies outliers | Interprets multiple statistics in context; recognizes distribution shape; understands variability |
| Doesn't notice patterns | Notices obvious patterns | Notices patterns and hypothesizes explanations | Asks sophisticated follow-up questions about patterns |

### Visualization & Communication

| Beginning | Developing | Proficient | Advanced |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Creates visualizations but they may be confusing or misleading | Creates graphs that mostly communicate but may have labeling issues | Creates clear, accurate visualizations; chooses types appropriately; adds titles and legends | Designs compelling visualizations that reveal nuance; considers audience and design principles |
| Explanations lack detail | Explains findings but with limited evidence | Explains findings with evidence and acknowledges limitations | Communicates with precision, clarity, and appropriate uncertainty |

### Statistical Reasoning (High School & College)

| Beginning | Developing | Proficient | Advanced |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Confusion about probability or hypothesis testing | Attempts hypothesis test but may misinterpret p-values | Conducts appropriate test; interprets correctly with caveats | Selects test method appropriately; recognizes limitations (sample size, assumptions) |
| Cannot distinguish correlation from causation | Recognizes correlation vs. causation in obvious cases | Consistently distinguishes and considers confounds | Proposes experimental designs to test causation |

### Ethical Reasoning

| Beginning | Developing | Proficient | Advanced |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Doesn't consider ethical issues | Recognizes privacy or fairness concerns with prompting | Identifies ethical issues proactively; considers impact on stakeholders | Anticipates ethical implications; proposes responsible practices; reflects on power and fairness |

## Formative Assessment Methods

### Observation in Action

Watch students as they work with data:
- Are they asking questions?
- Do they notice when something seems off?
- How do they handle unexpected patterns?
- Do they consider limitations?

**What to listen for:**
- "Wait, that seems really big/small. Is that reasonable?"
- "These two groups are different — why?"
- "I only asked my friends, so maybe it's biased"
- "Can we check if this is true some other way?"

### Think-Aloud Protocols

Ask students to explain their reasoning as they work:
- "Why did you choose a bar chart?"
- "What does this number tell us?"
- "If someone from another class saw this graph, what might they conclude?"

### Artifact Collection

Keep samples of student work across a unit:
- Early-unit: raw data collection, simple graphs
- Mid-unit: more sophisticated visualizations, calculations
- Late-unit: interpretations and explanations

Look for growth in question-asking, attention to bias, and sophistication of reasoning.

### Peer Review

Have students review each other's visualizations and analyses:
- "Is this graph clear? What would make it better?"
- "Do the conclusions follow from the data?"
- "What might you do differently?"

### Classroom Discourse

Discussions reveal understanding:
- Can students explain a finding to a peer?
- Do they ask probing questions?
- Can they push back respectfully on others' claims?

## Summative Assessment Methods

### Data Exploration Project

**Task:** Given a new data set (relevant to students' interests or curriculum), students:
1. Pose a research question
2. Explore the data using visualization and summary statistics
3. Identify patterns, trends, and anomalies
4. Describe what the data show and limitations of the data
5. Present findings to an audience

**Rubric focuses on:**
- Appropriateness of questions and analysis method
- Clarity of visualizations
- Accuracy of interpretations
- Acknowledgment of limitations and uncertainty

**Example prompts:**
- Elementary: "Here's data about animals. What questions can you answer? Create a graph and explain what it shows."
- Middle School: "Survey data from 100 students about study habits. Explore: what's typical? What's unusual? Why?"
- High School: "Climate data from 50 years. What patterns do you see? What questions would you ask a climate scientist?"

### Critical Reading: Graph and Data Critique

**Task:** Show students a published graph or dataset and ask:
1. What's the data source?
2. What question is this data supposed to answer?
3. What choices did the creator make? (Chart type, axes, colors)
4. Are those choices appropriate or misleading?
5. What alternative visualization might be better?

**Rubric focuses on:**
- Ability to identify visualization choices
- Understanding of how choices affect perception
- Quality of critique and redesign

**Example:** Provide a news article with a misleading graph. Students identify the problem and redesign.

### Hypothesis Test Report

**Task (High School+):** Students:
1. Pose a testable question (e.g., "Do students perform better on tests after sleeping 8+ hours?")
2. Collect or find relevant data
3. State null and alternative hypotheses
4. Conduct an appropriate statistical test
5. Interpret p-value and confidence intervals
6. Draw conclusions in plain language

**Rubric focuses on:**
- Clarity of research question and hypotheses
- Appropriateness of test method
- Correct interpretation of statistical results
- Careful wording (avoid overconfident claims)

### Ethics Case Study

**Task:** Present students with a real-world scenario involving data ethics (e.g., "A hospital wants to use patient data for research to develop new treatments. What ethical questions should they consider? What policies would you propose?").

Students:
1. Identify ethical issues
2. Describe who is affected
3. Consider different perspectives (patients, doctors, hospital administrators)
4. Propose responsible solutions
5. Justify their recommendations

**Rubric focuses on:**
- Depth of ethical reasoning
- Consideration of multiple perspectives
- Thoughtfulness of proposed solutions

### Portfolio Assessment

Collect work across the unit:
- Surveys designed and responses analyzed
- Visualizations created
- Interpretations and explanations
- Reflections on learning

Ask students to select pieces showing growth and write reflections:
- "What did I learn about working with data?"
- "How did my questions or visualizations improve over time?"
- "What data questions do I still have?"

---

## Assessment Timing & Frequency

**Formative (ongoing):**
- Daily: observation, artifact collection
- Weekly: peer review, think-aloud
- Bi-weekly: quick data exploration task

**Summative (end of unit or module):**
- One major data exploration project (2-4 weeks)
- One critical reading or graph critique
- One ethics discussion or case study
- Written reflection on learning

---

## Grading Guidance

Rather than point totals, assess on the rubric levels:

**Approach:**
- Most classroom work is formative (for learning, not grading)
- Major projects are assessed on the rubric
- Average performance is "Proficient"
- "Beginning" work needs reteaching and revision
- "Advanced" work earns highest marks
- Students revise work to improve

**Communicate:**
- Use rubric language with students: "This is good — it's proficient. Here's what would make it advanced..."
- Provide specific feedback: "Your graph is clear. Your explanation would be stronger if you noted that this data is only from Tuesday's class."
- Allow revision: "Revise your analysis; pay attention to how you describe the limitations."

## Parent Communication

Parents sometimes worry: "Why don't you test formulas?"

**Explain:**
- Calculators and computers do arithmetic.
- Understanding what the arithmetic *means* is the real skill.
- A student who can't compute standard deviation by hand but understands that it measures spread is data literate.
- A student who memorizes the mean formula but can't explain what it tells us hasn't learned it.

**Share examples:**
- "Your child designed a survey, collected responses, created visualizations, and explained what the data show. That's data literacy."
- "She noticed that two groups had the same mean but different spreads. That's sophisticated reasoning."

## Accessibility & Differentiation

**For students who struggle:**
- Smaller data sets
- Guided worksheet with explicit steps
- Pre-made visualizations to interpret before creating own
- Peer partnership for data collection

**For students ready for challenge:**
- Larger, messier data sets
- Design own study or survey
- Create interactive visualizations
- Conduct hypothesis tests and propose experiments

**For English learners:**
- Use data visualizations to build vocabulary
- Pair with bilingual peer when possible
- Provide sentence stems: "The data show... because..."
- Allow explanation in first language if possible

**For students with visual impairments:**
- Provide data in table format as well as graphs
- Use screen-reader compatible tools
- Create sonified data (audio representation)

---

## Self-Assessment for Students

**Reflection questions:**
- What question did I try to answer with data?
- What did I do well? What was harder?
- What surprised me about the data?
- What would I do differently next time?
- What more would I want to know?
- Did I consider fairness and ethics in my work?

**Growth-focused language:**
- "Beginning" = still learning
- "Developing" = making progress
- "Proficient" = solid understanding
- "Advanced" = sophisticated thinking

Encourage students to see assessment as feedback, not judgment.

---

## Conclusion

Data literacy is developed through repeated practice with real data and real questions, thoughtful feedback, and opportunities to revise and improve. Assessment should reveal what students understand and guide them toward deeper thinking. The goal is not to grade students; it is to help them become confident, critical readers of data and responsible participants in a data-driven world.
