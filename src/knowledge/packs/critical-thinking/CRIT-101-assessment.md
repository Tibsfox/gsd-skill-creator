# CRIT-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

This assessment framework evaluates the quality of reasoning, not the content of beliefs. Progress is measured by how a learner thinks, not what they think. A learner who evaluates evidence carefully, identifies fallacies in real-world contexts, recognizes their own biases, and changes their mind when warranted is making progress -- regardless of the specific conclusions they reach.

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing foundational awareness of reasoning quality. They notice that people can have different ideas, give simple reasons for their beliefs, and are learning to distinguish between what they observe and what they assume.

**Claims & Evidence:**
- Distinguishes fact from opinion in simple, clear-cut cases
- Gives reasons for beliefs ("I think X because Y")
- Identifies who said something and recognizes that different sources may disagree
- Understands that some things can be checked and verified

**Arguments & Reasoning:**
- Provides basic reasons to support a claim
- Recognizes that people can disagree about ideas without being enemies
- Follows simple argument chains ("If A, then B")
- Understands that "because" connects a reason to a claim

**Fallacies & Biases:**
- Notices when someone is "not being fair" in a simple argument
- Recognizes name-calling as an unfair way to argue
- Understands that wanting something to be true does not make it true
- Identifies when someone changes the subject during a disagreement

**Applied Thinking:**
- Asks "Is that true?" about some claims encountered in daily life
- Identifies basic persuasion tricks in advertising (e.g., using celebrities)
- Understands that not everything on the internet is true
- Can pause before accepting a claim and ask for reasons

**Metacognition:**
- Knows that people can make mistakes in their thinking
- Willing to say "I don't know" when uncertain
- Recognizes that they have changed their mind about some things
- Understands that being wrong is not a bad thing

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building systematic approaches to evaluating reasoning. They identify claims and evidence with increasing reliability, recognize common fallacies, and are developing awareness of their own reasoning patterns.

**Claims & Evidence:**
- Identifies claims and the evidence offered to support them
- Evaluates source reliability at a basic level (expert vs. non-expert, firsthand vs. hearsay)
- Understands the concept of burden of proof: the person making the claim provides evidence
- Distinguishes stronger evidence (data, research) from weaker evidence (anecdote, opinion)

**Arguments & Reasoning:**
- Maps simple arguments: identifies premises and conclusions
- Distinguishes deductive reasoning (certainty) from inductive reasoning (probability) with guidance
- Identifies some hidden assumptions in straightforward arguments
- Constructs basic counterarguments ("But what about...?")

**Fallacies & Biases:**
- Recognizes common informal fallacies (ad hominem, straw man, false dichotomy) in prepared examples
- Identifies confirmation bias in others' reasoning
- Understands that advertising uses emotional appeals alongside (or instead of) logical arguments
- Recognizes the bandwagon effect ("Everyone believes it, so it must be true")

**Applied Thinking:**
- Evaluates news articles for basic bias indicators (loaded language, one-sided sourcing)
- Uses simple decision frameworks to compare options
- Discusses ethical dilemmas with reasons rather than just feelings
- Identifies basic persuasion techniques in speeches and advertisements

**Metacognition:**
- Reflects on own reasoning when prompted
- Notices when emotions are influencing decisions
- Revises beliefs when presented with clear contradicting evidence
- Recognizes that confidence and accuracy are not the same thing

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level demonstrate independent critical thinking. They evaluate evidence across multiple sources, construct and evaluate multi-step arguments, identify biases in their own reasoning, and apply critical thinking to real-world situations.

**Claims & Evidence:**
- Evaluates evidence quality using an evidence hierarchy (anecdote through systematic review)
- Assesses source reliability using multiple criteria (expertise, methodology, potential bias, peer review)
- Applies proportional burden of proof: extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence
- Expresses appropriate degrees of certainty rather than binary true/false judgments

**Arguments & Reasoning:**
- Constructs and evaluates multi-step arguments with nested sub-arguments
- Distinguishes validity (correct logical form) from soundness (valid + true premises)
- Identifies hidden assumptions independently in complex arguments
- Steelmans opposing positions before constructing counterarguments

**Fallacies & Biases:**
- Identifies a wide range of fallacies in real-world contexts (media, politics, conversation)
- Recognizes cognitive biases in own thinking, not just in others
- Applies bias mitigation strategies proactively (consider-the-opposite, pre-mortem, checklists)
- Understands the fallacy fallacy: a flawed argument does not necessarily have a false conclusion

**Applied Thinking:**
- Conducts structured media analysis using evaluation rubrics
- Applies multiple ethical frameworks to the same dilemma (consequentialist, deontological, virtue)
- Uses Socratic questioning to explore assumptions and deepen understanding
- Makes decisions using explicit frameworks with identified criteria and trade-offs

**Metacognition:**
- Calibrates confidence accurately: stated confidence matches actual accuracy
- Regularly examines own assumptions and seeks disconfirming evidence
- Changes mind based on evidence without defensiveness
- Teaches reasoning skills to others effectively

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level demonstrate mastery of critical thinking across contexts. They evaluate sophisticated arguments, design bias-resistant processes, navigate genuine uncertainty, and contribute to intellectual discourse.

**Claims & Evidence:**
- Synthesizes evidence from multiple domains and methodologies
- Evaluates meta-analyses and systematic reviews critically
- Articulates epistemological principles: what counts as knowledge and why
- Navigates genuine uncertainty with intellectual honesty and appropriate calibration

**Arguments & Reasoning:**
- Constructs sophisticated arguments with nested sub-arguments and explicit qualifications
- Evaluates philosophical arguments and identifies category errors
- Identifies level confusions (e.g., confusing individual-level and group-level claims)
- Contributes to academic discourse with well-structured argumentation

**Fallacies & Biases:**
- Analyzes how fallacies and biases interact and compound in complex situations
- Designs bias-resistant decision processes for specific contexts
- Evaluates the limits of debiasing and the persistence of cognitive biases
- Recognizes subtle fallacies that masquerade as valid reasoning

**Applied Thinking:**
- Applies critical thinking to novel and genuinely complex domains
- Evaluates public policy arguments with attention to evidence, values, and trade-offs
- Leads ethical reasoning discussions with diverse participants
- Synthesizes diverse perspectives into coherent, nuanced positions

**Metacognition:**
- Demonstrates consistent intellectual humility across all domains
- Accurately assesses the boundaries of own knowledge and expertise
- Mentors others in metacognitive skills and calibration
- Models the examined life: regular, honest self-assessment of reasoning quality

## Formative Assessment

Formative assessment occurs during learning and provides feedback that guides improvement. In CRIT-101, formative assessment focuses on the quality of reasoning processes, not the correctness of specific conclusions.

### Reasoning Discourse Assessment

Observe learners during discussions and activities. Look for:
- Do they give reasons for their claims?
- Do they ask for evidence when others make claims?
- Do they acknowledge uncertainty appropriately?
- Do they respond to counterarguments rather than ignoring them?
- Do they change their position when presented with compelling evidence?

### Argument Analysis Review

When learners analyze arguments, evaluate:
- Can they identify premises and conclusions accurately?
- Do they find hidden assumptions?
- Can they distinguish valid from invalid reasoning?
- Do they evaluate the strength of evidence, not just its presence?
- Do they consider alternative explanations?

### Fallacy Recognition Check-Ins

During media analysis and discussion activities, assess:
- Can the learner spot fallacies in real-world content (not just textbook examples)?
- Can they explain why the fallacy is a problem, not just name it?
- Do they avoid committing the same fallacies in their own reasoning?
- Do they recognize the fallacy fallacy (a flawed argument may still have a true conclusion)?

### Reflection Journals

Periodic journal entries where learners reflect on:
- A belief they reconsidered this week and what prompted the reconsideration
- A bias they noticed in their own thinking and how they responded
- An argument they found persuasive and why
- Something they realized they do not understand as well as they thought

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment evaluates cumulative learning at the end of a unit or module. In CRIT-101, summative assessment should demonstrate the ability to reason carefully, evaluate evidence, and think about thinking.

### Critical Thinking Portfolio

A collection of work demonstrating growth in reasoning quality:

**Required components:**
- An argument analysis showing identification of premises, conclusions, hidden assumptions, and evaluation of validity
- A fallacy analysis of a real-world text (advertisement, editorial, or speech) with each fallacy identified, explained, and corrected
- A media evaluation demonstrating structured assessment of source reliability, evidence quality, and potential bias
- A reflection on how the learner's reasoning has developed, including specific examples of changed thinking

**Optional components:**
- A calibration report showing confidence-accuracy alignment over time
- An ethical reasoning essay applying multiple frameworks to a single dilemma
- A bias journal documenting instances of recognized and mitigated biases
- A counterargument exercise showing ability to steelman opposing positions

### Capstone Projects

Projects that demonstrate integrated mastery across multiple modules:

1. **Critical Analysis Report:** Select a controversial topic and analyze arguments on multiple sides. Evaluate the evidence and reasoning for each position. Identify fallacies and biases. Reach a reasoned conclusion with explicit acknowledgment of uncertainty and limitations.

2. **Media Literacy Audit:** Conduct a sustained evaluation of a media source (newspaper, news channel, blog, social media account) over two weeks. Document patterns of reasoning quality, common fallacies, evidence standards, and bias indicators. Present findings with specific examples.

3. **Debiasing Protocol Design:** Choose a specific decision context (hiring, medical diagnosis, investment, jury deliberation) and design a structured process to reduce the impact of cognitive biases. Explain which biases the protocol targets, how it mitigates them, and what its limitations are.

4. **Socratic Seminar Leadership:** Prepare and facilitate a Socratic seminar on a complex topic. Demonstrate ability to ask probing questions, build on participants' responses, and guide the group toward deeper understanding without imposing conclusions.

## Portfolio Suggestions

### Elementary Level (3-5)
- Fact vs. opinion sorting exercises with written explanations
- Simple argument maps (one reason supporting one conclusion)
- "Trick spotter" journal: examples of persuasion tricks noticed in daily life
- "I changed my mind" entries documenting belief revision with reasons

### Middle School Level (6-8)
- Multi-step argument analyses with hidden assumption identification
- Fallacy identification in real advertisements and media with explanations
- Source reliability evaluations comparing multiple sources on the same claim
- Ethical dilemma responses using at least two reasoning frameworks
- Calibration tracking showing confidence-accuracy alignment

### High School Level (9-12)
- Sophisticated argument evaluations with validity and soundness assessment
- Media literacy audits with sustained analysis of reasoning quality patterns
- Bias journals documenting self-recognized biases and mitigation strategies
- Steelmanning exercises showing genuine engagement with opposing positions
- Capstone projects demonstrating integrated critical thinking skills
- Calibration improvement portfolios showing growth over time
