# MFAB-101: Materials & Manufacturing -- Assessment Framework

This assessment framework evaluates material reasoning and selection skills, not factual recall. The focus is on how learners think about materials and manufacturing, not what material names or definitions they have memorized.

## Beginning

The learner is starting to notice that different materials exist and have different characteristics. At this level:

- Can name a few common materials (wood, metal, plastic) but may not distinguish within families
- Describes materials using basic sensory terms (hard, soft, heavy, light) without measurement
- Tends to pick materials based on familiarity or appearance rather than properties
- Does not yet test materials before selecting -- assumes based on looks or past experience
- May not realize that the same product could be made from different materials
- Treats all plastics as the same material and all metals as the same material
- Has limited awareness of how objects are manufactured
- May know that recycling exists but does not understand what is actually recyclable

**What success looks like at Beginning level:** The learner can identify that objects are made from specific materials and can sort materials into at least 3 groups (metal, plastic, wood/natural) by touch or observation, even if they cannot explain the properties that distinguish them.

## Developing

The learner is building material awareness and starting to connect properties to uses. At this level:

- Can identify materials in all five families with guidance (metals, plastics, ceramics, composites, natural)
- Measures at least 2-3 material properties using simple tests (strength, flexibility, weight)
- Starting to explain why certain materials are used for certain objects
- Can follow a simple fabrication process with instruction (cutting, gluing, sanding)
- Recognizes that materials have tradeoffs but may not weigh multiple criteria
- Beginning to understand that different plastics, metals, and woods have different properties
- Knows basic manufacturing categories (cutting, joining) but not selection criteria
- Can describe what recycling means and identify some recyclable materials
- Documents material tests but may not organize data systematically

**What success looks like at Developing level:** The learner can test two or three materials, compare results, and explain which material is better for a specific job and why, using at least two measured properties as evidence.

## Proficient

The learner independently applies material reasoning to design decisions. At this level:

- Classifies unfamiliar materials into the correct family based on properties
- Tests materials systematically with controlled, repeatable experiments
- Records data in organized comparison charts and draws conclusions from data
- Selects materials for design projects using multi-criteria decision matrices
- Explains why specific materials were chosen for real-world products
- Matches materials to compatible manufacturing processes
- Considers cost, availability, and environmental impact alongside performance
- Performs basic lifecycle analysis for materials in their designs
- Can identify manufacturing processes used to make common objects
- Documents material selection reasoning clearly enough for others to understand

**What success looks like at Proficient level:** The learner can take a design challenge with specific requirements, evaluate 3 or more candidate materials, use measured data and a decision matrix to select the best option, and write a clear justification that accounts for performance, cost, manufacturing, and environmental tradeoffs.

## Advanced

The learner demonstrates sophisticated material reasoning and lifecycle thinking. At this level:

- Predicts material behavior in novel applications based on property knowledge
- Integrates material selection with manufacturing process planning seamlessly
- Performs quantitative tradeoff analysis across many competing criteria
- Considers full lifecycle impact as a standard part of material selection
- Identifies materials in complex products and explains multi-material assembly choices
- Understands structure-property relationships at the molecular level
- Can evaluate and critique material selections in published designs
- Recognizes greenwashing and evaluates sustainability claims with evidence
- Mentors other learners through material selection processes
- Knows when "good enough" material selection is appropriate versus when precision matters

**What success looks like at Advanced level:** The learner can lead a material-process selection for a complex, multi-material product, producing a documented analysis that integrates performance data, manufacturing constraints, cost modeling, and lifecycle assessment, with clear justification for every tradeoff decision.

## Formative Assessment

Ongoing assessment strategies used during learning activities:

- **Property Test Review:** After material testing activities, check that data is recorded systematically. Are tests controlled (same size samples, same loading method)? Are results organized in comparison charts? Are conclusions supported by the data?
- **Family Classification Check:** Present an unfamiliar material and ask which family it belongs to. Can the learner explain their reasoning using properties, not just appearance? Can they predict other properties based on family membership?
- **Selection Reasoning Probe:** When a learner selects a material for a project, ask "Why this material?" Listen for multi-criteria reasoning (not just "it is strong" but "it is strong enough, lightweight, and costs less than the alternative"). Push for tradeoff acknowledgment.
- **Process Matching:** After learning about manufacturing processes, give a material-shape-quantity combination and ask which process would be appropriate. Can the learner explain why and identify alternatives?
- **Sustainability Check:** During material selection activities, observe whether the learner considers environmental impact spontaneously or only when prompted. Sustainability awareness should gradually become a default criterion, not an afterthought.
- **Vocabulary Development:** Listen for increasingly precise material vocabulary. "This plastic" should evolve to "this polypropylene" or "this number 5 plastic." "This wood" should evolve to "this softwood" or "this plywood."
- **Manufacturing Observation:** When examining products, can the learner identify how they were manufactured? Can they spot where parts were joined, where material was removed, where it was molded?

## Summative Assessment

Evidence of mastery gathered at key milestones:

- **Material Selection Portfolio:** A collection of 3-5 material selection exercises from different projects. Each entry documents the design requirements, candidate materials considered, property tests or research conducted, decision matrix used, final selection with justification, and any tradeoffs acknowledged. The portfolio should show growth in reasoning sophistication over time.
- **Material Analysis Report:** Given an unfamiliar multi-material product, disassemble or examine it and produce a written report identifying each material, explaining why it was chosen, describing the manufacturing processes used, evaluating the sustainability of each material choice, and proposing one material substitution that would improve some aspect (cost, weight, sustainability, durability).
- **Design Challenge:** Given a product brief with specific requirements and constraints, complete a full material-process selection. Must include requirements analysis, candidate identification, property comparison, process selection, cost estimation, sustainability evaluation, and justified final recommendation.
- **Lifecycle Presentation:** Select a common product and present its complete material lifecycle to an audience. Cover extraction, processing, manufacturing, use, and end-of-life for each material in the product. Identify the highest-impact stage and propose improvements. Answer audience questions about material choices.
- **Peer Teaching:** Explain material properties and selection methodology to someone who has not learned them. Guide them through a simple material selection exercise. Effective teaching demonstrates deep understanding of both the content and the reasoning process.

## Portfolio Suggestions

The materials portfolio should demonstrate growth in material reasoning and selection skills across multiple projects. Suggested portfolio structure:

- **3 to 5 material selection exercises** from different contexts (different products, different material families, different constraint priorities) showing breadth of material thinking
- **Each exercise includes:** design requirements, list of candidate materials, property data (tested or researched), decision matrix or comparison chart, final selection with written justification, reflection on tradeoffs made
- **A material diary entry:** describe one moment when you noticed a material choice in daily life that you would not have noticed before taking this pack (a restaurant choosing bamboo utensils, a building using exposed concrete, a product failing because of the wrong material)
- **A sustainability analysis:** for one product you use regularly, map the material lifecycle and propose a more sustainable alternative with evidence
- **A growth reflection:** a 1-page narrative describing how your understanding of materials has changed -- what do you see now that you did not see before?

Portfolios are assessed on reasoning quality (depth of property analysis and tradeoff thinking), evidence use (data-driven versus opinion-driven selections), growth evidence (improvement visible across exercises), and sustainability integration (whether environmental thinking becomes a natural part of the selection process).
