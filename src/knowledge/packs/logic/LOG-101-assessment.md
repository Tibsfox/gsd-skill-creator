# LOG-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

This assessment framework evaluates logical reasoning quality rather than factual recall. Progress is measured by a learner's ability to think clearly, construct sound arguments, recognize fallacies, and reason with precision. A learner who spots a fallacy, defends their thinking against criticism, and constructs a valid argument is making progress—regardless of how much formal notation they've memorized.

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing foundational awareness of logical structure. They recognize obvious patterns, understand simple if-then relationships, and are learning that arguments have reasons.

**Logical Thinking & Pattern Recognition:**
- Notices obvious patterns and can predict what comes next in simple sequences
- Understands "if-then" relationships in familiar contexts ("If it rains, then we stay inside")
- Sorts objects by one obvious property (color, size, shape)
- Recognizes that things follow rules or patterns

**Arguments & Reasoning:**
- Identifies what someone claims but struggles with their reasons
- Understands that statements need support but can't yet evaluate it rigorously
- Distinguishes between a reason and a non-reason (not a reason: "I don't know why")
- Attempts to give reasons for claims but often circular or vague

**Fallacies:**
- Recognizes obvious nonsense or "that doesn't make sense"
- Can identify when someone attacks the person instead of the argument (ad hominem) when explicitly pointed out
- Struggles to recognize more subtle fallacies
- May assume if something is stated loudly or by an authority, it must be true

**Symbolic Logic:**
- Understands that symbols stand for something (p = "it is raining")
- Struggles with logical connectives in symbolic form
- May confuse AND/OR logic
- Cannot yet construct truth tables independently

**Proof:**
- Understands that a proof is a series of steps
- Cannot yet write a proof independently
- May follow a provided proof step-by-step but struggle to create one
- Doesn't yet understand why proof is necessary (thinks "we can just test it")

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building systematic approaches to logical reasoning. They see argument structure, recognize some fallacies independently, translate between English and logic, and understand proof technique basics.

**Logical Thinking & Pattern Recognition:**
- Identifies patterns of increasing complexity, including rules and patterns not shown
- Understands if-then language consistently and applies it to new situations
- Sorts objects by multiple properties simultaneously
- Creates their own patterns and rules, explaining them clearly

**Arguments & Reasoning:**
- Identifies premises and conclusions consistently
- Distinguishes between a valid argument and a true conclusion
- Understands deductive reasoning and can recognize when it is valid
- Begins to recognize inductive arguments and assess their strength
- Can construct a simple argument with premises and conclusion, though may have logical gaps

**Fallacies:**
- Recognizes 3–5 common fallacies independently (ad hominem, false dilemma, hasty generalization)
- Understands that fallacies are failures of reasoning, not just "untrue" statements
- Can explain why a specific fallacy fails
- Beginning to spot fallacies in everyday arguments (advertisements, discussions)
- May still be susceptible to subtle fallacies

**Symbolic Logic:**
- Translates simple English statements to symbolic form and back
- Constructs truth tables for two-variable propositions with minor errors
- Understands AND/OR logic consistently
- Struggles with more complex propositions or longer chains
- Can identify some logical equivalences from truth tables

**Proof:**
- Understands direct proof: assume antecedent, derive consequent
- Can follow a proof and explain why each step works
- Can write simple direct proofs with 3–5 steps when given structure
- Understands proof by contradiction conceptually but struggles to write one
- Does not yet see why proof matters beyond "checking the answer"

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level use logic as a tool for thinking. They construct valid arguments, recognize subtler fallacies, work fluently with symbolic logic, and write clear proofs.

**Logical Thinking & Pattern Recognition:**
- Recognizes complex patterns and can describe the rule precisely
- Uses if-then reasoning to predict consequences and plan ahead
- Classifies concepts (not just objects) by logical properties
- Creates sophisticated rule systems with conditional logic

**Arguments & Reasoning:**
- Identifies premises and conclusions in complex texts
- Distinguishes between valid structure and true content
- Constructs multi-premise arguments that are valid and sound
- Evaluates inductive arguments for strength
- Can respond to counterarguments by distinguishing valid critique from fallacy

**Fallacies:**
- Recognizes at least 8 common fallacies and can name them
- Explains why each fallacy fails with clarity
- Spots fallacies in real-world arguments (news, political speeches, social media)
- Understands how propaganda uses fallacies to manipulate
- Constructs arguments intentionally avoiding fallacies

**Symbolic Logic:**
- Translates complex English statements to symbolic notation and back with accuracy
- Constructs truth tables for 3+ variable propositions correctly
- Identifies and proves logical equivalences (De Morgan's Laws, contrapositive, etc.)
- Recognizes valid argument forms (modus ponens, modus tollens, hypothetical syllogism)
- Applies symbolic logic to evaluate claims

**Proof:**
- Writes clear, logically valid direct proofs of 5+ steps
- Uses proof by contradiction effectively to prove statements
- Understands mathematical induction and applies it correctly
- Recognizes when to use each proof technique
- Explains why a proof works, not just the mechanics

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level are masters of logical thinking. They apply logic to novel domains, construct rigorous arguments, teach logical reasoning to others, and understand logical systems deeply.

**Logical Thinking & Pattern Recognition:**
- Creates sophisticated logical systems and rule hierarchies
- Uses logical reasoning to approach novel problems
- Recognizes logical structure in complex, real-world situations
- Teaches logical thinking to others and helps them improve

**Arguments & Reasoning:**
- Constructs rigorous, multi-stage arguments addressing counterarguments
- Analyzes logical structure in complex texts (philosophy, law, science)
- Distinguishes between different types of inductive reasoning and evaluates them rigorously
- Recognizes implicit premises and evaluates their truth
- Engages in sophisticated logical debate

**Fallacies:**
- Recognizes subtle fallacies and understands their psychological appeal
- Analyzes how fallacies are used in rhetoric, advertising, and propaganda
- Creates logical responses to fallacious arguments
- Teaches others to recognize and avoid fallacies
- Applies fallacy knowledge to critical media literacy and political reasoning

**Symbolic Logic:**
- Fluently translates between English and symbolic logic, understanding what is preserved and lost
- Constructs and manipulates truth tables for complex propositions
- Proves logical equivalences and valid argument forms
- Begins to understand predicate logic and quantifiers (all, some, none)
- Applies symbolic logic to computer science, mathematics, and philosophy problems

**Proof:**
- Writes rigorous proofs across multiple techniques (direct, contradiction, induction, cases)
- Recognizes proof patterns and applies them to novel mathematical problems
- Understands the relationship between logical validity and mathematical proof
- Begins to reason about formal systems themselves
- Teaches proof-writing to others

## Formative Assessment Strategies

### Classroom Discussions
- Ask: "Why do you think that?" and "How do you know?"
- Have learners explain their reasoning before accepting answers
- Encourage peer critique: "Does everyone agree? What would change your mind?"
- Celebrate correction: When someone spots an error or fallacy, that's progress

### Argument Analysis Activities
- Present short arguments and ask: "What's the conclusion? What are the premises? Is this valid?"
- Have learners construct arguments for their own claims ("You want X. Give me your best reasons")
- Ask: "Can you think of a case where this argument wouldn't work?"

### Fallacy Spotting
- When watching videos, reading news, or in conversation: "Do you see any fallacies here? Name it and explain why it fails"
- Collect fallacy examples from real-world sources
- Have learners create intentionally fallacious arguments for peers to critique

### Peer Review
- Learners critique each other's arguments and proofs
- Rubric: "Is this valid? Where is the strongest point? Where could it be stronger?"
- Emphasis on constructive feedback: "Here's what works. To make it stronger, you might..."

### Reflection
- Journal: "What makes a good argument? How do you know when reasoning fails?"
- Self-assessment: "Which fallacies do I catch easily? Which ones fool me?"
- Goal-setting: "What logical skill do I want to improve?"

## Summative Assessment Tasks

### 1. Argument Construction & Defense
**Task**: Construct a multi-premise argument for a claim, then defend it against counterarguments.
- Learner chooses a claim (opinion, belief, or position they want to support)
- Writes 2–3 premises supporting the claim
- Evaluates whether the argument is valid
- Anticipates at least two counterarguments and responds

**Rubric**:
- *Beginning*: Provides reasons but structure is unclear; struggles to evaluate validity
- *Developing*: Clear argument with valid structure; addresses some counterarguments
- *Proficient*: Multi-premise valid argument; thoughtful response to multiple counterarguments
- *Advanced*: Rigorous argument acknowledging complexity; sophisticated counterargument analysis

### 2. Fallacy Identification & Analysis
**Task**: Analyze a text (advertisement, speech, news article, social media post) for fallacies.
- Identifies at least 3 fallacies
- Names each fallacy correctly
- Explains why each is a fallacy (how does it fail?)
- Redesigns the argument more logically

**Rubric**:
- *Beginning*: Identifies obvious fallacies; may misname them; limited explanation
- *Developing*: Identifies 3+ fallacies; mostly correct naming; explains reasoning
- *Proficient*: Identifies both obvious and subtle fallacies; accurate naming; clear explanation; logical redesign
- *Advanced*: Analyzes rhetorical strategy; understands psychological appeal of fallacies; sophisticated redesign

### 3. Symbolic Logic & Truth Tables
**Task**: Construct truth tables, identify equivalences, or evaluate complex claims symbolically.
- Translates statements to symbolic form
- Constructs accurate truth tables for 2–3 variable propositions
- Identifies logical equivalences or proves a claim valid/invalid
- Explains the significance of the result

**Rubric**:
- *Beginning*: Translates some statements; incomplete or inaccurate truth tables
- *Developing*: Accurate translations; mostly correct truth tables; some equivalence recognition
- *Proficient*: Accurate translation and tables; identifies equivalences; correct logical evaluation
- *Advanced*: Complex propositions; proves equivalences; applies to novel logical problems

### 4. Proof Writing
**Task**: Prove a statement using an appropriate technique (direct proof, contradiction, induction, or cases).
- Statement chosen at appropriate difficulty for learner's level
- Clearly written steps
- Each step justified with a reason
- Conclusion clearly follows from premises

**Rubric**:
- *Beginning*: Attempts proof; steps unclear; many unjustified leaps
- *Developing*: Mostly coherent; most steps justified; minor logical gaps
- *Proficient*: Clear, valid proof; all steps justified; appropriate technique used
- *Advanced*: Rigorous proof; elegant approach; recognizes alternative techniques; teaches others

### 5. Real-World Application
**Task**: Apply logical reasoning to a problem in mathematics, science, decision-making, or ethics.
- Identifies the logical structure of the problem
- Constructs or evaluates arguments relevant to the problem
- Recognizes fallacies or unsupported claims in existing discourse about the problem
- Proposes a logically sound approach

**Rubric**:
- *Beginning*: Attempts to identify structure; reasoning has gaps
- *Developing*: Identifies main logical elements; mostly sound reasoning
- *Proficient*: Clear logical structure; sound reasoning throughout; recognizes fallacies
- *Advanced*: Sophisticated analysis; anticipates counterarguments; integrates multiple logical techniques

## Portfolio Suggestions

A logic portfolio might include:
- **Argument collection**: Examples of arguments analyzed or constructed at increasing complexity
- **Fallacy journal**: Fallacies spotted in real-world contexts with explanations
- **Truth tables & symbolic work**: Sample truth tables, equivalence proofs, logical translations
- **Proof portfolio**: Proofs written across different techniques, with self-reflection on clarity
- **Debates or discussions**: Transcripts or recordings of learner engaging in logical discourse
- **Real-world applications**: How learner has applied logic to their own thinking and decision-making

---

## Self-Assessment for Learners

Learners should regularly ask themselves:

**Understanding**:
- [ ] Can I explain what makes an argument valid?
- [ ] Do I recognize if-then reasoning in everyday contexts?
- [ ] Can I spot at least 5 common fallacies?
- [ ] Do I understand why a proof works, not just the steps?

**Application**:
- [ ] Can I construct a valid argument for something I believe?
- [ ] Can I respond to criticism of my arguments without dismissing it?
- [ ] Do I notice fallacies in advertisements, news, and social media?
- [ ] Can I write a clear proof when given a statement to prove?

**Growth**:
- [ ] What logical skill am I getting better at?
- [ ] What fallacies still fool me?
- [ ] What proof technique feels most natural to me?
- [ ] How has logical thinking changed how I approach problems?

## Parent & Mentor Assessment

Parents and mentors can informally assess logical thinking:

**Observations**:
- Does the learner give reasons for claims or just assertions?
- When you disagree, do they engage with your reasoning or dismiss it?
- Do they spot logical inconsistencies in arguments?
- Can they explain their thinking when asked "why" or "how do you know?"

**Prompts**:
- "Can you give me your best reasons for that?" (assesses argument construction)
- "What would change your mind?" (assesses reasoning flexibility)
- "Do you see any fallacies in what they're saying?" (assesses fallacy recognition)
- "Why do you think that way makes sense?" (assesses explanation ability)

**Red Flags** (growth opportunities):
- Asserts without reasoning
- Dismisses counterargument without engaging
- Assumes if something sounds good, it's true
- Confuses "I believe it" with "It's true"

## Assessment Across Grade Levels

### Elementary (3–5)
Focus on *argument structure* and *obvious fallacies*. Can learners identify claims and reasons? Can they spot when someone attacks the person instead of the argument?

### Middle School (6–8)
Add *fallacy naming* and *symbolic introduction*. Can learners name 5+ fallacies? Begin to understand AND/OR logic? Evaluate validity of simple arguments?

### High School (9–12)
Emphasize *symbolic fluency*, *proof writing*, and *real-world application*. Can learners construct truth tables? Write proofs? Apply logic to their own thinking and decisions?

### College & Beyond
Focus on *advanced systems* and *applications*. Can learners reason about formal systems? Apply logic in their field of study? Teach logical reasoning to others?

---

## Accommodations & Accessibility

**For learners who struggle with language**:
- Provide argument templates with blanks to fill in
- Use visual representations (argument diagrams, flowcharts)
- Allow spoken instead of written explanations
- Use examples from the learner's own experiences

**For learners who struggle with abstraction**:
- Stay concrete longer (use real-world examples before abstract notation)
- Use multiple modalities (games, diagrams, physical objects, discussions)
- Revisit key concepts at increasing levels of abstraction
- Celebrate progress at each level

**For learners who need acceleration**:
- Introduce symbolic notation earlier
- Assign proof-writing challenges
- Have them teach logical thinking to peers
- Explore advanced topics (formal systems, modal logic, philosophical logic)

---

## Conclusion

Assessment in logic focuses on reasoning quality: Can the learner think clearly? Construct valid arguments? Recognize failures of reasoning? Apply logical thinking to novel situations?

Progress is not measured by perfect answers but by increasingly sophisticated, rigorous, and flexible thinking. A learner who changes their mind when presented with a counterargument, who catches a fallacy mid-conversation, who writes a clear proof—that's success in logic.
