# TRADE-101: Trades & Applied Skills — Foundational Knowledge Pack

**Date:** 2026-02-20
**Version:** 1.0.0
**Status:** Foundational Knowledge Pack
**Context:** The skilled trades are the backbone of human civilization. Every building, bridge, road, pipe, wire, car, and machine was built or maintained by people with trade skills. Yet these professions are systematically devalued in education systems worldwide.

---

## Vision

Skilled trades are not fallback careers or lesser paths — they are intellectually demanding disciplines. A master electrician understands circuit theory, code compliance, load calculations, and customer safety. A carpenter must grasp geometry, material properties, structural loads, and precision measurement. A plumber designs systems, troubleshoots failures, and maintains public health. A welder must understand metallurgy, heat control, and strength specifications.

The gap between "knowing about" and "knowing how" matters profoundly. A student can memorize Ohm's law but not know how to safely wire a light switch. They can study leverage and load in physics but never grip a wrench or support a beam. They can understand the structure of wood in chemistry but never feel how grain direction affects cutting.

TRADE-101 teaches that **skilled work is building understanding of how the world actually works.** When you build something with your hands, you learn. When you fix something, you problem-solve in the most concrete way possible. When you create, you think like an engineer. When you trouble-shoot, you become a scientist.

This pack honors trades as what they are: civilizational infrastructure, intellectual challenge, creative satisfaction, and a viable path to prosperity and purpose.

## Problem Statement

**Why Trades Are Disappearing**

In the mid-20th century, shop class was standard in American schools. Students learned hand tools, basic woodworking, automotive fundamentals, and mechanical thinking. That knowledge became baseline: how to use a hammer, drive a nail, measure, cut, join, assemble, troubleshoot.

Today, most high schools have eliminated shop entirely. Students graduate unable to hang a picture, unclog a drain, or understand how the electrical system in their home works. Young people avoid trade professions because they lack basic tool literacy and don't see trades as viable. Employers report critical shortages of skilled tradespeople despite excellent career prospects: electricians, plumbers, carpenters, welders, and HVAC technicians earn $50,000-$100,000+ annually, own their own time, and build visible things.

**The Resulting Crisis**

The shortage is acute and growing. An apprenticeship in most trades is 4-5 years — but that training assumes foundational knowledge: ability to use basic hand tools safely, understand measurement and precision, read technical drawings, follow instructions carefully, and value safety absolutely.

Young people entering trade apprenticeships often lack this foundation. They don't know how to hold a hammer properly. They're uncomfortable with tools. Safety intuitions are absent. The apprenticeship becomes remedial education in basics that shop class once taught.

**What's at Stake**

When trades disappear from education, we lose:

1. **Tool literacy** — the ability to safely and effectively use hand tools and power tools
2. **Spatial reasoning** — understanding how 3D systems fit together, how loads distribute, how tolerances matter
3. **Problem-solving in the physical world** — diagnosing failures, designing solutions, testing and iterating
4. **Safety culture** — understanding that some things kill or injure, and respect for that reality
5. **Making things** — the deep satisfaction of creating something tangible, useful, and durable
6. **Career diversity** — young people see only office/tech/service jobs as viable options
7. **Dignity of work** — the understanding that using your hands and your mind to build or fix things is honorable

## Core Concepts

The five essential ideas that everything in this pack builds from:

1. **Tool Mastery & Safety** — Tools are extensions of the human hand. They amplify power and precision. Respect for tools means understanding their capabilities and limits. Safety protocols exist because people have died. Proper use of tools is precision, power, and safety combined. There is no substitute for hands-on practice and supervision.

2. **Materials & Properties** — Different materials behave differently. Wood has grain, strength direction, and movement. Metal has strength, weight, conductivity, and brittleness. Concrete has compressive strength but not tensile strength. Plastic has different rules than both. Choosing the right material for the job requires understanding properties: weight, strength, conductivity, durability, cost. The wrong material fails.

3. **Measurement & Layout** — Precision is everything. A 1/16-inch error compounds across projects. "Close enough" is not acceptable in structural work, electrical work, or plumbing. Measurement means understanding units, tools (rulers, levels, transits, calipers), and tolerances. Layout means marking before cutting, checking before fastening, verifying before finishing. Good trades people spend as much time measuring and planning as cutting and assembling.

4. **Joining & Assembly** — The world is made of parts joined together. Nails, screws, bolts, welds, solder joints, press fits, glue, mechanical fastening, electrical connections — every join is a potential failure point. Understanding how things join means understanding force transfer, stress concentration, fatigue, corrosion, and disassembly. The strongest join is not always the simplest. Reversibility sometimes matters.

5. **Systems Thinking** — Nothing stands alone. Electrical circuits form systems with loads, voltages, currents, and protection. Plumbing systems have pressure, flow, drainage, and venting. Structural systems have loads, supports, and paths. HVAC systems have heating, cooling, distribution, and ventilation. Building codes exist because bad systems kill people. Understanding trades means understanding how systems interconnect and fail.

## Skill Tree Architecture

Trades education progresses from basic tool literacy and safety through increasingly complex systems, independent project work, and specialization.

```
Foundation (K-2)
  ├── Tool identification and safe handling
  ├── Basic hand tools: hammer, screwdriver, wrench
  ├── Measurement with simple tools: ruler, tape measure
  ├── Basic joining: nails, simple screws
  └── Workshop safety awareness

Elementary (3-5)
  ├── Intermediate hand tools: chisels, planes, files
  ├── Precision measurement: measuring to 1/4 inch
  ├── Reading simple technical drawings
  ├── Basic woodworking: cutting, joining, finishing
  └── Introduction to electrical and plumbing concepts

Middle School (6-8)
  ├── Precision measurement: measuring to 1/16 inch, working with tolerances
  ├── Multi-step projects: planning, executing, finishing
  ├── Understanding material properties: why wood grain matters
  ├── Introduction to system thinking: circuits, water pressure, structure
  ├── Power tools under supervision
  └── Troubleshooting and problem-solving

High School (9-12)
  ├── Advanced project design and execution
  ├── Specialization paths (woodworking, electrical, plumbing, welding, automotive)
  ├── Reading and creating technical drawings
  ├── Understanding codes and standards
  ├── Cost estimation and material selection
  ├── Mentoring others on safety
  └── Real-world apprenticeship preparation

College+ (13+)
  ├── Master-level specialization
  ├── Business aspects: estimating, customer relations, liability
  ├── Teaching others the trade
  ├── Continuous learning in evolving standards
  └── Contribution to the profession
```

## Module Structure

TRADE-101 is organized into five complementary modules:

### Module 1: Tools, Safety & Workshop Practice

The foundation for all trades work. Students learn:
- Hand tool identification, proper use, and maintenance
- Power tool safety and basic operation
- Personal protective equipment (PPE) appropriate to each tool
- Workshop organization and housekeeping
- Emergency procedures and basic first aid
- Safety culture: why rules exist, how violations lead to injuries

From hammers and screwdrivers to table saws and angle grinders, from eye protection to hearing protection to cut-resistant gloves.

**Core question:** What can kill or injure you, and how do we prevent it?

### Module 2: Woodworking & Fabrication

Building things with wood. Students learn:
- Wood properties and identification (softwood, hardwood, grain)
- Measuring and marking precisely
- Cutting techniques (handsaws, circular saws, band saws)
- Joining methods (nails, screws, dowels, mortise & tenon, glue)
- Finishing (sanding, staining, sealing)
- Multi-step project planning and execution
- Reading and creating simple technical drawings

From a simple birdhouse to a dovetail joint to a small furniture project.

**Core question:** How do we join pieces of wood so they stay together and look good?

### Module 3: Electrical Fundamentals

Understanding electricity and safe wiring. Students learn:
- Electrical concepts (voltage, current, resistance, power)
- Circuits (series, parallel, protection)
- Wiring codes and safety standards
- Basic residential electrical: outlets, switches, circuit breakers
- Proper use of multimeters
- Troubleshooting electrical problems safely

From breadboard circuits to model house wiring to understanding the electrical panel in a real home.

**Core question:** What is electricity and how does it do useful work without killing us?

### Module 4: Plumbing & Mechanical Systems

Water, drainage, and HVAC. Students learn:
- Water properties and pressure
- Pipe materials and sizing
- Fittings, valves, and connections
- Drainage principles and slopes
- Venting and code requirements
- Basic HVAC concepts
- Troubleshooting leaks and blockages

From PVC pipe fitting practice to understanding a home's plumbing system to basic water pressure experiments.

**Core question:** How do we move water where we need it and remove it safely?

### Module 5: Welding, Metalwork & Construction

Joining metal and understanding structure. Students learn:
- Metal properties and types
- Metal cutting and shaping
- Welding fundamentals (arc, gas, basic techniques)
- Soldering and brazing
- Mechanical fastening with bolts
- Structural concepts and load paths
- Building codes and safety standards

From practicing basic welds to sheet metal fabrication to understanding why buildings stand.

**Core question:** How do we join metal so it holds under load?

## Assessment Approach

Assessment in TRADE-101 emphasizes **what students can do**, not what they can recite.

**Beginning Level (K-2, 3-5):** Can identify tools by sight? Can use a hammer without hitting your thumb? Can measure to the nearest inch? Can follow a two-step safety rule? Can you explain why that rule matters?

**Developing Level (5-8):** Can you select the right tool for a job? Can you measure to 1/4 inch consistently? Can you follow a four-step procedure independently? Can you explain why material properties matter? Can you use basic power tools safely under supervision?

**Proficient Level (8-12):** Can you plan and execute a multi-step project? Can you measure to 1/16 inch? Can you read a technical drawing? Can you diagnose why something failed? Can you troubleshoot electrical and plumbing problems? Can you explain code requirements and why they exist?

**Advanced Level (12+):** Can you design an original project? Can you read complex technical drawings? Can you mentor others on safety? Can you explain the reasoning behind engineering decisions? Can you work independently and make judgment calls?

All assessment is **safety-first**. A student can never be "advanced" in trades while cutting corners on safety.

## Pedagogy: Learning by Doing

The primary pedagogy is **hands-on, scaffolded practice with real materials under supervision**.

Students work with actual tools, actual materials, actual problems. They build, break, fix, and learn. They measure, cut, join, and finish. They troubleshoot when something doesn't work.

**Not appropriate for this pack:**
- Memorizing tool names from flashcards
- Reading about tools without handling them
- Watching videos of other people making things
- Theoretical study of circuits without building actual circuits

**Appropriate for this pack:**
- Hammering a real nail into real wood
- Using a real multimeter to measure voltage
- Assembling PVC pipes and fittings
- Practicing welds or soldering under supervision
- Building a real project from start to finish
- Troubleshooting a real electrical problem in a model circuit

## FAQ

**Q: Isn't this dangerous? What if kids get hurt?**

A: Yes, tools are dangerous. That's why safety is Module 1 and threaded through everything else. The answer to danger is not avoidance — it's training. A child can hurt themselves opening a can, climbing a tree, or riding a bike. We teach safe use, not abstinence.

**Q: My child wants to go to college. Why would they take trades classes?**

A: Many college students also work trades. Engineers benefit from understanding practical constraints. Doctors can fix things around their homes. College-bound students often regret not learning practical skills. TRADE-101 expands possibilities, doesn't limit them.

**Q: Isn't there a stigma around trade work?**

A: Only in education circles. In the real world, the electrician making $85K/year doesn't worry about stigma. The carpenter who owns her own business isn't concerned. The plumber who can't find enough work to handle all the calls doesn't feel bad. The stigma is cultural — and this pack helps dispel it.

**Q: Can you really learn trades in a classroom setting?**

A: Partially. A school can teach tool safety, basic techniques, design thinking, troubleshooting logic, and foundational knowledge. Real mastery requires 4-5 year apprenticeships with experienced practitioners. This pack prepares students for that apprenticeship by building foundation skills.

**Q: What's the outcome of TRADE-101?**

A: Students who complete this pack will be able to:
- Safely identify and use hand tools and basic power tools
- Measure precisely and read technical drawings
- Plan and execute multi-step projects
- Understand electrical, plumbing, and structural concepts
- Troubleshoot and problem-solve in the physical world
- Decide if they want to specialize further in trades
- Have a realistic sense of what apprenticeship requires

TRADE-101 is designed for:

- **After-school programs:** Maker spaces, community colleges, vocational schools
- **Apprenticeship preparation:** High school students considering trades

- **Homeschool:** Families with access to tools and workspace

Success requires:

1. **Supervised practice** — tools and projects require adult guidance
2. **Real materials and tools** — not simulations
3. **Project-based learning** — building real things, not worksheets
4. **Safety culture** — every activity rooted in prevention
5. **Diverse representation** — trades skills are for everyone, not just some students
6. **Connection to apprenticeships** — sustained study with master tradespeople
