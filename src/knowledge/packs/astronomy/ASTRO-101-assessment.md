# ASTRO-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

This assessment framework evaluates astronomy thinking skills rather than fact memorization. Progress is measured by the quality of observation, reasoning, and understanding, not by how many planetary facts or constellation names are memorized. A learner who asks compelling questions about cosmic scale, uses telescopes and star charts effectively, and reasons carefully from astronomical evidence is making progress — regardless of whether they know the names of all the constellations or remember which planet is fourth from the Sun.

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing foundational awareness of the cosmos and initial observation skills. They notice celestial objects, ask questions, and are learning the difference between what they directly observe and what requires reasoning or models.

**Observation & Sky Navigation:**
- Notices Moon in the daytime sky and recognizes it's the same object seen at night
- Identifies one or two bright constellations (Orion, Big Dipper)
- Understands that stars don't twinkle but planets can (without knowing why)
- Can point to where the Sun rises and sets

**Scales & Distances:**
- Knows that stars are very far away (recognizes they're "far")
- Understands Earth is round and humans live on its surface
- Recognizes that the Moon is closer than stars
- Begins to grasp "light-year" as a distance concept, though may confuse it with time

**Planetary Thinking:**
- Names the Sun, Moon, and some planets
- Knows Earth is a planet
- Recognizes that planets are spheres
- Observes the Moon looks different at different times

**Model Building:**
- Watches and listens to demonstrations of seasons, eclipses, and phases
- Follows along with physical models (globes, balls, lamps)
- Records observations in a journal or drawing
- Asks "Why?" questions about phenomena they observe

**Communication:**
- Describes what they see in the night sky using simple language
- Draws pictures of constellations or the Moon
- Shares observations with peers
- Uses terms like "planet," "star," "Moon" appropriately if imprecisely

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building systematic observation skills and beginning to understand the geometry and physics that explain celestial phenomena. They can use tools like star charts and planispheres, observe over extended periods, and make connections between observation and reasoning.

**Observation & Sky Navigation:**
- Uses a planisphere or star chart to locate named constellations
- Tracks the Moon's phase over several weeks
- Observes planets and notes their position relative to stars
- Understands that constellations move throughout the night and throughout the year
- Uses angle estimation ("hand-span," "fist") to measure distances in the sky

**Scales & Distances:**
- Understands light-years represent distance, not time
- Grasps that the Sun is much farther from Earth than the Moon is
- Can estimate relative planetary distances (Saturn is farther than Jupiter)
- Begins to understand that empty space is vast

**Planetary Thinking:**
- Describes planetary properties: size, color, position, whether it has moons
- Uses comparative planetology: "Mars is smaller than Earth," "Venus is hotter than Earth"
- Recognizes that planets and Earth follow similar rules (gravity, orbits)
- Understands that planets formed together in the solar system

**Model Building:**
- Creates or uses models (globe, scale models, digital planetariums like Stellarium)
- Explains phenomena using models: "The Moon phases happen because..."
- Makes predictions using models and then checks them against observations
- Understands the purpose of models: approximations that reveal key ideas

**Reasoning About Phenomena:**
- Explains why the Sun appears to move (Earth's rotation, not the Sun moving)
- Explains Moon phases geometrically (Moon's position relative to Earth and Sun)
- Predicts Moon phases or seasons for future dates using models
- Uses evidence to reason: "We know stars are far away because..."

**Communication:**
- Writes simple explanations of astronomy phenomena
- Creates labeled diagrams showing celestial objects and their relationships
- Presents observations to peers and listens to feedback
- Uses technical terms (orbit, rotation, revolution, eclipse) appropriately

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level demonstrate independent astronomical thinking. They design their own observations, analyze real data, reason from evidence about phenomena they can't directly see, and understand the connection between observation and theory.

**Observation & Sky Navigation:**
- Uses star charts and digital tools (Stellarium, planetarium apps) to explore the sky independently
- Plans observing sessions with specific targets and questions
- Records detailed observations: sketches, measurements, times
- Recognizes observational limitations and can work around them (light pollution, weather)
- Uses binoculars or telescopes appropriately for different targets

**Scales & Distances:**
- Grasps enormous distances: nearest star is 4.37 light-years, Andromeda is 2.5 million light-years
- Understands that light takes time to travel and therefore observes the past
- Predicts comparative distances using scale reasoning
- Understands logarithmic scales: thinking across factors of millions or billions
- Recognizes the implications of scale: most of the universe is empty space

**Planetary Thinking:**
- Understands comparative planetology: why Venus is a runaway greenhouse, why Mars lost its atmosphere
- Reads and interprets planetary images and data from missions
- Reasons about planetary geology from visual evidence
- Understands exoplanet discovery methods and what the data reveals
- Connects planetary science to Earth: understanding weathering, volcanism, tectonics

**Stellar Thinking:**
- Uses spectroscopy or spectral data to extract information about stars
- Reads the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram and interprets stellar properties
- Understands main sequence, giants, and white dwarfs as distinct stellar populations
- Traces stellar evolution: main sequence → giant → planetary nebula → white dwarf
- Understands that stellar life depends on mass

**Cosmology & the Universe:**
- Understands the evidence for the Big Bang: Hubble expansion, cosmic microwave background
- Grasps that galaxies are moving away from each other as space expands
- Reasons about dark matter and dark energy despite not directly observing them
- Understands uncertainty in cosmology: what we know, what we don't, what we're unsure about
- Makes predictions about cosmic structure and checks against data

**Model Building & Analysis:**
- Designs and implements observational studies to test ideas
- Builds mathematical or computational models to predict phenomena
- Analyzes errors and limitations in observations and models
- Iterates: observes, models, predicts, checks, refines

**Communication & Reasoning:**
- Constructs evidence-based arguments for astronomical ideas
- Critiques others' reasoning about astronomy using evidence
- Explains technical concepts (spectroscopy, orbital mechanics, cosmological redshift) clearly
- Connects astronomy to other domains: physics, chemistry, geology, history

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level demonstrate mastery of astronomical thinking. They design sophisticated investigations, reason about speculative domains (dark matter, the Big Bang), engage with real research, and teach others.

**Research & Investigation:**
- Designs original observational or analytical investigations
- Works with real astronomical data from professional archives
- Analyzes published research papers and critically evaluates methods
- Contributes to citizen science projects (galaxy classification, supernova hunting, etc.)
- Understands how astronomers develop and test hypotheses

**Theoretical Understanding:**
- Understands the physics underlying astronomical phenomena: gravity, thermodynamics, atomic physics
- Reasons about extreme conditions: neutron stars, black holes, the early universe
- Grasps the role of general relativity in modern astronomy
- Understands why certain questions are hard to answer (dark matter composition, the Big Bang's cause)

**Data Analysis & Interpretation:**
- Works with spectroscopic data to determine stellar composition and temperature
- Analyzes light curves to detect exoplanets or track supernovae
- Uses statistical methods to extract meaning from astronomical data
- Recognizes sources of error and uncertainty in observations
- Designs experiments to test competing hypotheses

**Teaching & Communication:**
- Explains complex astronomy concepts clearly to diverse audiences
- Leads observing sessions or planetarium shows
- Publishes findings or creates public communications about astronomy
- Mentors other learners in astronomical investigation
- Anticipates misconceptions and addresses them proactively

**Metacognition & Philosophy:**
- Reflects on the nature of astronomical knowledge: what can we know about distant objects
- Understands the interplay between observation, theory, and experiment
- Appreciates the historical development of astronomical ideas
- Grasps the cosmic perspective: how astronomy informs human meaning and place in the universe
- Engages with philosophical questions astronomy raises

## Formative Assessment

Formative assessment should happen throughout learning, informing next steps.

**Observation Sessions:**
- Plan a simple observing session with a specific target (constellation, planet, Moon feature)
- Observe for 10-15 minutes and record what you see
- Sketch at least one observation
- Note one thing you're curious about and want to investigate next
- Reflect: How confident are you using tools like star charts or apps?

**Model Creation:**
- Build or use a model of a phenomenon (seasons, Moon phases, solar eclipse, scale model)
- Use the model to make a prediction
- Check your prediction against observation or data
- Reflect: Where does the model match reality? Where does it break down?

**Data Interpretation:**
- Given spectra, light curves, or images, describe what you observe
- What does the data tell you about the object?
- What doesn't it tell you?
- What would you measure next to answer your question?

**Question Refinement:**
- Start with a broad astronomy question (Why do we have seasons? What are black holes?)
- Refine it to something testable (How does axial tilt affect seasonal temperature? How do black holes form?)
- Identify what observations or data would help answer it

**Peer Discussion:**
- Discuss an astronomy concept with a peer
- Listen to their explanation
- Ask clarifying questions
- Propose an alternative explanation and discuss evidence

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment should capture overall understanding at meaningful checkpoints.

**Observation Portfolio:**
- Collect 4-6 weeks of regular observations (daily or every few days)
- Include sketches, written descriptions, measurements, times
- Write a brief reflection on what you learned from systematic observation
- Can you explain patterns you observed?

**Concept Explanation:**
- Choose a major astronomy concept (seasons, Moon phases, stellar evolution, cosmic expansion)
- Explain it clearly enough that someone unfamiliar with astronomy could understand
- Use diagrams, models, or analogies
- Address: Why does this matter? How do we know this?

**Data Analysis Project:**
- Work with real astronomical data (spectra, light curves, planetary images, etc.)
- Extract meaning from the data: What does it tell you?
- Present your findings clearly with visualizations
- Reflect: What uncertainties remain? What would you measure next?

**Research Presentation:**
- Research a current astronomy topic (exoplanet discoveries, gravitational waves, dark matter searches, etc.)
- Explain what the question is and why it matters
- Describe the evidence and observations
- Assess current understanding: What do we know? What's uncertain? What's next?

**Demonstration of Understanding:**
- Teach a peer about an astronomy concept or observation technique
- Answer their questions
- Adjust your explanation based on their feedback
- Can they explain it back to you?

## Portfolio Suggestions

Build a portfolio documenting growth in astronomical thinking over time.

**Observation Records:**
- Monthly Moon observation sketches showing the phase progression
- Seasonal constellation observations showing which constellations are visible when
- Planet observations tracking their motion relative to background stars
- Notes on observing conditions, tools used, and reflections

**Models & Explanations:**
- Diagrams or sketches explaining seasons, Moon phases, eclipses, tides
- Scale models or digital representations of the solar system
- Spectra interpretations identifying elements
- HR diagram analyses identifying stellar types

**Data Interpretation Work:**
- Annotations of planetary images explaining geological features
- Spectroscopy data showing element identification
- Light curve analysis of variable stars or exoplanet transits
- Galaxy classification examples

**Questions & Investigations:**
- Log of questions asked about the cosmos and follow-up investigations
- Original observing plans and their outcomes
- Failed predictions and what you learned from them
- Reflections on what remains mysterious

**Growth Reflections:**
- Where have you grown in observation skills?
- How has your understanding of scale changed?
- What aspects of astronomy now feel understandable vs. still mysterious?
- How would you explain astronomy's importance to someone unfamiliar with it?

## Assessment Don'ts

Assessment should NOT:
- Require memorizing planetary facts (number of moons, surface gravity, etc.)
- Penalize not knowing constellation names if the learner can use tools to find them
- Reduce astronomy to math problems without physical understanding
- Focus on right answers instead of reasoning quality
- Discourage speculation about frontier questions (dark matter, multiverse, etc.)
- Create pressure or anxiety about "getting the right answer" to cosmological questions

Assessment SHOULD:
- Value curiosity and questioning
- Reward careful observation and honest reporting of what you see
- Assess reasoning quality, not answer memorization
- Credit effort in understanding difficult concepts
- Recognize that astronomy involves genuine unknowns (frontier science)
- Celebrate growth in thinking, not just final performance

## Accessibility & Differentiation

**For Learners with Visual Impairments:**
- Use audio descriptions of astronomical observations
- Employ tactile models of planetary positions and orbital mechanics
- Use sonification (converting data to sound) for spectral analysis
- Focus on non-visual ways to understand scale and distance

**For Learners Who Struggle with Reading:**
- Use diagrams and visuals extensively
- Offer video explanations alongside written materials
- Use audiobook versions of astronomy content
- Pair written material with discussion and hands-on activities

**For Advanced Learners:**
- Access real research-grade data and analysis tools
- Explore sophisticated theoretical concepts (general relativity, quantum field theory in astrophysics)
- Design original research projects
- Mentor other learners

**For Learners with Physical Limitations:**
- Use digital planetarium tools (Stellarium, NASA Eyes) instead of live observation
- Adapt observational activities to accessible locations
- Use virtual tours of observatories and space missions
- Focus on data analysis rather than hands-on model building (though both are possible)

---

## Connection to Growth Mindset

Astronomy assessment should emphasize growth and improvement:

- "This is difficult because you're learning it, not because you can't understand it"
- "Astronomers don't know most of the answers either — that's why we do research"
- "Getting better at observing takes practice; everyone starts where you are"
- "Wrong predictions are learning opportunities, not failures"
- "The fact that you wonder about these things shows you're thinking like an astronomer"

The history of astronomy is a history of humans asking better questions and revising answers based on evidence. That's the spirit of assessment: not judging knowledge, but honoring the process of inquiry.

---

## Final Notes

The best assessment in astronomy is curiosity. If a learner is asking questions about the cosmos, using tools to investigate, and building understanding through observation and reasoning, they're engaging in real astronomy. The specific constellation names or planetary facts matter far less than the development of cosmic thinking — the ability to grasp vast scales, reason from indirect evidence, appreciate both what we know and what remains mysterious, and maintain wonder while pursuing understanding.
