# LANG-101: Assessment Framework

## Core Assessment Principle

Language proficiency is not about knowing grammar rules or vocabulary lists. It's about communicative competence: Can the learner understand what they hear/read? Can they make themselves understood? Can they handle real-world communication in the target language?

Assessment should measure **ability to communicate**, not **knowledge about language**. This means:
- Focus on comprehension and production in authentic scenarios
- Prioritize growth over absolute performance
- Provide formative feedback for improvement, not just summative judgment
- Assess across multiple modalities (listening, speaking, reading, writing, cultural understanding)
- Recognize errors as evidence of learning, not failure

---

## ACTFL-Aligned Proficiency Levels

Assessment in LANG-101 uses proficiency levels adapted from ACTFL (American Council on the Teaching of Foreign Languages), the standard framework for language assessment in U.S. schools and universities.

### Beginning (Novice Range)

**Listening:** Learner understands only isolated words and high-frequency phrases. Requires native speaker to speak slowly and clearly.

**Speaking:** Production is limited to memorized words and phrases. Speech is slow, hesitant, with frequent pauses. Learner relies on one-word answers and prefabricated responses.

**Reading:** Learner recognizes only high-frequency words and simple, predictable text. Requires significant visual support (pictures, context clues).

**Writing:** Learner writes only isolated words or memorized phrases. Sentences are formulaic with frequent errors.

**Cultural Understanding:** Minimal. Learner is aware target language region exists but understands little about culture, values, or perspectives.

---

### Developing (Intermediate Range)

**Listening:** Learner understands most words in high-frequency, concrete contexts. Comprehension of authentic native speech is still limited; requires repetition and support.

**Speaking:** Learner produces simple sentences about familiar topics. Speech is slow with noticeable accent. Makes frequent grammatical errors but meaning is generally clear. Can handle simple social interactions (greetings, introductions, basic questions about familiar topics).

**Reading:** Learner reads short, simple texts on familiar topics. Uses context clues and bilingual support. Comprehension is adequate for survival reading (signs, menus, simple messages).

**Writing:** Learner writes simple sentences and short paragraphs. Errors are common but don't significantly impede meaning. Can write about familiar topics in basic form.

**Cultural Understanding:** Learner is aware of some cultural practices and differences. May stereotype or oversimplify.

---

### Proficient (Advanced Range)

**Listening:** Learner understands most of what native speakers say on familiar and some unfamiliar topics. Can follow conversations with native speakers, understand most of native-speed speech, though may miss some idioms or cultural references.

**Speaking:** Learner speaks fluently on familiar topics with generally accurate grammar. Pronunciation is mostly clear with occasional accent. Can handle complex social situations, explain ideas, express opinions. Makes errors but self-corrects or clarifies when communication breaks down.

**Reading:** Learner reads and comprehends authentic materials: articles, literature (age-appropriate), messages from native speakers. Can infer meaning of unfamiliar words from context. Reading speed approaches that of English reading.

**Writing:** Learner writes coherent paragraphs and can handle a variety of writing tasks (messages, summaries, short essays). Grammar and spelling are generally accurate. Can express complex ideas in writing.

**Cultural Understanding:** Learner demonstrates understanding of target culture and appreciates cultural differences. Can explain some cultural practices and begins to understand worldview differences.

---

### Advanced (Superior Range)

**Listening:** Learner understands native speakers in any context — conversations, lectures, media, dialects. Comprehends nuance, humor, sarcasm, cultural references. No longer needs special accommodations.

**Speaking:** Learner speaks fluently, accurately, and idiomatically. Pronunciation is near-native. Can engage in sophisticated discussion of complex topics, argue positions, narrate in multiple tenses, hypothesize. Sounds like an educated native speaker.

**Reading:** Learner reads literature, academic texts, journalism, and specialized materials with comprehension and analytical ability. Can engage critically with text, recognize author's purpose and perspective.

**Writing:** Learner writes for various purposes and audiences with sophistication. Can write persuasive essays, analyze literature, handle specialized writing. Errors are minor and rare.

**Cultural Understanding:** Learner has nuanced understanding of target culture, recognizes cultural variation within the language region, understands cultural values and worldview differences. Views culture as complex, not stereotyped.

## Formative Assessment Strategies

Formative assessment provides ongoing feedback to support learning. These strategies help learners improve while building skill.

### Module-Specific Formative Assessment

**Module 1: Sound Systems & Phonetics**
- Minimal pairs discrimination tasks (can learner hear the difference?)
- Pronunciation recording and self-feedback (learner records and compares to native speaker)
- IPA transcription exercises (can learner describe sounds using IPA?)
- Listening discrimination activities with immediate feedback
- Peer feedback on pronunciation attempts (supportive, specific feedback)

**Module 2: Grammar Patterns & Sentence Structure**
- Pattern identification in real language samples
- Sentence transformation exercises (active to passive, question formation, etc.)
- Error analysis: identifying and explaining errors in sentences
- Generating novel sentences using learned patterns
- Comparing patterns across languages to build understanding

**Module 3: Vocabulary & Reading Comprehension**
- Spaced repetition progress tracking (Are words being retained?)
- Cloze activities (fill-in-the-blank in context) — assess whether vocabulary knowledge transfers to real reading
- Reading comprehension questions on authentic materials
- Inferring word meanings from context
- Vocabulary quiz with varied item types (recognition, recall, use in context)

**Module 4: Speaking & Listening**
- Listening comprehension checks after authentic audio (Could learner understand?)
- Conversation participation observation (Does learner engage? Do they self-repair? Do they ask for clarification?)
- Speaking rubrics assessing fluency, accuracy, and appropriateness
- Recording analysis (learner listens to own speech and reflects on accuracy)
- Guided peer feedback on speaking attempts

**Module 5: Language, Culture & Identity**
- Reflection essays on cultural observations and language-culture connections
- Cultural analysis activities (What do these gestures/proverbs/customs reveal about values?)
- Perspective-taking discussions (How might this be understood differently in the target culture?)
- Personal language learning journey reflections
- Observations about how language shapes their own thinking

## Summative Assessment

Summative assessment evaluates overall achievement at end of unit or term. Should still emphasize communicative competence over rule knowledge.

### Performance-Based Summative Tasks

**Listening Assessment:** Learner listens to authentic material (podcast, video, conversation) and demonstrates comprehension (takes notes, answers questions, summarizes in English, translates key ideas).

**Speaking Assessment:** Learner engages in sustained conversation on assigned topic (with prompt card or partner), demonstrates fluency, accuracy, and appropriate use of target structures.

**Reading Assessment:** Learner reads authentic text and demonstrates comprehension through discussion, written response, or analysis of author's perspective.

**Writing Assessment:** Learner writes piece for real or simulated audience (email to pen pal, social media post, short article, personal narrative) demonstrating appropriate grammar, vocabulary, and organization.

**Integrated Assessment:** Learner completes task requiring multiple skills (e.g., listen to interview, read related article, write summary, discuss with classmates). Mimics real-world language use.

### Less Effective Summative Tasks (Avoid These)

❌ Multiple-choice grammar quizzes ("Which verb form is correct?")
❌ Verb conjugation tables ("Conjugate 'to be' in all tenses")
❌ Vocabulary lists ("Translate these 50 words")
❌ Fill-in-the-blank grammar exercises
❌ Translation (English to target language in isolation)

These measure rule knowledge or translation ability, not communicative competence.

## Portfolio Assessment

Language learning is fundamentally about growth. Portfolio assessment tracks this growth over time.

**Portfolio Contents:**
- Early language samples (recordings, writing samples) — to show growth
- Progress reflections ("What was hard? What improved? What am I most proud of?")
- Video recordings of conversation over time — visualizes fluency growth
- Reading materials sampled over time — shows progress from simplified to authentic
- Self-assessments against proficiency rubrics — builds metacognition
- Goals and evidence toward goals — makes learning directed

Portfolio assessment is powerful because:
- It makes growth visible (comparing early to later work is motivating)
- It builds metacognition (learners understand their own development)
- It's authentic (it's real language, not test tasks)
- It's motivating (learners see tangible evidence of improvement)

## Rubric: LANG-101 Communicative Competence

Use this rubric to assess learner ability across all modules:

| Criterion | Beginning | Developing | Proficient | Advanced |
|-----------|-----------|------------|-----------|----------|
| **Comprehension** | Understands only isolated words; requires heavy repetition | Understands most in familiar contexts; needs some repetition | Understands most; can catch meaning from context | Understands across contexts, accents, registers |
| **Production** | Limited to memorized words/phrases; slow, hesitant | Simple sentences on familiar topics; noticeable accent; frequent errors | Fluent speech on familiar topics; accurate grammar; some accent | Near-native fluency; accurate across complex topics |
| **Interaction** | Relies on one-word answers; doesn't initiate | Can sustain simple conversation; sometimes asks for help | Can sustain conversation; self-corrects; asks for clarification | Engages in sophisticated dialogue; handles communication breakdown smoothly |
| **Accuracy** | Many errors; some communication breakdown | Errors common but don't significantly impede meaning | Errors rare; don't impede communication | Errors minimal; near-native accuracy |
| **Cultural Awareness** | Minimal awareness | Some awareness of differences; may stereotype | Understands cultural differences; appreciates practices | Nuanced understanding; recognizes cultural variation and complexity |

## Self-Assessment: Learner Proficiency Checklist

Learners should regularly assess themselves. This builds metacognition and autonomy. Learners check the proficiency level where they can do these things:

**Beginning Level — I can:**
- [ ] Recognize and understand basic greetings and high-frequency words
- [ ] Produce memorized words and very simple phrases
- [ ] Recognize some cultural differences
- [ ] Identify some sounds that are different in the target language

**Developing Level — I can:**
- [ ] Understand most of a conversation about familiar topics
- [ ] Ask basic questions and respond to simple questions about myself
- [ ] Read short, simple texts with help
- [ ] Write simple sentences about familiar topics
- [ ] Explain some cultural practices and values

**Proficient Level — I can:**
- [ ] Understand conversations on familiar and some new topics
- [ ] Have sustained conversations and handle some unexpected topics
- [ ] Read authentic materials with comprehension
- [ ] Write paragraphs expressing ideas
- [ ] Explain how language and culture are connected
- [ ] Recognize patterns in grammar and vocabulary

**Advanced Level — I can:**
- [ ] Understand native speakers across contexts and topics
- [ ] Have sophisticated conversations on complex topics
- [ ] Read literature and specialized materials
- [ ] Write for various purposes and audiences
- [ ] Analyze cultural concepts with nuance
- [ ] Recognize how language shapes thought and perception

## Reducing Assessment Anxiety

Language learners often experience anxiety: fear of making mistakes, of being judged, of "not being good enough." Assessment should reduce this, not increase it.

**Best Practices:**
- **Separate assessment from grades** when possible. Assessment is information for learning, not judgment.
- **Emphasize growth** — How much have you improved since last month? Celebrate improvement, not perfection.
- **Make it low-stakes** — Not every speaking attempt should be graded. Most should be practice with feedback.
- **Peer assessment** — Sometimes have peers give feedback; builds community and reduces teacher-as-judge dynamic.
- **Self-assessment** — Learners assessing their own work builds autonomy and reduces anxiety about external judgment.
- **Celebrate approximation** — "You're trying a new structure and making errors. That's exactly how learning works. Great effort!"
- **Focus on communication** — If I understood you, you succeeded. Grammar perfection is secondary.

## Assessment Accommodations

Language learners are diverse. Some assessments may need accommodations:

- **Extended time** — Language processing takes longer; extending test time is fair
- **Oral instead of written** — Some learners are more oral; offer both options
- **Bilingual support** — Allowing use of dictionary or bilingual reference is fair and realistic
- **Graphic organizers** — Structured templates help organize thinking and reduce cognitive load
- **Reduced linguistic complexity** — Assessment of content can be done in simpler language structures
- **Authentic materials** — Assessing on real materials rather than simplified textbooks is often more fair; learners know this material is real

---

## Conclusion

Assessment in language learning should measure **what matters**: Can learners communicate? How much have they grown? What do they need help with next? The assessment methods here emphasize communicative competence, growth, and learner agency over grammar mastery and scores.
