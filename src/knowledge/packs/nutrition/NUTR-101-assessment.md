# NUTR-101 Assessment Framework

## Overview

This assessment framework evaluates nutritional understanding, food systems literacy, and healthy relationships with food rather than compliance with dietary rules. Progress is measured by the quality of thinking, not by eating a "correct" diet.

A learner who asks better questions about food, evaluates claims more critically, understands food systems more deeply, and makes conscious food choices is making progress — regardless of their individual food preferences, cultural practices, or family circumstances.

---

## Beginning

Learners at the Beginning level are developing foundational awareness of nutrition and food. They notice basic food categories, ask broad questions about nutrition, and are learning to recognize that different foods serve different purposes.

**Nutrients & Functions:**
- Notices that foods are different from each other
- Identifies some foods as having specific nutrients (with guidance)
- Recognizes basic categories: fruits, vegetables, proteins, grains, dairy
- Uses simple language to describe nutrients ("gives you energy")

**Digestion & Body Systems:**
- Knows that food goes to the stomach
- Recognizes that the body "uses" food somehow
- May be uncertain about the process between eating and body function
- Asks curious questions ("What happens after you swallow?")

**Food Systems:**
- Notices that food comes from somewhere
- May think food comes from stores without deeper understanding
- Asks questions about farms and food origins
- Beginning awareness that there are many people involved in food

**Nutritional Literacy:**
- Notices packages have labels
- Cannot yet read or interpret labels independently
- Asks questions about food marketing ("Why does the package show a happy kid?")
- May believe food advertising without question

**Health Integration:**
- Notices loose connections ("I feel tired after big meals", "Sugar makes me hyper")
- Doesn't yet connect nutrition to sleep, stress, or activity systematically
- Food choices driven primarily by taste preference or family pattern
- Developing awareness of personal hunger and fullness

---

## Developing

Learners at the Developing level are building systematic understanding of nutrition science. They understand basic nutrient functions, can trace digestion in simplified form, recognize food system complexities, evaluate simple claims, and are learning to apply nutrition knowledge to meal choices.

**Nutrients & Functions:**
- Identifies macronutrients (proteins, carbs, fats) and basic functions
- Names several vitamins and minerals and what they do
- Reads and interprets basic nutrition facts panels
- Understands that different foods have different nutrient profiles
- Recognizes that balanced nutrition means variety

**Digestion & Body Systems:**
- Traces food path through mouth → stomach → intestines (simplified)
- Understands that the small intestine is where nutrients enter the bloodstream
- Recognizes that different nutrients are processed differently
- Asks questions about factors affecting digestion

**Food Systems:**
- Traces a food from farm to table with help
- Understands basic agricultural practices (farming, harvesting, transportation)
- Recognizes that people work to produce food
- Asks questions about where food grows and who grows it
- Beginning awareness of food security (some people have food, others don't)

**Nutritional Literacy:**
- Reads nutrition labels with guidance
- Understands basic structure: nutrition facts panel, ingredients, serving size
- Evaluates simple claims ("Does this food really give you energy?") with support
- Beginning to notice marketing language ("Natural", "Healthy")
- Asks questions about food advertising

**Health Integration:**
- Notices that eating affects energy, mood, and digestion
- Beginning to understand that nutrition connects to sleep and stress
- Makes some food choices based on how foods make them feel
- Learning to listen to hunger and fullness cues

---

## Proficient

Learners at the Proficient level demonstrate solid, grade-appropriate understanding of nutrition science. They understand nutrients at appropriate depth, can explain digestion, recognize food systems complexities, evaluate nutrition claims using evidence criteria, and apply nutritional knowledge to real food choices.

**Nutrients & Functions:**
- Explains macronutrient functions in detail (protein for growth, carbs for energy, fats for cell structure and hormone production, etc.)
- Identifies key vitamins and minerals and explains their roles (calcium for bones, iron for oxygen transport, Vitamin C for immunity, etc.)
- Reads and interprets nutrition facts labels accurately
- Compares similar foods using nutrition information
- Understands that nutrient needs vary by person (age, size, activity, health status)
- Recognizes individual variation in nutrition (intolerances, allergies, preferences)

**Digestion & Body Systems:**
- Explains the full digestive process in age-appropriate detail
- Describes the role of stomach acid, enzymes, and bile
- Understands nutrient absorption in the small intestine
- Recognizes factors affecting digestion (food combinations, hydration, stress, health)
- Asks informed questions about digestive health
- Connects digestion to overall body function

**Food Systems:**
- Traces a food from source to table independently
- Understands multiple agricultural practices (conventional, organic, regenerative, local)
- Discusses trade-offs in food production (yield vs. environmental impact, cost vs. sustainability)
- Recognizes food security and food justice as real issues
- Understands environmental impacts of food (water use, pesticides, carbon emissions, land use)
- Appreciates cultural and regional variation in food systems

**Nutritional Literacy:**
- Reads and interprets nutrition labels independently and accurately
- Evaluates nutrition claims using basic evidence criteria
- Distinguishes between correlation and causation in food studies
- Identifies marketing language and tactics
- Asks informed questions about nutrition information sources
- Understands basic research design and identifies conflicts of interest
- Makes conscious decisions about what to believe

**Health Integration:**
- Understands connections between nutrition and sleep, stress, physical activity, mental health
- Creates nutritionally balanced meals that reflect personal preferences and cultural practices
- Makes food choices informed by evidence, personal values, and joy
- Demonstrates a healthy, sustainable relationship with food (flexibility, no food morality)
- Reflects on food choices without shame or judgment
- Advocates for their own food needs

---

## Advanced

Learners at the Advanced level synthesize nutrition knowledge across multiple contexts and apply it at higher levels of complexity. They may pursue specialized study or take leadership roles in food-related work.

**Nutrients & Functions:**
- Integrates biochemistry with nutrition (understands metabolic pathways at high school or college level)
- Explains enzyme activity, ATP production, protein synthesis
- Understands nutrient interactions and bioavailability
- Evaluates controversial nutrition topics using primary research
- Recognizes limitations of nutrition science (individual variation, research challenges, evolving knowledge)

**Digestion & Body Systems:**
- Explains digestion at cellular level, including hormone regulation
- Understands the gut microbiome and its role in health
- Discusses digestive disorders and their management
- Connects nutrition to broader physiological systems
- Engages with cutting-edge research in nutrition science

**Food Systems:**
- Analyzes food systems critically using systems thinking
- Evaluates sustainability, equity, and environmental impact comprehensively
- Understands food policy and its effects
- Engages with food justice and equity issues
- May advocate for or help create more just and sustainable food systems
- Connects food systems to global issues (climate, poverty, health equity)

**Nutritional Literacy:**
- Critically analyzes peer-reviewed nutrition research
- Understands research methodology, limitations, and potential bias
- Evaluates claims using sophisticated evidence criteria
- Identifies and discusses conflicts of interest and funding influences
- Communicates nutrition science clearly to others
- Teaches others to evaluate claims critically

**Health Integration:**
- Makes food choices consciously, aligned with personal values and evidence
- Integrates nutrition with other health dimensions systematically
- May specialize in nutrition-related fields (nutrition science, food systems, public health, etc.)
- Advocates for policies and practices that support nutritional health
- Demonstrates sophisticated understanding of food, culture, equity, and justice

## Formative Assessment (Ongoing)

Formative assessment happens continuously, informing instruction and supporting learning.

### Discussion & Questions
- Quality of questions about food and nutrition
- Depth of thinking in class discussions
- Ability to ask follow-up questions and push thinking deeper
- Respectful engagement with different food practices and values

### Observation Activities
- Observation journals (noting foods, digestion, energy, mood, sleep)
- Careful attention during label reading or food tasting
- Engagement with hands-on activities (enzyme experiments, food mapping, etc.)

### Sorting & Categorization
- Ability to categorize foods by nutrient type
- Accuracy of sorting activities
- Reasoning for sorting choices

### Label Reading & Interpretation
- Can locate information on labels
- Accuracy of reading and interpreting numbers
- Comparison and analysis of multiple labels

### Investigations & Experiments
- Quality of observation and data recording
- Thoughtfulness of conclusions
- Ability to connect results to learning goals

### Peer Teaching
- Can explain concepts to classmates
- Quality of explanation (clarity, accuracy, depth)
- Ability to answer peers' questions

### Written & Creative Work
- Clarity of written explanations
- Thoughtfulness of meals or plans designed
- Quality of visual representations (maps, diagrams, infographics)

---

## Summative Assessment (End of Module/Unit)

Summative assessment evaluates what learners have learned after instruction.

### Portfolio
- Compilation of work throughout the unit (observations, reflections, best work)
- Learner self-selection of pieces showing growth
- Written reflection: "What did I learn? What am I still wondering? How has my thinking changed?"
- Demonstrates growth from beginning to proficient

### Nutrition Claim Evaluation
- Given a real nutrition claim, learner evaluates evidence
- Identifies sources, assesses quality, reaches conclusion
- Explains reasoning clearly
- Demonstrates critical thinking

### Meal or Meal Plan Design
- Designs a nutritionally balanced meal or week of meals
- Considers learner's own preferences, constraints (budget, allergies, time)
- Explains nutritional choices made
- Demonstrates understanding of balance and nutrition

### Food System Tracing
- Selects a food and traces from source to consumption
- Documents the journey (farming, processing, distribution, cooking)
- Reflects on sustainability, equity, environmental impact
- Demonstrates systems thinking

### Written Reflection
- Reflects on personal food experiences and learning
- Discusses connections between nutrition and other health dimensions
- Addresses food values, cultural practices, joy in eating
- Shows growth and developing healthy food relationship

### Research Project (High School/College)
- Researches a nutrition question using primary sources
- Evaluates research methodology and limitations
- Synthesizes findings and reaches conclusions
- Communicates results clearly
- Demonstrates sophisticated understanding

### Group Discussion or Debate
- Discusses a nutrition-related issue (food systems, claims, policy, equity)
- Supports positions with evidence
- Listens respectfully to other perspectives
- Thinks critically and asks good questions

### Teaching or Advocacy Project (Optional)
- Creates materials to teach others (infographic, video, presentation, social media campaign)
- Explains nutrition concept or claim clearly
- Demonstrates understanding through ability to teach

## Assessment Principles

### What We Value
- Growth in understanding and thinking
- Quality of questions and critical thinking
- Ability to evaluate evidence and claims
- Appreciation for food systems and equity
- Developing healthy food relationship
- Practical application of knowledge

### What We Don't Measure
- Weight, body size, or appearance
- Food choices compliance ("eating healthily")
- Whether learner follows a specific diet
- Calorie counting or food restriction accuracy
- Morality of food choices

### Avoiding Harm
- Never use body-focused language
- Never compare learners' food choices or bodies
- Respect diverse food practices and cultural traditions
- Support learners with food insecurity
- Have mental health resources available
- Avoid pressuring eating or restriction
- Recognize eating disorders and seek help

---

## Differentiation

### For Younger Learners (K–2)
- Focus on sensory experience and observation
- Use pictures, sorting, tasting activities
- Keep explanations concrete and simple
- Emphasize fun and exploration

### For Elementary Learners (3–5)
- Connect to concepts they're learning in science
- Introduce nutrient names and basic functions
- Begin label reading and comparison
- Discuss farm-to-table in concrete terms

### For Middle School (6–8)
- Integrate with chemistry and cells concepts
- Deeper understanding of digestion and metabolism
- Critical evaluation of claims
- Food systems with environmental and social dimensions

### For High School (9–12)
- Biochemistry integration
- Research evaluation and methodology
- Food policy and systems analysis
- Specialized interests (nutrition science, food justice, agriculture, etc.)

---

## Self-Assessment & Reflection

Throughout the unit, learners can reflect on their own learning:

- "What did I learn about nutrients?"
- "How has my thinking about food systems changed?"
- "Do I feel more confident reading labels? Why?"
- "How do I evaluate nutrition claims now?"
- "What am I still wondering about?"
- "How is my relationship with food changing?"

Reflection builds metacognition and ownership of learning.

---

## Family Connection

Assessment doesn't end at school. Family conversation supports learning:

- Share what you learned with family
- Use your nutrition knowledge in family cooking
- Discuss food choices together
- Notice connections between nutrition and how you feel
- Ask family about their food values and practices

---

## Final Note

The goal of NUTR-101 assessment is not compliance or restriction. It's developing nutritional understanding, critical thinking, food systems awareness, and a healthy, joyful relationship with food. Assessment should support that goal, not undermine it through shame, comparison, or food morality.
