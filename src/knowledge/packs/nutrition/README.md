## About NUTR-101

**Why nutrition?**

Nutrition touches everyone. Every person eats, thinks about food, and makes food choices. Yet most people learn nutrition in fragments: pyramids in elementary school, calories in health class, contradictory messages in media.

NUTR-101 creates integrated, evidence-based understanding of:
- **Nutrients and their functions** in the body
- **Digestion and metabolism** — how food becomes energy and structure
- **Food systems and agriculture** — where food comes from and the environmental/social dimensions
- **Nutritional literacy** — how to read labels, evaluate claims, recognize marketing
- **Health integration** — how nutrition connects to sleep, stress, activity, and mental health

## How to Use This Pack

### Option 1: Full Curriculum
Teach all 5 modules sequentially over a semester or year. Each module takes 15–40 hours depending on grade level.

### Option 2: Integrated
Pull individual activities into existing science, health, or social studies classes.

### Option 3: Focused Unit
Choose 2–3 modules that align with your learning goals.

**Example sequences:**
- **Grades K–2:** M1 (nutrients) + M5 (cooking and food joy)
- **Grades 3–5:** M1 → M2 → M3 → M5 (linear pathway)
- **Grades 6–8:** All 5 modules (depth increases with chemistry and systems thinking)
- **High School:** All 5 modules + research projects and biochemistry depth
- **College:** M1–M5 + specialized study in nutrition science, food policy, agriculture, etc.

## Key Features

### Evidence-Based
All content is grounded in nutrition science, not diet culture. We teach what the research actually says, not what social media promotes.

### Culturally Responsive
Diverse cuisines, food practices, and cultural food traditions are centered, not marginalized.

### Food-Insecurity Aware
Acknowledges that nutritional advice without food access is not helpful. Includes resources for families in food-insecure situations.

### Accessible
Activities adapt for food allergies, restrictions, preferences, and accessibility needs. No specialized equipment required.

### Systems-Focused
Nutrition is taught in context: How do sleep, stress, activity, and mental health interact with food? How do agriculture, environment, and economics shape what we eat?

### Without Diet Culture
Never uses "good foods" and "bad foods" language, never mentions weight or appearance as health measures, never pressures eating or restriction.

## Assessment Philosophy

Assessment evaluates growth in understanding and literacy, not compliance with nutritional rules.

**What we measure:**
- Can students identify nutrients and their functions?
- Do they understand digestion and body systems?
- Can they trace food systems and discuss sustainability?
- Do they evaluate claims using evidence?
- Can they design balanced meals reflecting personal values?
- Do they demonstrate a healthy relationship with food?

**What we don't measure:**
- Weight or body size
- Food choices compliance
- "Good" vs. "bad" eating
- Calorie counting accuracy

## Parent Guidance

This pack teaches nutrition science, not dieting. That's an important distinction.

See `NUTR-101-vision.md` for detailed parent guidance including:
- How this pack differs from diet culture
- Language to use at home that supports healthy food relationships
- What to do if your family experiences food insecurity
- How to talk about nutrition without weight or shame

**Key principle:** Support your child's innate hunger and fullness cues. Avoid using food as reward or punishment. Model a peaceful relationship with food.

## For Educators

### Getting Started
1. Read `NUTR-101-vision.md` to understand the pedagogy
2. Review the modules in `NUTR-101-modules.yaml`
3. Choose your pathway (linear, spiral, or interest-led)
4. Pull activities from `NUTR-101-activities.json` aligned to your timeline

### Accessibility & Adaptation
All activities are designed to adapt:
- Food allergies? Use different foods in sorting and tasting activities.
- Food insecurity? Focus on systems and literacy; adjust meal planning to realistic scenarios.
- Diverse abilities? All activities have scaffolding options.

### Cultural Responsiveness
- Include cuisines from learners' backgrounds
- Value traditional foods and preparation methods
- Discuss food history and cultural significance alongside nutrition
- Avoid the assumption that any one diet is "best"

### Avoiding Diet Culture
- Use "more often" and "sometimes" instead of "good" and "bad"
- Never mention weight, BMI, or appearance
- Don't pressure eating or restriction
- Include diverse body sizes and types in all visuals
- Have mental health resources available
