# BUS-101: Business & Law -- Assessment Framework

This assessment framework evaluates business reasoning, legal literacy, financial judgment, and ethical thinking -- not memorization of terminology. The focus is on how learners analyze situations, make decisions, and consider consequences, not what vocabulary they can recite.

## Beginning

The learner is starting to develop business and legal thinking. At this level:

- Can identify that businesses sell things and people buy them, but understanding of why is limited
- Treats money as simple (you have it or you do not) without understanding earning, saving, or budgeting tradeoffs
- Follows rules without understanding why rules exist or how they protect people
- When presented with an ethical dilemma, tends toward a single perspective (usually their own)
- Can name some types of businesses but cannot explain how they differ structurally
- Struggles to distinguish between needs and wants in spending decisions
- Identifies fairness in simple situations but cannot articulate what makes something fair
- Needs significant guidance to complete business or legal reasoning tasks

**What success looks like at Beginning level:** The learner can describe what a business does, identify basic needs and wants, and express opinions about fairness, even if the reasoning is undeveloped.

## Developing

The learner is building business and legal reasoning with guidance. At this level:

- Understands that prices change based on supply and demand, at least in familiar examples
- Can create a simple budget but may not balance it or consider all expenses
- Recognizes that different business types exist and can name basic differences (who owns it, who is responsible)
- Identifies 2-3 stakeholders in a business scenario but misses less obvious ones
- Can follow the logic of a simple contract but may not spot missing elements or ambiguity
- Beginning to understand that laws protect people and that rights come with responsibilities
- When presented with an ethical dilemma, considers two perspectives but may not weigh them
- Can read simple financial information but struggles to draw conclusions from it

**What success looks like at Developing level:** The learner can explain basic economic concepts with real-world examples, create a guided budget, and identify the most important stakeholders in a business decision.

## Proficient

The learner independently applies business and legal reasoning. At this level:

- Analyzes market situations using supply and demand reasoning without prompting
- Creates realistic budgets with appropriate categories, tradeoff decisions, and justifications
- Compares business structures and recommends appropriate types for given scenarios with reasoning
- Reads and interprets simplified financial statements, extracting meaningful insights
- Identifies all essential elements of a contract and spots ambiguity or missing terms
- Understands basic legal rights (consumer, employee, citizen) and when they apply
- Considers multiple stakeholders in ethical analysis, including non-obvious ones
- Distinguishes between legal compliance and ethical responsibility
- Can explain business and legal concepts to others clearly and accurately
- Makes financial decisions based on analysis rather than impulse

**What success looks like at Proficient level:** The learner can analyze a new business situation, identify the economic forces at play, evaluate it from multiple stakeholder perspectives, and make a reasoned recommendation supported by evidence.

## Advanced

The learner demonstrates sophisticated business judgment and legal reasoning. At this level:

- Applies economic models to predict market behavior and evaluate policy proposals
- Creates comprehensive financial analyses with projections, ratios, and trend identification
- Evaluates complex business structures considering tax, liability, governance, and strategic implications
- Reads and analyzes real legal documents (contracts, terms of service, regulations) critically
- Applies multiple ethical frameworks to business dilemmas and recognizes framework limitations
- Understands how law, regulation, and market forces interact to shape business behavior
- Identifies systemic issues (market failures, regulatory capture, structural inequality) in economic systems
- Mentors other learners through business reasoning and legal analysis tasks
- Recognizes when a situation requires professional legal or financial advice
- Integrates knowledge across modules to analyze complex, real-world business scenarios

**What success looks like at Advanced level:** The learner can take a complex real-world business case, analyze it from economic, legal, financial, and ethical perspectives, identify the key tradeoffs, and present a well-reasoned recommendation that accounts for multiple stakeholder interests.

## Formative Assessment

Ongoing assessment strategies used during learning activities:

- **Economic Reasoning Check:** When discussing prices, markets, or business decisions, does the student reference supply, demand, and incentives? Or do they attribute prices to arbitrary decisions ("they charge that because they want to")? Push for causal reasoning.
- **Budget Review:** Is the budget realistic and complete? Does it include all major categories? Can the student explain why they prioritized certain expenses over others? Are tradeoff decisions explicit and justified?
- **Contract Element Identification:** Given a real agreement (rental lease, terms of service, employment offer), can the student identify the parties, obligations, rights, duration, and consequences of breach? Do they spot ambiguous or missing terms?
- **Business Structure Matching:** Given a business scenario (freelance artist, group of doctors starting a practice, social media startup seeking investors), can the student recommend a structure and explain the tradeoffs?
- **Ethical Reasoning Quality:** When presented with a dilemma, does the student identify multiple stakeholders or just consider one perspective? Do they acknowledge complexity, or do they default to simple right/wrong thinking?
- **Financial Statement Comprehension:** Can the student extract key numbers from a financial statement and explain what they mean in plain language? Can they compare two periods and explain what changed?
- **Legal Literacy Application:** Can the student identify the legal dimensions of everyday situations (signing a lease, accepting a job offer, clicking "I agree")? Do they know when to seek professional advice?

## Summative Assessment

Evidence of mastery gathered at key milestones:

- **Business Plan Portfolio:** A complete business plan for a realistic venture, including market analysis, financial projections (revenue, costs, break-even), business structure choice with justification, legal requirements, and ethical considerations. Assessed on realism, completeness, financial accuracy, and quality of reasoning.
- **Legal Document Analysis:** Given an unfamiliar real-world document (lease, employment agreement, or terms of service), summarize its key terms in plain language, identify the rights and obligations of each party, flag any concerning or unusual terms, and recommend whether to sign with modifications. Assessed on comprehension accuracy and critical analysis.
- **Ethics Case Study:** Analyze a real business ethics case (corporate scandal, regulatory failure, or ethical dilemma). Identify all stakeholders, describe the ethical principles at stake, evaluate the options that were available, assess what actually happened, and recommend what should have been done differently. Assessed on depth of stakeholder analysis, quality of ethical reasoning, and use of evidence.
- **Economic Analysis:** Given real-world economic data (price changes, market trends, policy proposals), explain what is happening using economic concepts, predict likely outcomes, and identify who benefits and who is harmed. Assessed on analytical accuracy and reasoning quality.
- **Peer Teaching:** Explain a business or legal concept to someone who has not studied it. The ability to make complex ideas clear and accurate for a non-expert audience demonstrates deep understanding beyond memorization.

## Portfolio Suggestions

The business and law portfolio should demonstrate growth in reasoning, judgment, and real-world application across multiple activities. Suggested portfolio structure:

- **3 to 5 completed projects** from different modules, showing breadth of business and legal thinking
- **Each project includes:** problem context, analysis process, key findings or decisions, supporting evidence (data, research, legal references), and reflection on what was learned
- **A realistic budget** that the student created and refined, with explanation of priorities and tradeoffs
- **A contract or legal document analysis** showing the ability to read, interpret, and critically evaluate legal language
- **An ethics case analysis** demonstrating multi-stakeholder reasoning and ethical judgment
- **A growth narrative:** a 1-page reflection on how the student's understanding of business, money, or law has changed from their earliest work to their most recent
- **A "biggest surprise" entry:** something the student learned that fundamentally changed how they think about business, money, or law, with explanation of why it surprised them and how it affects their decisions

Portfolios are assessed on reasoning quality (depth and soundness of analysis), real-world connection (application to genuine situations), growth evidence (improvement visible across projects), and reflection depth (quality of metacognitive analysis about learning).
